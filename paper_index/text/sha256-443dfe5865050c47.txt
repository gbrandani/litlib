Loop extrusion: theory meets single-molecule experiments Edward J. Banigan and Leonid A. Mirny Abstract

Chromosomes are organized as chromatin loops that promote segregation, enhancer-promoter interactions, and other genomic functions. Loops were hypothesized to form by ‘loop extrusion,’ by which structural maintenance of chromosomes (SMC) complexes, such as condensin and cohesin, bind to chromatin, reel it in, and extrude it as a loop. However, such exotic motor activity had never been observed. Following an explosion of indirect evidence, recent single-molecule experiments directly imaged DNA loop extrusion by condensin and cohesin in vitro. These experiments observe rapid (kb/s) extrusion that requires ATP hydrolysis and stalls under pN forces. Surprisingly, condensin extrudes loops asymmetrically, challenging previous models. Extrusion by cohesin is symmetric but requires the protein Nipbl. We discuss how SMC complexes may perform their functions on chromatin in vivo. Introduction The genome is a set of extremely long (w1 cm) polymer chains constituting chromosomes, which encode cellular structure and function in their sequences. Despite their complexity, these large molecules are spatially and dynamically organized, which promotes and regulates critical cellular functions including gene expression, DNA replication and damage repair, chromosome compaction, and segregation [1e4]. It has long been Current Opinion in Cell Biology 2020, 64:124–138

known that chromatin loops are an essential component of chromosome compaction and organization [5e7], but the molecular biophysical mechanisms by which organization is coordinated were unknown. Here, we discuss the culmination of several decades of theory and experiments showing that a novel molecular motor activity known as ‘loop extrusion’ can dynamically grow and organize chromosome loops throughout the cell cycle. Loop extrusion is a motor-driven process by which a protein complex gradually grows and enlarges chromatin/DNA loops by reeling in the chromatin fiber or DNA. This unusual motor activity is primarily ascribed to a widely conserved class of protein complexes known as structural maintenance of chromosomes (SMC) complexes, which includes cohesin and condensin (although other protein motors may extrude small (generally <10 kb) DNA loops [8e17]). The sizes and positions of loops generated by SMC complexes can be controlled by various biomolecular and biochemical factors, including proteins that can pause or stop extrusion [18e25] and proteins that alter SMC complex dynamics [18,26e32]. Consistent with the hypothesis of loop extrusion, SMC complexes are ring-like structures that can open and topologically or pseudo-topologically enclose one or two strands of DNA (e.g., encircling either a DNA strand or the base of a DNA loop). Motor activity could be driven by one or both of the ATPase domains, each of which is located at the end of a SMC protein subunit. The SMC proteins are long coiled coils that end at a SMC dimerization interface known as the hinge. The ATPases are connected to each other by a kleisin subunit, which can associate with proteins called Hawks (HEAT proteins associated with kleisins) and Kites (kleisin-interacting winged-helix tandem elements) that are involved in SMC complex function [4,33,34]. Over the years, loop extrusion by SMC complexes has been introduced to explain a broad range of genomic structures and functions: mitotic chromosome compaction and segregation [35e38], meiotic chromosome organization [39], interphase domains [20,24], and functional interactions (e.g., enhancer-promoter, VDJ, protocadherins) [1,4,40e43,161,162]. Other possible roles of loop extrusion may include mechanisms of meiotic recombination, transport of proteins along the

[Page 2]

genome, dosage compensation, and DNA damage repair [2,4,44]. However, until recently, loop extrusion was an appealing but unproven hypothesis. A series of recent single-molecule experiments have directly imaged DNA loop extrusion by condensin and cohesin, bringing to life decades of inference and provocative hypotheses.

The genesis of loop extrusion The idea of protein motors that can extrude DNA loops dates back at least as far as the 1980s, to studies of VDJ and bacterial recombination [166,167] and type I restriction enzymes [45]. In experiments studying VDJ recombination, Wood and Tonegawa [166] observed that diversity (D) segments were unfailingly connected to joining (J) segments in a single orientation. They proposed an associative tracking model, in which each of two subunits of a DNA-bound recombinase translocates until finding its target segment [166], in effect extruding a DNA loop. Similarly, bacterial restriction and recombination enzymes were proposed to search for their restriction sites by DNA tracking, while bound at another site [45,167]. It was subsequently postulated that such ‘DNA reeling,’ observed for specific enzymes, could be a general mechanism of chromosome folding that generates an array of consecutive w100 kb loops [46], as is observed by electron microscopy [5e7] of mitotic chromosomes, and furthermore, that reeling might facilitate long-ranged enhancer-promoter interactions. Genetic screens [47] and in vitro [48,49] and in situ [50] chromosome condensation experiments implicated the SMC complex condensin as an essential factor for mitotic chromosome compaction. Several authors hypothesized that SMC complexes might act as motors [174,52]. It was later proposed that the SMC complex condensin might translocate and progressively grow DNA loops to compact mitotic chromosomes [53,54] and furthermore that the SMC complex cohesin might perform the same activity in other contexts [54]. Indeed, lengthwise compaction offers an elegant mechanism for generating linearly ordered regions of cis chromosomal contacts [55]. In parallel, most studies of cohesin focused on its role in sister chromatid cohesion [56], but several in vivo experiments suggested a role for cohesins in forming intrachromosomal loops [42,57,58]. However, despite the knowledge that SMC complexes are ATPases [59e61], there was no evidence that they possessed a motor function. Nonetheless, active loop extrusion by condensin as a mechanism of lengthwise chromosome compaction was modeled quantitatively in a theoretical and computational physical model a decade later [35], when it was shown that an ensemble of loop-extruding motors could dynamically linearly compact a segment of DNA/

chromatin. In this model, loop-extruding complexes could bind to and unbind from chromatin stochastically, and they contained two motor subunits that could translocate in opposite directions, thus extruding the intervening DNA as a loop (Figure 1a); extrusion continues until encountering another condensin or unbinding from DNA. This model leads to the emergence of a linear array of large loops, each reinforced by multiple extruding complexes at its base, making the loops robust to dissociation of individual extruders (Figure 1b). Subsequent theory and simulations were necessary to determine that loop extrusion could actually generate experimentally observed mitotic chromosome compaction and conformations. 3D polymer simulations showed that a linearly ordered ‘brush’ of consecutive polymer loops could reproduce Hi-C maps for metaphase chromosomes [62]. Simulations and theory then showed that such an array of consecutive loops can emerge from the collective activity of many loop extruders that dynamically exchange with the solvent in steady state [38]. These simulations showed that the boundaries assumed in the original computational model by Alipour and Marko [35] are not necessary for the formation of a dense array of extruded loops. Moreover, boundaries are inconsistent with Hi-C maps of metaphase chromosomes [62]. Finally, 3D polymer simulations showed that loop extrusion leads to the compact morphologies and spatial resolution of sister chromatids in a physiologically plausible time [37]. Experimentally, both Hi-C and experiments with purified proteins in cell extracts indicated that condensin is one of a small set of proteins required to compact metazoan mitotic chromosomes [36,63,64].

Progressive growth of the loop extrusion hypothesis Loop extrusion also offers a powerful mechanism for explaining cohesin-driven chromosome organization in interphase cells in higher eukaryotes [20,24]. Hi-C experiments revealed that cohesin organizes chromatin into 100 kb - 1 Mb regions of high self-contact frequency (topologically associating domains, TADs) [23,27,30,32,65e69], which are typically bordered by convergently oriented CCCTC-binding factor (CTCF) binding sites (i.e., sites ‘pointing’ into the TAD; Figure 1c) [18,19,21e25,32,65,68,69]. This seemingly requires a mechanism for 1D scanning along the chromatin fiber, which can be achieved by active loop extrusion. Thus, cohesin SMC complexes were hypothesized to translocate along chromatin, extruding loops until encountering properly oriented CTCF sites [20,24] (Figure 1c). Thus, loop extrusion can generate

Despite the appeal of loop extrusion as explaining a variety of chromosome organization phenomena, the model was postulated and developed without direct observations of the SMC complex motor activity and loop-extruding function. In fact, condensins were widely thought to compact chromosomes by randomly bridging DNA segments [81e84] or by DNA supercoiling [44,48,53,85,86]. These results in particular led to the proposal of sliplink models, in which passive cohesin complexes are loaded at a particular site, and diffusively slide along DNA, with their motion biased by the ‘osmotic pressure’ of cohesin complexes that are subsequently loaded [95,96]. Thus, as indirect evidence both favoring and disfavoring loop extrusion mounted, loop extrusion remained as only a hypothesis.

Direct observations of loop extrusion in single-molecule experiments The first direct observation of condensin motor activity came from total internal reflection fluorescence (TIRF) experiments observing quantum-dot-tagged yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) condensins translocating along double-tethered DNA curtains [97]. In these experiments, lambda DNA was stretched and tethered at its two ends, and condensin molecules were observed to move at speeds of 60 bp/s, for distances of >10 kb, with a bulk hydrolysis rate of w2 ATP/s per condensin. This translocation speed is much less than the 1 kb/s compaction rate observed in vitro for Xenopus condensin [98] and the 300e800 bp/s rate of chromosome arm juxtaposition by bacterial SMC complexes measured by

[Page 4]

Hi-C and ChIP-seq experiments in Bacillus subtilis [78] and Caulobacter crescentus [77]. Moreover, the measured bulk hydrolysis rate provides only a lower bound on the hydrolysis rate during translocation because the fraction of functional, DNA-bound condensin complexes is unknown. Nonetheless, this report provided critical evidence in favor of loop extrusion because condensin translocation combined with anchoring of a different part of the condensin to DNA would be sufficient to generate a loop. Consistent with this idea, the TIRF experiments showed directed translocation of freely floating DNA adjacent to DNA curtains loaded with condensins [97] (which could be interpreted as loop extrusion in trans, possibly also previously observed in single-molecule experiments with B. subtilis SMC complexes [99]). Moreover, a contemporaneous structural study revealed that the kleisin (Brn1) and a Hawk (Ycg1) subunit of yeast condensin can act as a ‘safety belt’ that anchors condensin to DNA [100]. However, perhaps due to the stretching and tethering of the DNA molecules or because of the relatively high-salt conditions (125 mM NaCl) [97], loop extrusion by condensin on a single DNA molecule remained elusive. Experiments by Ganji et al. [101] provided the critical breakthrough and, for the first time, directly imaged loop extrusion. In these experiments, lambda DNA was tethered at two ends, but in a loose configuration so that the tension within the molecule was relatively low, typically <1 pN. By using dyed DNA molecules and flowing in yeast condensin at lower salt conditions (50 mM NaCl), they could observe tightening of the DNA molecules and accumulation of DNA at individual foci colocalized with single condensin molecules (Figure 2a). 10’s of kb of DNA were pulled into these foci at high speeds of up to 1.5 kb/s, while hydrolyzing w1 ATP/s [101]. These measurements demonstrate the plausibility of rapid mitotic chromosome compaction by loop extrusion during prophase and prometaphase [36]. To visualize the structures generated by condensin, Ganji et al. [101] applied a flow perpendicular to the DNA (Figure 2b). The DNA focus stretched under flow, and high-temporal-resolution imaging revealed that the accumulated DNA formed an extruded loop; moreover, progressive DNA loop extrusion (rather than compaction by large jumps) could be observed in real time while the flow was applied. Importantly, this experiment also revealed that condensin extruded a loop, rather than a supercoil. As a result, this experimental setup would be used in several subsequent single-molecule experiments observing DNA loop extrusion by SMC complexes.

Ganji et al. [101] also made the surprising, but important, observation that DNA loop extrusion by yeast condensins proceeds asymmetrically, in a one-sided manner. Furthermore, experiments with a genetic mutant show that robust, directed one-sided extrusion apparently depends on anchoring by Ycg1-Brn1 [100,101]. As we will further discuss, these observations contrast with previous models, which assumed twosided extrusion [20,24,35,37,38] (Figure 1a). This contrast poses the major theoretical challenge of whether one-sided extrusion can perform the large variety of tasks credited to two-sided extrusion. Subsequent observations of loop extrusion by Kong et al. [102] suggest a possible resolution to this issue by imaging putative two-sided extrusion by human condensins I and II. Here, a modified DNA curtain assay was used in which DNA was tethered at its two ends to a barrier and a flow was applied so that the DNA appeared to be Ushaped (Figure 2c). Condensins were observed to bind DNA, which resulted in rapid, processive narrowing of the U shape, interpreted as loop extrusion. Interestingly, narrowing appeared to occur symmetrically in,

Moreover, whether two-sided extrusion might occur due to structural differences between the different species of condensin or dimerization of the condensin molecules is unclear. Photobleaching assays suggest that the majority of extruding condensins are individual complexes, but w30e50% are dimers. The single-molecule experiments described are the first direct visual observations of DNA loop extrusion by condensins and cohesins. They also report four motor properties: (1) the extrusion speed of w1 kb/s (w300 nm/s) [101e103,106,107], (2) the stall force of w1 pN [101,103,106], (3) the ATP hydrolysis rate of at least w1 s 1 [97,101,102,106,107], and (4) the different types of interactions between the SMC complexes and DNA [101,106,107]. As we will further discuss, these properties are critical to understanding the physiology, mode, and mechanism of loop extrusion by SMC complexes in vivo.

Loop extrusion on crowded chromosomes Loop extrusion on naked DNA is fast, with translocation proceeding at speeds of 0.5e2 kb/s on naked DNA, comparable with DNA polymerase (w1 kb/s [113]) and much faster than RNA polymerase (w1 kb/min, e.g., see [114]). From matching Hi-C experiments for metazoan cells to simulations, it is estimated that loops extruded by cohesins and condensins [20,27,36,152] are w100e 200 kb on average, while the complexes remain bound to DNA for w5e30 min [32,58,115e118,133]. Together, these measurements suggest speeds of order 0.1 kb/s. This speed is likely only a few 10’s of nm/s on nucleosomal DNA, while in vitro extrusion on naked DNA is in the 200e700 nm/s range. This observation suggests that various obstacles or cohesin- or condensin-binding factors

Extrusion of nucleosome-bound DNA

Chromatin is loaded with a variety of other factors that could potentially interact with, pause, or stall loop extrusion. One such factor is Pds5, which competes with Nipbl to bind cohesin [109,124,170]. By displacing the processivity factor Nipbl, Pds5 might halt loop extrusion [4,32]. Moreover, displacement could occur intermittently, contributing to the observed rapid turnover of Nipbl [110]. Together, these effects could lead to a lower average extrusion speed in vivo as compared with in vitro.

Previously, it was shown that nucleosomes can act as permeable barriers that slow cohesins diffusing along DNA [87,105]. However, Kong et al. [102] and Y. Kim et al. [106] observed that human condensins and cohesins, respectively, could bypass individual nucleosomes sparsely bound (w10 kb apart) to DNA without displacing them. This resulted in extrusion of nucleosome-bound DNA with mean speeds (in nm/s) and processivities (in mm) comparable with those of extrusion of naked DNA. While it is reasonable to think that a single 11 nm nucleosome should be able to fit through a (50 nm SMC complex ring, it is less obvious how larger machinery, such as clusters of nucleosomes or RNA polymerase, might be compatible with chromatin loop extrusion. Overcoming other DNA motors

From the recent single-molecule experiments, several of the groups computed a force-velocity relation and stall forces of w1 pN [101,103,106], consistent with previous estimates from experiments with magnetic and optical tweezers [119e122]. This stall force is relatively small compared to that of various other DNA translocases, such as RNA polymerase (>10 pN [114]), type I restriction enzymes (>4 pN [17]), and bacterial FtsK (10’s of pN [14,16]) (but it is comparable to the w1 pN stall force of the chromatin remodeler RSC [12]). Conceivably, stronger DNA translocases could overpower loop extrusion by SMC complexes. Indeed, it was previously shown that transcription can push or displace cohesins freely diffusing on DNA in vitro [87] and bound to chromosomes in vivo [88e90,123]. These observations raise the question of how >100 kb loops can be extruded in vivo. Hi-C experiments and simulations of bacterial chromosomes suggest a surprising solution [79]. Generally, RNA polymerases can act as ‘moving barriers’ to loop extrusion by bacterial condensins and push condensins in the direction of transcription. Loop extrusion in the presence of other obstacles and regulatory factors

It is also well known that the protein CTCF stalls or pauses progressive loop formation by cohesin [18,20,22,24,32,41], and it has recently been shown that pausing depends on cohesin binding to the N-terminal domain of CTCF [125e128]. However, experiments additionally suggest that CTCF also interacts sterically with cohesin to pause extrusion [125,127,128]. This raises the possibility that other structures could also sterically stall loop extrusion. Despite this observation, processive juxtaposition of bacterial chromosome arms suggests that loop extrusion is not necessarily hindered by all steric obstacles [78]. Such translocation steps are larger than the size of an SMC complex (w50 nm) but in accord with measured SMC-driven DNA compaction steps measured in magnetic tweezers experiments [98,119,121,122,139]. However, these estimates are based on the bulk ATPase rate, which could underestimate the hydrolysis rate because the fraction of SMC complexes that are active at any moment is unknown. There are several proposals for how SMC complexes might progressively extrude loops. These are reviewed in more detail elsewhere [4,33,34]. Briefly, there are (i) loop capture/pumping models in which DNA segments are captured and stored by a topologically or pseudotopologically bound SMC complex [140e142], (ii) scrunching models in which the SMC coiled coils cyclically bend and straighten to reel DNA into a loop [33,97], (iii) inchworm models in which the kleisin subunit cyclically stretches and contracts to drive translocation [143,144], (iv) a ‘climbing’ model in which complexes in a dimer alternately bind and unbind DNA [145,146], (v) a myosin-like model in which the SMC complex exhibits walking-like motion along DNA [51,52,97,147], and (vi) slip-link models in which SMC complexes passively diffuse [95,96]. Observations of active translocation by SMC complexes [78,101e 103,106,107,138], the ATP dependence of TAD [137] and mitotic chromosome formation [64], and modeling of bacterial chromosomes [80] argue against the in vivo relevance of diffusive slip links. Given the experimentally observed conformations of SMC complexes [102,140,148e150], the loop capture and scrunching models seem to be the most plausible, but the mechanism could differ for different SMC complexes [146]. Moreover, mechanisms such as loop capture and scrunching are not mutually exclusive [4,33,34].

The reason for this surprising compaction shortfall is that neighboring condensins that translocate away from each other to extrude chromatin into loops leave a stretch of unextruded chromatin (a ‘gap’) between them. We and others also used simulations to demonstrate that pure one-sided extrusion is insufficient to perform other critical SMC functions, including the formation of interphase TADs [152] and the juxtaposition of bacterial chromosome arms [80,152]. Our modeling suggests that SMC complexes could overcome these challenges if they are capable of becoming ‘effectively two-sided’ [151,152]. This could occur, for example, through a strand switching mechanism [33,142,151,152] or dimerization, both of which are compatible with the loop-capture model for motor activity [142]. Directional switching has not yet been directly detected. However, experiments by Golfier et al. [103] in Xenopus extracts are suggestive of switching; loops extruded by asymmetric (one-sided) condensins and loops extruded by symmetric (two-sided) cohesins grow at the same rate, so each side of a cohesin translocates at half the speed of the active side of a condensin. This observation is consistent with the idea that condensin and cohesin each have a single motor for extrusion; for condensin, this motor extrudes along a single substrate DNA strand, while for cohesin, the motor can rapidly switch the substrate strand, and thus, the direction along which extrusion proceeds on DNA. Golfier et al. [103] also observe that while cohesin can extrude loops either symmetrically or asymmetrically, symmetrically extruded loops grow at twice the speed of asymmetrically extruded loops. This is consistent with a single motor that switches between two DNA strands, where for some cohesins one strand cannot be extruded into a loop.

Another key puzzle with is how and why some condensins perform an apparently one-sided mode of loop extrusion [101]. The original computational loop extrusion models generally assumed that extrusion is two-sided, which explained a variety of chromosome organization phenomena in metaphase and interphase [20,24,35,37,38].

Alternatively, this observation could indicate that cohesins are one-sided but typically form dimers. Dimerization has been observed or suggested by a number of other experiments [99,106,121,145,146,153e 157].

One-sided and effectively two-sided extrusion

Asymmetric two-sided extrusion

To determine whether one-sided condensins can linearly compact metazoan mitotic chromosomes, we developed a mathematical model for one-sided loop extrusion [151]. We found that, irrespective of the processivity and abundance of extruders, pure one-sided extrusion can linearly compact a chromatin polymer fiber only 10-fold. Experiments by Golfier et al. [103] also show that loop extrusion by Xenopus condensins is predominantly asymmetric, but it may proceed in a two-sided manner. In particular, w50% of condensins perform two-sided extrusion with slow and fast sides extruding with different speeds (vfast > vslow > vfast/10) [103]. Theoretical and computational modeling indicates that even with this degree of asymmetry, two-sided extrusion can

[Page 9]

linearly compact chromosomes 1000-fold if the residence time is long enough to allow condensins to close gaps between slowly extruding sides ([151], EJ Banigan and LA Mirny, unpublished, 2020). This condition is satisfied for experimentally measured condensin residence times [115,133], velocities [101e103], and linear densities [133,168,169]. Mixtures of one- and two-sided extruders

Another interpretation of the experiments in Xenopus extracts is that metaphase DNA compaction is driven by a mixture of one- and two-sided condensin complexes [103], in which w20% are two-sided and the rest are one-sided. Our previous modeling work showed that a 20/80 mixture is incapable of achieving a high degree of linear compaction, and a mixture with at least 85% two-sided extruders is required to achieve strong (1000-fold) compaction [151,152]. However, further modeling work shows that this minimum threshold can be lowered if the residence time of two-sided condensins is longer than that of one-sided condensins. In such a system, one-sided condensins (and associated gaps) only transiently impede extrusion by two-sided condensins. Thus, the chromosome can be compacted primarily by two-sided extrusion, even if most of the loop-extruding condensins (and/or loop extrusion events) are one-sided. For example, in a hypothetical system in which 60% of chromatin-bound condensins are one-sided (and 40% are two-sided), 1000-fold compaction can be achieved if the residence time of the two-sided condensins is w20-fold longer than that of the one-sided condensins (EJ Banigan and LA Mirny, unpublished, 2020). Such a scenario is generally consistent with the hypothesis that vertebrate condensin I, which is evolutionarily conserved from yeast [44], is primarily one-sided (as observed), while condensin II is primarily two-sided. Furthermore, this scenario is also consistent with fluorescence recovery after photobleaching (FRAP) experiments showing that the residence time of condensin I is shorter than that of condensin II [115,133] and Hi-C experiments and analysis demonstrating that small loops anchored by condensin I are nested inside of larger loops anchored by condensin II [36]. Z-loops

Yet another solution is suggested by recent in vitro experiments imaging the extrusion dynamics of multiple yeast condensins on a single DNA molecule [108]. As described in the discussion of DNA binding, these experiments show that one condensin does not serve as a barrier to the translocation of another condensin on DNA. This permits effectively two-sided loop growth by pairs of one-sided condensins (Figure 3a). Z-loopmediated compaction could lead to a diffuse quasicentralized distribution of condensins [152], as

Current Opinion in Cell Biology 2020, 64:124–138

recently imaged [133,158], while only moderately compacting chromosomes into an interconnected gel [158,159] or chain of chromatin rings (Figure 3b). Moreover, while condensin bypassing has not been directly observed on chromatin, it may impact mitotic chromosome compaction in vivo; mutation of the SMC4 ATPase site inhibits Z-loop formation by yeast condensins and, puzzlingly, results in hypercompaction of human mitotic chromosomes [160], rather than undercompaction as the one-sided extrusion model predicts [151,152]. We predict that to achieve hypercompaction without Z-loops, linear compaction by condensin must perform effectively two-sided extrusion mediated by mechanisms other than Z-loops. Thus, one goal of future experiments will be to determine how condensin eliminates unlooped chromatin gaps and organizes mitotic chromosomes, whether by two-sided or effectively two-sided extrusion or by another mechanism.

The future of loop extrusion Single-molecule experiments have demonstrated that condensins and cohesins can extrude DNA loops that are 10’s of kb in size [101e103,106,107]. DNA loop extrusion is an appealing mechanism of 3D chromatin organization because it can take advantage of onedimensional features of the polymer genome, facilitating lengthwise compaction, cis loop formation, and 1D scanning [35,46,147]. The high velocity of extrusion (w1 kb/s) suggests that SMC complexes can compact and organize the genome by generating w100e1000 kb loops within minutes [152]. Nonetheless, future experiments will aim to explore the effects of in vitro conditions (such as higher, physiological ionic strengths and nucleosome-bound DNA) and observe the in vivo occurrence of loop extrusion. Such experiments could also provide insights into the mechanisms by which SMC complexes bind to DNA, interact with each other, translocate, and grow chromatin loops, as well as how they interact with other chromosomal elements and processes. These experiments will further challenge, develop, and translate the powerful conceptual framework of loop extrusion into an understanding of how SMC complexes promote genome function, from chromosome compaction and segregation to DNA repair and gene expression.
