SUMMARY

The SWR1 chromatin remodeling complex is recruited to +1 nucleosomes downstream of transcription start sites of eukaryotic promoters, where it exchanges histone H2A for the specialized variant H2A.Z. Here, we use cryoelectron microscopy (cryo-EM) to resolve the structural basis of the SWR1 interaction with free DNA, revealing a distinct open conformation of the Swr1 ATPase that enables sliding from accessible DNA to nucleosomes. A complete structural model of the SWR1-nucleosome complex illustrates critical roles for Swc2 and Swc3 subunits in oriented nucleosome engagement by SWR1. Moreover, an extended DNAbinding a helix within the Swc3 subunit enables sensing of nucleosome linker length and is essential for SWR1-promoter-specific recruitment and activity. The previously unresolved N-SWR1 subcomplex forms a flexible extended structure, enabling multivalent recognition of acetylated histone tails by reader domains to further direct SWR1 toward the +1 nucleosome. Altogether, our findings provide a generalizable mechanism for promoter-specific targeting of chromatin and transcription complexes.

INTRODUCTION Eukaryotic gene expression is fundamentally regulated by the architecture of chromatin, the complex of DNA and proteins that forms the structural basis of chromosomes. Central to this architecture are nucleosomes, the basic units of chromatin, each consisting of a segment of DNA wound around a core of histone proteins.1,2 A canonical nucleosome is composed of a histone octamer containing two each of H2A, H2B, H3, and H4 histones.3 However, the dynamics of gene regulation are influenced by the incorporation of histone variants, particularly H2A.Z, which replaces the canonical H2A primarily in +1 nucleosomes downstream of transcription start sites (TSSs) (reviewed in Giaimo et al.4). H2A.Z-containing nucleosomes represent 5,000 out of a total of 60,000 nucleosomes in the yeast genome.5,6 The H2A.Z-H2B dimer replaces nucleosomal H2A-H2B through the activity of the SWR1 chromatin remodeler, a 14-component complex harboring the catalytic Swr1 subunit that uses the energy of ATP hydrolysis to perform the histone dimer exchange

reaction.7–10 The SWR1 complex is known to be recruited genome-wide to nucleosome-free regions (NFRs) flanking the TSS.11,12 Despite its critical role in the transcription process, both the molecular mechanism of specific targeting of SWR1 to promoter-proximal nucleosomes, and the mechanism of histone exchange, are not well understood. SWR1 is divided into two major modules that assemble with the amino (N)-terminal or carboxy (C)-terminal halves of the Swr1 subunit, respectively, named N-SWR1 and CSWR1.11,13–15 The C-SWR1 module comprises residues 680– 1,400 of the Swr1 subunit, which includes the two RecA-like ATPase domains and the intervening insert domain that integrates into the RuvBL1/2 heterohexameric core. The remaining C-SWR1 subunits—Swc2, Swc3, Swc6, and Arp6—assemble onto the Swr1-RuvBL platform. Prior cryoelectron microscopy (cryo-EM) studies have resolved the C-SWR1 complex bound to a nucleosome, revealing that C-SWR1 forms a stable nucleosome-binding core.16 The N-SWR1 complex comprises the N-terminal 680 amino acids of the Swr1 protein and the 8 subunits—Swc4, Swc5, Swc7, Bdf1, Yaf9, Arp4, and two copies

In contrast to C-SWR1, which adopts a relatively rigid structure and stably engages the nucleosome, the extreme flexibility of N-SWR1 and weak nucleosome association has hindered its direct visualization in cryo-EM structures.16 Biochemical and biophysical studies have documented the preferred affinity of SWR1 for nucleosome-free DNA over nucleosome core particles (NCPs) and shown that SWR1 can undergo one-dimensional (1D) diffusion along naked DNA substrates until it encounters a nucleosome.11,17 To elucidate the structural basis for the free DNA-SWR1 interaction, we used cryo-EM to solve the structure of purified, endogenous yeast SWR1 complex bound to DNA. The resulting structure reveals that the C-SWR1 subcomplex forms extensive interactions with an 50-bp span of nucleosome-free naked DNA in an ‘‘open’’ conformation amenable to 1D diffusion. The open conformation transitions to a closed state upon recognition of specific nucleosomal features by the Swc2 subunit. In addition, we unveil an extended DNA-binding a helix within the Swc3 subunit that senses nucleosome linker length and is required for SWR1 recruitment and activity on +1 nucleosomes in cells. Finally, a model for the N-SWR1 subcomplex that presents a flexible extended structure, allowing multivalent recognition of acetylated histone tails, completes the salient features of the promoter-targeting mechanism of SWR1. RESULTS Structural basis of SWR1 complex binding to nucleosome-free DNA The affinity of SWR1 for nucleosome-free DNA of length > 40 bp over NCPs suggests that SWR1 has a mechanism for sensing extra-nucleosomal DNA.11 Previous cryo-EM structures of SWR1 bound to a nucleosome showed that the C-SWR1 module makes several contacts with nucleosomal DNA and histones, but how the enzyme senses long linker DNA was unclear.16 Further, the ability of SWR1 to undergo 1D diffusion along free DNA suggested a specific mechanism of DNA binding independent of nucleosome interactions.17 To elucidate the molecular basis for DNA binding, we used cryo-EM to solve a 3.4-Å-resolution structure of the endogenous yeast SWR1 complex bound to a 147-base pair (bp) DNA fragment in the presence of the nonhydrolyzable ATP analog ATPgS (Figures 1A, S1A, and S2A–S2J; Table 1). The resulting structure reveals that the C-SWR1 subcomplex forms extensive interactions with a roughly 50-bp span of naked DNA (Figures 1A, S2B, and S2E). The primary contacts with naked DNA occur with the Swr1-ATPase domain (Swr1ATPase) and the Swc6 subunit (Figures 1A and S2E), which notably also bind to nucleosomal DNA at super-helical location 2 (SHL2) and SHL6, respectively, in the context of the SWR1nucleosome complex.16 The stable association of the Swr1ATPase with naked DNA in the cryo-EM structure is consistent with the influence of nucleotide binding on 1D diffusion of SWR1 on DNA (Figure S3A).17 Contacts between Swr1ATPase and the DNA phosphate backbone closely match those within the nucleosome-bound structure, though Swr1ATPase binding confers a lesser bend to naked DNA, likely owing to the additional histone-induced DNA bending in the

nucleosomal context (Figure 1B). In the nucleosome-bound SWR1 structure, the Snf2-family-conserved ‘‘gating helix’’ of the Swr1ATPase was shown to access the minor groove of the nucleosomal DNA at SHL2 and also interact with the ‘‘brace’’ motif, another Snf2-family ATPase element that bridges the two RecAlike domains (Figures 1C and 1D).16,18,19 By contrast, in the DNA-bound complex, the difference in the minor groove conformation causes the gating helix to extend further away from the ATPase domain, which destabilizes the gating-brace interaction and causes the brace to adopt an extended conformation that is now stabilized by an interaction with the ‘‘suppressor helix’’ (SuppH),19 another Snf2-family-specific element (Figures 1D and S3B; Video S1). This results in an open conformation of the Swr1ATPase that closely resembles the nucleotide-free (apo) states of related ATPases, e.g., nucleosome-bound Snf2 and Mot1 bound to a DNA-TBP (TATA-binding protein) substrate (Figure S3C),20,21 notwithstanding that there is clear density for an ATPgS molecule bound to the Swr1ATPase (Figure S2I). To further elucidate the influence of nucleotide binding on the Swr1ATPase conformation in the context of DNA binding, we solved a cryo-EM structure of the SWR1-DNA complex in the absence of added nucleotide (Figures S1B and S3D). Comparison of the this SWR1(apo)-DNA structure with the SWR1(ATPgS)-DNA structure shows a near-identical conformation of the Swr1ATPase (Figures S3D and S3E). Altogether, we conclude that sensing of the DNA minor groove conformation by the Swr1ATPase gating helix in free DNA and nucleosome states dictates an open vs. closed conformation of the Swr1ATPase independent of nucleotide state (Video S1). We further speculate that the open Swr1ATPase conformation is conducive to 1D diffusion on DNA and that binding of ATP may stabilize this conformation to further facilitate diffusion. Previous cryo-EM studies of nucleosome-bound SWR1 attributed a Swr1ATPase-associated density to the Swc2 subunit,16 and structures of the INO80-nucleosome complex have shown that the Ies2 subunit contains a structurally similar segment, termed the Ies2 ‘‘throttle,’’ that binds to the Ino80 ATPase and is proposed to play a role in coupling ATPase activity to substrate binding.22,23 In our SWR1-DNA structure, the Swc2 throttle (Swc2throttle) forms an extended 41-residue a helix that creates an additional rigid brace between the RuvBL hexamer and both lobes of the Swr1ATPase that may further stabilize an open, sliding-permissive conformation (Figures 1A, 1E, S2G, and S2H; Video S1). The extended Swc2throttle and open Swr1ATPase conformation appears to be a specific state adopted upon binding to naked DNA, as the Swr1ATPase in the free SWR1 complex is completely flexible (Figures S1C and S3F). In addition to the primary nucleosomal contact of the Swr1ATPase domain at SHL2, the Swc6 subunit makes contact at nucleosome SHL6 via a solvent-exposed basic patch, but the path of the DNA along this patch differs substantially in the context of free DNA binding (Figure 1F). Given the flexible nature of this interaction observed in the cryo-EM density (Figures S2E and S3G), we postulate that the Swc6-DNA interaction is subordinate to the Swr1ATPase-DNA interaction and provides additional stability for the SWR1 complex on DNA. Following the Swc2throttle, Swc2 forms several interactions with the RuvBLs on a path toward the Arp6 subunit on the opposite side of the RuvBL hexamer, where an ‘‘Arp6-anchor’’ segment of Swc2 (residues 657–685) makes adjacent interactions with the Swr1-insert domain and Arp6 (region ‘‘VIII’’), wherein Swc2 F669 inserts into a deep hydrophobic pocket of Arp6, and E672 forms an electrostatic interaction with Arp6 R431 (Figures 2A, 2B, 2F, and S4I). The association between Arp6 and Swc2 has been previously shown to be important for integration of these two subunits in yeast SWR1.11,14 The remaining 100 amino acids in the C-terminal region of Swc2 (Swc2CTR) contain the eukaryotic-conserved ‘‘YL1c domain,’’ followed by a shorter, fungal-specific domain. This region could be traced from the Arp6 anchor motif into a hitherto unmodeled density of the cryo-EM map that resides near the nucleosome dyad (Figures 2F and S4J). Previous cryo-EM studies showed that exclusion of Swc3 from the complex results in the complete loss of this dyad-proximal density.16 Because our cryo-EM map data precluded de novo model building, we used AlphaFold2 to generate a predicted structure of a subcomplex composed of Swc3 and the Swc2CTR,27–29 which unambiguously fit into the cryo-EM density and allowed connection with the adjacent Swc2 Arp6-anchor (Figures 2F, S4J, and S5A–S5D). The resulting model shows that two adjacent motifs within the Swc2CTR play a role in recruiting Swc3 to the complex (Figure 2F, region ‘‘IX’’). First, the HIT-like fold of the Swc2-YL1c domain forms extensive interactions with the surface of the Swc3 core (Figure 2F). Second, the fungal-specific domain forms a b-hairpin (Swc2 b-wedge) that penetrates through the center of the Swc3 core (Figure 2F). Together, these elements constitute a Swc3-Swc2 subcomplex that significantly contributes to the SWR1-nucleosomal interface. Swc3 plays a multifaceted role in engaging the NFRproximal nucleosome The Swc3 core is made up of two adjacent domains, each containing segments from both N- and C-terminal regions of the protein (Figures 3A and S5A–S5D). We refer to the first as the Swc3docking domain as it provides a surface for the association of Swc2, Swc6, and Arp6 subunits (Figure 3B). The second domain adopts a pleckstrin homology (PH) fold, with multiple insertions involved in making nucleosomal contacts (Figure 3B). Our model of the Swc3-Swc2 subcomplex in the SWR1nucleosome structure illuminates several key interactions formed between Swc3 and the nucleosome (Figures 3C–3E). One of the insertions of the PH domain contains an a helix with a highly positively charged surface positioned to interact with the nucleosomal DNA backbone near SHL1 (‘‘Swc3 nucleosome helix’’; Figures 3A–3C and S5E). We were additionally able to clearly trace the histone H3 N-terminal tail as it exits the nucleosome at this location into a cleft formed between the Swc3 PH and docking domains, with the Swc2 b-wedge forming the back wall of the H3-binding cleft (Figures 3C and S5E). This contact is formed with H3 residues 28–35 and does not appear to involve interactions that would be affected

by H3 post-translational modifications (PTMs). Instead, we speculate that the coordinated binding of the H3 tail and SHL1 DNA likely anchors Swc3 stably in place, thus presenting a stable barrier that blocks DNA re-wrapping. Further, the sequestration of the highly basic H3 N terminus likely counteracts its stabilizing effect on DNA wrapping,30 thus further promoting unwrapping. In addition to the regions of Swc3 that could be directly observed in the cryo-EM structure, two conspicuous features are predicted within a 275-residue-long insert within the PH fold—an 80-residue-long a helix (LH), followed by a small helix-turn-helix (HTH) motif (Figures 3A, 3B, and S5A–S5D). The N terminus of the long a helix is visible at the edge of the cryo-EM density where, along with the C-terminal end of the long insert, it forms a large basic groove that cradles the linker DNA at the nucleosome exit on the NFR side (Figures 3D, 3E, and S5F). Between this ‘‘linker cradle’’ and the nucleosome, the DNA appears to adopt a sharp bend, possibly induced by strain imposed on the DNA by the relative positioning of the linker cradle interaction and the nucleosome edge (Figures 3D, S4A, and S4C). Further, there is weak additional density suggestive of small protein domain interacting with the DNA at the location of the bend, but the low resolution of this region in the cryo-EM map prohibits its identification (Figure S5F). As both the DNA and Swc3 LH can be reasonably expected to extend from the linker cradle as a rigid structure, we produced a simulated structure containing the extended NFR DNA and the complete LH (Figures 3D and S5F). Notably, LH is positively charged on one face along the entire 12-nm length—a specific patterning of basic residues suggesting that this segment adopts an a-helical structure (Figures 3D and S5G). Circular dichroism spectroscopy of a bacterially expressed fragment corresponding to Swc3 residues 177–255 provides biophysical evidence supporting the a-helical character of this fragment (Figure S5H). The bacterially expressed LH fragment also exhibits high affinity for double-stranded DNA in an electrophoretic mobility shift assay (EMSA), with a marked preference for DNA fragments > 40 bp, closely mirroring the preference of purified endogenous SWR1 (Figure 3F).11 To assess the overall contribution of Swc3 to DNA binding, we compared purified, endogenous SWR1 to SWR1 lacking Swc3 from mutant yeast and observed a roughly 3-fold reduction in free DNA binding (Figure 3G). Hence, we propose that this Swc3 region, including LH, has a role in sensing NFR accessibility and targeting SWR1 for specific histone exchange at promoter-proximal nucleosomes. The LH is a generally conserved feature in Swc3 of budding yeasts, though helix length is variable, with the 80-residue S. cerevisiae Swc3 LH representing the longer end of the range (Figure S5I). The predicted Swc3-HTH domain (residues 271– 322) that immediately follows LH is better conserved and was predicted in all fungal Swc3 proteins analyzed (Figures S5A and S5I). Although the HTH is a common DNA-binding motif, we observed a relatively low DNA affinity for a bacterially expressed Swc3 HTH by EMSA (Figure S5J). The function of conserved HTH requires further investigation.

Integrative structural model of the N-SWR1 acetylhistone-binding module The N-SWR1 subcomplex contains nine subunits and comprises approximately 40% of the total mass of the SWR1 complex. Prior biochemical studies have provided insights into the approximate architecture of N-SWR1 (Figure 5A).13,15 However, aside from a crystal structure of a ternary complex formed by Arp4, Act1, and a fragment of the Swr1 helicase-Sant-associated (HSA) domain,34 high-resolution structural information on N-SWR1 assembly is lacking due to its apparent flexibility, which has hindered its visualization in cryo-EM structures.16 We thus used an integrative modeling approach that combines existing partial and homologous structures, AlphaFold2-predicted structures, as well as crosslinking-mass spectrometry (XL-MS) data to generate a complete model for the structured domains of N-SWR1 (Figures 5B, 5C, and S7A–S7D; STAR Methods).

The N-terminal 140 amino acids of Swr1 constitute a Swc7-interacting domain (Figure 5B).15 The function of the Swc7 subunit remains elusive, and it has been shown to be dispensable for SWR1 histone exchange activity.13,15 The predicted a-helical fold of Swc7 closely resembles observed structures of DNAbinding domains, such as the Sap1 replication initiation factor (Figure S6E)35; further investigation is needed to validate its putative DNA binding ability and function within SWR1. Swr1 residues 241–251 comprise a Bdf1-interacting domain composed of a single b strand that complements a parallel b strand within the extra-terminal (ET) domain of Bdf1 and is stabilized through hydrophobic packing of inward-facing residues (Swr1 P242, I244, L246, and V248) against the hydrophobic cleft of the ET domain (Figures 5A–5C). This interaction closely mirrors that observed between the ET domain of Taf14 and the Taf2 C terminus within the transcription factor IID (TFIID) complex,36 except that the Taf2 b strand runs in the opposite direction compared with Swr1 (Figure S7F). Bdf1 ET also interacts with the Yaf9 coiled-coil domain, consistent with the finding that Yaf9 deletion also results in loss of Bdf1 from the complex (Figures 5A and 5B).13,15 The Swr1 segment immediately following the Bdf1-interacting domain (residues 250–307) forms a ternary subcomplex with the C terminus of Swc4 (370–476) and the Yaf9 subunit, with the coiled-coil interaction between Yaf9 and Swc4 forming the structural foundation of this subcomplex (Figures 5A–5C). The Swr1-extended HSA domain (351–437) is predicted to comprise a continuous a-helical platform for two separate actin-associated protein subcomplexes. Arp4, Swc4, and one actin subunit sequentially assemble along the N-terminal end of the HSA helix to form the first HSA subcomplex (Figures 5A– 5C). A similar module exists in the evolutionarily related INO80 complex, with actin, Arp4, and Arp8 assembling on the HSA helix of the Ino80 subunit. This INO80 module has been shown to interact with extra-nucleosomal DNA to determine nucleosome spacing.37 Curiously, the SWR1 HSA module exhibits a similar positively charged surface, though no DNA-binding activity has been observed structurally or biochemically (Figure S7G).11 Although the Swc5 subunit has been shown to be critical for SWR1 histone exchange activity,14,15,38 it has largely eluded structural characterization, except for an N-terminal fragment that acts as a chaperone for H2A-H2B.38 Subunit deletion analyses showed that stable incorporation of Swc5 requires actin, the Swr1 HSA, and the Swr1 C-terminal region.13,15 Our model reveals how these four components co-assemble, with a conserved segment of Swc5 (residues 213–228) simultaneously interacting with the Swr1 HSA, the Swr1 C terminus, and actin (Figures 5C and 5D). The N-SWR1 complex harbors three domains that interact with acetylated histone tails. DISCUSSION Mechanism of promoter-targeting and nucleosome capture by SWR1 In this paper, we have elucidated the molecular mechanism of SWR1 recognition of nucleosome-free promoters and provided structural insights on the ensuing capture of the +1 nucleosome harboring acetylated histone modifications. In our model, the Swc3 subunit, along with its basic long helix (LH), imparts a bias for SWR1 to bind the extended stretch of naked DNA present at the NFR of gene promoters. The SWR1 complex exhibits substantially lower affinity for NCPs compared with naked DNA or nucleosomes with extended linker DNA.11 Gene body regions of chromatin have short linker DNA segments between nucleosomes and are expected to form a more compact structure that sterically occludes binding surfaces,42 especially from large complexes such as SWR1. Consequently, encounters with chromatin segments comprising tightly spaced nucleosomes, such as in gene bodies, during the three-dimensional (3D) diffusion phase of target search are more likely to result in short-lived or transient chromatin interactions by SWR1.11 In this context, SWR1 mutant complexes lacking Swc3 showed slightly greater in vitro histone exchange activity than WT enzyme, raising the possibility of an autoinhibitory role of Swc3 that could suppress aberrant histone exchange in the absence of NFR binding. We postulate that as SWR1 encounters an NFR via 3D diffusion, the Swr1ATPase can engage with nucleosome-free DNA

and adopt an open sliding conformation that subsequently enables 1D diffusion of SWR1 on the NFR (Figure 6A). The existence of a 1D diffusive conformation for SWR1 would enable opportunities for regulating SWR1 activity prior to nucleosome engagement. Previous studies have shown ATP binding, and not necessarily ATP hydrolysis, to enhance the diffusivity of other Snf2-family chromatin remodelers on chromatin in living cells43 or on DNA in vitro,44 raising the possibility that other remodelers share a similar target search mechanism. Once SWR1 is engaged with NFR, additional DNA interactions made by Swc6, Swc3, and possibly Swc2 enhance retention of the complex (Figure 6B). Being independent of ATP-hydrolysis, SWR1 scanning along the NFR is likely undirected, i.e., toward 1 or +1 nucleosomes. The N-SWR1 module contains two subunits, Bdf1 and Yaf9, that recognize acetylation patterns on the +1 nucleosome flexible histone tails catalyzed by the Nua4 and SAGA (Spt-Ada-Gcn5 acetyltransferase) histone acetyltransferase complexes, respectively.45 The flexible and extended structure of the N-SWR1 module would facilitate sensing of these histone PTMs during the NFR scanning stage, acting as a grapple that biases SWR1 diffusion toward +1 nucleosomes (Figure 6B). Thus, the stepwise binding of SWR1 to the NFR and tethering to acetyl-histones in the +1 nucleosome provide a stable interaction specifically for accessible promoters of poised or active genes. As nucleosomes have been previously shown to act as a barrier to SWR1 diffusion on DNA,17 encounters with nucleosomes during NFR scanning would be expected to trigger the process of nucleosome capture prior to histone exchange. This transition would involve a major structural rearrangement as the Swr1ATPase relocates from the linker DNA to its active position on nucleosomal DNA at SHL2. We propose this rearrangement to be primarily driven by recognition of the nucleosome acidic patch and adjacent DNA (SHL6) by the Swc2 arginine anchor and Swc2 nucleosome helix elements, respectively (Figure 6C). During this stage, the engagement of the NFR DNA, nucleosomal DNA at SHL1, and H3 tail by the Swc3-Swc2 subcomplex further stabilizes SWR1 on the nucleosome. Recognition of SHL1 DNA by the Swc2throttle triggers a conformational transition of the Swr1ATPase into a more compact or ‘‘closed’’ state on SHL2 DNA, resulting in a bulge on nucleosomal DNA that destabilizes histone-DNA contacts and facilitates subsequent DNA unwrapping.16 Finally, Swc6 engagement with the NFR-distal acidic patch and SHL7 DNA results in the fully engaged complex (Figure 6D). Implications of the SWR1-promoter-targeting mechanism The extended basic a helix (LH) of Swc3 may represent a general nonspecific DNA-binding motif for chromatin-interacting factors. Although a helices that insert into DNA major grooves are well established, examples of extended basic a helices interacting with the DNA phosphate backbone are few. A recent study found that the Cac1 subunit of the yeast chromatin assembly factor 1 (CAF-1) complex contains a 12.8-nm-long DNA-binding a helix, very similar in length and charge to the Swc3 LH, which may enable DNA-size selection for proper nucleosome assembly during replication.46

The anchoring of Swc3 near the dyad through DNA and histone interactions stabilize the unwrapped state of the nucleosome.

In yeast, the SWR1 complex shares the acetyl-histone-binding Bdf1 subunit in common with the general transcription factor TFIID,47 and TFIID additionally contains the YEATS family TAF14 subunit that binds H3K9ac like Yaf9 of SWR1.48 TFIID is necessary for proper recruitment and assembly of the RNA polymerase II (RNA Pol II) transcription preinitiation on promoter DNA.49 In metazoans, TFIID is targeted to core promoters primarily through the recognition of DNA sequence motifs surrounding and downstream of the TSS by several of its TBP-associated factor (TAF) subunits.50 By contrast, the mechanism of promoter targeting by yeast TFIID (yTFIID) is unclear, as yeast promoters lack apparent TAF-binding sequence motifs. Instead, in yeast the TSS and downstream promoter are typically occupied by the +1 nucleosome, suggesting

6860 Cell 187, 6849–6864, November 27, 2024

that yTFIID may be recruited via the NFR in a non-sequence-specific manner and subsequently positioned by the +1 nucleosome.51 Thus, the DNA-binding activity of TAFs combined with the readout of acetylated histone tails may confer a similar hierarchical promoter recognition mechanism for yTFIID as we propose for SWR1. In SWR1-nucleosome structures determined thus far, the nucleosome is oriented such that subsequent histone dimer exchange would occur on the NFR-distal side of the nucleosome. Although in vitro histone exchange assays show that SWR1 readily exchanges both H2A-H2B dimers within a nucleosome,10 a preference for exchanging the NFR-distal histone dimer has been observed.52 We propose that this orientational preference is established early in the recruitment of SWR1 to the nucleosome, such that the initial binding of enzyme to NFR DNA and delivery by 1D diffusion to the acetylated +1 nucleosome forces an NFRoriented approach to substrate, which ultimately results in the Swr1 ATPase and Swc6 subunit aligned for histone exchange at the NFR-distal face of the nucleosome (this study and Willhoft et al.16). Whereas single-molecule fluorescence studies suggest

[Page 14]

that the same SWR1 complex does not processively exchange histone dimers on both sides of the same nucleosome,16,52 any processive exchange would involve a specific reorientation of SWR1 on the nucleosome after the first round of exchange. A recent study shows that homotypic nucleosomes containing two copies of H2A.Z are rare in yeast chromatin,53 and ChIPexo mapping reports that H2A.Z is indeed more prevalent at the NFR-distal than the NFR-proximal side of the +1 nucleosome in yeast.54 Altogether, these biophysical, genomic, and structural studies converge to elucidate the mechanism underlying global promoter sensing and nucleosome capture by SWR1 for oriented H2A.Z exchange at the +1 nucleosome. The functional role for H2A.Z at this location in the RNA Pol II initiation pathway is enigmatic and a challenge for future studies.

Limitations of the study Several parts of the SWR1 complex that are likely to be important for nucleosome targeting and capture mechanisms are only visualized at lower resolutions in our cryo-EM reconstructions, limiting our ability to elucidate the functional importance of these elements. In the cryo-EM reconstructions of the SWR1-DNA complex, the entire Swc3 module is flexible, limiting characterization of the Swc3-DNA interactions formed in this state. Although the Swc3 core is visualized in the nucleosome-bound state due to stabilizing interactions with nucleosome core, the parts of Swc3 that interact with the long linker DNA, including the DNA-binding Swc3 LH, are too flexible to resolve in our cryo-EM structures. Similarly, the entire N-SWR1 module is highly flexible in all our structures, precluding detailed visualization of its architecture and interactions with nucleosome. Thus, more advanced approaches will be necessary to allow direct structural characterization of Swc3 and N-SWR1 in the context of free DNA and nucleosome binding.

METHOD DETAILS Native SWR1 complex purification Native SWR1 complex was purified from S. cerevisiae ASAP-SWR1 strain containing dually-tagged Swr1-3xFLAG and RVB1-MBP, using sequential affinity purification as described in Sun et al.76 with some modifications. The yeast cells were grown in YAPD at 30 C. 12 liters of cells were harvested at OD (600 nm) of 4.0 and pelleted by centrifugation. The cell pellet was washed twice with water and once with resuspension buffer (200 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 1 mM EDTA, 40% glycerol, 100 mM potassium acetate (KOAc), 1x protease inhibitor mix (0.34 mg/ml PMSF, 0.66 mg/ml benzamide hydrochloride, 2.74 mg/ml pepstatin A, 0.56 mg/ml leupeptin, and 4 mg/ml chymostatin)). The cells were pelleted by centrifugation and flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen. The frozen pellets were lysed under liquid nitrogen using a Freezer Mill 6870 (SPEX) for 30 minutes (1 minutes on, 1 minutes off, 15 cycles). The lysed powder was pooled and 100 ml of lysis buffer (150 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 1 mM EDTA, 20% glycerol, 300 mM KOAc, 2 mM MgCl2, 6 mM bME, 1x protease inhibitor mix, 1x phosphatase inhibitor mix (1 mM NaF, 20 mM b-glycerophosphate) was added. The lysate was nutated for 30 minutes at 4 C then clarified by centrifugation at 37,000 rpm for 2 hours in a 45Ti rotor (Beckman Coulter). The clarified lysate was incubated with 6 ml of amylose resin (NEB) for 80 minutes at 4 C. The beads were washed 5 times with wash buffer A (20 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 0.5 mM EDTA, 10% glycerol, 0.1% Triton X-100, 500 mM KOAc, 1 mM MgCl2, 5 mM bME, 1x protease inhibitor mix) then another five times with wash buffer B (20 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 0.2 mM EDTA, 10% glycerol, 0.01% IGEPAL CA-630, 300 mM KOAc, 1 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM DTT, 1x protease inhibitor mix). The protein was eluted by incubating the resin with elution buffer A (wash buffer B containing 20 mM maltose) for 15 minutes at 4 C. Elution was repeated four times and the pooled eluate was incubated with 2 ml of anti-FLAG M2 resin (Sigma-Aldrich) for 4 hours at 4 C. The beads were washed five times with wash buffer C (same as wash buffer B but 500 mM KOAc instead of 300 mM), five times with wash buffer B, then eluted with elution buffer B (wash buffer B containing 0.5 mg/ml 3xFLAG peptide (APExBio) for 1 hour at 4 C. Elution was repeated four times and the eluates were pooled and concentrated in an Amicon Ultra-4 centrifugal filter (Millipore) unit with a 50 kDa molecular weight cutoff. The concentrated eluate was further purified via glycerol gradient ultracentrifugation. A 15-45% glycerol gradient containing 20 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 0.2 mM EDTA, 1 mM MgCl2, 300 mM KOAc, 0.01% IGEPAL CA-30, 0.25 mM Tris (2-Carboxyethyl) phosphine hydrochloride (TCEP) was made with a Biocomp Gradient Master. The sample was centrifuged at 35,000 rpm for 15 hours at 4 C using a SW55Ti rotor (Beckman Coulter). Fractions were collected manually, and the peak fractions were pooled. The pooled fractions were concentrated Amicon Ultra-0.5 centrifugal filter (Millipore) unit with a 50 kDa molecular weight cutoff and diluted 5-fold in storage buffer (20 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 0.2 mM EDTA, 1 mM MgCl2, 10% glycerol, 300 mM KOAc, 0.005% IGEPAL CA-630, 0.25 mM TCEP). The concentration and dilution were repeated 4 times, followed by a final concentration step. The concentrated SWR1 complex was flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen prior to storage at -80 C. The mutant SWR1 swc3D and swc2(CTRD) complexes were purified similarly, except that the glycerol gradient ultracentrifugation was omitted. Nucleosome reconstitution Mononucleosomes were reconstituted using recombinant histone octamers containing S.cerevisiae histones H2A and H2B, and D. melanogaster histones H3 and H4. Histones were expressed and purified from E. coli and subsequently used to reconstitute octamers as described previously.78 Nucleosomes used in cryo-EM experiments were reconstituted with DNA comprising the ‘Widom 601’ nucleosome positioning sequence,79 flanked by 60 bp or 7 bp of linker DNA (derived from the pUC19 cloning plasmid) on either side (i.e. ‘‘60-N-7’’ arrangement; sequence provided in Table S4). Nucleosomes used in histone exchange assays were reconstituted with DNA sequences derived from the 5’ end of the yeast RPS14B gene (sequence provided in Table S4). DNA fragments were generated by large scale polymerase chain reaction and subsequently purified by anion exchange chromatography on a Macro-Prep DEAE column (Bio-Rad) followed by ethanol precipitation. To reconstitute nucleosomes, DNA and histone octamers were mixed in equimolar ratio and dialyzed against a gradient of continuously decreasing salt concentration.78 Reconstituted nucleosomes were purified by glycerol gradient ultracentrifugation, concentrated to 2 mM, and stored at 4 C.

Cryo-EM structure determination Cryo-EM sample preparation and data collection For the SWR1(ATPgS)-DNA(147bp) sample, native SWR1 was mixed with 147 bp duplex DNA and ATPgS for a final concentration of 125 nM, 300 nM, and 1 mM respectively. The DNA was produced by PCR and contained the 5’ end of the yeast RPS14B gene (sequence provided in Table S4). The final buffer was 20 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 0.2 mM EDTA, 2 mM MgCl2, 100 mM NaCl, 0.01% IGEPAL CA-630, 3.5% glycerol, and 0.25 mM TCEP. The reaction was incubated for 10 minutes at 30 C then placed on ice. The sample was crosslinked with 0.05% glutaraldehyde (Polysciences) for 3 minutes on ice before being applied to Quantifoil 2/1 (400 mesh) grids (4 ml each) coated with graphene oxide according to Patel et al.80 The sample was incubated for 5 minutes on the grid at 4 C then flash frozen in liquid ethane using a Vitrobot Mark IV (Thermo Scientific) with a 6 second blot time and blot force of 12 at 4 C and 100% humidity. For the SWR1(apo)-DNA(54bp) sample, native SWR1 was mixed with 54 bp duplex DNA at final concentrations of 80 nM and 160 nM, respectively. The DNA was synthesized (IDT) (sequence provided in Table S4). The final buffer was 20 mM HEPES pH 7.6, 0.25 mM EDTA, 2.3 mM MgCl2, 75 mM NaCl, 0.01% IGEPAL CA-630, 5% glycerol, and 0.25 mM TCEP. The reaction was incubated for 5 minutes at 20 C then placed on ice. The sample was crosslinked with 0.05% glutaraldehyde for 3 minutes on ice before being applied to Quantifoil 2/1 (400 mesh) grids (4 ml each) coated with continuous carbon. The sample was incubated for 5 minutes on the grid at 4 C then flash frozen in liquid ethane using a Vitrobot Mark IV (Thermo Scientific) with a 3 second blot time and blot force of 10 at 4 C and 100% humidity. For the SWR1-nucleosome sample, native SWR1 was mixed first mixed with ADP and incubated for 5 minutes at room temperature before adding NaF and BeCl2 and incubating for another 5 minutes at room temperature. Nucleosomes (60-N-7) were then added and the mixture was incubated at room temperature for 10 minutes (sequence provided in Table S4). Glutaraldehyde was then added followed by 5 minutes incubation on ice. The final reaction mixture comprised 80 nM SWR1, 160 nM nucleosomes, 1 mM ADP, 10 mM NaF, 8 mM BeCl2, 0.05% glutaraldehyde, 20 mM HEPES-KOH pH 7.6, 1.5 mM MgCl2, 0.25 mM TCEP, 0.01% IGEPAL CA-630, 1% glycerol. The crosslinked sample (4 ml) was added to glow discharged Quantifoil 4/2 grids with a thin continuous carbon layer and incubated for 5 minutes on the grid at 4 C. The grids were mounted in a Vitrobot Mark IV (Thermo Scientific) maintained at 4 C and 100% humidity, and subsequently blotted (3 seconds, blot force = 10) and flash frozen in liquid ethane. For the free SWR1 sample, glutaraldehyde to a final concentration of 0.05% was added to 3 ml of native SWR1 (0.6 mM) in 25 mM HEPES, 0.2 mM EDTA, 1 mM MgCl2, 100 mM KOAc, 1 mM DTT, 0.25 mM TCEP, 1% glycerol buffer and incubated for 3 minutes at 4 C. The crosslinked sample (4 ml) was added to glow discharged Quantifoil 0.6/1.0 grids mounted in a Vitrobot Mark IV (Thermo Scientific) maintained at 4 C and 100% humidity, and subsequently blotted (3 seconds, blot force = 5) and flash frozen in liquid ethane. All cryo-EM data were collected at the Beckman Center for Cryo-EM at the Johns Hopkins School of Medicine, on a Titan Krios G3i (Thermo Scientific) operating at 300 keV. Dose-fractionated micrographs were captured on a K3 direct electron detector (Gatan) in counting mode at a calibrated magnification of 48,5433, resulting in an image pixel size of 1.03 Å. The SerialEM software package was used for semi-automated acquisition.55 Each micrograph was fractionated into 64 frames within a 4 second exposure (62.5 milliseconds per frame), with a total electron dose of 54 e/Å2 (0.85 e/Å2 per frame) at the specimen level. Cryo-EM image processing For all datasets, micrograph frames were aligned in patches of 820 pixels and dose-weighted using MotionCor2.56 Full-frame CTFs were estimated using GCTF.57 All further image processing was performed in RELION (v3.1.3).58 Particles were initially picked from motion/dose corrected micrographs using Laplacian of Gaussian (LoG) picker in RELION. For the SWR1-DNA and SWR1-nucleosome datasets, the LoG-picked particles were classified in 2D, and the high-quality representative classes were used as references to re-pick particles using template picking in RELION. Extracted particle picks were then subjected to 2D classification, and all particles belonging to classes that were not obvious junk were selected for further processing. For the SWR1-DNA sample, which was prepared using graphene oxide substrate, most particle images with graphene edges were discarded at this stage. The resulting heterogeneous particle sets were then subjected to multiple rounds of 3D classification to select for particle sets that resulted in the highest quality ‘‘global’’ reconstruction. For all samples, the RuvBL core of SWR1 was resolved at the highest resolution, while the quality of the cryo-EM map in the more peripheral parts of the complex suffered from conformational heterogeneity. Thus, to improve the quality of the reconstructions, specific regions were masked and subjected to further 3D classification and/or refinement. The angular search ranges for masked 3D classifications and refinements were restricted based on the alignments calculated in the global 3D refinements. For the SWR1(ATPgS)-DNA(147bp) sample, the global refinement resulted in an overall resolution of 3.4 Å (all map resolutions reported herein are ‘‘gold standard’’ FSC = 0.143).81 The subsequent focused refinement areas comprised the RuvBL core, the Arp6/ Swc6 subcomplex, or the Swr1ATPase-DNA subcomplex, which were refined to average resolutions of 3.3 Å, 3.6 Å, and 4.5 Å, respectively. For the SWR1(apo)-DNA(54bp) sample, the global refinement resulted in an overall resolution of 5.8 Å. The subsequent focused refinement areas comprised the RuvBL core, the Arp6/Swc6 subcomplex, or the Swr1ATPase-DNA subcomplex, which were refined to average resolutions of 5.6 Å, 7.2 Å, and 7.1 Å, respectively.

Atomic model building and refinement In order to construct comprehensive atomic models for the SWR1(ATPgS)-DNA(147bp) and SWR1-nucleosome structures, the focused-refined maps were post-processed using DeepEMhancer,59 and then combined into composite maps after aligning them to one another using the globally refined structure as a reference, and removing overlapping densities via tight masking. Additional maps that were filtered by local resolution were also used to guide model construction in some cases. The published cryo-EM structure of yeast SWR1-nucleosome complex was used as the primary starting atomic model (PDB ID 6GEJ).16 The model for the SWR1-DNA complex was built first, as the cryo-EM reconstruction was higher quality. Regions that were in conflict or were missing from the previously published cryo-EM structure were rebuilt using AlphaFold2 models as a starting point, mainly including the Swc2 subunit and part of the Swr1 insert domain. Manual model building was performed in Coot,82 followed by flexible fitting using Isolde in Chimera X.61,83 Models were then subjected to iterative cycles of real-space refinement in PHENIX,60 and manual outlier adjustment in Isolde. The refined model for the SWR1-DNA structure was used as a starting model for the SWR1-nucleosome structure, with the missing pieces taken from the previously published structure (PDB ID 6GEJ)16 or AlphaFold2 predictions. Starting model restraints were then enforced within PHENIX to limit divergence from the starting model. The final models were validated using MTriage84 and MolProbity85 within PHENIX. The refinement statistics are given in Table 1 and show values typical for structures in this resolution range. The FSC curve between the model and the map shows good correlation up to 3.2 Å resolution for the SWR1-DNA structure and up to 6.7 Å resolution for the SWR1-nucleosome structure, according to the FSC = 0.5 criterion.86 All AlphaFold2 predictions were carried out using the ColabFold AlphaFold2_mmseqs2 Notebook on Google Colab (https://colab. research.google.com/github/sokrypton/ColabFold/blob/main/AlphaFold2.ipynb).27–29 Creation of structure figures Molecular graphics and analyses were performed with UCSF ChimeraX,62 developed by the Resource for Biocomputing, Visualization, and Informatics at the University of California, San Francisco, with support from National Institutes of Health R01-GM129325 and the Office of Cyber Infrastructure and Computational Biology, National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases. ChIP-exo 5.0 assay Yeast culture, chromatin preparation, immunoprecipitation, and library preparation S. cerevisiae strains containing FLAG-tagged Htz1 or TAP-tagged SWR1 mutants were grown in YPD media to an O.D600 of 0.6-0.8 at 25 C. Formaldehyde (final concentration of 1% v/v) was used to crosslink cells at room temperature for 15 minutes and glycine (125 mM) was used to quench. The ChIP-exo 5.0 assay was performed as described previously.33 For each ChIP-exo sample, crosslinked yeast cell pellets from 70 mL of culture were resuspended in 1 mL FA lysis buffer (50 mM HEPES-KOH, pH7.5, 150 mM NaCl, 2 mM EDTA, 1% Triton X-100, 0.1% sodium deoxycholate and complete protease inhibitor (Roche) along with 0.5 mL of 0.5 mm acid-washed zirconia beads. Lysis was performed by bead beating for 3 cycles of 3 min on and 5 min off in a Mini-Beadbeater-96 machine (Biospec). Whole cell lysates were centrifuged at 14,000 rpm for 3 min at 4 C. Cytoplasmic supernatants were discarded and nuclear pellets were washed in 600 ml FA lysis buffer. Samples were centrifuged as above. Supernatant was discarded, and the chromatin pellet was resuspended in 600 mL FA lysis buffer with 300 mL 0.1 mm zirconia beads. Chromatin suspensions were sonicated in a Bioruptor (Diagenode) for 8 cycles of 15 sec on and 30 sec off. Samples were centrifuged at 14,000 rpm for 10 min at 4 C to collect sonicated chromatin fraction which was predominantly enriched with DNA fragments in a range of 200-500 bp in size (verified by agarose gel electrophoresis). Anti-FLAG M2 antibody-coupled resin (Sigma #8823) was used for immunoprecipitation of FLAG-tagged Htz1. Rabbit IgG (Sigma #I5006) was incubated with protein G Mag Sepharose (Cytiva) at 4 C for 6-8 hours, and unbound IgG was washed away prior to immunoprecipitation of TAP-tagged SWR1.
