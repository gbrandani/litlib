Abstract

[Page 3]

The interplay between transcription factors (TFs) and nucleosomes is central to gene regulation, yet most studies adopt a protein-centric perspective that largely overlooks DNA sequence contributions. Here, we identify four distinct nucleosomal DNA classes, including the canonical WW/SS pattern (W = A/T, S = G/C). All four nucleosome types are widespread across the human genome both in vivo and in vitro. Nucleosomes sharing the same pattern are rotationally in phase, whereas different patterns exhibit specific rotational offsets, reflecting distinct DNA sequence features associated with nucleosome positioning. Analysis of 744 TFs across eukaryotes shows that the WW/SS and anti-

WW/SS patterns are associated with TF binding in chromatin. Differences in the proportions of these

patterns in a region, quantified as ΔNPS values, correlate closely with in vitro nucleosome occupancy.

Integrating ΔNPS with in vivo nucleosome occupancy identifies four distinct modes of nucleosome–TF

interaction and links nucleosomal DNA patterns to nucleosome organization around TF binding sites.

[Page 4]

Introduction Understanding the interaction modes between transcription factors (TFs) and nucleosomes is central to gene regulation1-3. The traditional view holds that TFs compete with nucleosomes for access to their binding sites4,5, a model supported by ChIP-seq analyses showing that TF-bound genomic regions are typically nucleosome-depleted regions (NDRs)6. However, numerous studies have demonstrated that a subset of TFs, including pioneer factors, can directly bind nucleosomal DNA7,8. Most studies of nucleosome-TF interactions have relied on protein-centric experimental approaches,

such as electrophoretic mobility shift assays9,10, single-molecule fluorescence methods11,12, cryogenic

electron microscopy13-15, SELEX16, and protein microarray17. These experiments typically employed a limited number of nucleosomal DNA templates, including the synthetic ‘601’ sequence18 or a small set

of genomic loci with strong nucleosome positioning sequences (NPSs), such as yeast Hsp8219,

ribosomal protein L30 genes20, mouse ALBN1, NRCAM, and CX3CR117, and the human lin28b locus21.

Consequently, prior work has focused primarily on TFs rather than their DNA binding partners.

Nucleosome-TF interactions are influenced by the rotational phasing of TF binding motifs within

nucleosomes22-25. Early studies of the glucocorticoid receptors showed that motif accessibility depends

on rotational positioning22, with similar effects observed for p5323,24 and SRY-box 225. Rotational positioning is defined by which face of the DNA duplex contacts the histone core 26 and is strongly determined by DNA sequence27-34, including the AA/TT32 and RR/YY34 patterns. These motifs exhibit ~10-bp periodicity across eukaryotic genomes, such as yeast and nematodes34,35. One well-characterized DNA sequence motif is the WW/SS pattern (W = A/T, S = G/C), in which WW dinucleotides preferentially occupy minor-groove bending sites (minor-GBS), whereas SS dinucleotides are enriched at major-groove bending sites (major-GBS)36. This pattern exhibits a ~10-bp periodicity and is structurally stabilized by conserved “sprocket” arginine residues that insert into minor grooves 3739, favoring interactions with AT-rich DNA that narrows the minor groove40-42. These periodic

interactions form the structural basis for rotational positioning.

[Page 5]

An opposite sequence motif, termed anti-WW/SS, places WW and SS dinucleotides in reversed groove contexts43, leading to less favorable histone–DNA interactions. We previously showed that anti-WW/SS nucleosomes are enriched in mammalian genic regions44 and potentially stabilized by H4 and H2A histone N-tails45, but their role in nucleosome-TF interactions remained unclear. Here, we identify four distinct nucleosomal DNA patterns in the human genome (Types 1–4). Nucleosomes sharing the same pattern are rotationally in phase, whereas different types exhibit defined phasing relationships. We show that Type-1 (WW/SS) and Type-4 (anti-WW/SS) nucleosomes

are preferentially associated with TF binding. The difference in their regional abundance correlates strongly with in vitro nucleosome occupancy (NO), and integration of this metric with in vivo NO reveals

multiple modes of nucleosome-TF interaction.

Results

Four types of nucleosomes are widespread across the human genome

A major difference between nucleosome organization in vitro and in vivo is that nucleosomes are

irregularly spaced in vitro but exhibit regular spacing in vivo46-48. To assess whether nucleosomal DNA

sequences also differ between these conditions, we extracted nucleosome core particle (NCP) DNA from a human in vitro nucleosome map48 and an in vivo map from GM12878 cells49, both generated using single-end sequencing (Supplementary Table 1). Aligned reads were extended to 147 bp to derive NCP DNA fragments. Analysis of WW and SS dinucleotide frequencies across the 12 minor- and 12 major-groove bending sites (GBS) (Fig. 1a) revealed four distinct sequence patterns (Types 1–4; Fig. 1b and Supplementary Fig. 1). Type-1 nucleosomes display the canonical WW/SS pattern, with WW enriched in minor-GBS and SS enriched in major-GBS. Type-2 and Type-3 nucleosomes exhibit intermediate (“mixed”) patterns: Type-2 shows higher WW and SS frequencies in minor-GBS than in major-GBS, whereas Type-3 shows the opposite trend. Type-4 nucleosomes represent the inverse (anti-WW/SS) pattern, with WW enriched in major-GBS and SS enriched in minor-GBS.

[Page 6]

To validate the four sequence-type classification, we performed two additional analyses. First, we analyzed seven independent in vivo nucleosome datasets from diverse human cell lines (Supplementary Table 1). Second, we examined 147-bp NCP DNA fragments derived from a pairedend MNase-seq dataset in human lymphoblastoid cells50. Both analyses consistently reproduced the four nucleosome sequence patterns (Supplementary Figs. 2–9). These results confirm the robustness of the four nucleosomal DNA sequence types across multiple independent datasets. Notably, the observed patterns are independent of MNase-seq protocol (single-end versus paired-end) and cellular

context (in vivo versus in vitro). In both in vivo (GM12878) and in vitro datasets, Type-1 nucleosomes are the most abundant class,

followed by Type-4, Type-2, and Type-3 nucleosomes (Supplementary Tables 2–3). In GM12878 cells,

these four types account for 34.2%, 31.0%, 20.2%, and 14.6% of nucleosomes, respectively

(Supplementary Table 4).

To assess the effect of sequencing depth, we calculated nucleosome-type fractions across eight in vivo

datasets with varying coverage (Supplementary Table 5). The average fractions closely matched those

observed in GM12878 cells, with Type-1 nucleosomes being most abundant (34.9%), followed by

Type-4 (31.0%), Type-2 (20.0%), and Type-3 (14.2%). These results indicate that nucleosome-type distributions are robust across datasets with varying sequencing depths. Comparison of nucleosome-type fractions reveals notable differences between in vitro and in vivo (GM12878) landscapes (Supplementary Table 4). The in vitro map contains ~3% more Type-1 nucleosomes and ~3% fewer Type-4 nucleosomes than the in vivo map, whereas differences for Type2 and Type-3 nucleosomes are minimal (~1%). These results suggest that Type-1 nucleosomes are energetically favored in vitro, consistent with nucleosome positioning in the absence of chromatinassociated proteins reflecting intrinsic histone–DNA preferences. Supporting this interpretation, we found that Widom’s ‘601’, ‘603’, and ‘605’ sequences51 all conform to the Type-1 pattern, which are known to exhibit high affinities for histone octamers relative to other sequences.

[Page 7]

These observations imply that chromatin-associated proteins, such as TFs, chromatin remodelers, and RNA polymerase, redistribute nucleosomes with different DNA-encoded patterns. Notably, this redistribution exerts stronger effects on Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes than on Type-2 and Type-3. Consistently, the difference in abundances between Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes is 9.8% in vitro, nearly three-fold higher than the corresponding difference in vivo (3.3%). In contrast, the shift between Type-2 and Type-3 nucleosomes is more modest (7.6% in vitro versus 5.6% in vivo, Supplementary Table 4).

In summary, two key conclusions emerge. First, all four nucleosome types are broadly distributed across the human genome in both in vitro and in vivo conditions. Second, Type-1 and Type-4

nucleosomes exhibit substantially larger differences between in vitro and in vivo contexts than Type-2

and Type-3, indicating that chromatin proteins exert stronger regulatory influence on nucleosomes with

these two sequence patterns.

The four types of nucleosomes exhibit distinctive genomic distribution

To investigate how the four nucleosome types are distributed across the genome in vivo and in vitro,

we first computed normalized NO at every base pair and then calculated pairwise correlations between

nucleosome maps (Fig. 2 and Supplementary Figs. 10-11). Both in vivo and in vitro datasets revealed a consistent pattern. The full nucleosome map showed the highest correlation with the Type-1 map (0.74 in vivo, 0.76 in vitro; Fig. 2a and Supplementary Fig. 11a), consistent with the fact that Type-1 nucleosomes are the most abundant class (Supplementary Table 4). The second-highest correlation was observed between the full map and the Type-4 map (0.72 in vivo, 0.71 in vitro; Fig. 2d and Supplementary Fig. 11d). Lower correlations were observed for Type-2 (0.60 in vivo, 0.63 in vitro; Fig. 2b and Supplementary Fig. 11b) and Type-3 (0.45 in vivo, 0.54 in vitro; Fig. 2c and Supplementary Fig. 11c), reflecting their lower abundance. Notably, the ranking of these correlations mirrors the relative frequencies of the four nucleosome types (Supplementary Table 4).

[Page 8]

Comparisons among the four nucleosome-type maps revealed several additional insights. First, Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosome maps exhibited the strongest cross-correlation in NO (0.51 in vivo, 0.47 in vitro; Supplementary Fig. 10c and 11g). Second, Type-2 and Type-3 maps showed the next highest correlation values (0.41 in vivo, 0.39 in vitro; Supplementary Fig. 10d and 11h). In contrast, all other pairwise comparisons displayed relatively low correlations, ranging from 0.13–0.24 in vivo (Supplementary Fig. 10a–b, 10e–f) and 0.27–0.32 in vitro (Supplementary Fig. 11e–f, 11i–j). These results indicate that nucleosomes with specific DNA sequence patterns tend to co-localize

across the genome. In particular, Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes frequently occur together, whereas Type-2 and Type-3 nucleosomes form a distinct grouping. This suggests that the four nucleosome

types exhibit non-random spatial organization along chromatin, likely reflecting DNA-encoded rotational

preferences and their interactions with chromatin-associated factors.

Nucleosomes with different sequence patterns have specific rotational phasing

To examine the relative positioning of the four nucleosome types across the genome, we applied the

distance autocorrelation (DAC) and distance cross-correlation (DCC) functions described previously52.

The DAC function measures the positional relationships between pairs of NCPs from the same set

(e.g., all Type-1 nucleosomes)34,48,53, whereas the DCC function measures positional relationships between NCPs from two different sets (e.g., Type-1 versus Type-4 nucleosomes). We first computed the DAC function for the complete nucleosome set, which contains all four sequence patterns. In the in vitro dataset, weak peaks are observed at ~10-bp intervals (10, 20, 30, and 40 bp; Fig. 3a), consistent with previous analyses of the same dataset48. These periodicities reflect overlapping nucleosome positions with similar rotational orientations, indicating that DNA is wrapped such that the helical face contacting the histone octamer repeats every full turn of the DNA helix. In contrast, the in vivo dataset showed no such periodicity (Fig. 3a), suggesting that the intrinsic rotational pattern is disrupted by chromatin-associated factors.

[Page 9]

We then computed the DAC function for each nucleosome type separately. Strikingly, all four nucleosome types exhibited strong ~10-bp periodicity in both in vivo and in vitro datasets (Figure 3b– e). Type-1 nucleosomes showed clear peaks at ~10-bp intervals (Fig. 3b), as did Type-2 (Fig. 3c), Type-3 (Fig. 3d), and Type-4 (Fig. 3e) nucleosomes. These results indicate that nucleosomes sharing the same sequence pattern are rotationally in phase, irrespective of cellular context. The first peak corresponds to nucleosomes shifted by 10 bp relative to the reference position (starting at 0 bp). Subsequent peaks show slight deviations from exact 10-bp spacing (Fig. 3b–e), consistent

with a helical periodicity of ~10.25 bp observed in yeast nucleosomes52. This value closely matches previous estimates, including 10.18 ± 0.05 bp per turn measured by hydroxyl radical footprinting of 5S

DNA in reconstituted nucleosomes54, as well as structural estimates of 10.30 bp per turn from the 147-

bp NCP crystal structure55 and 10.23 and 10.15 bp per turn from 146-bp NCP structures56.

Analysis of the DCC function between different nucleosome types yielded several additional insights.

First, DCC peaks were generally stronger for in vivo datasets than in vitro (Fig. 3f-k), likely to reflect the

larger number of in vivo NCPs (Supplementary Tables 2-3). Second, Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes

displayed strong out-of-phase peaks at ~5, ~15, ~25, ~35, and ~45 bp in both datasets (Fig. 3h),

indicating a consistent half-turn rotational offset. Third, the same out-of-phase relationship was observed between Type-2 and Type-3 nucleosomes (Fig. 3i). Finally, all other pairwise comparisons produced two sets of peaks, one at ~13, ~23, ~33 bp and another at ~18, ~28, ~38 bp, across multiple type pairs (Fig. 3f–g, j–k), further demonstrating that nucleosomes with distinct sequence patterns have characteristic rotational spacing. Because the sum of all individual DAC and DCC functions recapitulates the DAC function of the complete nucleosome population (Supplementary Table 6-7), these patterns reveal that nucleosomes with different sequence motifs are arranged in a highly regular but previously hidden rotational organization.

Based on these results, we propose a rotational phasing model for the four nucleosome types, using Type-1 nucleosomes as the reference (Fig. 3l). Type-1 nucleosomes are spaced at ~10-bp intervals, reflecting their in-phase rotational alignment. Type-4 nucleosomes, which are out of phase with Type-1, are offset by ~5 bp (Fig. 3h). In summary, we identify four distinct nucleosomal DNA sequence patterns associated with nucleosome

rotational positioning. Nucleosomes with the same sequence pattern align rotationally in phase, whereas nucleosomes with different patterns adopt specific, predictable rotational offsets relative to

one another.

Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes are associated with TF binding

Because Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes show the largest differences between in vitro and in vivo

maps (Supplementary Table 4), we hypothesized that these two nucleosome types influence TF binding in vivo, and that such effects should be reduced at motifs not bound by TFs. We used CTCF as a model system because its nucleosome organization has been extensively characterized53, 57-61. Recently, it has been shown that CTCF can readily displace a well-positioned nucleosome covering its motif, provided that the nucleosome is not marked by CpG methylation or repressive histone modifications57. We began by examining NO around CTCF motifs. As expected, in vivo NO shows a characteristic pattern: nucleosomes are depleted directly over CTCF-bound motifs (GSM935611) and form strongly ordered arrays in the flanking regions (solid line, Fig. 4a), consistent with previous studies53,59,61. In contrast, unbound motifs exhibit a prominent occupancy peak with a normalized value of ~1.25

[Page 11]

(dashed lines, Fig. 4a). The in vitro NO data reveals an occupancy peak of similar height for both bound and unbound motifs (Fig. 4b), indicating that CTCF motifs intrinsically favor nucleosome formation in the absence of CTCF. Together, these results suggest that CTCF binding promotes local nucleosome depletion, whereas, in its absence, the motif is preferentially occupied by a nucleosome. We next examined the distribution of the four nucleosome types around CTCF-bound motifs in vivo. Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes show clear changes at the motif relative to flanking regions (~2–3%; Supplementary Fig. 12a), whereas Type-2 and Type-3 nucleosomes exhibit minimal variation (<1%;

Supplementary Fig. 12b), suggesting that Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes play a dominant role in

shaping local nucleosome organization.

To assess whether the observed ~2–3% differences in Type-1 and Type-4 fractions in vivo are

statistically significant, we performed two comparisons: between CTCF-bound and unbound motifs, and

between motif centers and positions ±1 kb from the center. The ±1 kb regions were chosen because (i)

they are minimally influenced by the sequence specificity of CTCF binding motifs, and (ii) they

approximate the genomic background levels of Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes. CTCF-bound motifs

exhibit a significantly higher fraction of Type-1 nucleosomes than unbound motifs (Student’s t-test, P <

10-7) and the ±1 kb flanking regions (P < 10-10) (Fig. 4c). Conversely, CTCF-bound motifs show a significantly lower fraction of Type-4 nucleosomes than unbound motifs (P < 10-3) and the ±1 kb regions (P < 10-10) (Fig. 4d). To capture the opposing trends between Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosomes in vivo, we used the ΔNPS metric, defined as the percentage difference between Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosome positioning sequences (NPS) within a given region44. CTCF-bound motifs exhibit significantly higher ΔNPS values than unbound motifs (P < 10-7) and their ±1 kb flanking regions (P < 10-11) (Fig. 4e). Together, these results demonstrate that the differences in Type-1 and Type-4 nucleosome fractions are statistically significant. Notably, nucleosomes and CTCF cannot occupy the same DNA molecule simultaneously at

In vivo nucleosome occupancy and ΔNPS exhibit distinct patterns around TF binding sites To investigate how TFs influence nucleosome organization at their genomic binding sites, we compared, for each TF, its in vivo NO profile with its ΔNPS profile. For CTCF, we observed a clear DIP/PEAK pattern: in vivo NO shows a pronounced dip over bound motifs (GSM935611), while the ΔNPS profile shows a corresponding peak (red and blue lines in Supplementary Fig. 13a). Because GC content has previously been linked to in vitro NO at TF sites62, we also examined GC content around CTCF motifs. Indeed, GC content peaks at the motif center, mirroring the in vitro NO profile (Supplementary Fig. 13c). A second pattern, DIP/DIP, was observed for SPI1: both in vivo NO and ΔNPS exhibited a dip at bound motifs (Supplementary Fig. 13b). The nearest ΔNPS peak is located ~70–80 bp away from the center of the motifs. The in vitro NO profile also shows a dip, correlating well with ΔNPS. However, GC

[Page 13]

content behaves differently here: it shows a peak at the motif (Supplementary Fig. 13d), indicating that GC content does not correlate with intrinsic NO in this case. Two additional patterns were identified in other TFs. NFIC displayed a PEAK/PEAK pattern, where both in vivo NO and ΔNPS profiles show peaks at bound motifs (Supplementary Fig. 13e). NFYA showed a PEAK/DIP pattern, with in vivo NO showing a peak but ΔNPS showing a dip (Supplementary Fig. 13f). For both TFs, the in vitro NO profile matches the ΔNPS profile. GC content correlates with intrinsic NO for NFIC (Supplementary Fig. 13g), but not for NFYA (Supplementary Fig. 13h).

Using these four patterns, we classified 512 mammalian TFs and 232 yeast TFs into four groups,

DIP/PEAK, DIP/DIP, PEAK/PEAK, and PEAK/DIP, according to their in vivo NO and ΔNPS profiles (Supplementary Table 8-10). Representative average profiles for each group are shown in Fig. 5a-d.

We also identified an AMBIGUOUS category for TFs in GM12878 cells lacking either a clear peak or

dip in their in vivo NO profiles (Supplementary Fig. 15).

We classified the 512 mammalian TFs into two datasets: a stringent set containing 410 TFs and a non-

stringent set containing 512 TFs (Supplementary Table 9). The stringent set includes only TFs with a

single dominant profile pattern across all examined cell lines. In contrast, the non-stringent set includes all TFs, including those with tied dominant patterns, which are assigned to each corresponding profile group. Importantly, the distinct profile groups found in human TFs are conserved across species: the same categories are observed in mouse TFs (Supplementary Fig. 16) and yeast TFs (Supplementary Fig. 17). This indicates that these patterns likely represent evolutionarily conserved modes of nucleosomeTF interactions. Discussion Our analysis of 12 in vivo and in vitro nucleosome maps and 744 TFs across multiple species yields several key findings. First, we identify four distinct nucleosomal DNA patterns that are widespread

[Page 14]

across the human genome in both in vivo and in vitro contexts. Second, nucleosomes with these patterns exhibit specific rotational offsets, revealing previously unrecognized DNA-encoded rules for nucleosome positioning. Third, two patterns, Type-1 and Type-4, are strongly associated with TF binding, with greater enrichment or depletion at bound than unbound motifs. Fourth, the difference in their relative abundance, quantified as ΔNPS, correlates closely with in vitro NO around TF-binding sites. Finally, integration of ΔNPS with in vivo occupancy profiles reveals four distinct modes of nucleosome–TF interaction.

Based on our data, we propose a unified model for the modes of nucleosome-TF interactions in vivo (Fig. 6), derived from the stringent dataset. For simplicity, the model focuses on direct interactions

between TFs and nucleosomes, although additional factors, such as ATP-dependent chromatin

remodelers, are likely to contribute.

(1) DIP/PEAK group (41% of TFs). These TFs have strong intrinsic nucleosomes located directly over

their binding motifs. The in vivo depletion of nucleosomes suggests direct competition between TFs

and these intrinsically positioned nucleosomes. In other words, TF binding displaces strong DNA-

encoded nucleosomes.

(2) DIP/DIP group (32% of TFs). These TFs have strong intrinsic nucleosomes positioned ~70–80 bp away from the motif center. Thus, motifs are situated either in linker DNA or near the nucleosome edge, making them easily accessible. The absence of a strong DNA-encoded nucleosome at the motif itself is likely to contribute to the NDRs observed in vivo. (3) PEAK/PEAK group (11% of TFs). These TFs have strong intrinsic nucleosomes centered on their motifs. The enrichment of nucleosomal DNA in vivo suggests that these TFs bind DNA while it remains wrapped around a nucleosome, binding “at the center” relative to the nucleosome ends, though not necessarily at the dyad. These TFs appear to recognize or stabilize a well-positioned nucleosome. (4) PEAK/DIP group (3% of TFs). These TFs lack strong DNA-encoded nucleosomes near their motifs (ΔNPS shows a dip), yet nucleosomes are enriched in vivo. This suggests that nucleosome positioning

[Page 15]

is imposed not by DNA sequence but by protein factors such as chromatin remodelers or even the TFs themselves. Our data indicates that, for DIP/DIP TFs, NDRs may arise because the local DNA sequence disfavors the formation of strong, stable nucleosomes. In this case, the genomic region is intrinsically nucleosome-poor, and TF binding does not require competition with nucleosomes. In contrast, TFs in the DIP/PEAK group are associated with strong intrinsic nucleosomes positioned over their motifs, as reflected by high ΔNPS values. For these TFs, NDRs likely result from direct competition, in which

DNA-encoded nucleosomes are displaced or excluded upon TF binding. Together, these results show that NDRs can arise through at least two distinct sequence-driven mechanisms: (i) the absence of

strong intrinsic nucleosomes (DIP/DIP) and (ii) TF–nucleosome competition at sites with strong intrinsic

nucleosomes (DIP/PEAK).

Our analyses validate the existence of four nucleosomal DNA types across multiple independent in vivo

and in vitro MNase-seq datasets, with Type-1 and Type-4 being the most prevalent patterns. This

distribution is further supported by nucleosome maps generated using a chemical cleavage method

based on hydroxyl radicals44, indicating that Type-4 nucleosomes are unlikely to arise from artefacts

such as asymmetric MNase trimming. The mechanistic basis underlying the formation of Type-4 nucleosomes remains to be fully elucidated. However, our previous work44 showed that these nucleosomes are enriched in mammalian genic regions, suggesting a potential role in facilitating transcription elongation. In the present study, we further observe that Type-4 nucleosomes are enriched around binding sites of TFs exhibiting the DIP/DIP pattern, consistent with a role in facilitating TF access. Together, these findings suggest that Type-1 and Type-4 DNA patterns may act as intrinsic sequence features that modulate interactions between nucleosomes and chromatin-associated proteins.

Methods Genome-wide nucleosome positioning (MNase-seq) datasets Both in vitro and in vivo nucleosome maps from yeast and higher eukaryotes were used in this study, comprising ten in vivo datasets and two in vitro datasets (Supplementary Table 1). The ten in vivo nucleosome maps included eight human datasets, one mouse dataset, and one yeast dataset. The human datasets were derived from eight cell lines: GM12878 (GSM920558), K562 (GSM920557),

MCF-7 (GSE51097), H1-hESC (GSM1194220), HepG2 (GSM3718063), HeLa (GSE100401), HCT116

(GSM3889219), and IMR90 (GSM1095279). The mouse dataset was generated from mouse

embryonic stem cells (mESC; GSM2183911), and the yeast dataset was obtained from cells grown to late-log phase in synthetic complete medium (GSM651368). Two in vitro nucleosome positioning

datasets were also analyzed—one from humans (GSE25133) and one from yeast (GSE13622).

Nucleosome occupancy maps were obtained either directly from ENCODE or generated by aligning

MNase-seq short reads to reference genomes using Bowtie2 (Supplementary Table 1). For example,

datasets without pre-computed occupancy files, MNase-seq reads in FASTQ format were aligned using Bowtie2 with parameters: --sensitive -I 0 -X 500 --no-discordant. For single-end datasets, mapped reads were extended from their 5′ ends to a canonical fragment length of 147 bp. For paired-end datasets, read pairs with insert sizes between 120 and 180 bp were retained. These fragments accounted for 75%, 73%, 46%, and 31% of all paired reads in the H1-hESC, MCF-7, HepG2, and HCT116 MNase-seq datasets, respectively, and were used for downstream analysis. Nucleosomes on repetitive elements are included. For one paired-end dataset (GSE36979), positions of 147-bp nucleosomal DNA fragments were obtained from published data50.

[Page 17]

To generate occupancy profiles for each nucleosome type, nucleosomes were classified as Type-1, Type-2, Type-3, or Type-4 based on their DNA sequence patterns. For each human cell line, BAM files were obtained from UCSC or generated using Bowtie2, and corresponding SAM files were processed. For single-end datasets, dyad positions were defined by adding 73 bp to the start position of mapped reads. For paired-end datasets, dyad positions were defined as the midpoints of mapped read pairs. In all datasets, nucleosomal DNA fragments derived directly from MNase-seq reads were used to capture alternative nucleosome positioning within a cell population. Nucleosomal DNA fragments (147 bp)

centered on dyads were then extracted from the reference genome and assigned to one of the four

sequence types.

Genome-wide NO for each type was computed from dyad counts using a center-weighted scheme59.

This approach provides improved boundary resolution compared to alternative methods59 and

produces smoothing of dyad counts consistent with previously generated NO maps (e.g., GM12878;

GSE35586)49.

The mESC dataset was provided as center-weighted scores, which were converted to BedGraph

format using the same center-weighting scheme59 prior to downstream analysis. For yeast datasets, NO profiles were generated directly from dyad counts using the same center-weighting procedure59. Classification of nucleosomes with different NPS patterns Nucleosomal DNA sequences were classified into four types, e.g., Type-1, Type-2, Type-3, and Type4, based on the relative abundance of WW and SS dinucleotides in minor- and major-GBS44. Type-1 nucleosomes exhibit the canonical WW/SS pattern. For a 147-bp NCP fragment, if (1) the total number of WW dinucleotides in the 12 minor-GBS is greater than that in the 12 major-GBS, and (2) the total number of SS dinucleotides in the 12 major-GBS is greater than that in the 12 minor-GBS, the fragment is classified as Type-1. Type-4 nucleosomes display the anti-WW/SS pattern, the inverse of Type-1. A 147-bp fragment is assigned to Type-4 if (1) WW dinucleotides are more abundant in the 12 major-

[Page 18]

GBS than in the minor-GBS, and (2) SS dinucleotides are more abundant in the 12 minor-GBS than in the major-GBS. Type-2 and Type-3 nucleosomes represent “mixed” patterns. Type-2 fragments contain more WW and more SS dinucleotides in minor-GBS than in major-GBS. Type-3 fragments contain more WW and more SS dinucleotides in major-GBS than in minor-GBS. For any genomic region, we quantify the relative contributions of the two complementary sequence patterns using ΔNPS, defined as ΔNPS = Type 1 (%) – Type 4 (%), which reflects the balance between

WW/SS and anti-WW/SS nucleosome patterns.

Type-1 through Type-4 nucleosomes were identified in both in vivo (GM12878; Supplementary Table 2)

and in vitro datasets (Supplementary Table 3). Overall, the number of nucleosomes detected in the in

vitro dataset is approximately one-third of that detected in vivo (compare Supplementary Tables 2, 3).

Genome-wide protein-DNA interaction (ChIP-seq) datasets

In total, 2,235 human and mouse ChIP–seq datasets were obtained from ENCODE across eight

human cell lines (Supplementary Table 1). For 1,202 datasets (53.8%), position-specific scoring

matrices (PSSMs) for TF binding motifs were retrieved by matching TF names to entries in the HOCOMOCO v11, JASPAR 2022, and FactorBook databases. These PSSMs were used to represent the sequence variability of TF binding motifs. For each of the 1,202 datasets, processed BED files analysis (Supplementary Tables 8, 10). TF binding sites were identified by scanning each ChIP fragment with the corresponding PSSM using MEME suite tools. When multiple motif hits were present within a fragment, the highest-scoring site was retained for downstream analysis. For yeast, we used the processed ChIP-seq dataset63,64 in which the center of the binding motif was already provided for each ChIP fragment.

[Page 19]

For CTCF, unbound motifs were defined as follows. The human genome was scanned using the CTCF position-specific scoring matrix (PSSM; CTCF_human_h11MO.meme) with FIMO at a significance threshold of 10-4. CTCF-bound sites were obtained from ChIP–seq data in GM12878 cells ChIP-defined binding sites were excluded to generate a set of unbound motifs. From this set, an equal number of unbound motifs were randomly selected to match the number of ChIP-derived bound sites for downstream analysis.

Classification of TF patterns using in vivo nucleosome occupancy and ΔNPS profiles

Transcription factors were classified into five categories, DIP/PEAK, DIP/DIP, PEAK/PEAK, PEAK/DIP,

and AMBIGUOUS, based on the patterns of in vivo NO and ΔNPS profiles centered on TF bound

motifs.

Classification criteria were defined using the relative positions of global maxima and minima within

each profile, normalized by the full dynamic range of the signal:

DIP/PEAK: The in vivo NO profile exhibits a global minimum (or a value below the lower one-third of its dynamic range), while the ΔNPS profile exhibits a global maximum (or a value above the upper onethird of its range) at the motif center. DIP/DIP: Both the in vivo NO and ΔNPS profiles show a global minimum (or values within the lowest one-third of their respective ranges) at the motif center. PEAK/PEAK: Both the in vivo NO and ΔNPS profiles display a global maximum (or values within the upper one-third of their ranges) at the motif center. PEAK/DIP: The in vivo NO profile shows a global maximum (upper one-third of its range), while the ΔNPS profile shows a global minimum (lower one-third of its range) at the motif center.

[Page 20]

AMBIGUOUS: TFs whose in vivo NO profiles exhibit no clear peak or dip, i.e., values at the motif center fall between the lower and upper one-third thresholds, were placed in the AMBIGUOUS group. This classification scheme ensures that each TF is assigned based on robust, quantitative features of nucleosome organization and intrinsic DNA-encoded nucleosome strength around its binding motifs. Pattern Labeling Some transcription factors exhibited more than one TF pattern across different cell lines. To derive a

consensus label for each TF, we counted the occurrences of all patterns observed for that TF and

assigned as its consensus classification the pattern(s) tied for the highest frequency. Thus, a TF could have a single consensus label (e.g., DIP/DIP) or multiple labels if ties occurred (e.g., DIP/DIP,

DIP/PEAK, and AMBIGUOUS). In addition to this comprehensive dataset, we generated a stringent

dataset in which TFs with more than one consensus label were removed. The stringent dataset,

therefore, contains only TFs that exhibit a single, consistent pattern across all examined cell lines.

Inter-nucleosome distance correlation function

To quantify the relative spatial organization of nucleosomes, we applied DAC and DCC functions52. For each pair of nucleosomal DNA fragments, we computed the distance between their dyad positions. When identical nucleosome sequences occur multiple times at the same genomic location, their contributions are weighted multiplicatively; for example, if two sequences occur 5 and 10 times, respectively, their DAC contribution is counted as 5 × 10 = 50.
