SUMMARY

The molecular details governing transcription factor (TF) binding and the formation of accessible chromatin are not yet quantitatively understood—including how sequence context modulates affinity, how TFs search DNA, the kinetics of TF occupancy, and how motif grammars coordinate binding. To resolve these questions for a human TF, erythroid Krüppel-like factor (eKLF/KLF1), we quantitatively compare, in high throughput, in vitro TF binding rates and affinities with in vivo single-molecule TF and nucleosome occupancies and in vivo-derived deep learning models. We find that 40-fold flanking sequence effects on affinity are consistent with distal flanks tuning TF search parameters and captured by a linear energy model. Motif recognition probability, rather than time in the bound state, drives affinity changes, and in vitro and in nuclei measurements exhibit consistent, minutes-long TF residence times. Finally, in vitro biophysical parameters predict in vivo sequence preferences and single-molecule chromatin states for unseen motif grammars.

INTRODUCTION Sequence-specific transcription factor (TF) binding to DNA is essential for spatiotemporal regulation of eukaryotic gene expression.1 However, the molecular details driving TF binding and the establishment of accessible chromatin are not understood from first principles—including how TFs search DNA, how local sequence context modulates affinity, the kinetics of TF binding, and how motif grammars establish TF-occupied, nucleosome-depleted regions that regulate gene expression. A physical model requires understanding how a TF searches for and identifies its cognate motif on accessible DNA and how binding subsequently influences nucleosome occupancy. The classical two-state model of protein-substrate binding posits that variable affinities across DNA sequences are governed by dissociation rate, while diffusion-limited association rates remain constant. This view conflicts with the speed-specificity paradox in TF search,2 neglects nonspecific diffusion along DNA,3–7 and cannot explain observed sequence-dependent association rates.8,9 Recent work with the bacterial lac repressor10 found that the probability of transitioning from a nonspecific to a specific interaction, rather than bound time, dictated affinity. Determining whether this holds for weaker, less specific eukaryotic

TFs would enable a quantitative microscopic model of TF search and motif recognition in eukaryotes. Furthermore, measurements of TF-DNA binding kinetics in vitro and in vivo demonstrate surprising discrepancies. In live cells, reported residence times (typically measured via single-molecule fluorescence without resolving genomic localization) are seconds long and within an order of magnitude of nonspecific interactions.11–17 By contrast, in vitro-measured residence times are orders of magnitude longer than nonspecific interactions.16 Recent work identified long-duration binding events in vivo,18–21 suggesting that technical limitations may contribute to underestimating live cell residence times. Non-imaging methods quantifying TF off rates at pre-identified binding sites would provide an orthogonal approach to dissect binding kinetics in cells. Beyond kinetic discrepancies, in vitro-derived thermodynamic parameters poorly predict in vivo binding, with appreciable TF binding at <1% of predicted motifs.22–25 One possible explanation is that sequence context outside of the motif may impact TF search and motif recognition. Indeed, motif-flanking sequences can tune TF occupancy through a range of mechanisms—by creating a more favorable DNA shape at the motif,26–31 a funneled energy landscape through low-affinity motifs,32,33 or opportunities

Finally, genomic enhancers are not just composed of one isolated motif. Rather, these elements contain many motifs,40,41 enabling potential for TF-TF cooperativity through direct physical interactions (i.e., DNA allostery)42,43 or indirect mechanisms (i.e., nucleosome competition or cofactor recruitment).44–47 Integrating in vitro measurements, thermodynamic models, and in vivo observations across motif grammars promises a mechanistic framework for linking sequence features to TF and nucleosome occupancy. To do so, we chose Krüppel-like factor 1 (KLF1/eKLF) as a model TF, a C2H2 zinc finger protein (C2H2ZF) within the most common class of human TFs.48 KLF1’s three-finger DNA-binding domain (DBD) is highly conserved across the KLF family,49 which enacts wide-ranging developmental roles50 with KLF1 regulating erythropoiesis and the fetal-to-adult hemoglobin expression switch.51 We measure KLF1’s binding kinetics and affinity to thousands of DNA sequences in vitro and find that the probability of recognizing the motif, rather than the time spent in the bound state, dictates affinity. Motif-flanking sequence drives ∼40-fold affinity variation that is well-described by a linear energy contribution per flanking base pair (bp), with proximal effects likely driven by direct contacts from an unconventional binding mode. Distal sequence helical turns away from the motif tune affinity up to 2-fold, in a manner consistent with sequence-dependent TF search. We then deploy single-molecule footprinting (SMF)52–54 on genome-integrated constructs in an erythroleukemia cell line to quantify TF and nucleosome occupancies along individual molecules. A footprinting-based measure of KLF residence time at known motifs in nuclei agrees with in vitro measurement on a timescale of minutes. We implement thermodynamic models to explain how nucleosome competition drives nonlinear KLF occupancy across motif numbers and spacing. Lastly, our in vitro flanking sequence models predict how sequence context tunes in vivo TF binding, as observed with SMF and base-resolved deep learning models trained on chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) data.55 Together, our findings demonstrate that quantitative TF binding measurements in vitro and in vivo provide a biophysical understanding of the sequence features that establish accessible chromatin elements. RESULTS High-throughput measurement of KLF1 binding kinetics and affinities To measure KLF1 affinity in vitro, we designed a library of 11,812 DNA sequences containing variants within the consensus nine-bp KLF motif (CCMCRCCCN). This library spans all single, all double, and some triple mutations of a high-affinity motif (CCACGCCCA), which we define as the consensus motif, extensive programmed variation in motif-flanking sequence, and negative controls lacking the motif (STAR Methods; Figure 1A). We then performed ‘‘highthroughput sequencing’’-‘‘fluorescent ligand interaction profiling’’ (HiTS-FLIP)56 by sequencing the library on an Illumina MiSeq and using the resultant flow cell to quantify DNA binding of purified, flu-

orescently labeled KLF1 DBD (Figure 1B). We assayed binding kinetics by imaging while flowing in KLF1 to measure association rates relative to a sequence with a consensus motif and intermediate strength flank (ka/ka,ref, Figures S1A–S1D) and, post-equilibration, flowing in buffer alone to measure dissociation rates (kd) (STAR Methods; Figures 1C, 1D, and S1A). HiTS-FLIP binding energies (ΔG) linearly relate to biolayer interferometry (Octet) measurements for flank and motif mutations (Figures 1F, S1G, and S1H). The ratios of association and dissociation rates yield relative affinities (STAR Methods) consistent with dissociation constants (KD) from equilibrium measurements across KLF1 concentrations (Figures S1E and S1F). The KLF1 position weight matrix (PWM) constructed from motif mutations yields a CACCC-box, the canonical Sp/KLF family motif, with finger 1 contributing the least to overall affinity as observed with homologs KLF4 and Sp157–59 (Figure 1E). KLF1 affinity is driven by motif recognition probability We first tested whether our binding measurements aligned with a recent model10 positing a nonspecific DNA-associated TF ‘‘testing’’ state, from which the TF either specifically binds the motif (kon,μ) or dissociates into solution (koff,NS) (Figure 1G). If affinities for different targets vary due to altered probabilities for specifically binding the motif (ptot = kon,μ/(kon,μ+koff,NS)), then this three-state microscopic model predicts an anticorrelation between macroscopic association and dissociation rates (Figure 1H). Indeed, we observed this coupling, as previously seen for the prokaryotic lac repressor,10 for the eukaryotic TF KLF1 across motif and motif-flanking mutations in HiTS-FLIP and Octet measurements (Figures 1I, 1J, S1I, and S1J). On surface-based platforms, KLF1 can rebind in trans to another DNA molecule within the cluster before dissociating (Figure S1K). By incorporating this possibility into the microscopic model, we find that at constant DNA density, the observed association rate variability and the macroscopic rates’ anticorrelation are not artifacts of rebinding in trans (Figures S1L–S1N). Indeed, within-cluster DNA density is highly consistent between library members on HiTS-FLIP (Figure S1O) and, on Octet, is measured and controlled when loading DNA (Figure S1G). Consistent with our modeling results, a separate library of motif and flank mutations with ∼20-fold lower HiTS-FLIP cluster density (Figure S1O) yields up to 2-fold faster dissociation rates, while the macroscopic rates’ anticorrelation remains (Figures S1P and S1Q). The three-state model predicts that if microscopic reassociation (kon,μ) is blocked during dissociation (e.g., with a binding competitor) and dissociation from the testing state (koff,NS) is fast, then variability in macroscopic dissociation rates (kd) converges on the microscopic dissociation rate (koff,μ). Indeed, dissociation with the dark competitor (high concentration of unlabeled KLF1) showed that macroscopic dissociation rates substantially decreased in variability, collapsing near the fit microscopic dissociation rate (Figures 1I and S1P). Since the dark competitor should eliminate all rebinding, including in trans, it provides an absolute upper bound on measured dissociation rates (Figure 1I). Indeed, the measured microscopic dissociation rate is consistent even at lower DNA cluster densities (Figure S1P). KLF1 affinity is tuned by motif-flanking sequence Mutations to the flanking sequence (nucleotides outside of the core, 9 bp motif) substantially modulate KLF1 affinity (Figure 1H). As sequences with flank mutants but no core motif (3,295 sequences) show no measurable binding (Figures S2A and S2B), observed variability is due to altered occupancy at the motif rather than binding to additional sites in the flanks.34–39 To determine the distance at which flanking bases can affect motif binding, we assayed binding to the KLF1 motif embedded in quasi-random sequences with increasing lengths of constantsequence flanks (STAR Methods; Figure 2A). These sequences (containing the motif and proximal bases) were embedded either at a fixed position within variable background sequences or at variable positions within a fixed background sequence (STAR Methods; Figure 2A). As expected, variation in binding kinetics (i.e., ptot) is largest without constant flanking sequence and de-

creases as more flanking bases are held constant (Figure 2B). There is ∼40-fold variability in affinity (KD) without constant flanks (Figure 2C). This variability decays to 2-fold with three flanking bases constant and remains present with 15 flanking bases constant (Figure 2C). Thus, KLF1 binding is tuned not only by immediately proximal flanks (∼2 bp) but also by distal flanks. An extended PWM captures flanking sequence effects To quantify the flanking sequence determinants that tune KLF1 affinity, we fit a PWM model that includes 10 bases on each side of the core motif (Figure 2D). Both the training and validation data are well-fit (r2 = 0.92 and 0.92; Figures 2E and S2C). This extended PWM (60 parameters) performed similarly to a more complex and less interpretable machine learning model (∼1.6M parameters, r2 = 0.97 and 0.95; Figures S2D and S2E), suggesting that most variability is explainable by a linear binding energy contribution from each flanking base. Consistent with this, KLF1 ChIP studies often identify longer motifs with guanines in the − 1 and − 2 positions.60–63 We next explored mechanisms for how proximal flanks tune affinity. First, motif-adjacent bases could create low-affinity motifs overlapping the core motif.33 However, comparing the core motif PWM scores for sites overlapping the motif in low- and high-affinity groups showed no change in the energy landscape (Figure S2F). Second, proximal bases could alter DNA shape at the motif.26–31 Using Deep DNAshape,64 which captures the influence of seven flanking bases, to predict shape parameters (minor groove width [MGW], propeller twist, helical twist, and roll) at the motif (Figure S2G), we found a few correlations between affinity and shape parameters. However, the correlation signs are inconsistent between datasets or yield negligible changes in shape parameters (Figures S2H and S2I). These data suggest that proximal flanks do not perturb affinity through DNA shape, consistent with findings that C2H2-ZFs largely rely on base rather than shape information.65 Lastly, proximal flanks could act through direct contacts, as base interactions adjacent to the canonical DNA triplets are

known to occur with C2H2-ZFs.66 Indeed, homolog Sp1 has a unique binding mode where its first finger contacts five bases: the canonical triplet and two 5′ -adjacent bases.57–59 Mutating these two 5′ -adjacent bases from guanines to threonines decreases Sp1’s affinity,57 mirroring KLF1’s 5′ flank effects. Indeed, direct flank contacts are consistent with proximal flank changes contributing more to binding energy (up to 1.4 kBT) than some core motif changes (down to 0.9 kBT) (Figures 1E, 2F, and S2J). Thus, these two 5′ bases, which we have described as proximal flanking sequence, should likely be considered as part of KLF’s ‘‘core motif,’’ which we define as the bases with direct contacts and energetic contributions during specific protein binding. Taken together, KLF1 binding has substantial sequence context dependence that is captured by a linear energy model, with direct contacts during specific binding likely driving effects from positions − 1 and − 2. To do so, we measured affinity to sequences where the core motif is flanked by 5 constant bp and moved along the background sequence (Figure 3A). These data show that affinity varies across positions and therefore variable distal sequence context. Autocorrelation analysis reveals that affinity changes exhibit a periodicity of ∼10.5 bp, the helical pitch of B-DNA (Figures 3B and 3C). The spectral peak amplitude at ∼10.5 bp is largest with fixed flanks of 3–5 bp (sequence variation 7–9 bp away from the motif center) and decays for longer and shorter fixed flanks (Figures 3D and S3A–S3C). Consistent with AT enrichment in favorable distal flanks (Figure 2G), we observed a strong correlation between KD and GC content (in a 4 bp window) one helical turn away from the motif (r = 0.60; Figure 3E). This correlation extends across the flanks with a periodicity of ∼10.5 bp in both the sliding and fixed position motif libraries, with highest-affinity sequences depleted in GC content helical turns from the motif (Figures 3F, 3G, S3D, and S3E). Since the KLF1 motif is GC-rich, we asked if ‘‘motif-

like’’ flank sequences correlated with decreased affinity. Scoring flanks with the PWM (see STAR Methods) revealed strong correlations between ‘‘motif-likeness’’ at certain positions and KD, with the degree of correlation exhibiting the same periodic trend (Figures S3F–S3H). Distal flanks tuning hopping probabilities during TF search These periodic trends cannot be explained by allosteric interactions through DNA between two binding sites43,67 for two reasons. First, control sequences without the core motif show no measurable binding, demonstrating that KLF1 is not specifically binding the flanks (Figures S2A and S2B). Second, the correlation is opposite to that expected from allostery: motif-like sequences helical turns away decrease measured affinity. Additionally, DNA shape preferences do not explain these effects, as sequence context one helical turn away negligibly affects Deep DNAshape-predicted parameters (Figures S2G–S2I). Since the observed periodicity matches B-DNA’s, we wondered if TF search dynamics might explain distal sequence effects. TF search occurs through 3D diffusion, inter-segmental

jumps, and rotation-coupled (sliding) and rotation-uncoupled (hopping) 1D diffusion along DNA.4,9 We hypothesized that a GC-rich or motif-like sequence could decrease the hopping probability, thereby reducing the likelihood of hopping one groove over onto the motif and, instead, increasing the likelihood of sliding and dissociating before reaching the motif. We therefore modified our microscopic model of TF binding (Figure 1G) to separate the nonspecific-to-specific transition into two parts: TF search for the motif along DNA and, once there, recognition of the motif. We incorporated a model for target search9 in which a TF combines groove-tracking sliding and hopping between major grooves (Figures 3H and S3I) and then evaluated the model using stochastic reaction diffusion simulations on discretized DNA (see STAR Methods). Here, the TF’s probability of motif recognition (ptot) is deconvolved into the probability of finding the motif during 1D diffusion on DNA (pfind, a function of sliding and hopping) and the probability of binding the motif once on top of it (pbind) (Figures 3H and S3I). To evaluate hopping landscape impacts on affinity, we systematically tuned the hopping probability at individual positions. The model predicts that a higher hopping probability is unfavorable adjacent to the motif and favorable at integer helical turns away (Figure 3I). This is consistent with our experimental data if lower motif-likeness (i.e., GC content) and weaker nonspecific binding increase hopping probabilities. Therefore, we used the motif-likeness to calculate a hopping probability at each position and simulated the affinity for our experimental sequences. These results recapitulate the ∼10.5 bp periodicity in autocorrelation across physically reasonable parameter ranges,9 with or without groove-tracking sliding (Figures 3J, 3K, S3J, and S3K). The model reproduces the dependence of the autocorrelation power spectra on constant flank length more strongly when groovetracking sliding is included, but a hopping-only model cannot be ruled out (Figures 3D, 3K, and S3J–S3L). This dependence can be understood intuitively: long constant flanks attenuate sequence context effects on motif search (Figure 2C), while short constant flanks allow proximal sequence effects to outweigh distal ones (Figure 2F). With evidence against DNA allostery or shape driving distal effects (Figures S2A, S2B, and S2G–S2I), flanks tuning the hopping probability in this ‘‘goldilocks zone’’ during target search best explains our experimental observation that sequence context perturbs the probability of transitioning from a nonspecific-to-specific interaction (ptot, Figure 2B). While KLF1’s preference for two 5′ flanking bases is likely driven by direct contacts, preference for AT-rich sequence helical turns away from the motif is consistent with sequence content tuning KLF1 hopping rates during TF search. Tandem in vivo SMF and in vitro kinetics of KLF1 binding Given the strong flanking sequence effects observed in vitro, we wondered if sequence context drives TF occupancy and chromatin architecture differences in cells. SMF provides high-resolution accessibility information47,52–54 capable of quantifying TF and nucleosome binding events on single molecules of engineered and genome-integrated sequences.47 This method entails incubating isolated nuclei with methyltransferase (here, GpC methyltransferase M.CviPI), which deposits methylation on accessible bases. While this short residence time is a technical challenge, it also presents an opportunity to measure in nuclei off rates at cognate motifs by methylating for increasing durations. As KLF1 dissociates, we expect its footprint of DNA protection to be ‘‘eroded’’ through the continuing action of methyltransferases methylating previously bound sites (Figure 4A). To measure dissociation in cells, we built a library of synthetic constructs in two sequence backgrounds containing variable numbers (0–7×) of the KLF1 consensus motif with 15 bp spacing in between motifs (Figure 4B). Each motif was flanked with GpCs for consistent proximal bases and to ensure high-quality footprinting with M.CviPI. To demonstrate KLF-specific binding, we included versions with a single motif mutation (Figure 4B) that ablates binding >400-fold in vitro (Figure 1E). As a control, we added a construct with one high-affinity CTCF motif, as CTCF has a longer residence time than other mammalian TFs in live cell measurements16,68–70 and in vitro residence times of ∼30 min.71 Furthermore, since this CTCF motif is almost always bound in cells, its flanking bases are highly accessible (due to statistical positioning of nearby nucleosomes52,54,72,73), allowing a measure of the M.CviPI methylation rate at accessible bases. Lastly, we included a construct with six STAT3 motifs since STAT3 has a fast 8-s residence time in single-particle tracking experiments.74 We integrated these constructs at the AAVS1 safe-harbor locus in an erythroleukemia cell line, K562, as KLF1 regulates erythropoiesis60 (Figure 4C). RNA-seq in K562s confirms high expression of KLF1, lower levels of a number of additional KLFs, and expression of a few related but less conserved specificity proteins (Sps) (Figure S4A). Since the DBD is highly conserved across the KLF family, with most containing identical amino acids for the predicted DNA-contacting residues,49 we refer to in vivo occupancy measurements as ‘‘KLF’’ binding. To directly compare in vivo and in vitro measurements, we first quantified KLF1 association and dissociation in vitro for these SMF-compatible constructs. Consistent with independent binding, in vitro apparent association rates (the rates of fluorescence appearance) linearly scale with motif number (Figure S4B), while the in vitro dissociation rate is constant, with a residence time of 1.4 min (Figure 4D). No binding was observed for the mutated KLF1 motif (Figure S4B). Next, we methylated nuclei from K562s with these constructs for incubations ranging from 2 min, the shortest time possible with sufficient methylation to reliably footprint (Figure S4C), to 15 min. DNA was enzymatically converted75 to preserve methylation information during targeted amplification and Illumina sequencing (Figure S4D). To quantify TF and nucleosome binding, we modified a probabilistic model47 that takes in methylation signal and motif locations and assigns the most likely configuration to each molecule (Figure S4F; STAR Methods). To model the decrease in motif protection due to KLF dissociation, we wrote ordinary differential equations (ODEs) describing KLF irreversibly dissociating with rate kd and M.CviPI then irreversibly methylating the accessible motif with rate km (Figure 4G). These ODEs allow KLF dissociation before methylation of the flanks, in which case a footprint would never be detected. While KLF could reassociate before methylation, we excluded this possibility by assuming that KLF concentration drops once cells are permeabilized and resuspended. ation rate, we can quantify TF residence times longer than 30 s (Figure S4G). KLF binding exhibits cooperativity in cells but not in vitro Since KLF motifs are enriched for clusters within the genome (Figures S5A and S5B), we next examined how motif number influences KLF occupancy in cells. The probabilistic model calls from SMF data (Figures 5A and S4F) show that average TF occupancy is nonlinear with motif number: no binding with one or two motifs, then roughly linearly increasing from three to seven motifs (Figure 5B). Nucleosome clearance demonstrates a sigmoidal trend, with molecules fully protected up to three motifs and then completely accessible after five motifs (Figures 5C and S5C). Mutated motifs exhibit neither increased TF occupancy nor decreased nucleosome occupancy across motif numbers, consistent with KLF-specific binding (Figures 5B and 5C). This low-end thresholding in KLF occupancy (Figure 5B) occurs only when TFs compete with other DNA-binding proteins, such as nucleosomes, in cells, while in vitro binding scales linearly with motif number (Figure S4B).

Thermodynamic model explains in vivo KLF binding across motif number Thermodynamic partition functions77–79 provide a framework for modeling non-equilibrium steady-state TF and nucleosome binding at the single-molecule level.47 In these models, energies are assigned to TF-DNA and nucleosome-DNA interactions, with an added nucleosome destabilization energy term when TFs recruit chromatin remodelers (Figure 5D). Energy parameters are fit by maximum likelihood estimation by enumerating all possible binding microstates, summing their energetic interactions, and computing their Boltzmann probabilities. Directly fitting such a model to the KLF dataset is intractable due to dissociationinduced uncertainties in absolute in vivo occupancies. Instead, we fit the average nucleosome occupancy distribution along each construct, a metric robust to TF dissociation (Figure S5D). We first fit a simple competition model involving only TF-DNA and nucleosome-DNA binding energies (Figure S5E). This model produced linear occupancy changes and failed to capture sigmoidal accessibility or low-end thresholding in TF occupancy (Figures S5F and S5G). Prior SMF studies47 attributed such nonlinearities to TF-recruited chromatin remodelers, such as BAF.

[Page 10]

Indeed, a three-parameter model47 including a nucleosome destabilization term when TFs are present (Figure 5D) can recapitulate nucleosome occupancy, observed single-molecule states, and the lack of KLF binding at low motif numbers (Figures 5B, 5C, 5E, S5F, and S5H). Since endogenous KLF contains structured and disordered domains beyond its DBD, we also tested a model wherein protein-protein interactions (PPIs) enabled KLF molecules to stabilize each other’s binding (Figure S5E). While this model recapitulates low-end thresholding, it more poorly fits KLF and nucleosome occupancies (Figures S5F and S5G). The stronger performance of the nucleosome destabilization model is supported by evidence, both in vitro and in vivo, that KLF1 interacts directly with BAF subunits80–82 and other cofactors83–85 to remodel chromatin.86 In this model, the effective TF binding energy combines the TF-DNA binding energy with the free nuclear TF concentration. Using the derived relationship between fitted energy and TF concentration (see STAR Methods), we compared the partitionfunction fit energy with the in vitro extended PWM-predicted energy to estimate the concentration of free nuclear KLF molecules. We inferred ∼30,000 molecules per nucleus, which is within an order of magnitude of many human TF counts87 and comparable to ∼15,000 KLF1 molecules per cell measured during erythropoiesis88 (Figure S5I). Increased motif spacing lowers KLF occupancy in vivo, but not in vitro, due to competition with nucleosomes To test our thermodynamic model’s predictive power for unseen motif configurations, we designed a library with increased motif spacings from 15 bp to 25 and 40 bp for two, four, and six motifs (Figure S5J). The nucleosome destabilization model predicts that farther spacing decreases TF binding and shifts nucleosome occupancy into shallower and wider stretches of accessibility (Figures 5F, 5G, and S5K). Experimentally, increased motif spacing indeed caused these changes to both TF and nucleosome occupancy (Figures 5F–5H and S5K). Predictions are more accurate for nucleosomes than for TFs, likely because nucleosomes’ lack of dissociation enables more accurate footprinting. While PPIs between KLFs could drive motif-spacing dependence, this model failed to predict changes to nucleosome occupancies (Figure S5L). To validate these observations, we used base-resolved deep learning models BPNet89 and ChromBPNet55 trained on data from HEK293 cells with KLF1 overexpression to predict ChIP and assay for transposase-accessible chromatin (ATAC) signals, respectively, from DNA sequence. With increasing spacings between two KLF motifs, BPNet predicts decreasing TF occupancy, and ChromBPNet predicts decreasing accessibility (Figure 5I). Consistent with nucleosome competition, rather than PPIs driving motif-spacing dependence, these model-predicted decreases occur monotonically over length scales incompatible with interactions between KLF molecules. We next measured in vitro binding affinity across motif spacings, where KLF1 alone is binding DNA, and found no trend (Figures S5M and S5N). Thus, decreased KLF occupancy in cells with increased spacing is likely due to nucleosome competition (Figure 5J). In this model, less dense motifs impose a greater energetic penalty because more extensive nucleosome displace-

ment is required to maintain full TF binding (Figure 5J). In summary, thermodynamic models explain how nucleosome competition and chromatin remodeler recruitment modulate KLF binding across motif number and spacing in cells. In silico experiments support relevance of sequencecontext effects in vivo We next asked if the sequence-context effects on affinity observed in vitro are sufficient to tune KLF binding in cells. First, we tested whether in vitro binding predicts ChIP signal in cells by making HiTS-FLIP measurements for DNA sequences underlying HEK293 KLF1 ChIP peaks. In vitro association rates showed minimal correlation with ChIP signal (r = 0.1), suggesting that the multitude of additional factors, such as other TF motifs and chromatin state, substantially tune binding in cells and preclude direct prediction of KLF1 occupancy from in vitro affinity alone (Figure S6A). To regress out indirect effects from other motifs, we performed in silico marginalization with the KLF1 BPNet model. For each base at each position (either in the motif or flanks), we measured its impact on predicted ChIP signal. We fixed that base (keeping the rest of the motif constant) and averaged the change in predicted signal across 1,000 random sequence contexts relative to predictions where the base was not fixed. Previously, this approach extracted affinities for yeast TFs Pho4 and Cbf1 comparable to in vitro measurements.90 For KLF1, the in silicoderived PWMs are qualitatively comparable to in vitro-derived PWMs for both the core motif and flanks, including the two 5′ motif-adjacent guanines (Figures 6A and 6B). Furthermore, the BPNet PWM weights linearly relate to in vitro-derived weights (core motif r2 = 0.90, flank r2 = 0.74; Figure 6C). Similar to our in vitro findings, the BPNet-derived sequence preferences reveal a preference against GC-rich flanks one helical turn away (r = 0.6; Figures 6B and 6D) and a ∼10.5 bp periodicity in the correlation between GC preference and predicted TF binding (Figures S6B and S6C). Together, these results demonstrate that in vitro sequence preferences for motifs and flanks are highly consistent with the sequence determinants of KLF1 occupancy in cells, as distilled genome-wide through deep learning models. Since in vitro experiments were performed with only KLF1’s DBD, this concordance suggests that KLF1’s sequence preferences in cells are driven by the DBD with minimal contribution from its other structured or disordered domains. Combined extended PWM and thermodynamic models predict in vivo flanking sequence effects We next asked whether sequence context could quantitatively predict in vivo molecular states by combining our extended PWM (for predicting TF energies from flanking sequence) with our thermodynamic model (for predicting binding within chromatin from TF energies). Extended PWM energies were transformed into partition-function-compatible energies, enabling prediction of single-molecule states from flanking sequence perturbations without additional fit parameters (Figure 6E). To test this combined model, we designed a 300-member SMFcompatible library in which each sequence contains four consensus motifs spaced 32 bp apart, with flanks consistent across all four sites but variable across the library (Figure 6F).

In agreement with in vitro findings, in vivo KLF occupancy is largely driven by the first two flanking bases (which likely directly contact KLF1), and variability decreases with longer constant flanks (Figures 6H and S6J). In the variability that still arises from distal flanks, we investigated if it reflects the in vitro preference against higher GC content one helical turn away from the motif. Indeed, in vivo KLF occupancy decreases as distal GC content increases for sequences with fixed proximal flanks (Figures 6I and S6K). Consistent with BPNet results, this experiment shows that proximal flanks strongly tune in vivo KLF occupancy, while distal flanks (which we posit alter TF search dynamics) also tune binding, albeit more subtly. Beyond TF binding changes, nucleosome occupancy (a metric robust to KLF dissociation during methylation) shows a sigmoidal relationship to predicted TF energies, with molecules ranging from fully inaccessible to fully accessible (ρ = 0.53; Figure S6L). The combined models recapitulate this sigmoidal response, matching moderately well when using the TF concentration inferred earlier (Figure S5I) and well when directly fitting TF concentration in this experiment (r = 0.51 and 0.88, respectively; Figure S6L). As expected, transient KLF1 overexpression increases the fit TF concentration (Figures S6M–S6P). Collectively, these data show that in vitro affinities predict in vivo sequence preferences, sequence context substantially tunes binding in cells, and biophysical and deep learning models can quantitatively predict these effects. DISCUSSION Our study provides tandem in vitro and in vivo measurement of KLF1 equilibrium and kinetic binding and implements biophysical models to predict in vivo occupancy of unseen sequences from in vitro affinities. We found that, as in prior lac repressor studies,10 KLF1 binding strength is driven by motif recognition probability. This observation holds across these bacterial and eukaryotic TFs despite their distinct DNA recognition modes. The dimeric lac repressor’s helix-turn-helix conformationally changes and bends DNA upon specifically binding its 20– 21 bp palindromic motif.91 By contrast, monomeric C2H2-ZFs like KLF1 bend DNA less, with conformational changes restricted to finger linker regions upon specifically binding shorter non-palindromic motifs.58,59 Thus, this microscopic model of DNA binding is likely relevant across human TF families even though binding mechanisms vary. Furthermore, anticorrelated relationships between macromolecular association and dissociation rates have been observed for other systems when sequence or salt conditions are perturbed.92–95 We believe our theoretical framework10—a sequential binding pathway with one or several testing states that the searching molecule must pass through before specifically binding—may be generally applicable to many kinetic systems in molecular biology. Mutations affecting earlier steps in a binding pathway primarily modulate macroscopic association rates, while mutations affecting later steps primarily modulate macroscopic dissociation rates,10 allowing this model to account for such experimental observations.96 KLF1’s motif recognition probability depends on both proximal and distal sequences outside of the core motif. This flank-

dependence may explain how different KLF family members could regulate distinct targets despite highly conserved core motifs.61,97,98 Since direct contacts with finger one likely underlie proximal flank preferences, KLF1’s core motif likely should include flank positions − 2 and − 1. High-throughput quantitative measurement of flank effects, as performed here, may reveal overlooked core motif bases for other TFs. Extending such measurements across zinc fingers and other TF families could test the generalizability of distal flank preferences and distinguish whether GC content (i.e., DNA stiffness) or motif-likeness drives these preferences. We anticipate that in vitro single-molecule target search assays9 could resolve the molecular details underlying distal flank effects. Our observation of a minutes-long in nuclei residence time for KLF contradicts the model that TF-DNA interactions are highly transient in mammalian cells.16,99,100 Locus-resolved singlemolecule tracking (SMT) of the yeast TF Gal4 also reports minutes-long residence times,21 interactions previously undetected by non-locus-specific SMT.101 We anticipate that methods such as these, which track TF kinetics at specific loci rather than across the nuclear milieu, will clarify TF binding timescales and their relationship to downstream processes (e.g., transcriptional bursting). We note, however, that footprintingbased measurements will only quantify an upper bound of TF residence time for TFs with fast off rates due to the time required to methylate accessible sites (Figures S4G and S4H). However, more efficient or concentrated enzymes may extend the measurable range. Here, we show that accurate biophysical prediction of in vivo chromatin configurations for unseen motif grammars (e.g., spacing and affinity) is possible using in vitro-derived TF binding parameters and thermodynamic assumptions about TF-chromatin interactions. Pairing in vitro and in vivo measurements demonstrates that inter-motif spacing can perturb TF binding via nucleosome competition without requiring structural or DNA allosteric interactions as in the enhanceosome model.102 Recently, we demonstrated that gene expression can be explained from in vivo TF binding and simple physical models.47 Together, these studies highlight that, with high-resolution and quantitative measurements, cellular processes can be understood from biophysical parameters and statistical mechanics models. Our models accurately predict chromatin states with few physical and interpretable free parameters, in contrast to highly parameterized ‘‘black box’’ deep learning models. Moreover, we do not need to invoke highly cooperative biological state changes, such as phase separation or condensation, to describe in vivo TF binding in our observed contexts. Incorporating more biophysical information into these models, such as nucleosome-DNA sequence specificity and additional TF affinities and concentrations, should further improve prediction of chromatin configurations at genomic regulatory elements. Limitations of the study Our in vitro measurements use only KLF1’s DBD due to challenges in purifying full-length proteins, while SMF experiments in cells relied on endogenous full-length KLF1, which contains effector domains and intrinsically disordered regions. These non-DBD domains could mediate PPIs and drive in vivo-specific

cooperativity (e.g., with respect to motif spacing). However, including PPI energies in our partition-function model could not explain the data as well as nucleosome-eviction models. In vivo-derived deep learning models predict motif-spacing effects beyond the range of direct PPIs, supporting the nucleosome destabilization model. Still, we cannot fully exclude potential PPIs between KLF1 molecules. While KLF1 is the most highly expressed KLF in K562s (Figure S4A), and our conclusions are consistent under KLF1 overexpression, other KLF family members may also bind the inserted motifs in cells due to the highly conserved DBD and similar sequence preferences. Finally, TFs bound to nucleosomal DNA are likely invisible to accessibility-based readouts like SMF. We therefore cannot exclude the possibility that KLF1 binds directly to nucleosomal DNA. Indeed, the homolog KLF4 is considered a classical pioneer factor because it weakly binds nucleosomal DNA in vitro.103 However, compared with specific motif binding, these low-affinity nucleosome interactions, if they exist for KLF1, are unlikely to substantially affect energetics in our thermodynamic model. Future ChIP-SMF experiments (i.e., SMF readouts on pulled-down molecules) may clarify potential pioneering activity in cells.

EXPERIMENTAL MODEL AND STUDY PARTICIPANT DETAILS Cell lines and cell culture Cell culture was performed as described in DelRosso et al.104 Briefly, all experiments were performed in K562 cells (female, ATCC CCL-243). Cells were cultured in a controlled humidified incubator at 37◦ C and 5% CO2, in RPMI 1640 (Gibco, 11-875-119) media supplemented with 10% FBS (Omega Scientific, 20014T), and 1% Penicillin-Streptomycin-Glutamine (Gibco, 10378016). Reporter cell lines were generated as in Tycko et al.105 Briefly, reporter cell lines were generated by TALEN-mediated homologydirected repair to integrate donor constructs into the AAVS1 locus by electroporation of 1 × 106 K562 cells with 1 ug of reporter donor plasmid and 0.5 ug of each TALEN-L (Addgene no. 35431) and TALEN-R (Addgene no. 35432) plasmid. After 2 days, the cells were treated with 1,000 ng ml− 1 puromycin antibiotic for 7 days to select for a population where the donor was stably integrated in the intended locus. These cell lines were not authenticated. All cell lines tested negative for mycoplasma. Overexpression was performed by electroporation of 1 × 106 K562 cells (selected to have desired constructs) with 2 ug of KLF1 overexpression plasmid (Addgene no. 120488) or 2 ug of pMAXGFP (Addgene no. 177825) as a control. Measurements were made 48 hours after electroporation. In vitro library design A total of 22,517 sequences were designed for in vitro experiments. The vast majority of these (including all results in Figures 1, 2, and 3) contains sequence variations in a 174 bp long variable region, varying the motif sequence, the motif position, the flanking sequence context around a motif, the number of motifs and motif spacing, and combinations thereof. Background sequences were generated randomly and any sub-sequences that resembled the KLF motif were removed. Unless stated otherwise, all HiTS-FLIP results in Figures 1, 2, and 3 used background seq3 (see Table S1), where this background was varied in a number of different ways (see below) to create varying sequence contexts. The flanking sequence context around a motif was varied both by directly mutating the flank for a constant motif position, and by changing the motif position (with varying length and identity of constant flank sequence proximal to the motif) in a constant sequence background. The 20 constant flank sequences used were generated randomly and any sub-sequences that resembled the KLF motif were removed. For the fixed motif position, flanks were mutated in a number of different ways, including randomizing the sequence 60 bp to the left and/or right of a motif (with varying length and identity of constant flank sequence proximal to the motif) and point mutating smaller regions in the 15 bp closest to the motif to the left and/or right (with varying length and identity of constant flank sequence proximal to the motif). For these flank variations with fixed motif positions, no removal of sub-sequences that resembled the KLF motif was performed. Many of these flanking sequence context variations were also included in sequences where the strong consensus motif (CCACGCCCA) had been mutated to a dead motif (TACTTAGAT) (Figures S2A and S2B, 3,295 sequences measured). To directly compare in vitro binding signals to binding observed in vivo in KLF1 chromatin immunoprecipitation experiments (Figure S6A), 200 sequences taken from KLF1 ChIP-seq peaks in HEK293 cells (ENCODE: ENCSR859BMR) were included in the library. Sequences were taken in a 173 bp window around the peak centers. Peaks were chosen to get a broad range of peak signal values, from the sub-set of peak sequences that only contained one clear KLF1 motif. KLF1 motifs were counted using PWMScan106 with a PWM model from K562 cells (ENCODE: ENCSR550HCT) with a cutoff score of 1312 for counting a sub-sequence as a KLF1 motif. HiTS-FLIP measurements High-throughput biophysical binding measurements were performed using the sequenced MiSeq flow cell and a custom prism total internal reflection fluorescence microscope described in She et al107 and Becker et al.108 Briefly, the XY-stage, Z-stage, lasers, syringe pump, objective and camera were salvaged from an Illumina GAIIx instrument, and integrated with a fluidics adaptor that was designed to interface with Illumina MiSeq flow cells. The instrument was further equipped with a temperature control system and laser control electronics. Imaging of TMR - KLF1 DBD and TMR fiducial marker fluorescence was performed with a 532 nm laser and a 590 nm center wavelength and 104 nm guaranteed minimum 93% bandwidth bandpass emission filter (Semrock). Imaging for DNA amount quantification was performed with a 660 nm laser and a 664 nm long pass emission filter (Semrock). Following sequencing, MiSeq flow cells were prepared for binding experiments. First, DNA not covalently attached to the flow cell surface was removed by washing the flow cell with 100% formamide at 55 ◦ C. To remove residual fluorescence from the sequencing, the flow cell was then washed with a cleavage buffer (100 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8, 100 mM NaCl, 0.05% Tween20, 100 mM TCEP) for 15 minutes at 60 ◦ C. Fiducial marker oligo and a primer for double stranding (Table S3) were then hybridized to the single stranded DNA on the flow cell surface in a number of steps. Deep learning model for flanks in vitro The training, validation and test data for the deep learning flank model consisted of the same DNA sequences and measured KD values as for the flank PWM model. The 174 bp sequences each contain the consensus KLF1 binding motif CCACGCCA. This motif was then cut out from the sequence along with the 30 closest nucleotides flanking each side of the motif. PyTorch was used to implement the model with both the transformer and positional encoder class inheriting from torch.nn.Module. The model follows the classic transformer schema, starting off with an embedding layer, followed by a positional encoding layer. Single-molecule state calling model State calls were performed as in Doughty et al47 with a few modifications. Briefly, single-molecule states were assigned to each read using a maximum-likelihood approach. First, all possible single-molecule states (for a given amplicon) were enumerated (and precomputed to speed up future computations). A single-molecule state represents the occupancy status of each motif, as well as the position (start and end coordinates, snapped to the nearest GpC) of all nucleosomes covering the molecule. The full list of states was enumerated by taking the Cartesian product of the power set of the motifs (each can be bound or unbound) and all possible positions of as many nucleosomes will fit along the molecule (mandating that the nucleosomes start and end at GpCs, have at least one accessible GpC in a linker between any pair of two nucleosomes, have at least 25 bp in between bound TFs and nucleosomes, overlap the molecule with at least 30 bp, and are between 110-140 bp in length). For each hypothetical state (TF occupancy and nucleosome positioning), the expected methylation signal was computed by determining whether each GpC is accessible, protected by a nucleosome, or protected by a TF. These GpC occupancy states were then converted into probabilities using different methylation probabilities (unobserved) for each of the three possible occupancy states. Methylation probabilities were decreased for short methylation times. This results in a matrix of size (number of states) x (number of GpCs) representing the probability that GpC j is methylated in state i. This matrix is then converted into a matrix P representing the probability of observing a converted base after the EM-seq reactions using the methylated (pUC19) and unmethylated (lambda) conversion rates from the controls. The maximum (log-)likelihood state for each input molecule (from single-molecule methylation matrix M, which is (number of GpCs) x (number of molecules)) was computed using a Bernoulli likelihood across all GpC positions in vectorized format, resulting in a vector of the most likely state for each input molecule. The code is available at https://github.com/ GreenleafLab/klf_manuscript_code/blob/main/classify_single_molecule_binding_v4.py. Partition function model The partition function model was constructed as in Doughty et al47 with a few modifications. Briefly, the steady-state non-equilibrium thermodynamic model was constructed by deriving a function that assigns each molecule an energy. This energy function takes as input every possible single-molecule configuration from the state-calling model and counts the number of TFs (integers) and nucleosomes (including fractions of nucleosomes) occupying the enhancer. Each bound TF or nucleosome contributes a linear binding energy to the total energy of the molecule, except that nucleosomes bound to molecules with TFs also bound have their affinity reduced. Thus, three parameters (ΔETF, ΔEnuc, ΔEremodel) are used to assign each molecule an energy. A given set of these parameters determines an energy, and therefore a Boltzmann probability, for each molecular state. The nucleosome energy was assigned to the value fit in Doughty et al47 due to the same locus being used. In Figure 5, we then used maximum likelihood estimation to fit the two parameters (ΔETF, ΔEremodel) that optimize the probability of seeing all observed molecules. The Simple Competition (Figures S5E–S5G) was fit as above, although forcing the value of Eremodel to 0. The Protein-Protein Interaction (Figures S5E–S5G) was fit as above, although the three parameters fit are ΔETF, ΔEnuc, and ΔEPPI, where ΔEPPI is an energy of interaction that is then multiplied by the number of pairs of bound TFs in order to calculate the overall energy of the molecular state. In Figure S6L, the now fixed parameters (ΔEnuc, ΔEremodel) are used, and the TF concentration ([TF]) is either fit directly or inferred (from value in Figure S5I) to derive ΔETF from the PWM-predicted energies. The models were fit separately to each biological replicate of each experimental condition, and statistics were done on the independent fit values.
