DNA bendability inside the nucleosome regulates INO80’s nucleosome positioning Graphical abstract

In brief Shukla et al. find that the INO80 chromatin remodeler positions nucleosomes at specific genomic positions, including the 5′ end of genes. INO80 recognizes DNA shape rather than a unique DNA sequence. Nucleosome movement is blocked when INO80 encounters inflexible DNA inside the nucleosome, mediated by the DNA-rigidity-sensitive binding of its Arp5 subunit.

Highlights

• The INO80 remodeler positions nucleosomes at gene promoters by ‘‘reading’’ DNA shape

• DNA needs to bend where it first enters for INO80 to move nucleosomes

• Nucleosomes are moved by bent DNA forming bulges that move around the nucleosome

• INO80’s Arp5 subunit contacts DNA and histones and regulates bent DNA movement

SUMMARY

While some ATP-dependent chromatin remodelers are negatively regulated by short tracts of DNA sequences (i.e., poly d(A) or GC-rich), the INO80 chromatin remodeler is regulated by DNA not readily identified by its sequence but rather by its physical properties. The underlying reason for these differences appears to be the unique mechanism by which INO80 mobilizes nucleosomes. We find that the INO80 chromatin remodeler mobilizes nucleosomes by displacing DNA from the histone octamer and creating DNA ‘‘bulges’’ that translocate around the octamer in a wave-like manner. Nucleosome movement is blocked by inflexible nucleosomal DNA that interferes with the initial formation of DNA bulges and is linked to INO80’s accurate positioning of nucleosomes at the +1 position of yeast gene promoters. Some of the interactions of the Arp5 subunit are lost when bound to inflexible DNA and may act as sensors to regulate INO80 remodeling in a DNA-shape-dependent manner.

INTRODUCTION Transcription, DNA replication, and repair are regulated by ATPdependent chromatin remodelers that reorganize chromatin to make DNA accessible or alter its composition at specific DNA regions. Each of these remodelers contains an ATP-dependent DNA translocase domain that binds and moves along nucleosomal DNA, which facilitates moving or displacing nucleosomes or exchanging canonical and variant histones.1–3 The INO80 family of remodelers swaps out different variant or canonical H2A for demarcating regions of chromatin involved in gene promoters, heterochromatin formation, origins of replication, and DNA damage repair.4–7 INO80, the founding member of this family, also spaces nucleosomes equally apart and uniquely positions nucleosomes in the genome.8–12 Mutations, deletions, and overexpression of INO80 and its subunits are associated with heart disease13,14 and cancer.15–17 The Korber lab found that in vitro INO80 positions nucleosomes like that observed in vivo18 and its DNA specificity is due to physical differences in the DNA rather than a well conserved DNA motif.9,19 The distinct physical properties are a more negative propeller twist 80–120 bp from the dyad axis or a more positive propeller twist in the DNA 30–60 bp from the

dyad axis compared to nucleosomes positioned by salt dialysis.9,19 These data suggest that DNA shape and mechanics are read by INO80. INO80’s DNA specificity is notably different from that of SWI/SNF, CHD1, and SWR1, where short poly(dA) or GC-rich tracts inhibit the ATPase domain from translocating on DNA.9,12,20 It remains to be shown that inflexible DNA directly blocks INO80 from moving nucleosomes. Although binding of the Arp8 module of INO80 to extranucleosomal DNA regulates nucleosome mobilization, rigid extranucleosomal DNA has not been shown to do so.10,12,19 There were no significant differences in the rates of nucleosome movement with rigid or flexible extranucleosomal yeast genomic DNA combined with 601 core nucleosomes (Extended Data, Figures 6C and 6D with 1- to 1.8-fold difference in Basu et al.21). Until now, there have been no studies testing whether rigid DNA 30–60 bp from the dyad axis on the entry side of nucleosomes arrests INO80 remodeling and whether it is a crucial factor in INO80’s DNA specificity. We now find that rigid DNA with a more positive DNA twist at the entry side arrests nucleosome movement by INO80 and reflects that previously observed for INO80 on yeast genomic DNA. Our study is the first to show that DNA rigidity regulates the nucleosome mobilization activity of an ATP-dependent chromatin remodeler.

We discovered, while delineating the step where DNA shape and bendability are critical for remodeling, that INO80 displaces ∼30 bp of DNA from the histone octamer, creating DNA loops or bulges that move across the nucleosome surface. The DNA specificity of INO80 is linked to its mechanism for mobilizing nucleosomes, which is quite distinct from that of ISWI, CHD1, and SWI/SNF chromatin remodelers, where small DNA distortions move around the histone octamer. RESULTS DNA on the entry side of nucleosomes halts INO80mediated nucleosome movement Mechanical unwrapping of nucleosomes and RNA polymerase II (RNAPII) transcribing through nucleosomes have both shown the asymmetric nature of the 601 nucleosome.22–24 On the one side, the DNA is stiffer and more readily peeled off, while the other is innately curved, binds tighter to the histone octamer,

and is a barrier to enzymes such as RNAPII. We investigate whether the asymmetry of the 601 nucleosome influences the capacity of Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Sc) INO80 to mobilize nucleosomes by changing INO80’s orientation on nucleosomes (Figure 1A).11,18,19,21 Nucleosomes with 70 and 5 bp of extranucleosomal DNA on the (− ) and (+) sides (70N5) are remodeled >10 faster than the reverse, with 5N70 nucleosomes (Figures 1B, S1A, and S1B; Table 1). To better understand this DNA sequence specificity, we reverse the central 72 bp DNA bound to the H3-H4 tetramer, referred to as M1, or separately switch the flanking 36 bp of DNA bound to the H2A-H2B dimer, referred to as M2 (Figure 1D). The (+) side of the central 72 bp region is intrinsically bent, with 3-TA dinucleotides spaced ∼10 bp apart, energetically favored for histone octamer binding, in contrast to the other more rigid side.24 Altering the flanking DNA has a stronger effect on INO80-mediated nucleosome mobilization than changing the orientation of the core DNA. The rate of moving 70N5 nucleosomes by INO80 remodeling was reduced 22-fold by switching the flanking 36 bp of 601 DNA that binds the H2A-H2B dimer (Figures 1E and S1A; Table 1). These data indicate that nucleosome movement by INO80 is negatively regulated by DNA bound to the H2A-H2B surface on the entry side of nucleosomes. DNA bound to the H3-H4 tetramer surface impacts INO80 remodeling to a lesser effect, as seen by a 4-fold reduction in the rate of remodeling when reversed. Switching the orientation of INO80 binding with 5N70 nucleosomes and reversing the direction of the central 73 bp DNA did not change the already severely blocked INO80 remodeling (Figures 1G and S1B; Table 1). When the less favorable tetramer DNA orientation is paired with the preferred flanking 36 bp DNA, i.e., M2 5N70, we observed a 5-fold increase in the rate of nucleosome movement, resembling that seen when the core 73 bp DNA is swapped in the 70N5 nucleosome (Figures 1G and S1B; Table 1). Together, these results show that INO80 remodeling can be regulated by the DNA bound to the incoming H2A-H2B dimer and the H3-H4 tetramer surface. Those substrates where nucleosome movement is blocked exhibit ATP hydrolysis that is uncoupled from nucleosome movement, as they have the same or higher rate of ATP hydrolysis compared to those that are moved efficiently (Figures 1E– 1H). Similar uncoupling of ATP hydrolysis from nucleosome movement has been observed when Arp5 is absent25–27 or the acid patch in the histone octamer28 or Arp8 module binding to extranucleosomal DNA8,10 is lost. The ATPase activity was 3-fold higher with M2 than 601 70N5 nucleosomes, indicative

of the ATPase domain working more efficiently (Figures 1E and 1F; Table 1). Our data suggest that the combination of rigid DNA on the H2A-H2B entry side and adjacent H3-H4 is required for hyperactive ATPase activity, as 601 5N70 nucleosomes with flexible DNA on the adjacent H3-H4 side and rigid DNA on the H2AH2B side halt nucleosome movement without increasing ATPase activity (Figures 1G and 1H; Table 1). Inflexible DNA at the entry side of nucleosomes blocks INO80-mediated nucleosome movement The predicted intrinsic propeller twist of DNA from − 38 to − 72 bp from the dyad axis of M2 nucleosomes is more positive than that of DNA from the same region of 601 nucleosomes (Figure 2A; Table S1). These differences in propeller twist resemble that seen for nucleosomes positioned by INO80 on yeast genomic DNA versus nucleosomes assembled by salt dialysis.19 The DNA from − 51 to − 87 bp from the dyad axis in M2 nucleosomes is predicted by DNAcycP2 to be intrinsically less prone to bend than the corresponding DNA region in 601 nucleosomes21,29 (Figure 2B; Table S2). The most dramatic difference in DNA bendability is predicted at the entry point of nucleosomes. We searched for yeast genomic DNAs that are highly prone to position nucleosomes in vitro when remodeled by INO80 that are nucleosome-free when first assembled by salt dialysis (Figures 2C, step 1, S2B, and S2C).19 The DNAcycP2 analysis found that DNA from − 36 to − 72 bp from the dyad center of nucleosomes positioned by INO80 is generally more intrinsically inflexible, similar to that observed in M2 nucleosomes (Figures 2C, step 2, S2B, and S2C; Table S3). The entire 36 bp of inflexible DNA is required to efficiently arrest INO80 remodeling The rigid 36 bp flanking DNA was divided into three sections, and each section was inserted into the corresponding locations on the entry side of 70N5 601 nucleosomes (Figure 2E). The predicted intrinsic DNA bendability of these three DNA constructs reflects different states of DNA inflexibility. The M2.1 and M2.3 constructs, with DNA inserted at − 65 to − 72 and − 37 to − 53 bp from the dyad axis, respectively, either decrease DNA flexibility near the entry side or further inside the nucleosome—specifically at − 68 to − 87 or − 45 to − 68 bp from the dyad axis (Figure 2F; Table S2). Less DNA bendability at either of these two positions is alone insufficient to block INO80 remodeling, as evidenced by only a 1.4- to 1.6-fold reduction in the rate of remodeling (Figures 2G and S4). Insertion of inhibitory DNA at − 55 to − 64 bp from the dyad axis replaces an AT-rich region at superhelical location (SHL)− 6 with a GC-rich DNA (M2.2) and reduces DNA bendability over a larger region, − 44 to − 87 bp from the dyad axis. The M2.2 DNA, however, did not have the same level of intrinsic inflexibility as the M2 DNA near the entry side of nucleosome, from − 64 to − 87 bp from the dyad axis (Figure 2F; Table S2). M2.2 nucleosomes were remodeled 4-fold slower than 601 nucleosomes (11/2.7 pM/s), which is ∼5-fold lesser effect than with M2 nucleosomes (Figures 2G and S4; Table 1). These experiments show the extent of DNA inflexibility required to block INO80 remodeling and that the entire 36 bp region is required to efficiently block INO80 remodeling. The GC-rich DNA might also inhibit the ATPase domain from translocating along DNA, as with other chromatin remodelers9,12,20 but, as we show next, this does not occur with INO80. The initial rate of DNA movement on the entry side was the same for M2 and 601 70N5 nucleosomes, as measured by the loss of DNA crosslinking at the starting position 54 nucleotides (nt) from the dyad axis (Figures 3B and S5C). DNA displacement monitored by overall net loss of DNA crosslinking was 2.5-fold faster on the entry side for M2 nucleosomes than 601 (Figures 3C and S5C). Faster DNA displacement in M2 nucleosomes is consistent with DNA on the entry side of the H3-H4 tetramer being more readily displaced due to its rigidity and lack of conforming to the curvature of the histone octamer. DNA would also need to be displaced from the tetramer in this region for DNA to move and rebind 20 bp from its starting position. DNA movement on the exit side was tracked for both M2 and 601 nucleosomes (Figures S5A, S5B and S5D), and 21 bp of DNA moves out faster and to a greater extent with M2 than with 601 70N5 nucleosomes (Figures 3F and S5D). These data indicate that the higher ATPase activity observed earlier with M2 70N5 nucleosomes (Figures 1E–1H) is likely due to the ATPase domain moving more efficiently on nucleosomal DNA, owing to the higher tendency of DNA being displaced at the entry side of the H3-H4 tetramer. The higher ATPase activity also shows that the ATPase domain cycles in a processive and abortive fashion when further DNA movement is blocked, which is not seen when DNA is less likely to be displaced from the tetramer surface, as observed with 601 5N70 nucleosomes. The step missing for productive nucleosome movement appears to be the ability to bend DNA and form DNA loops, rather than ATPase domain movement and hydrolysis. After DNA was displaced from the entry side and 21 bp of DNA exited, displaced DNA on the entry side rebounded to the histone octamer 20 bp from its starting position and an additional 11 bp of DNA moved out on the exit side, for a total of 32 bp— which were not observed with M2 nucleosomes (Figures 3D and 3G). DNA bending is likely required to move DNA farther inside the nucleosome and form a loop or bulge on the histone octamer surface before DNA can rebind 20 bp from its starting position at the entry side of nucleosomes. Although the 21 bp movement is considerably faster and more efficient with M2 than 601 nucleosomes on the exit side, the rate of moving 32 bp out on the exit side is 13-fold slower with M2 nucleosomes (Figures 3F and 3G; Table 1). Rebinding of DNA at the entry site appears to be required to move 32 bp DNA out on the exit side. DNA also moved 2-fold slower from its starting position on the exit side with M2 nucleosomes (Figure 3H). Moving the DNA bulge onto the exit side of nucleosomes is blocked by rigid DNA on the entry side The DNA bulge over the tetramer surface is expected to move toward the exit side of nucleosomes. Our original histone-DNA tracking method was modified to capture transient DNA bulges on the exit side by stopping remodeling with photocrosslinking (not quenched) and comparing when stopped with EDTA and competitor DNA before photocrosslinking (quenched, see Figure 4C). Less DNA is detected moving 32 bp on the exit

side when unquenched than with quenching (Figures 4D, S7A and S7B). Due to the size of the displaced bulge, DNA repositions 32 bp from its starting position. There is no decrease in 21 bp of DNA moving out on the exit side of M2 nucleosomes when samples are not quenched versus quenched, consistent with DNA not being actively displaced at the exit side (Figure 4E). These differences between M2 and 601 nucleosomes indicate that DNA can move out of the exit side in a manner that depends on DNA bending at the entry side and DNA bulge formation (i.e., 601), or alternatively, independently of DNA bending in a less productive manner (i.e., M2). These data are consistent with displaced DNA on the entry side moving farther inside the nucleosomes, forming a DNA bulge over the tetramer surface, and moving over the H2A-H2B surface before finally exiting the nucleosome. In this model, tighter binding to the tetramer on the side where the DNA bulge initially forms is expected to interfere with DNA lifting off the tetramer surface and bulge formation (Figures 1E and 1G). The DNA specificity of INO80 at the H2A-H2B interface is not shared with ISW2, another nucleosome spacing factor We found that the DNA specificity of INO80 is not shared by ISW2, an ATP-dependent remodeler that equally spaces nucleosomes in arrays and is mediated by a non-catalytic subunit bound to extranucleosomal DNA, similar to INO80.37–39 ISW2 moved 5N70 nucleosomes only 2-fold slower than 70N5 nucleosomes, which is much less than the estimated >10-fold difference seen with INO80 (Figure 7A compared to Figure 1B; Table 1). At the +1 nucleosome positions of gene promoters and other positions where INO80 alone positions nucleosomes, 36 bp of DNA on the flanking sides of these nucleosomes are similarly inflexible.9,19 For the first time, we show that these inflexible yeast genomic DNAs, when placed at the entry side of nucleosomes, arrest nucleosome movement by INO80 and provide direct evidence for how they function in vivo. The DNA specificity of INO80 is distinct from that previously observed for SWI/SNF, RSC, CHD1, and SWR1, where short tracts of poly (dA) or GC-rich DNA interfere with the ATPase domain’s twisting of DNA and/or translocating along DNA.9,12,20 Instead, the DNA specificity of INO80 is due to larger stretches of DNA that are minimally ∼36 bp with

no well-defined consensus sequence. Rather than interfering with the ATPase domain moving on DNA, we found that rigid DNA on the entry side of nucleosomes, 36 to 72 bp from the dyad center, increases the ability of INO80 to displace 20 bp of DNA from the entry side and move 21 bp of DNA out the exit side of nucleosomes while still blocking nucleosome movement. During INO80 remodeling, the formation of a ∼30 bp DNA loop or bulge on the surface of the histone octamer near the H3-H4 tetramer surface was seen by DNA footprinting. Increased binding of Arp5 DBD at SHL− 2 and SHL− 3 and Ino80’s ATPase domain at SHL+2 was observed when the DNA bulge is formed, flanking the tetramer surface, and likely promotes or stabilizes DNA displacement at this region. Both the increased binding of DBD at SHL− 2 and SHL− 3 and the binding of the ATPase domain at SHL+2 are lost when rigid DNA is on the entry side of nucleosomes, coupled with the loss of the DNA bulge over the tetramer surface. DNA bound to the tetramer is also found to impact nucleosome movement by INO80, as switching the orientation of the tetramer-bound DNA interfered with remodeling. The orientation of the core 73 bp DNA that blocks remodeling places a ∼15 bp region, intrinsically bent at SHL− 2, causing DNA to bind tighter to the histone octamer, which is adjacent to where DNA is first displaced. Consistent with our model, tighter binding to nucleosomal DNA specifically at this region would be expected to impede the formation of DNA bulges over the tetramer surface. While most INO80 interactions are the same between M2 and 601 nucleosomes, a few significant differences are seen by histone-Arp5/Ies6 crosslinking and DNA footprinting with ScINO80. Previously, the grappler domain of ScArp5 was shown to bind nucleosomes near loop1, between α1 and α2 helices of histone H3, by photoaffinity labeling of Arp5 from residue 80 of histone H3 and peptide mapping.35 Grappler domain binding to the H3-H4 interface has also been seen in the cryo-EM structures of CtINO80:hexasomes, where DNA is partially lifted off the H3-H4 tetramer surface,36 but not for CtINO80:nucleosomes.11,28 One reason for this interaction not being detected by cryo-EM with nucleosomes is likely the dynamic nature of the grappler domain with only a subset of the different bound conformations being stable under cryogenic conditions.11,28,34 In ScINO80:nucleosomes, binding of the grappler domain to the loop1 region is sensitive to changes in DNA flexibility and is lost when rigid DNA is at the entry side. Binding of Arp5 DBD to nucleosomal DNA at SHL− 2, proximal to residue 80 of H3, is similarly lost when rigid DNA is at the entry side of nucleosomes. These results together show a general loss of Arp5 binding in this vicinity is likely the reason for the failure of enhanced binding at SHL− 2 and SHL− 3 during M2 nucleosome remodeling. Recently, binding of Arp5 DBD to nucleosomal DNA has been shown to be required for displacing DNA at the entry side, and now, we provide evidence that the DBD along with the grappler domain is important for the passage of displaced DNA and the formation of the initial DNA bulge.35 Although Ino80’s ATPase domain likely moves along DNA in a 1–2 bp stepwise manner like other remodelers, it is unique in that it does not mobilize nucleosomes by ∼1 bp distortions in a corkscrew fashion through the nucleosome like other remodelers.42–46 Instead, INO80 displaces ∼20–30 bp of DNA off the surface of nucleosomes, forming a DNA loop or bulge, which moves to the other side of the nucleosome. SWR1, a paralog of INO80, has been observed to displace large stretches of DNA from the histone octamer like that observed here for INO80, but SWR1 does not move nucleosomes.47 While these two

[Page 13]

complexes differ in their ability to mobilize nucleosomes, it is not surprising, given their extensive structural similarity and shared subunits, that they both displace large stretches of DNA off the histone octamer surface for the purpose of either exchanging histones or mobilizing nucleosomes. There are also reports that SWI/SNF might form DNA loops on nucleosomes, but SWI/SNF does not have the same DNA specificity as INO80.18,48 Limitations of the study In this study, DNA loops are inferred from increased DNA accessibility over the H3-H4 tetramer surface or loss of DNA contacts with the H2A-H2B dimer surface. An alternative explanation for the increased DNA accessibility detected by DNA footprinting is loosening of histone-DNA contacts with DNA being displaced a short distance from the histone octamer rather than forming DNA bulges. DNA moving 32 bp from its starting position after initial displacement from the H2A-H2B surface, as seen with histone-DNA site-specific crosslinking, is, however, inconsistent with loosening histone-DNA interactions. In the future, time resolved cryo-EM studies of INO80 nucleosome remodeling intermediates may further confirm the formation of DNA bulges during remodeling. Our study does not determine if INO80 has the same DNA specificity with hexasomes.33,36,49 The ATPase domain and Arp5 module are positioned quite differently on hexasomes compared to nucleosomes, yet they share some remodeling properties. Arp5 interactions are universally vital for hexasome and nucleosome remodeling and are also important for the DNA specificity of INO80. Binding of the DBD of Arp5 to DNA is equally required for nucleosome and hexasome remodeling, even though the DBD of Arp5 relocates from SHL− 3 to SHL+1 and SHL− 3 when bound to hexasomes.33,36 The DBD is important for INO80’s ‘‘sensing’’ of DNA flexibility and therefore might function the same with hexasomes. In contrast, Arp5 interactions with the acidic pocket of nucleosomes are not responsive to changes in DNA flexibility and are also not required for hexasome remodeling.36 Arp5’s grappler domain switches from contacting the acidic pocket of nucleosomes to binding the exposed surface of the H3-H4 tetramer of hexasomes.33,36 As seen by histone-Arp5 crosslinking, the grappler domain contacts the same H3-H4 tetramer surface in nucleosomes as in the cryo-EM structure of INO80 bound to hexasomes,35 and these interactions are involved in ‘‘sensing’’ DNA flexibility. These similarities suggest that INO80 might have DNA specificity with hexasomes like that of nucleosomes, although at different DNA positions. Further work is needed to confirm if DNA shape regulates INO80 remodeling of hexasomes.

Model building and refinement An AlphaFold330 prediction of the 70N5 M2 nucleosome was used as the starting model for the M2 nucleosome structure. The coordinates of the M2 nucleosome AlphaFold3 prediction were rigid body docked into the cryo-EM density of the M2 nucleosome using UCSF ChimeraX 1.6.145.32 The model was partially rebuilt in Coot 0.9.8.1.53 The atomic model was improved by ISOLDE 1.6.056 and real space refined in PHENIX 1.20.1–4.54,55 PDBs 8ATF (ATPase domain), 8AV6 (Rvb1/2, Arp5/Ies6, Ies2, 601 nucleosome), and AlphaFold3 predictions (Arp5 grappler) were used for figure generation of the INO80-M2 nucleosome complex using ChimeraX 1.6.145.32 Therefore, we fitted previously determined cryo-EM structures (8AV6, 8ATF)11 using rigid body fitting in ChimeraX. Next, domains that typically move slightly from structure to structure in relation to the prominent Rvb1/2 module, such as the ATPase, Arp5/Ies6, Ies2, the nucleosome, and grappler arms, were individually fitted into the map using rigid body fitting in ChimeraX. Due to the resolution limit, we did not adjust side chains or model loops at this stage.
