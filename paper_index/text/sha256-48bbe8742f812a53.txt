Is euchromatin really open in the cell? Kazuhiro Maeshima, 1,2,3,4,*,@ Shiori Iida, 1,2,4 Masa A. Shimazoe, 1,2 Sachiko Tamura, 1 and Satoru Ide 1,2 Genomic DNA is wrapped around a core histone octamer and forms a nucleosome. In higher eukaryotic cells, strings of nucleosomes are irregularly folded as chromatin domains that act as functional genome units. According to a typical textbook model, chromatin can be categorized into two types, euchromatin and heterochromatin, based on its degree of compaction. Euchromatin is open, while heterochromatin is closed and condensed. However, is euchromatin really open in the cell? New evidence from genomics and advanced imaging studies has revealed that euchromatin consists of condensed liquid-like domains. Condensed chromatin seems to be the default chromatin state in higher eukaryotic cells. We discuss this novel view of euchromatin in the cell and how the revealed organization is relevant to genome functions.

Highlights Euchromatin in higher eukaryotic cells forms condensed domains by the intrinsic property of chromatin. Condensed chromatin seems to be the default chromatin state in higher eukaryotic cells. Active transcription and other DNA transactions happen on the accessible surface of condensed domains. Liquid-like property inside a condensed domain gives a certain degree of accessibility for gene expression.

Euchromatin and heterochromatin in brief

The nature of gene activation is to keep the target region on the domain surface by large transcription complexes for the information output.

Growing evidence has demonstrated that strings of nucleosomes, consisting of DNA wrapped around core histones [1], are rather irregularly and dynamically folded as chromatin in the cell (Figure 1A,B) [2–9]. Super-resolution fluorescence microscopy and chromosome conformation capture (3C) derivatives like Hi-C (see Glossary) have revealed that chromatin forms various chromatin domains, such as the topologically associating domains (TADs), in the higher eukaryotic cell (Figure 1A) [10–17]. According to typical textbook models, chromatin can be categorized into two types (euchromatin and heterochromatin) based on its degree of compaction: open/sparse and closed/dense chromatin regions, respectively (Figure 1C) [18,19]. Euchromatin and heterochromatin were originally observed in 1928 by Heitz as weakly stained euchromatin and darkly stained heterochromatin [20]. They have specific combinations of histone modifications (e.g., active and inactive ones) and play distinct roles in the regulation of genomic functions [21–24]. However, is the whole of euchromatin really open (Figure 1C)? New pieces of evidence from versatile genomics and advanced imaging studies have revealed that this view on euchromatin is not necessarily correct. Euchromatin in higher eukaryotic cells essentially forms condensed domains (or globules) with sizes ranging from 100 to 300 nm in diameter, while only limited regions (i.e., domain surfaces or boundaries between domains) seem to be open (Figure 2, Key figure). We discuss a novel view of euchromatin as condensed liquid-like domains within the cell and how this view is relevant to genome functions.

Condensed chromatin domains are observed in euchromatin Recently, some studies have shown evidence that euchromatin forms condensed domains (or globules). Three-dimensional structured illumination microscopy (SIM) [25] of fixed cells and focused ion beam scanning electron microscopy (FIB-SEM) [26] of cryo-preserved cells have spatial resolutions of ~110 and <10 nm, respectively. Glossary Cohesin: a ring-shaped protein complex that organizes chromatin into a 3D structure, presumably by encircling two chromatin fibers into its ring. CTCF: a protein that specifically binds to the DNA sequence, CCCTC. CTCF frequently co-localizes with cohesin on the genome and functions in cohesin positioning. Enhancer: a short DNA region where specific transcription factors bind. Enhancers are thought to facilitate recruitment of transcription machinery to their target promoters from remote locations. Euchromatin: chromatin regions that are enriched in genes, early replicating, and often under active transcription. Initially, euchromatin was characterized by its sparse staining pattern in interphase nuclei, then later associated with A-compartment in Hi-C. Active histone marks (e.g., histone acetylation) are more abundant in euchromatin than heterochromatin. Focused ion beam scanning electron microscopy (FIB-SEM): an electron microscopy technique that allows imaging of entire nuclear volumes in a resolution of <10 nm in the x, y, and z planes. Heterochromatin: chromatin regions that are condensed, gene-poor, late replicating, and transcriptionally inactive. Heterochromatin was first characterized by its dense staining pattern, then later associated with B-compartment in Hi-C. Repetitive sequences are enriched in heterochromatin. Hi-C: a sequencing-based technology used to analyze the spatial organization of chromatin in a cell population. Hi-C can detect two genomic regions in close physical proximity. Histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor: agents that inhibit histone deacetylase and result in increased acetylation of histone tails. Histone modifications: posttranslational modifications on histone tails (Figure 1B). They impact interactions of histone tails with DNA or other proteins, then locally alter the physical nature of chromatin, playing a key role in gene expression (e.g., H3K27ac, H3K27me3, etc.). Movements of two neighboring nucleosomes were inferred to be correlated in the condensed loop domain, but not in the extended loop (Figure 4A). The results showed that two nucleosomes less than 150 nm from each other have correlative movements, whereas more distant nucleosomes do not, suggesting that nucleosomes are organized as condensed domains with diameters of ~150 nm each [29]. These condensed domains are estimated to consist of 500–1000 nucleosomes covering 100–200-kb human genomic regions. The study also revealed that nucleosomes inside the condensed domains behave like a liquid on the ~150 nm/~0.5 s scale, by analyzing the mean-square displacement between two neighboring nucleosomes (two-point MSD) (Figure 4B) [29]. Here, a liquid-like state means one with diffusive movement that does not have a crystal-like long-range order. The two-point MSD is increased with cohesin depletion or hyperacetylation of histone H3 and H4 tails, suggesting both cohesin and nucleosome– nucleosome interactions constrain the local fluctuation of nucleosomes inside a domain [29]. The imaging analyses discussed earlier indicate that higher eukaryotic chromatin cannot simply be categorized into open euchromatin and closed heterochromatin. Each forms condensed domains where only domain surfaces or boundaries between domains can be open and accessible by other proteins (Figure 3B). Given that nucleosomes in condensed domains behave like a liquid, these domains may retain a certain degree of internal chromatin movements, providing accessibility for gene activation.

Chromatin accessibility is similar between euchromatin and heterochromatin Overall accessibility (or sensitivities to nuclease) may not be so different between the euchromatin and heterochromatin domains because of the following reasons. First, both euchromatin and heterochromatin are organized as condensed domains. Second, the 3D position within a loop domain (e.g., on the surface or inside the condensed domain) would essentially be stochastic since a chromatin loop is irregularly folded into a condensed domain. Indeed, a study using micrococcal nuclease (MNase) as a tool to map chromatin accessibility found in human cells that euchromatin has similar DNA accessibilities as heterochromatin (Figure 4C) [32]. It showed that epigenomic features such as histone modifications and functional DNA elements did not apparently correlate with MNase accessibility. The only exceptions are active transcription start sites (TSSs), bivalent poised TSSs, enhancers, and regions flanking active TSSs and transcribed regions, which are marked with histone modifications H3K27ac and range only from 200 to 1500 bp [32]. These findings demonstrate that constitutively open regions are very short and limited in euchromatin and the remaining regions are folded irregularly into condensed domains (Figure 4C). Furthermore, using the restriction enzyme AluI, it was also shown that euchromatin and heterochromatin have very similar accessibilities in mouse liver nuclei [33]. This AluI study measured the digestion rate and the fraction of accessible DNA at almost all of the genomic AluI sites and obtained results consistent with [32]. Moreover, a fractionated chromatin study using local chromatin density demonstrated that overall densities of euchromatin and heterochromatin are similar in human cells (Figure 4D) [34]. In this study, weakly crosslinked chromatin was subjected to sedimentation velocity centrifugation, which fractionated the chromatin according to its degree of compaction or density [from fractions #1 (upper) to #5 (lower)] (Figure 4D) [34]. Locally open chromatin was fractionated into upper fractions, while locally condensed chromatin was sedimented to lower fractions (Figure 4D). In the following sections, we discuss how condensed domains form and the relevance of these domains to cellular functions.

How are condensed domains formed? A condensed domain is made of interdigitated nucleosome strings like a melted polymer via ten long histone tails of each nucleosome, which are highly positively charged (Figure 1B). Note that chromatin, unlike an ungummed fiber (e.g., an electric cable or spaghetti), is rather sticky with electrostatic nucleosome–nucleosome interactions. In addition to this stickiness, cohesin seems to define boundaries between the condensed domains, together with CTCF [15,27]. How does cohesin contribute to this process is an open question. Can cohesin (~40 nm diameter ring) pass through the condensed chromatin domain (100–300 nm diameter) by a loop extrusion mechanism, in which a DNA fiber is constantly pushed out through the cohesin ring to form a loop (see Outstanding questions) [41,42]? Do we need another mechanism [43–45]? Furthermore, a molecular crowding effect [46,47] and cations like Mg2+ [48] are important for domain formation. Cations can condense chromatin by neutralizing its negative charge [49]. Acetylation of histone tails weakens local nucleosome contacts [50] and chromatin stickiness because it neutralizes the positive charges of histone tails and perturbs the electrostatic interaction between nucleosomes. Histone tail acetylation on the domain surface, which can activate transcription in the corresponding region (Figure 2), also creates repulsion forces between chromatin domains. This may prevent the fusion of chromatin domains and contribute to domain individualization. Deacetylation of histone tails may induce fusion of domains. Indeed, extensive deacetylation of histone tails occurs during mitosis [51] and histone deacetylation is a major contributor to mitotic chromosome condensation [52]. These findings suggest that condensed chromatin domains can work as building blocks of mitotic chromosomes (Figure 3B) [16] (see also [53,54]). Consistently, electron microscopy, photo-activated localization microscopy (PALM), and stochastic optical reconstruction microscopy (STORM) imaging have revealed large-scale structures/chromatin domains with an approximate 200-nm diameter in mitotic chromosomes [16,37,55–57]. This block system creates a smoother process for chromosome assembly and disassembly. The memories of epigenetic markers in these building blocks can be retained easily throughout the cell cycle. But presumably with the involvement of condensins, topoisomerase IIα, cohesin, and other factors [58–60], how are mitotic chromosomes assembled from condensed domains or building blocks (Figure 3B)? This remains to be explored.

the binding regions on the surface of a chromatin domain, resembling a buoy (Figure 4E4), and these regions will remain transcriptionally active (transcription initiation and RNAPII pausing/ elongation) (Figure 4E5). Without the binding of large complexes, or lacking a buoy, these regions may become transcriptionally inactive as they revert back into their domains due to their liquid-like nature (Figure 4E1). The physical exclusion of large transcription factors contributes to keeping the region transcribed on the domain surface. Actively transcribed genes have a greater probability and/or longer time staying on the domain surface. Can this buoy model explain transcriptional bursts, which is stochastic switching between the ON and OFF states of transcription [63]? Once an actively transcribed gene moves from the condensed domain surface back toward the domain core, transcription ceases or turns off. This might be an off-state of the transcriptional burst. Enhancer–promoter interaction [64] may also act as a buoy (Figure 2). Active enhancers are often nucleosome-free or sensitive to DNase and MNase [32,65] and enriched with H3K27 acetylation [66], suggesting their conformation is relatively open and retained at the domain surface. Activated enhancers interact with the gene promoter and help it to float up to the domain surface to be transcribed (Figure 2). The emergent genes might be retained at the surface by enhancer–promoter interactions and interactions with a transcription condensate (e.g., RNAPII, BRD4, and Mediators) (Figure 2). On the domain surface, a buoyed gene may also interact with various histone-modifying enzymes and/or large nucleosome remodelers [67,68]. Partial decondensation of the condensed domains with histone tail acetylation or the action of remodelers can increase accessibility to various protein complexes to turn on gene transcription or other processes. Indeed, hyperacetylation of histone tails by a histone deacetylase (HDAC) inhibitor transforms condensed domains into rather extended loops [29]. Highly acetylated regions, like super-enhancers [69], may resemble this kind of organization. This buoy model is also applicable for other essential genome functions that require interactions with very large structures, such as protein complexes for DNA replication or DNA repair/ recombination proteins [70–73].

Resistance to genomic DNA damage Condensed domains are more resistant to radiation and chemical damage than the decondensed forms because condensed chromatin presumably has lower levels of reactive radical generation and poorer access for chemical attacks [74,75]. Recent genome-wide analysis using mouse mesothelioma cells also corroborates that chromatin condensation shields DNA from damage [76]. This point is especially important for actively transcribed regions in euchromatin, which are directly connected to genome activities. The notion of condensed euchromatin domains provides a theoretical basis for various combinations of HDAC inhibitor treatments and radio-/chemotherapies. Decondensed chromatin has greater susceptibility to γ-rays, heavy ions, and chemicals. Therefore, HDAC inhibitors and similar drugs (which decondense chromatin) strongly enhance the cytotoxic effects of cisplatin and other DNA-damaging drugs in cancer cells [77], as well as those of radiation therapy involving γ-rays or heavy ions and may prove a new means to target cancer.

Indeed, an in vitro study showed that nucleosome arrays with cations developed into large chromatin condensates around 10 μm in size [79]. These condensates behaved like liquid droplets [79] formed by liquid–liquid phase separation [80]. Consistently, when a restriction enzyme was injected into mitotic cells to fragment chromatin, chromosomes became round bodies resembling liquid droplets [52]. Fragmented chromatin relieves the physical constraints imposed by long strings of interacting nucleosomes and forms liquid droplets [52,81]. Besides an increase in chromatin accessibility, what roles can this liquid-like property of chromatin play in genome functions such as transcription, DNA replication, and DNA repair/recombination [82]? This is an intriguing issue. Contrary to accepted dogma, euchromatin domains are not fully open, but rather condensed. Condensed chromatin seems to be the default chromatin state in higher eukaryotic cells. Given that both euchromatin and heterochromatin form condensed domains, how is heterochromatin physically different from euchromatin? In addition to their histone modifications (e.g., active and inactive ones) and non-histone components [21–24], another difference is local nucleosome motion; heterochromatin is less mobile and more constrained than euchromatin [16,83–86]. Since local chromatin motion can govern genomic DNA accessibility for target searching [78] or recruiting a piece of machinery [87], increased motion in euchromatin empowers transcription competency in condensed domains. Many heterochromatin-specific factors (including histone modifications, proteins, and RNAs) have been characterized [21–24]. How these factors contribute to the suppression of local chromatin motion in heterochromatin remains to be investigated. Finally, the condensed euchromatin and heterochromatin domains should change during cell differentiation or developmental processes.
