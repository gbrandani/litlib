DNA tightly wraps around the histone octamer to form nucleosomes, the basic repeating units of chromatin. Chromatin remodelers, which are adenosine triphosphate (ATP)–dependent DNA translocases, convert the energy of ATP to overcome the histoneDNA contacts to slide and/or edit the nucleosome (1). They play a pivotal role in modulation of chromatin structure and a variety of chromatin-dependent cellular functions. This diverse group of enzymes encompasses four major types: SWI/SNF, ISWI, CHD, and INO80. Whereas the SWI/SNF enzymes slide nucleosomes to mediate the formation of accessible chromatin, ISWI enzymes slide nucleosomes and sense the linker DNA length, promoting the formation of evenly spaced nucleosome arrays and compressed chromatin (2, 3). Chromatin remodelers share highly conserved motor domains, which contain two RecA-like lobes and function in coupling ATP hydrolysis to DNA translocation (4). At the heart of chromatin remodeling is DNA translocation driven by the motor protein, the mechanism of which has been pursued for a longtime (4–6), and is the focus of research in recent years (3, 7–9). In the adenosine diphosphate (ADP) and apo states, the motor proteins induce a DNA bulge at the nucleosome binding site (10–14), known as super-helical location 2 (SHL2). Yet, the two DNA strands experience staggered movements. One strand shows significant distortion at SHL2, while the complementary strand shows minimal movement. As a result, only a 1⁄2-bp bulge is observed, rather than full 1 bp. When and how the complementary strand DNA is translocated remains unknown (3, 7, 9, 15). Moreover, in order to prevent excessive or inappropriate chromatin remodeling that could disrupt chromatin structure, the translocation activity is negatively regulated (16–19), the mechanism of which remains enigmatic.

Presumably constrained by the absence of actual translocation, only three states of the remodeling reaction are captured in the previous studies (10–14, 20). By employing ATP to sustain the translocation reaction, we enable the motor to traverse all potential stages of the remodeling cycle and determine nine high-resolution cryo–electron microscopy (cryo-EM) structures of ISWI bound to the nucleosome during active translocation. Our findings discover several additional functional states and reveal mechanistic insights into the chemo-mechanical coupling of the remodeling reaction. Structural determination of ISWI bound to the nucleosome during active remodeling The ISWI enzymes are highly conserved from yeast to humans (21). They contain the central adenosine triphosphatase (ATPase) motor domains (Lobe1 and Lobe2), flanked by regulatory domains at both ends (Figs. 1A and fig. S1) (16). Whereas AutoN locks the motor domains in an inactive conformation in the absence of the nucleosome substrate, NegC inhibits ISWI with unclear mechanisms (16, 19, 22). The C-terminal DNA binding domain (DBD) binds to linker DNA of the nucleosome, promoting the ISWI activity (22–24). ISWI interacts with different auxiliary subunits to form distinct complexes in cells, which extend the ability of the motor in sensing chromatin environments (25, 26). To illustrate the fundamental mechanism of the remodeling motor, we used the yeast Isw1 protein and the 30N0 nucleosome to reconstitute the remodeling reaction (fig. S2A). Chromatin remodelers utilize the energy of adenosine triphosphate (ATP) hydrolysis to slide nucleosomes, regulating chromatin structure and gene activity in cells. In this work, we report structures of imitation switch (ISWI) bound to the nucleosome during active ATP hydrolysis and remodeling, revealing conformational transitions of the remodeling motor across the adenosine triphosphatase (ATPase) cycle. The DNA strands are distorted accordingly, showing one full base-pair bulge and a loss of histone contact at the site of motor binding in the adenosine diphosphate* b and Apo* states. We also identify several important elements for regulation of the remodeling activity. Notably, an enzyme conformation exiting the remodeling cycle reveals a linker DNA–sensing brake mechanism. Together, our findings elucidate a multistate model of ISWI action, providing a comprehensive mechanism of DNA translocation and regulation underpinning chromatin remodeling.

Structure in the ADP* state and the full 1 bp DNA bulge at SHL2 The structure in the ADP* state was reconstructed to an overall resolution of 2.7 Å, with the local resolutions of the motor and the nucleosome estimated to be ~2.8 Å, and ~2.6 Å, respectively (Fig. 2A and figs. S4 and S8A). The motor and nucleosome in the ADP*+ conformation adopt conformations identical to those in the ADP* state (fig. S8, B and C). So, we mainly use the ADP* state for the discussion below. The prominent feature of the ADP* state is the bulging of the guide strand and the additional distortion of the tracking strand at SHL2 (Fig. 2B). Relative to the ADP state, the enzyme in the ADP* state keeps the open conformation (fig. S8D), but tilts further away from the nucleosome surface, pulling up the guide

strand at SHL2 (Fig. 2C). The guide strand translocation passes the histone-DNA contacts at SHL2.5, with the phosphate group at positions -50 moving forward by 1 nucleotide (fig. S8, E to G). The tracking strand at SHL2 alters its position slightly, yet substantially. The tracking strand makes closer contacts to Lobe2 in the ADP* state, with the phosphate group at position 53 forming a new H-bond to the main chain of R529 (Fig. 2D). Perturbation of this interaction by R529 deletion (ΔR529) markedly reduced the remodeling activity, supporting a critical role of this interaction in chromatin remodeling (Fig. 2E and fig. S8, H and I). The tracking strand movement results in a loss of the DNA-histone contact between the phosphate group at position 50 and the main chain of F84 of histone H3 (Fig. 2F). The disruption of histone-DNA interaction is in line with the DNA-loop model but does not involve a large DNA loop on the octamer surface as proposed before (6). Compared with the ATP state, 1 bp DNA is fully translocated into SHL2 in the ADP* state. The phosphate groups at positions 45 and -50 of the guide strand move forward by 1 nucleotide, with translocation distances about 6.7 Å and 4.4 Å, respectively (Fig. 2G). The counterparts of the tracking strand also move by 1 nucleotide. The DNA bases at SHL1 and SHL3 in the ADP* state adopt conformations similar to the relaxed ATP state. The 1 bp DNA bulge is stored at SHL2, where the bases remain stacking and pairing, but are twisted differently (Fig. 2H). Therefore, the DNA distortion is concentrated at SHL2 in the ADP* state, instead of spreading over the SHL2-SHL3 region in the ADP state (fig. S8F). The bulged guide strand at positions of -49 and -50 interacts with the bulky side chain of W600, which is well conserved among chromatin remodelers and important for remodeling (10). The guide strand in the ADP* state, and the ADP state as well, binds to the side chains of R639 and K643 of the Brace helix (Fig. 2I). To test the importance of these interactions, we introduced mutations of R639A and K643A. The mutations slightly perturbed the ATPase activity <2-fold (fig. S6E) but noticeably reduced the remodeling activity toward both the 100N100 and 60N0 nucleosomes (Fig. 2E and S8, H and I). These findings suggest that the Brace-guide strand interaction is important for coupling ATP hydrolysis to DNA translocation. ATP is held by the P-loop (motif I) of Lobe1 (Fig. 1E). The γ-phosphate of ATP is sensed across the Lobe1-Lobe2 interface by the two arginine fingers (motif VI, R614 and R611), effectively gluing the Lobe1-Lobe2 interface together. Upon ATP hydrolysis and phosphate release, the two catalytic lobes open up by bidirectional movements, with Lobe2 making a bigger step (Fig. 1F). The side chain of R611 swings toward motif V and interacts with the main chains of G582 and A580 in the ADP state (Fig. 1G). Consistent with the previous study (27), the mutation causes noticeable defects in chromatin remodeling toward both nucleosome substrates, without much perturbation on the ATPase activity (Fig. 1I and fig. S6, E to G). Motifs V to VI are highly conserved across the chromatin remodeler family, suggesting a general mechanism of DNA distortion and translocation.

Regulation of chromatin remodeling by PosC and RYA In some of the ADP-bound particles, we identified a conformation not previously reported, which we refer to as ADP+ (Fig. 4, A and B). The local resolutions of the motor and the nucleosome in the ADP+ conformation were estimated to be 3.0 and 2.9 Å (fig. S4), respectively. Different from the more extended configuration in the ADP state, the entry DNA in the ADP+ conformation is marginally visible (Figs. 4A and fig. S4). PosC and RYA are the notable features of the ADP+ conformation, the motor of which otherwise adopts a structure identical to the ADP state (fig. S10A). PosC consists of three elements: an N-terminal α1, a short α2 and a C-terminal loop (Lc) (Figs. 4C and fig. S10B). Whereas α1 is conserved within the ISWI family proteins, α2 and Lc are more variable (fig. S1). α1 is connected to the Brace helix through a disordered sequence (the Brake element, more discussion below), and binds to the surface of Lobe2 (Fig. 4C). The α1-Lobe2 interaction is also observed in the crystal structure of autoinhibited ISWI (fig. S10C) (19), suggesting that α1 has a tendency to bind to Lobe2, but the structural plasticity may account for its absence in the canonical ADP state. α2 contacts SuppH of Lobe1, which is the regulatory hub of chromatin remodelers (28, 29). The Lc loop packs against the Gating helix, which interacts with SHL2 DNA and is essential for DNA translocation (11). RYA contains the well-known arginine anchors (RAs), R769 and R771, and recognizes the acidic patch of H2A-H2B (Fig. 4D and fig. S10D) (22, 30–33). RYA extends about ten more residues from the C terminus of the RA motif. The high-resolution map allows us to identify two tyrosine residues (Fig. 4D). Y775 interacts with a hydrophobic surface outside the acidic patch, whereas Y780 binds to a positively charged surface of histone H3. We refer to them as the tyrosine anchors (YAs). The functions of PosC and YAs are not known before. To test the importance of PosC, we introduced mutations, L731D in α2 and W745A in Lc, to disrupt the binding interactions (Fig. 4C), and

assessed the ATPase and remodeling activities. The mutations did not alter the ATPase activity much (fig. S6E). While the wild-type (WT) enzyme remodeled the 100N100 nucleosome efficiently, and the PosC mutations substantially reduced the activity (Fig. 4E and fig. S10E). The mutants also showed marked defects in centering the 60N0 nucleosome to varying degrees (fig. S10F). These data indicate that PosC promotes the coupling of ATP hydrolysis to DNA translocation. To test the importance of the YAs, we introduced Y775D and Y780A mutations. Both mutations noticeably diminished the remodeling activities toward the 100N100 and 60N0 nucleosomes (Fig. 4E and fig. S10, E and F). These findings suggest that anchorage to the nucleosome surface via the YAs is important for efficient remodeling. A tyrosine residue is also found near the RAs of the remodelers Brg1 and Chd1 (fig. S10, G and H) (13, 34), suggesting that the RYA motif carrying arginine and tyrosine anchors may work as a cooperative entity in a broad biological context. Structure in the ADPS state and decoupling DNA translocation from motor opening The structure of ADPS state was reconstructed at a global resolution of 3.0 Å, with the local resolutions of the motor and the nucleosome estimated to be 3.2 and 2.9 Å, respectively (Fig. 5A and fig. S4). Comparisons with canonical ATP and ADP states indicate that the motor in the ADPS state shows a globally open conformation similar to the ADP state (Fig. 5, B and C), but the local conformation of motifs V-VI resembles that in the ATP state (Fig. 5D). Specifically, R611 within the catalytic pocket forms a H-bond with the main chain of G581 of motif V, which does not interact with the tracking strand DNA (Fig. 5E). The DNA in the ADPS state is undistorted (Fig. 5F). Therefore, the motor opens the DNAbinding cleft in the ADPS state, but the DNA reverts to the relaxed state, underscoring the importance of local conformational changes in motifs V and VI for the coupling of ATP hydrolysis to DNA translocation. Discussion In this study, we determined nine cryo-EM structures of ISWI bound to the nucleosome during the ATP-driven reaction, which reveal the conformational changes of the enzyme, and its interactions with the nucleosome. We propose a multistate remodeling model that includes an inner core of the remodeling cycle and an outer regulatory layer (Fig. 7). Given that the motor-nucleosome interactions observed in ISWI are highly conserved among chromatin remodelers, the proposed inner cycle provides a unifying mechanism of DNA translocation for chromatin remodeling. By contrast, the regulatory elements, Brake and PosC in particular, are not found in the SWI/SNF or ALC1 family enzymes (34, 36), indicating that the outer regulation layer is ISWI specific. The inner core of the remodeling cycle Notably, upon ATP hydrolysis and phosphate release during the DNA translocation reaction, Lobe2 rotates away from Lobe1. The conformational change of the motor triggers a structural transition of motif V and induces a 1/2 bp DNA distortion at SHL2 (movie S1). The motor inclines further and progresses into the ADP* state, which induces a full 1 bp DNA bulge at SHL2. As a result, the tracking strand loses the contact with the histone at SHL2.5. The motor enters the Apo* state by ADP release without triggering additional conformational change. Alternatively, the motor in the ADP state goes through the Apo state and then enters the Apo* state through a tilting movement similar to the transition from the ADP to ADP* states. ATP rebinding causes the closure of the DNA-binding cleft, in which the Gating helix pushes the tracking strand from behind, preventing the DNA translocation from slipping back as proposed before (11). The rearrangement of motifs V-VI releases the tight grip on the tracking strand and thus enables the 1 bp DNA displacement from SHL2 toward the dyad direction. In the ADPB state, we identified a regulatory element, Brake, which provides insights into the mechanism of ISWI inhibition. Specifically, L466 is in a loop conformation in the ADP+ complex, but folds into a helical conformation in the ADPB state. To avoid the steric clash, Lobe2 rotates away from the tracking strand as discussed above (Fig. 6C). The conformations of motifs V-VI become similar to those in the relaxed ADPS state (Fig. 6G). Therefore, the formation of Brake helix is associated with global and local structural rearrangements of the motor, leading to the inactive conformation. To test this notion, we introduced the mutations of L466P and L466G to disrupt the extended conformation of the Gating helix, and the mutations of I675D and I675A to abrogate the Brake-Gating interaction in the ADPB state (Fig. 6F). Consistent with our model of Brake inhibition, the mutations increased the remodeling activities toward the 100N100 nucleosome, with the I675D mutant enhancing the activity by over 20-fold (Fig. 6H and fig. S11C). These findings support the idea that the Brake, and the extended Gating helix, serve to attenuate the translocating activity of the motor. The mutations substantially altered the remodeling outcomes in the centering assays. The WT enzyme sensed the linker DNA and translocated the end-positioned 60N0 nucleosome to more centered positions, which migrated slowly in gels (Fig. 6I). In contrast, the mutants generated multiple but fast-migrating bands, suggesting that the nucleosomes were probably translocated off the end and positioned variably (35) (Figs. 6I and fig. S11D). In particular, the I675D mutant yielded few slow-migrating products. These data suggest that the mutants show compromised activity in sensing the linker DNA length. Taken together, our findings suggest that Brake reduces the motor’s translocation speed and regulates the sensitivity to the linker DNA length. Brake functionally mirrors NegC (residues 703 to 734 in yeast Isw1) in linker DNA sensing (16, 18). The major component of NegC is α1 (residues 703 to 727), which is connected to Brake through a flexible sequence (Fig. 6B). In the ADPB state, Brake interacts with the Gating helix, and α1 binds to the edge of Lobe2, suggesting they function together to maintain the inactive structure. The α1 (NegC) mutation may destabilize the inactive ADPB state, providing a rationale of NegC in linker DNA sensing (16, 18).

Regulation of the remodeling activity The high reaction potential associated with the DNA distortion positions the ADP and ADP* states as key regulation nodes. These high-energy intermediate states are either stabilized by PosC and RYA in the ADP+ and ADP*+ conformations or, if destabilized, collapse to the inactive ADPS and ADPB states. The detection of the ADPS state illustrates a mechanism that the energy of ATP hydrolysis is dissipated without being converted to DNA translocation. Future studies are needed to quantify the efficiency of energy conversion. The ISWI enzymes are well known for regulation by linker DNA (40–44). Whereas the motors within the inner core of the remodeling cycle preferentially bind to the nucleosome with more extended entry linker DNA, those in the regulatory states, the ADPB state in particular, are enriched on the side where the entry linker DNA is barely detectable (fig. S4). Our findings suggest a model for the linker sensitivity of ISWI through a brake mechanism (movie S2). We envision that the DBD dynamically samples the linker DNA length. The DBD binds to a long linker DNA (16, 22–24, 42), tethers the enzyme to the nucleosome, stabilizes the core remodeling states, and thus promotes the remodeling activity. When the DBD is transiently released from the linker DNA, the enzyme is prone to enter the ADP+ or ADP*+ conformations, in which the motor

maintains the active conformation through the interactions of RYA and PosC. The ADP+ and ADP*+ conformations outside the core remodeling cycle should not be underappreciated, as the motor may re-enter the remodeling cycle when the DBD rebinds the linker DNA during the dynamic process of linker DNA sampling. We speculate that the motor would have a chance to collapse into the inactive ADPB state without RYA and PosC. Upon encountering a short linker DNA, the DBD tethering is lost. The enzyme is prone to enter the ADPB state, in which RYA dislodges from the nucleosome surface, and Brake binds to the Gating helix. The Gating helix in turn extends more, resulting in the opening of the motor and enzyme inactivation. When the Brake inhibition is lifted, the motor in the ADPB state may recover from the loose-binding mode to reengage the nucleosome tightly and induce DNA distortion. This idea is consistent with the pause phase of ISWI, which can re-enter the remodeling cycle in response to the linker length (17). The mechanism of active inhibition by Brake provides a rationale for the optimal linker sensing, as the translocation phase (the inner reaction cycle) driven by the Lobe1-Lobe2 motor domains is insensitive to the linker DNA length (17). The mechanism of Brake inhibition involves a step of Gating helix extension and enzyme opening, which was unnoticed before based on crystal structure and AlphaFold prediction (12, 14, 22). It will be interesting to study whether the Brake inhibition is applicable to the ISWI complexes (26). RYA is found in different classes of remodelers, with YAs in human ISWI (Y742) and Brg1 (Y1379) subjected to phosphorylation modifications in cells (45), suggesting a regulatory mechanism by cellular kinase signaling. PosC includes three functional elements, α1, α2 and Lc. NegC is essentially α1, a structural element binding to Lobe2. α1 has a dual function in stabilization of the inactive ADPB state, and the active ADP+ and ADP*+ conformations as well. In contrast, Brake is specifically present in the ADPB state to inhibit the translocation reaction, playing a prominent role in the linker DNA sensitivity. Previous studies hypothesized coordinated actions of two SNF2H protomers on a nucleosome (20). The four regulatory states identified in this study are enriched at the double-bound nucleosomes, suggesting some kind of cooperative binding. The two motors do not directly interact with each other. Yet, we cannot exclude the possibility that they may achieve co-binding allosterically. The co-binding events do not implicate cooperative action, as indicated by the variability of the second bound motors, which can be in the active ADP+ and ADP*+ conformations, or in the inactive ADPB and ADPS states. Different from the hypothesis of coordinated ATP hydrolysis (20), the two ISWI protomers on a nucleosome are able to bind the same kind of nucleotide, as observed in the ATP-ATP and ADP-ADP+ complexes. Notably, the motors of the ADP-ADP+ complex induce local DNA distortion and translocation at both sides of the nucleosome (fig. S5, G to J), suggesting independent actions of the motors.

The energy liberated during ATP hydrolysis is gradually converted into the reaction potential stored in the 1 bp DNA bulge, while ATP binding triggers abrupt release of the strain, ratcheting the DNA distortion forward. Notably, the observed 1 bp DNA bulge and loss of histone-DNA contact in the ADP* and Apo* states argue against the long-standing models of “loop propagation” and “twist diffusion” (6, 37). The loop propagation model postulates that a large DNA loop forms on the nucleosome surface, which disrupts multiple histone-DNA interactions while preserving rotational phasing of DNA bases. However, the localized 1 bp bulge and altered base twisting at SHL2 in the ADP* and Apo* states contradict this mechanism. Similarly, the twist diffusion model proposes that an additional base pair is accommodated within a segment of nucleosomal DNA without breaking histone contacts, as observed in crystal structures of nucleosome core particles (38). While twist diffusion theoretically incurs minimal energy cost, this model is inconsistent with the disrupted histone-DNA interactions observed in the ADP* and Apo* states. It will be interesting to characterize the energy penalty associated with the 1 bp bulge in these states. Except at SHL2 bound by the motor, no DNA bulge in other positions of the nucleosome was observed. We did not find buffering of more than one base pair DNA within the nucleosome (39). These findings suggest that propagation of the 1 bp DNA distortion to the exit side is transient and efficient. Alternatively, the less populated states may be obscured due to particle averaging in structure reconstruction.

Materials and methods Protein expression and purification. Gene of Isw1 (residues 69 to 1129) was cloned from S. cerevisiae genomic DNA, then inserted into pET-15b vector containing a small ubiquitin modifying protein (SUMO) tag at the N terminus. The H4-like L3 loop (residues 145 to 160) of Isw1ΔL was replaced with a flexible linker (GGASGS). All mutants, including Isw1ΔL, were generated using the QuikChange method and all constructs were verified by DNA sequencing. The WT and mutants were expressed in the Escherichia coli strain Rosetta (DE3). Cells were cultured in LB medium to an absorbance at 600 nm (A600nm) ~0.8 and induced with 0.5 mM isopropyl-β -d-thiogalactoside (IPTG) at 16°C overnight. Cells were harvested by centrifugation at 4000 rpm. (Beckman, Rotor JA4.2) for 15 min, resuspended in 20 mM Tris-HCl, 1 M NaCl, 1mM benzamidine and 1 mM PMSF, pH 8.0, and then lysed by high pressure homogenizer (ATS) at 4°C. The cell lysate was cleared by centrifugation at speed 19,000 rpm. (Beckman,Rotor JA20) for 1 hour. Protein purification included a pulldown step by Ni-NTA resins, after a washing step, the protein was eluted with 20 mM Tris-HCl, 150 mM NaCl and 250 mM imidazole, pH 8.0, and the fusion tag was removed by SUMO protease (Ulp) at 4°C for about 1 hour. Proteins were further purified by ion-exchange column (Source 15Q, Cytiva) and gel-filtration chromatography (Superdex 200, Cytiva) and finally eluted in 150 mM NaCl, 10 mM HEPES, 5 mM DTT, pH 7.5. The WT and mutants were all purified follow this protocol. The purified proteins were stored at −80°C at a concentration of 5 to 10 mg/mL.

Cryo-EM sample preparation and data collection The Isw1ΔL–nucleosome complexes under condition 1 were obtained by mixing 3 μM protein with the 30N0 nucleosome (1 mM) in the reaction buffer containing 10 mM Tris-HCl, 50 mM NaCl, 5 mM ATP and 5 mM MgCl2, pH 8.5. The reaction mixture was incubated for 5 min at 4°C before cryo-plunging. The Isw1ΔL– nucleosome complexes under condition 2 were obtained by mixing 6 μM protein with 2 μM nucleosome (30N0) in the buffer containing 10 mM Tris-HCl, 50 mM NaCl, 0.5 mM ATP and 3 mM MgCl2, pH 8.5. The reaction mixture was incubated for 10 min at 4°C before cryo-plunging. The Isw1ΔL–nucleosome complexes under condition 3 were obtained by mixing 6 μM protein with 2 μM nucleosome (30N0) in the buffer containing 10 mM Tris-HCl, 50 mM NaCl, 0.05 mM ATP, 3 mM MgCl2,pH8.5. Pyruvate kinase (PK) at 0.25mg/ml and 4 mM phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP) were added to compensate for the low ATP concentration. The reaction mixture was incubated for 1 min at 4°C before cryo-plunging. Quantifoil gold R2/1 grids with 200 mesh size were subjected to glow discharge in air for 70 s using a PDC-32G-2 Plasma Cleaner set to low power. Samples (4 μl) were blotted for 3 s with FEI Vitrobot IV at 8°C and 100% humidity. Grids were initially screened using FEI Tecnai Arctica operated at 200kV. Qualified grids were transferred to 300 kV FEI Krios G3i equipped with K3 direct electron detector and GIF Quantum energy filter (Gatan). Cryo-EM data were acquired with the defocus set in the range of -1.4 to 1.8 μm, and at a nominal magnification of 81,000x, which renders a final pixel size of 0.54125 Å/pixel. The total electron dose was 50e−/Å2 fractionated in 32 frames (exposure time 2.56 s). Lastly, 25,373, 14,419, and 8622 movies were collected for cryo-grids made under condition 1, 2, and 3, respectively. Cryo-EM data processing The three datasets were processed separately at the early

stages in a similar way. Raw movies were imported into cryoSPARC (46). Patch motion correction and patch CTF estimation were used to obtained drift-corrected exposures. 24,645, 15,158, and 8420 exposures with CTF fit resolution <7 Å were selected for further processing. Blob picker and template picker were used sequentially. After rounds of two-dimensional (2D) classifications, 5,178,258, 3,354,717, and 1,919,307 particles were selected respectively. Heterogeneous refinement was performed using reference maps generated from ab-initio reconstruction, junk particles without well-defined nucleosome were removed at these steps. 3D classification with spherical mask focused on SHL2 position resulted in classes with single-bound, double-bound, and weak motor density. In dataset 1, reference-free focused 3D classification on single-bound particles can readily resolve ATP, ADP and ADP* states, and other classes with ambiguous motor density were discarded. The particles in the Apo and Apo* states were extracted by using particles and maps of Apo and Apo* from dataset 3 as seeds and references. For double-bound particles, side A and side B were arbitrarily named to indicate each motor. Two masks for motor on each side were generated and were used for particle subtraction. These particles were then treated as single-bound particles. Due to the scarcity of Apo and Apo* states in dataset 1, we did not intentionally extract them for the double-bound particles reconstruction. In dataset 2, the single-bound particles were processed similarly as above. For the double-bound particles, the side with longer DNA linker was denoted as side A, and the other side as side B. Focused 3D classification on side B can directly resolve ADPS, ADP+ and ADPB states. The ADP* and ADP*+ particles are resolved by combining the related particles from dataset 1, followed by further 3D classification. After the motor density on side B was subtracted, the motors left on side A were treated as singlebound particles. In dataset 3, the single-bound and double-bound particles were treated with a similar strategy as dataset 2. To resolve the Apo and Apo* states, reference maps with and without nucleotide density were initially generated by focused 3D classification on nucleotide region. These reference maps were subsequently used for iterative heterogeneous refinements on ADP-like and ADP*-like particles until clean Apo and Apo* states were resolved. At the final stages, cryoSPARC in-built reference based motion correction was used to improve map resolution, and particles from different datasets with the same state were combined, resulting in 9 distinct states: ATP (1,080,568, 2.3Å), ADP (1,029,438, 2.5Å), ADP* (429,751, 2.7Å), ADPS (144,976, 3.0Å), ADP+ (220,250, 2.9Å), ADP*+ (93,305, 3.0Å), Apo (59,121, 3.0Å), Apo* (53,155, 3.0Å), ADPB (406,214, 2.8Å). Motor and nucleosome of each state were subtracted and locally refined.
