Cooperation of chromatin remodeling SWI/ SNF complex and pioneer factor AP-1 shapes 3D enhancer landscapes Received: 3 June 2021 Accepted: 25 October 2022

Bennett K. Wolf1,2,6, Yanding Zhao3,4,5,6, Andrew McCray1,2,6, William H. Hawk1,2, Luke T. Deary1,2, Nicholas W. Sugiarto1, Ian S. LaCroix1,2, Scott A. Gerber1,2, Chao Cheng 3,4,5 & Xiaofeng Wang 1,2

Published online: 15 December 2022 Check for updates

The mechanism controlling the dynamic targeting of SWI/SNF has long been postulated to be coordinated by transcription factors (TFs), yet demonstrating a specific TF influence has proven difficult. Here we take a multi-omics approach to interrogate transient SWI/SNF interactors, chromatin targeting and the resulting three-dimensional epigenetic landscape. We utilize the labeling technique TurboID to map the SWI/SNF interactome and identify the activator protein-1 (AP-1) family members as critical interacting partners for SWI/SNF complexes. CUT&RUN profiling demonstrates SWI/SNF targeting enrichment at AP-1 bound loci, as well as SWI/SNF–AP-1 cooperation in chromatin targeting. HiChIP reveals AP-1– SWI/SNF-dependent restructuring of the three-dimensional promoter– enhancer architecture and generation of enhancer hubs. Through interrogation of the SWI/SNF–AP-1 interaction, we demonstrate an SWI/ SNF dependency on AP-1-mediated chromatin localization. We propose that pioneer factors, such as AP-1, bind and target SWI/SNF to inactive chromatin, where it restructures the genomic landscape into an active state through epigenetic rewiring spanning multiple dimensions.

Switch/Sucrose-Nonfermentable (SWI/SNF) chromatin-remodeling complexes are multimeric chromatin remodelers composed of 10–15 subunits that are assembled through different combinations of over 25 proteins1–4. Three major SWI/SNF subcomplexes—BAF, PBAF and GBAF (also known as non-canonical BAF or ncBAF)—have been identified in human cells5–9. These are thought to accomplish functionally diverse epigenetic tasks specified by the structural heterogeneity after complex formation. These differentiated subcomplexes are speculated to interact with a multitude of transcription factors (TFs) and epigenetic factors to regulate gene expression4. Although it is well established that SWI/SNF complexes maintain an open and accessible chromatin

environment10–14, the mechanism by which SWI/SNF complexes target to specific regions remains unclear. Many TFs have been reported to interact with SWI/SNF complexes and colocalize on chromatin via chromatin immunoprecipitation sequencing (ChIP-seq)4,15,16, but the mechanism governing this interaction and the implications for SWI/ SNF complex targeting are unknown. In line with other studies, our previous work and others have demonstrated SWI/SNF complex enrichment at enhancers17–19. As emerging data highlight the importance of higher-order enhancer interactions in regulating transcription20–23, the targeting of SWI/SNF complexes to enhancers may integrally regulate multidimensional enhancer landscapes.

In this Article, using our previously validated SMARCB1 addback model, we take a multi-omics approach to investigate the dynamics of SWI/SNF complexes’ association with other epigenetic regulators, chromatin targeting and contribution to higher-order enhancer interactions.

Results

TurboID proximity-based labeling of SWI/SNF subunits Previous attempts to characterize SWI/SNF cofactor interactions have been limited in their sensitivity, especially with regard to transient interactions such as the binding of TFs5,17,24. To accurately profile SWI/ SNF complex interactors we used TurboID, a proximity labeling technique that utilizes an engineered biotin ligase fusion to label interacting proteins25. Because this method has not been validated in an SWI/ SNF context, we first sought to determine whether SWI/SNF-TurboID fusion proteins effectively recapitulate the biochemical behavior of normal subunits (Fig. 1a). We generated HEK293T lines expressing a set of core SWI/SNF subunits and subcomplex-specific members of BAF, PBAF and GBAF fused to the TurboID protein, including SMARCD1, SMARCB1, DPF2, BRD7, PHF10, GLTSCR1L/BICRAL and BRD9 (Extended Data Fig. 1). We then strove to determine whether fusion of TurboID to SWI/SNF subunits would interfere with complex assembly by performing co-immunoprecipitation with core subunit SMARCC1, which exists in all three subcomplexes9 (Extended Data Fig. 1). Fusion of TurboID negatively impacted complex assembly variably across each transgene, with the exception of Turbo-SMARCD1, which strongly associated with SMARCC1 (Extended Data Fig. 1), highlighting the importance of validating the efficacy of TurboID fusion to individual subunits. Our results demonstrate that Turbo-SMARCD1 is functionally incorporated into each SWI/SNF subcomplex, making it an excellent candidate for follow-up studies. The dynamics of SWI/SNF assembly are not directly observable through traditional biochemical assays, yet understanding this assembly is critical for assessment of the effects of SWI/SNF mutations. We have previously shown that SMARCB1 addback in G401 cells (a SMARCB1-deficient rhabdoid tumor cell line) restores the assembly of BAF and PBAF SWI/SNF subcomplexes17 (Extended Data Fig. 2a). We introduced proximity labeling in this model system to directly probe novel assembly dynamics and cofactors as canonical SWI/SNF complexes are rapidly restored. To accomplish this, we introduced Turbo-SMARCD1 into our G401 SMARCB1-inducible system via lentiviral vector, rendering a double-engineered cell line constitutively expressing Turbo-SMARCD1 while SMARCB1 re-expression is controlled by tetracycline treatment, thus allowing us to directly capture SWI/SNF interactions with temporal control during SMARCB1 addback and restoration of BAF and PBAF subcomplexes (Extended Data Fig. 2a). To further ensure that Turbo-SMARCD1 is both incorporated during reassembly and does not impair reassembly, we performed sedimentation assays in this double-engineered cell line and examined the migration pattern of Turbo-SMARCD1 (Fig. 1b and Extended Data Fig. 2b). We observed that expression of Turbo-SMARCD1 is lower than endogenous SMARCD1, but displays a migration pattern markedly similar to endogenous SMARCD1, whereby both proteins colocalized to the same fractions as other BAF-complex members (Fig. 1b and Extended Data Fig. 2b). Importantly the sedimentation patterns of other subunits

were not altered by the introduction of Turbo-SMARCD1. These results indicate that Turbo-SMARCD1 has similar biochemical activity to endogenous SMARCD1 and is incorporated into the SWI/SNF complex both before and after SMARCB1 addback. To uncover SWI/SNF-complex-interacting proteins throughout complex reassembly, we probed for SMARCD1 proximal proteins before and after SMARCB1 re-expression. Proteins biotinylated by Turbo-SMARCD1 at each time point were enriched by streptavidin beads and subjected to tandem mass tag–mass spectrometry (TMT-MS; Fig. 1c,d). Given the high activity of TurboID, we also made and included miniTurbo-SMARCD1 to mitigate artifacts of background labeling. There were minimal differences in hits identified by both Turbo and miniTurbo-SMARCD1 constructs, validating the specificity of labeling (Extended Data Fig. 2e). As expected, Turbo-SMARCD1 efficiently labeled known SWI/SNF subunits when compared with an NLS-Turbo control. Importantly, although SWI/SNF subunits are identified as true hits at day 0, there is a substantial enrichment for certain subunits upon re-expression of SMARCB1. This dependency was most noticeable in labeling of BAF subunits, further validating our previous report that SMARCB1 is essential for BAF-complex integrity (Fig. 1c,d)17. Preferential labeling of BAF subunits upon SMARCB1 addback was validated by western blot (Fig. 1e). Furthermore, hundreds of novel interactors identified by Turbo-SMARCD1 MS were also labeled in SMARCB1-dependent and -independent manners (Fig. 1f, Extended Data Fig. 2d and Supplementary Table 1). These hits include the Mediator complex, the DNA repair complex, the transcription elongation complex, MLL complexes, the NURD complex and activator protein-1 (AP-1) members, some of which have been implicated in previous studies24,26–29 (Fig. 1e,f and Extended Data Fig. 2c,d). Interestingly, the hits encompass a broad range of transcriptional regulators, such as transcriptional activators (TCF20), transcriptional repressors (YAP1), chromatin readers (BRD4), chromatin writers (CBP/P300) and even other chromatin remodelers (CHD4-6,8-9) (Extended Data Fig. 2d). These data demonstrate that, as a proof of concept, proximity-based labeling by Turbo-SMARCD1 can accurately measure the dynamics of SWI/SNF complex assembly and capture many transiently interacting partners, making it a valuable tool for the study of SWI/SNF complex biology.

Dynamic targeting of SWI/SNF subcomplexes regulated by SMARCB1 Various epigenetic regulators, including SWI/SNF complexes, collaborate to establish the chromatin landscapes that govern gene regulatory networks27,30. Yet, how SWI/SNF interacts with the nuclear milieu to target and remodel specific genomic features is completely unclear. To connect the SWI/SNF interactome identified by TurboID experiments with SWI/SNF targeting at cis-regulatory elements during reconstitution of BAF and PBAF, we decided to profile the genomic features and chromatin landscapes that SWI/SNF targets during SMARCB1 addback by using CUT&RUN. CUT&RUN31 was used to profile the core and all subcomplexes: SMARCC1 (core subunit), ARID1A (BAF only), PBRM1 (PBAF only) and BRD9 (GBAF only). As expected, we observed increased targeting of SMARCC1 and ARID1A upon SMARCB1 addback in a time-dependent manner (Extended Data Fig. 3a,b). Before SMARCB1 addback, subunits representing all three subcomplexes were enriched at promoter regions (Fig. 2a), representing 48%, 68% and 46% of all targets for BAF, PBAF and GBAF, respectively. SMARCB1 addback induced a shift of enrichment to distal regions for all three subcomplexes, with BAF and GBAF being the most profound (Fig. 2a), proving SMARCB1 dependency for SWI/SNF enhancer targeting. Although SMARCB1 is not present in the GBAF complex, reconstitution of BAF and PBAF through SMARCB1 expression has a major impact on GBAF complex targeting. Furthermore, the conserved nature of this shift in targeting of BAF, PBAF and GBAF indicates a potential cooperation among subcomplexes at enhancer regions. Taken as a whole, the CUT&RUN data allow us to deduce that the BAF-complex functions primarily at distal regions, the PBAF complex functions at promoter regions, and the GBAF complex functions at both, with a preference towards distal regions upon SMARCB1 addback. To further dissect the dynamic targeting of these three subcomplexes, we compared their binding profiles against each other (Fig. 2b and Extended Data Fig. 3b,c). Surprisingly, we found a substantial overlap of targeting between all three subcomplexes (Fig. 2b). Upon SMARCB1 addition, the core subunit SMARCC1 is primarily found at positions correlated with ARID1A, indicating that BAF is the predominant subcomplex (Fig. 2b). Interestingly, BRD9 is also consistently found at positions of ARID1A binding, further suggesting a potential cooperation of BAF and GBAF complexes at enhancers (Fig. 2b and Extended Data Fig. 3b,c). PBRM1 peaks also consistently overlap ARID1A peaks, preferentially at promoters (Fig. 2b and Extended Data Fig. 3b,c). The colocalization of all three subcomplexes indicates a cooperative nature, and further studies on this novel relationship could prove critical to understanding canonical SWI/SNF function. Although genomic features may lay the groundwork for SWI/SNF targeting, histone binding is also probably a major determinant of SWI/SNF occupancy. To accurately assign SWI/SNF complex targeting to chromatin states, we performed CUT&RUN of four major histone modifications over the previously described time course. Consistent with our previous report, we observed increased H3K27ac (K27ac) and H3K4me1 (K4me1) marks upon SMARCB1 addback, concurrent with gained SMARCC1 binding positions enriched at transcription start site (TSS)-distal regions, indicating enhancer activation (Fig. 2c,d). Our analysis additionally denotes novel interrelationships of subcomplex targeting and histone modifications. ARID1A encompasses the majority of gained enhancer peaks (K27ac and K4me1 signal increase), definitively implicating BAF complex in enhancer activation (Fig. 2c). Intriguingly, PBRM1 and BRD9 peaks in distal regions gained K27ac signal while K4me1 remains unaffected (Fig. 2c and Extended Data Fig. 4a,b). These histone modification fluctuations suggest that BAF complex may activate enhancer regions, PBAF and GBAF may activate primed enhancers (Extended Data Fig. 4d). To better understand the context of SWI/SNF genomic targeting in relation to TFs, we performed motif analysis of SMARCC1, ARID1A, PBRM1 and BRD9 peaks. We observed strong enrichment of AP-1 motifs in all four subunits upon SMARCB1 addback, with ARID1A showing the strongest AP-1 motif enrichment (Fig. 2e and Extended Data Fig. 3d). PBRM1 was enriched for SP1, ETS and Ronin, which are all known promoter motifs33,34. Interestingly, we also observed strong CCCTC-binding factor (CTCF) motif enrichment, consistent with the previous reports

that SWI/SNF and CTCF may colocalize7,8. These results indicate a critical relationship with AP-1 across all SWI/SNF subcomplexes, and each additionally harbors a unique subset of TF motif enrichment.

Cooperation between the SWI/SNF complex and AP-1 complex We returned to the Turbo-SMARCD1 data to determine whether any of the proteins identified by motif analysis were also found to be in close proximity with SWI/SNF complexes. Although we did not identify factors such as CTCF or SP1 that were identified by SWI/SNF CUT&RUN motif analysis, AP-1 family members ( JUNB, JUND and FOSL2) were relatively strong hits within the TurboID dataset (Fig. 1f, Extended Data Fig. 2c and Supplementary Table 1). AP-1 members have been implicated in cooperation with the SWI/SNF complex in development24,35, although direct evaluation of the endogenous AP-1 and SWI/SNF subcomplex interactions and colocalization have not been studied. We selected three AP-1 members ( JUNB, JUND and JUN) for further validation by western blot, and confirmed their in vivo biotinylation (Fig. 3a). Interestingly, we found that JUND and JUNB are constitutively biotinylated, and the activated form of JUN (phospho-Ser73-Jun, pJUN)36,37 is only labeled following SMARCB1 re-expression. This suggests that AP-1 may associate with residual SWI/SNF complexes, whereas activated pJUN integration requires SMARCB1, resulting in enhanced SWI/SNF targeting. To test this hypothesis, we immunoprecipitated AP-1 members and blotted for SWI/SNF subunits, confirming an interaction with AP-1 that is drastically enhanced upon SMARCB1 addback (Fig. 3b and Extended Data Fig. 5a). We subsequently performed CUT&RUN of pJUN, JUND and JUNB over the previously described time course (Fig. 3c,d). As expected, we observed increased pJUN targeting in a SMARCB1-dependent manner, while JUND and JUNB targeting are minimally affected (Extended Data Fig. 5b). Consistent with our hypothesis, pJUN targeting is mostly shared with SWI/SNF-complex core subunit SMARCC1 (86–94%), and at day 3 most overlaps with BAF-complex subunit ARID1A (94%) rather than PBRM1 (68%) and BRD9 (58%) (Fig. 3c). Increased overlapping of SWI/SNF complex subunits with JUND is also observed, but only half of JUNB sites overlap with SWI/SNF subunits (Fig. 3c and Extended Data Fig. 5b). We performed heatmap analysis comparing the AP-1 and SWI/SNF subunit signal intensity at JUND (Fig. 4a and Extended Data Fig. 5c), JUNB (Extended Data Fig. 5d) and pJUN peaks (Fig. 4b). Interestingly we found that upon SMARCB1 addback, ARID1A, BRD9 and pJUN gained peaks colocalize with day 0 JUND peaks, whereas PBRM1 targeting at these loci remains static. Analysis of pJUN peak positions revealed enrichment of all SWI/SNF subcomplexes, particularly at distal regions (Fig. 4b). Consistent with these results, we identified example positions where (1) SMARCB1-dependent SWI/SNF subcomplexes gained regions were primed by JUNB and JUND (Fig. 3d); (2) shared BAF and GBAF subcomplexes gained regions colocalizing with AP-1; (3) GBAF and residual PBAF colocalize with AP-1 in the absence of SMARCB1; and (4) SMARCB1-dependent gained targeting of BAF complex, together with AP-1 binding and de novo formation of active enhancers (Fig. 3d). To further demonstrate this causal relationship, we performed AP-1 depletion using small interfering RNA (siRNA) against JUNB, JUND and JUN. As expected, AP-1 depletion leads to decreased SMARCB1-dependent recruitment of ARID1A and SMARCC1 at AP-1 regions (Fig. 4c,d and Extended Data Fig. 5e). Although several previous studies showed correlation between AP-1 and SWI/SNF-complex targeting, it remains inconclusive or controversial whether AP-1 acts as the guiding factor for SWI/SNF targeting14,24,35,38. After establishing AP-1-coordinated BAF complex targeting to enhancer regions and demonstrating subsequent chromatin remodeling and activation at these regions, we decided to investigate the consequences of this activity on higher-order chromatin interactions. Specifically, we wanted to ascertain how BAF complex targeting at enhancer regions affects enhancer–promoter interactions (enhancer loops). As enhancer regions gain K27ac and K4me1 signal upon BAF recruitment, we hypothesized that the BAF complex may aid in regulating, establishing and maintaining higher-order enhancer loops. To test this, we adopted a recently developed technique known as hi-chromatin immunoprecipitation (HiChIP)40,41, or proximity ligation-assisted ChIP (PLAC)-seq42, which allowed us to examine three-dimensional (3D) chromatin interactions defined by a single immunoprecipitated protein. To examine both active enhancer and active promoter interactions, we performed K27ac HiChIP at days 0 and 3 within our SMARCB1 addback G401 cell-line model (Fig. 6a,b). We used HiC-Pro43 and hichipper44 to process our samples as reported in previous papers, and identified ~25,000 K27ac loops (false discovery rate (FDR) < 0.01) at day 0 and ~70,000 loops (FDR < 0.01) at day 3 of SMARCB1 addback (Extended Data Fig. 7a,b). We then categorized the loops by their differential gain (logarithm of fold change (log2FC) > 0) or loss (log2FC < 0) at day 3 compared to day 0 (FDR < 0.01) and divided them into promoter–promoter (P-P), enhancer–promoter (E-P) and enhancer–enhancer (E-E) loop types based on anchor positions (Fig. 6c). We saw a drastic increase in all loop types at day 3 when compared to day 0, notably including 5,896 gained E-P loops (Fig. 6c). As SMARCB1 addback leads to enhancer activation and gained K27ac signal, it is expected that these gained loop anchors would also positively correlate with increased K27ac signal. To validate this, we plotted the FC in the loop signal against the CUT&RUN K27ac FC in the signal at loop anchors. Only 1,828 gained loops had a significant change (log2FC > 1) in K27ac signal at loop anchors (Fig. 6d). E-P loops that gain in strength without significant changes in K27ac signal have been previously ascribed to changes in 3D chromatin loop strength, yet the mechanism by which this would occur has yet to be determined41. As the only perturbation to our cell system was the addback of SMARCB1, we sought to identify whether the BAF complex may play a role in this strengthening of 3D chromatin features. By overlaying ARID1A CUT&RUN data (day 3/day 0) on the HiChIP loop anchors

and plotting the log2FC of the ARID1A signal against the log2FC in loop strength, we were able to see that an increase in ARID1A signal (log2FC > 1) was found at 6,715 gained loops, a substantially larger amount than was found by the K27ac signal (Fig. 6e). In contrast, only 4,154 and 3,310 significantly gained loops were found in similar analyses of BRD9 and PBRM1, respectively, indicating that the BAF complex plays the major role in regulating enhancer loops, and PBAF and GBAF complexes play a notable but lesser role (Extended Data Fig. 7c–f). As BAF-complex enrichment correlated with K27ac enrichment, this result is not completely unsurprising, but we decided to further investigate whether the BAF enrichment alone would correlate with loop strength. To do this, we removed any loops that contained an enriched or decreased K27ac signal (day 3/day 0, log2FC < −1 or log2FC > 1) at the anchors and replotted the loop log2FC against the ARID1A signal log2FC. As we hypothesized, a significant gain (log2FC > 1) in ARID1A signal resulted in 4,892 loops also significantly increasing in strength, indicating that the BAF complex does in fact play a direct role in strengthening chromatin-loop interactions (Fig. 6f). This was further confirmed by assessing the overlap of ARID1A HiChIP loops at day 3 with K27ac HiChIP loops at days 0 and 3, and stratifying these loops by K27ac 1D signal and 3D loop strength (Fig. 6g). ARID1A loops defined 3,527 K27ac loops that were substantially strengthened without a significant change (log2FC > −1 or log2FC < 1) in K27ac signal, directly implicating ARID1A and the BAF complex as proteins affecting higher-order chromatin structure and dynamics (Fig. 6g). As our CUT&RUN analysis had demonstrated the importance of the AP-1 family in SWI/SNF targeting, we similarly analyzed K27ac loops in relation to pJUN, JUND and JUNB (Fig. 6h,i and Extended Data Fig. 7g–j). Notably, pJUN signal enrichment at day 3 was also found to correlate with K27ac loop strength increasing, with 6,950 significantly gained K27ac loops at pJUN signal enrichment, and, even in the absence of K27ac signal enrichment, 5,213 loops were still correlated with pJUN enrichment (Fig. 6h,i). To further examine how AP-1-guided BAF-complex targeting regulates enhancer interactions, we depleted AP-1 and performed H3K27ac HiChIP. As expected, we observed 8,468 lost loops upon SMARCB1 addback at day 3 in AP-1-depleted cells compared to control cells (Extended Data Fig. 7k), and, among these lost loops, the majority are E-P loops (5,588). To assess the transcriptional consequences of these K27ac loop changes, we performed RNA-seq for days 0 and 3 of our time-point experiment and annotated EP K27ac loops by the log2FC in gene expression. The assessment of all annotated loops demonstrated 2,340 upregulated genes and 1,831 downregulated genes, with 165 genes being significantly upregulated (log2FC > 1) and 29 significantly downregulated (log2FC < −1) (Fig. 6k). Discussion

This work presents an all-encompassing multi-omics study of SWI/SNF dynamics: their cofactor interactions via proximity-based labeling, genome-wide targeting of all subcomplexes, their correlation with histone modifications and pioneer factor AP-1 members, and their impact on higher-order chromatin architectures in vivo through HiChIP. Assessing our data as a whole, we propose a model where SMARCB1 addback stabilizes the BAF complex, allowing for interaction with and stabilization of pJUN. AP-1 acts as a pioneer factor to bind nucleosomes directly, and subsequently targets the BAF complex to these closed regions. The BAF complex stabilizes the pJUN-nucleosome interaction and begins remodeling the chromatin region. The BAF complex recruits histone acetyltransferases, which generate an enrichment in H3K27ac nucleosomes, as reported by others. At the same time, the BAF complex also attracts and interacts with loop-forming proteins such as the Mediator complex, forming and strengthening E-P hubs to generate transcriptional changes. Our development of a functional TurboID-SWI/SNF tool catalyzes broad interactome profiling of SWI/SNF partners in a variety of cell-state contexts. The temporally controlled use of this proximity-labeling system can support novel interactome profiling across a variety of models, such as development and cancer plasticity, allowing the field of chromatin remodeling to easily assess both homeostatic/normal and disease-specific SWI/SNF interactomes. Within this study alone, many of the hits found by Turbo-SMARCD1 have confirmed previously implicated interactions or discovered novel players in the chromatin remodeling field. The identification of interactions with histone acetyltransferase proteins, the NURD complex, AP-1 complex and the Mediator complex are of particular interest, as they both confirm previous hypotheses and support future areas of study. We hope further proximity-labeling experiments across different cell environments will aid in the identification of all SWI/SNF-pioneer factor interactions and define novel agonists and antagonists of SWI/SNF function. Through interactome and genome-wide profiling, we have established that SWI/SNF targeting is guided by the AP-1 complex. Although the system we utilized preferentially looks at novel BAF targeting, which demonstrated the most notable correlation with AP-1 binding, we also were able to identify AP-1 targeting of PBAF and GBAF to a lesser extent. As AP-1 TFs have previously been suggested to act as pioneer TFs35, a class of TFs that can bind nucleosomes directly to induce activation, it is not surprising that AP-1 would interact with SWI/SNF during the course of initial chromatin remodeling. Other studies utilizing SMARCA4 addback in SCOOHT cells found that the ATPase module defines BAF and PBAF subcomplexes identity and differential targeting at promoters and enhancers10, and determined that the AP-1 motif is enriched at promoters38. Taken together, we believe that it is likely that most pioneer TFs target SWI/SNF activity through nucleosome binding, as has been alluded to in previous studies14,24,35,38. SMARCB1 plays a major role in the stability of the BAF complex and is required for effective binding and activation of enhancer regions17,19. By utilizing this SMARCB1 addback system, we were able to dissect the process and identify the players involved in BAF targeting. Of key importance, the identification of pioneer factor binding by AP-1 to target reconstituted BAF indicates a broadly applicable model for chromatin landscape activation across normal development and chromatin landscape dysregulation in cancer development. The demonstration that this SWI/SNF targeting both stabilizes and generates higher-order chromatin interactions is also of great note and has implications in the study of diseases associated with SWI/SNF aberrations. Ranging from autism to cancer, SWI/SNF mutations have been identified as putative

https://doi.org/10.1038/s41594-022-00880-x

drivers of disease states4, yet most research has focused solely on how these mutations affected the 1D chromatin landscape. Our results indicate that more focus should be placed on how SWI/SNF mutations impact changes within the 3D chromatin architecture, as these alterations will probably have broad effects across the transcriptome.

Concanavalin A-coated beads (Bang Laboratories) activated in binding buffer (20 mM HEPES (pH 7.5), 10 mM KCl, 1 mM CaCl2, MnCl2) were then added to the cell suspension (10 μl of beads per sample) and incubated with rotation at room temperature for 10 min. Sample processing and data analysis

CUT&RUN alignment and peak calling. Fastq files of the paired-end histone, TF and SWI/SNF CUT&RUN data were generated from the Illumina HiSeq platform. The samples were processed with the BBTools suite (version 38.42). Reads were deduplicated with the clumpify tool using flag -dedupe -subs=2, and subsequently trimmed with bbduk using the parameters --ktrim=r --k=21 --mink=11 --hdist=1 --tpe --tbo. The trimmed fastq files were then aligned to the Hg19 human genome using bowtie2 aligner52. Specifically, we used parameter ‘--end-to-end --very-sensitive --no-mixed --no-discordant -q -I 10 -X 700–phred33’ for the alignment. The aligned SAM files were further converted and sorted into BAM files using samtools software53. Bam files were converted to bed files after removing chrM, chrUn and reads containing ‘random’ as the chromosome position. Bed files were further cleaned by removing regions contained in the hg19 blacklist from ENCODE (v2)54. TF binding peaks were called by using the MACS2 software (v.2.1.1.20160309) with parameters at default ‘callpeak -g hs -f BAMPE -q 0.05 -B’55. Peaks were further cleaned by selecting signalValue greater than 5. Histones and SWI/SNF peaks were called by using the Epic2 software (v.0.0.41) with default parameters setting56,57. Peaks were further cleaned by the following criteria: FDR ≤ 0.05, log2FC (signal/background) ≥ 1, and read count > 20 within the peak (with a read count greater than 10 for BRD9 samples). Histone CUT&RUN was run with duplicates, and TF and SWI/SNF CUT&RUN was run in triplicate. For each factor, replicate bigwiggle files were merged using bigWigMerge, the resulting bedgraphs were sorted with the command ‘sort -k1,1 -k2,2n’, averages of the signal were calculated based on replicate quantity, and bedGraphToBigWig was used on the default parameters to convert the merged bedgraphs into merged bigwiggle files. Replicates correlation and peak merge and motif identification. To evaluate the reproducibility of sample replicates, two independent methods, bin enrichment analysis and correlation analysis, were used. In the bin enrichment analysis, we first identified all significant peaks binding with the SWI/SNF subunits in the two experimental batches. Following that, we divided all peaks into small bins, each of 100 bp in size, and then the number of shared bins was counted. Fisher’s exact test was used to estimate the statistical significance, and the percentage of shared bins indicates the level of consistency. The enrichment analysis at the bin level is robust compared with peak-level analysis—different peaks vary dramatically in their sizes and thus peak-based analysis is more sensitive to broader peaks. Using Pearson correlation analysis, we calculate the SWI/SNF subunit binding signals at each bin using the data from the two batches. The correlation of the bin signals between the two batches indicates the consistency. Our results from both bin enrichment analysis and correlation analysis indicate that the replicates are highly consistent, so the peaks were merged for downstream analysis. Peak files of each of the replicates were merged to create a conserved peak file for each histone modification, TF and SWI/SNF factor. Specifically, the bedtools ‘intersect’ function was used to take the overlapped peak regions from different replicates with a minimum length longer than 100 bp. We further separated the merged peak regions into TSS-proximal and -distal regions based on the distance to the TSS. TSS-proximal regions (±2 kb) were identified by utilizing the diffloop package function ‘getHumanTSS’ with 2 kb of padding on either side58. The bedtools ‘intersect’ function was then used to identify all peaks from each factor’s CUT&RUN merged peaks that overlapped TSS-proximal positions. Peaks that overlapped the TSS-proximal regions and peaks that did not have any overlap with TSS-proximal regions were separately concatenated and merged using bedtools

on default parameters for ‘intersect -wa’ to generate factor-specific proximal and distal peaksets, respectively. Heatmaps of other factors at these proximal and distal positions were then generated using the deepTools suite59,60 (v.3.3.1) functions ‘computeMatrix’, ‘plotHeatmap’ and ‘plotProfile’. The ‘findMotifsGenome’ function from the HOMER software (v4.11.1) was used to examine the motif enrichment of SWI/ SNF and AP-1 peaks with parameter ‘-size 200’. Enhanced chromatin occupancy analysis. EchO analysis has previously been utilized to identify whether TF-bound regions were nucleosomal or nucleosome-free. We thus used this program to identify whether our factors of interest were binding nucleosomal DNA or free DNA during the course of SMARCB1 addback. EChO analysis was conducted as described previously39. EChO (version 1.0) was applied to merged peak files and merged fragment bedpe files. EChO was used on default with the ‘foci’ parameter, resulting in an EChO bed file that contained the peak file’s local minima of read lengths within the original bedpe file. The day-7 EChO bed files were then selected for foci below 120 bp with the bash script ‘$4<120 {print $0}’ ‘BEGIN{s=1}; { if(s==1){ line=$0; chr=$1; stop=$3+50; size=$4; s++}else{ if(chr==$1 && $2<stop){ if($4<size) {line=$0; chr=$1; stop=$3+50; size=$4 } }else{ print line; line=$0; chr=$1; stop=$3+50; size=$4 }}}’’. This script identifies the true minima (foci) below 120 bp within a region by screening a 50-bp window on either side of the foci until finding the smallest fragment size foci within the local region. Bedtools ‘intersect’ was used with default parameters to map these identified foci onto the original peaksets that were used for EChO bed file generation. These peaks, from day 7, therefore contain the region identified by EChO to be the foci with average fragment size under 120 bp, which are considered to be nucleosome-free regions of DNA. EChO analysis was subsequently conducted on all other time point’s peak files in the same manner, to create EChO bed files containing all foci. For each individual factor, default bedtools ‘intersect’ was used on the day 7 < 120 bp peak file and the day 0/1/3 EChO bed files to identify foci from the factor’s previous time points that fell within the day 7 nucleosome-free peaks. As there can be multiple foci identified within a peak region, the smallest foci were determined for each peak and these foci were used to generate violin plots with ggplot2. RNA-sequencing analysis. Sequencing was performed on an Illumina HiSeq instrument to obtain an average of raw 150-bp paired-end reads per sample. Raw.bcl files were demultiplexed using the Illumina bcl2fastq2 pipeline. Briefly the adaptors were trimmed using ‘Trimmomatic’, and sample quality was assessed using fastqc. Trimmed reads passing quality assessment were then aligned to the hg19 human genome with STAR, and gene count matrices were generated with featureCounts. Differential gene expression analysis was then conducted using DESeq261, with each day in the time point being compared to day 0. Statistical significance was evaluated using the Wald test provided in DESeq2.

Assay for transposase-accessible chromatin sequencing analysis Fastq files of the paired-end ATAC-seq data were acquired from illumina HiSeq. BBDuk software was used to trim the adaptors with the parameter ‘ktrim=r k=23 mink=11 hdist=1 tpe tbo’ (http://jgi.doe. gov/data-and-tools/bb-tools/). The trimmed fastq files were then aligned to the Hg19 human genome using bowtie2 aligner52. We used the ‘very-sensitive’ mode for alignment. The aligned SAM files were further converted to bedgraph files using Deeptools software with the function ‘bamCoverage’ and corrected for Tn5 insertion53. MACS2 software was used for calling peaks based on the bedgraph files from ATAC-seq data with the parameter ‘-g hs -q 0.01 --nomodel --shift 0’55. Hi-chromatin immunoprecipitation analysis. Hi-chromatin immunoprecipitation alignment and loop calling. Fastq files of the paired-end HiChIP data were acquired from Illumina HiSeq. The annotation file

The HiChIP interaction pairs were identified by the HiC-Pro program with the following parameter setting: MIN_MAPQ = 10, BOWTIE2_GLOBAL_OPTIONS = --very-sensitive -L 30 --score-min L,−0.6,−0.2 --end-to-end --reorder, BOWTIE2_ LOCAL_OPTIONS = --very-sensitive -L 20–score-min L,−0.6,−0.2 --end-to-end–reorder, LIGATION_SITE = GATCGATC, GET_ALL_INTERACTION_CLASSES = 1, GET_PROCESS_SAM = 0, RM_SINGLETON = 1, RM_MULTI = 1, RM_DUP = 1, BIN_SIZE = 20000 40000 150000 500000 1000000, MATRIX_FORMAT = upper, MAX_ITER = 100, FILTER_LOW_ COUNT_PERC = 0.02, FILTER_HIGH_COUNT_PERC = 0, EPS = 0.1. DNA loop analysis was conducted using hichipper (v.0.7.5)44 with default parameters, utilizing the HiC-Pro allValidPairs file and the filtered CUT&RUN peaks for the corresponding factor and time point. Differential loop analysis and classification. The diffloop package (v.1.16.0)58 was used for all downstream analysis. Non-significant (FDR > 0.1) and self-ligated loops were filtered out. Loops present (≥5 paired-end tags) in one replicate but absent in the other were also removed. The function ‘quickAssoc’ was used to identify significantly differential loops (FDR < 0.01) for downstream analysis. For each protein HiChIP analyzed, corresponding merged bedgraph files from day-0 and day-3 CUT&RUN datasets were used with the function ‘annotateAnchors’ to annotate differential loop anchors by the log2FC (log2(day3/ day0)) in signal across the anchor. The anchor with the maximum absolute log2FC was used to represent the entire loop for annotation. Promoters were defined with the function ‘getHumanTSS’, with 2 kb of padding on either side. Enhancers were defined as the union of day-0 and day-3 K27ac CUT&RUN peaks, ±1 kb of padding, with the promoter regions removed. RNA annotation was conducted utilizing the ‘annotateLoops. dge’ function, and loops were annotated with CUT&RUN data as previously described. Gained loop genes were defined as log2FC > 1 for loop strength or CUT&RUN signal change, depending on the feature analyzed. Lost loop genes were defined as log2FC < −1 for loop strength. P values were calculated utilizing the Kruskal–Wallis method with the ‘ggpubr’ package (https://rpkgs.datanovia.com/ggpubr/).

Peak overlap analysis The ChIPpeakAnno package was used to examine the overlap between peak files. The ‘findOverlapOfPeaks’ function was used with the parameter ‘maxgap = 0L, minoverlap = 1L, connectedPeaks = keepAll)’. The sizes of the peaks identified from CUT&RUN and ATAC-seq analysis vary substantially, causing bias in quantifying the statistical significance for peak overlap. To overcome the bias issue, we divided each merged peak set of ATAC-seq and CUT&RUN experiments into small bins (100 bp in size). The overlaps of two peaksets were then converted into a comparison of the bins covered by them. Shared bins were counted to be evaluated, and the statistical significance was examined using Fisher’s exact test.

Quantify the SWI/SNF binding signal in ATAC-seq regions The binding of SWI/SNF in the chromatin accessibility region was calculated by the normalized reads of SWI/SNF factors. Specifically, the ‘multiBamSummary’ function provided in Deeptools software was used to calculate the read counts of each SWI/SNF subunit in the ATAC-seq peaks59,60. The raw counts matrix was further normalized by the sequencing depth for downstream analysis.

Statistics and reproducibility MS, RNA-seq, ATAC-seq, CUT&RUN and HiChIP experiments were performed with two or three replicates to allow statistical analysis. Western blots are representative of two independent experiments. All attempts at replication were successful.
