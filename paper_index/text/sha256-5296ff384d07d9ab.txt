1 Introduction In eukaryotes, long genomic Deoxyribonucleic Acid (DNA) is compactly packaged into chromatin. The elementary structural unit of chromatin is the nucleosome, which consists of 147 base pairs (bp) of DNA wrapped around the surface of a histone octamer. The histone octamer is composed of pairs of the four core histones, namely H2A, H2B, H3 and H4.1 Histone octamer is positively charged, so it binds to negatively charged DNA via strong electrostatic interactions.2,3 The existence of nucleosomes not only optimises DNA storage but also modulates gene expression and regulation.4–7 Consequently, the presence of nucleosomes significantly contributes to DNA transcription, replication and repair.8–10 Positions of nucleosomes across the DNA have been revealed experimentally across different organisms, from yeast to human genomes using micrococcal nuclease (MNase).11,12 Experimental studies have also been employed to demonstrate how DNA flexibility affects the mechanical stability of nucleosomes (reviewed in ref. 13), for example, Ngo et al. (2015)14 demonstrated that the more flexible the DNA sequence is, the more stable it stays bound to the histone core, i.e. the DNA is less likely

In parallel, various computational models have been developed to search for signals that determine nucleosome binding affinity based on sequence information.15–18 These models can accurately predict nucleosome positions because they are trained on experimental data, but they often lack information on mechanistic DNA features. A different set of models, that include DNA physical properties, such as its curvature, bendability,19,20 salt-induced unwrapping time21 and deformation free energy,22–26 have been used to make predictions on nucleosome positioning and mechanical stability. Each of these models focuses on particular aspects that are crucial for efficient DNA wrapping into nucleosomes. For example, Ruscio et al. (2006)27 showed that the formation of kinks in the DNA does not entail considerable energetic costs, which allows for an efficient wrapping into nucleosomes. Whereas, Battistini et al. (2009)23 demonstrated that kinks cannot occur anywhere but coincide with relatively flexible regions of the sequence. Liu et al. (2018)25 revealed that accessibility and directionality of nucleosome sliding are modulated by DNA bending energy profiles. Helmut Schiessel and his collaborators4,13,28–30 have been investigating sequence dependent nucleosome positioning and dynamics using continuous and discrete models. Yoo et al. (2021)26 reproduced the nucleosome positioning data based on the energetics of the inside-out conformational transitions of DNA minicircles. Although these predictions elucidate certain aspects of efficient DNA wrapping into a nucleosome, computational DNA structure models can be further exploited to elucidate the reasons for the heterogeneous binding properties of DNA sequences.

Computational models for DNA structure vary from highlevel molecular dynamics simulation models31–33 to low-level coarse-grained models which describe DNA structure and dynamics at various mesoscopic scales.4,13,28–30,34–38 Coarsegrained models provide an efficient framework to systematically and efficiently investigate and analyse DNA behaviour around histones. Motivated by these benefits of coarsegrained modelling, we choose this direction for the prediction and analysis of DNA nucleosome configuration. Most of the existing models of DNA in a nucleosome regard DNA as a system of rigid base pairs (for example, the model used by the Schiessel group4,13,28–30 and the recent study of Yoo et al. (2021)26). However, it has been shown that regarding individual bases, and not base pairs, as rigid bodies leads to a model, which is significantly more consistent with atomistic scale molecular dynamics simulations.39,40 Therefore we use the cgDNA+ model which is a novel coarse-grained model of not only individual DNA bases but also phosphates, with an associated software package.36,40 The inclusion of phosphates in the model allows not only further improves model predictions;40 modelling phosphates allows for an accurate representation of DNA-nucleosome binding sites. We use this model to produce a novel algorithm that predicts the configuration and energetic costs of DNA wrapping into a nucleosome. Using the aforementioned novel method, we identify the sequences which require less energy to achieve nucleosome configuration and are, thus, favourable for nucleosome formation. We predict detailed sequence dependent configurations of DNA in a nucleosome. We also answer the question as to whether persistence length estimate, another flexibility measure of DNA,41–43 can directly be used to predict nucleosome formation.

Experimental data

To define constraints for a DNA configuration in a nucleosome, we used data from X-ray crystallography experiments. Specifically, we analysed 30 Protein Data Bank59 structures with the following PDB IDs: 1aoi, 1eqz, 1f66, 1id3, 1kx3, 1kx4, 1kx5, 1p3a, 1u35, 2cv5, 2fj7, 2f8n, 2pyo, 3afa, 3av2, 3b6f, 3b6g, 3lel (two structures), 3wkj, 4jjn, 5ay8, 5f99, 5zbx, 6jr1, 6kvd, 6ke9, 6k1j, 6le9 and 6l9h. For fitting the reference points and frames to DNA bases and base pairs, the Curves+47 procedure was utilised, while the phosphate points and frames were computed as in ref. 40. To assess the biological relevance of our energy predictions, we used the Saccharomyces cerevisiae data set of nucleosomal and linker DNA sequences. The set of sequences was taken from Chen et al. (2016),24 in which the authors selected a subset of sufficiently different DNA sequences with experimentally verified nucleosome formation score.11 The sequences provided by Chen et al. (2016) are of length 150 bp, but since our model requires a sequence of a typical nucleosome DNA length of 147 bp, we choose the middle 147 bp (subtract one base pair from the start and two from the end of the sequence). In total, we used 1880 nucleosomal sequences and 1740 linker sequences.

Comparison of energetic costs and persistence length

Our initial hypothesis was that sequences with lower persistence length (more flexible sequences) are more likely to form nucleosomes. Therefore, for the same nucleosomal and linker DNA sequences as in Fig. 5a, we expected that the persistence length of linker sequences would be higher than that of nucleosomal sequences. However, our computation results suggest the opposite effect. Fig. 5b demonstrates that the persistence length of linker DNA is not higher than that of nucleosomal DNA. The mean persistence length values of linker and nucleosomal DNA are 229.57 bp and 231.52 bp, respectively. The two sample t-test with unequal variances confirms that the mean value of the linker DNA is statistically significantly smaller than that of the nucleosomal DNA with p o 0.01 (Kolmogorov–Smirnov was used to verify the normality of the data). Therefore, we demonstrated that even though the linker DNA sequences are less rigid than the nucleosomal DNA sequences (lower mean persistence length), they require more energy to wrap into nucleosomes. This result indicates that the intrinsic shape and flexibility at specific locations, rather than the average flexibility, measured by persistence length, determines the energetic cost of DNA wrapping into a nucleosome.

We also computed the Pearson correlation coefficient between persistence length and energy. We found that for the linker DNA sequences the coefficient was weakly negative (0.24), and for the nucleosomal DNA sequences – weakly positive (0.16). To understand the difference, we looked at the percentage of different dinucleotides in those sequences (ESI,† Fig. S3 and S4). We found that variations in AA/TT and AT dinucleotides were dominant in the linker DNA sequences with the highest/lowest energy and persistence length (ten highest energy/persistence length sequences). High numbers of AA/TT dinucleotides corresponded to high persistence length and low energy sequences. High numbers of AT dinucleotides were present in the sequences with low persistence length. High energy sequences had a large number of both AA/TT and AT dinucleotides. To further investigate the linker DNA sequences and the effect of AA/TT and AT motifs, we looked at the numbers of poly-AA/poly-TT and poly-AT tetranucleotides, hexanucleotides, octanucleotides and decanucleotides in all the linker sequences. In particular, we calculated Pearson correlation coefficients between the number of these motifs in the sequences and their energy/persistence length (Table S1, ESI†). We found that correlation coefficients of persistence length with the number of poly-AT motifs are negative (the strongest correlation (0.63) for tetranucleotides (see ESI,† Fig. S5a for linear regression plot together with the raw data for this case)). Whereas, correlation coefficients of persistence length with the number of poly-AA/poly-TT motifs are positive (the strongest correlation (0.43) for octanucleotides). The correlation coefficients of the energy and the number of poly-AA/poly-TT and poly-AT motifs were of opposite signs and weaker than those of the persistence length and the number of these motifs, namely negative with poly-AA/poly-TT motifs (the strongest correlation (0.16) for octanucleotides) and positive with poly-AT motifs (the strongest correlation (0.33) for octanucleotides (see ESI,† Fig. S5b for linear regression plot together with the raw data for this case)). These effects of poly-AA/poly-TT and poly-AT motifs elucidate the reasons why the energy of linker DNA sequences, which are rich in these motifs, differs from that of random sequences.

4 Discussion and conclusion This work was motivated by the need for further investigations of the mechanical properties of DNA that ensure successful DNA wrapping into nucleosomes. To address this problem, we initially considered experimental structures of nucleosomes and found clear similarities among the positions of phosphates. Using this data, we provided quantitative constraints that a DNA sequence has to satisfy to wrap around the histone core. We then developed an energy minimisation procedure which, for any given DNA sequence of length 147 bp, provides its minimal energy required to wrap into a nucleosome. In this procedure, we used a computationally efficient and mechanically accurate coarse-grained cgDNA+ model to predict the energetic costs of DNA wrapping into a nucleosome. Another significant outcome of this optimisation algorithm is a detailed sequence-dependent optimal configuration. The configuration includes inter and intra base pair coordinates, as well as phosphate coordinates. We verified that the method accurately predicts nucleosome wrapping energies for nucleosomal and linker DNA sequences of yeast. We further used this modelling framework to compare the wrapping energy estimates with the persistence length measure of the same DNA sequence. Surprisingly, we demonstrated that the less rigid (lower persistence length) linker DNA sequences require more energy to wrap into nucleosomes than the more rigid (higher persistence length) nucleosomal DNA sequence. Overall, this result is unexpected as persistence length is often considered as a measure of the energy cost of deforming DNA.64–66 However, our study highlights that the specific deformations, in our case, nucleosome wrapping, cannot be generally predicted by the persistence length measure. This result suggests that either the intrinsic shape or the local stiffness of the DNA and not its average flexibility, might determine its propensity to wrap into nucleosomes. The dominance of DNA shape versus stiffness for the sequence affinity in nucleosome formation is in agreement with previous studies.30,67 The importance of local stiffness is in agreement with the study by Virstedt et al. (2004)68 which suggests that local, and not global, DNA flexibility plays the key role in determining energetic costs of nucleosome formation. We also found that there is only a weak correlation between the persistence length and energy estimates and the correlation is opposite for the linker and nucleosomal DNA sequences. We provided an analysis of the sequences to elucidate the reasons for the difference in the correlation between the nucleosomal

and linker DNA sequences. The main difference is that linker DNA sequences contain large numbers of poly-AA/poly-TT and poly-AT motifs. High numbers of these motifs lead to extreme values of persistence length, namely a high number of AT motifs means low persistence length, whereas a high number of poly-AA/poly-TT motifs means high persistence length. The effect is weaker but the opposite for the energy. Therefore, the presence of these motifs elucidates the reasons for the differences in the energy and persistence length estimates for linker and nucleosomal DNA sequences. These results are in agreement with previous studies which suggest that the presence of poly-AA/poly-TT and poly-AT motifs negatively correlate with nucleosome occupancy scores.11,69 We note that our energy values are an order of magnitude larger than the DNA nucleosome wrapping energies found in the literature.26,29,70,71 There might be a few explanations for this difference. Firstly, the number of degrees of freedom is considerably larger in our model compared to the rigid base pair models, and even more so compared to the models with only one or a few scalar coordinates per base pair. Secondly, the cgDNA+ model (in agreement with the underlying molecular dynamics simulations) overestimates the stiffness of DNA, as can be seen from our high persistence length values. Therefore, although our energy estimates are considerably higher than those in the literature, the details (high number of degrees of freedom) of the cgDNA+ model ensure that our optimisation procedure can be used for an accurate comparative study. In addition, the output of our algorithm is not only the minimum energy but also optimised detailed configuration. One limitation of our method is that our optimisation algorithm only finds local, and not global, energy minimum. There is no easy solution to this problem when using our current approach, which allows only for a series of local modifications on DNA configuration, and not its global rearrangements. The use of global optimisation procedures, such as simulated annealing or genetic algorithms, is computationally too expensive, given the high dimensionality of the problem. A possible alternative approach would be to reformulate the problem, so that the energy and the constraints on the phosphates would be expressed in the same set of coordinates (cylindrical, Euclidean or cgDNA+). Then the constraints could be included in the gradient descent as Lagrange multipliers, and the global energy minimum could be found. However, this requires a substantial mathematical effort and remains a direction for our future work. There are multiple extensions of this work. First, the configuration of DNA in a nucleosome, containing the positions and orientation of phosphates, could be used for modelling the affinities of nucleosome-binding proteins. Secondly, this computational framework can be further applied to investigate various biologically relevant questions. For example, the effect of DNA methylation and hydroxymethylation on the sequences’ ability to wrap into nucleosomes could be explored, as cgDNA+ model allows for these modifications. Furthermore, since this procedure is computationally efficient, the effect of other modifications, such as deletion or insertion of nucleotides, could be

[Page 9]

efficiently investigated. This modelling framework could also be used to predict nucleosome occupancy in different biologically significant genome regions. Lastly, this modelling framework could also be extended to investigate DNA interactions with other proteins. To this end, specific protein-binding constraints have to be defined and a similar optimisation procedure can be carried out to estimate the energetic costs of any DNA sequence to bind to that protein.
