SUMMARY

Intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs) of proteins are defined by molecular grammars. This refers to IDR-specific non-random amino acid compositions and non-random patterning of distinct pairs of amino acid types. Here, we introduce grammars inferred using NARDINI+ (GIN) as a resource that uncovers IDR-specific and IDRome-spanning grammars. Using GIN-enabled analyses, we find that specific IDR features and GIN clusters are associated with distinct biological processes, intra-cellular localization preferences, specialized molecular functions, and functionalization as assessed by cellular fitness correlations. IDRs with exceptional grammars, defined as sequences with high-scoring non-random features, are harbored in proteins and complexes that enable spatial and temporal sorting of biochemical activities within the nucleus. Overall, GIN can be used to extract sequence-function relationships of individual IDRs or clusters of IDRs, to redesign extant IDRs or design de novo IDRs, to perform evolutionary analyses through the lens of molecular grammars and GIN clusters, and to make sense of IDR-specific disease-associated mutations.

INTRODUCTION Eukaryotic proteomes are composed of proteins with folded domains1 and intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs).2–8 IDRs are implicated in a range of molecular functions and cellular processes.3,9–14 The amino acid alphabet is used in a biased manner,8–10 and a subset of this non-random alphabet is arranged in non-random ways along the linear sequence.15–18 IDR-specific molecular grammars are defined by non-random amino acid compositions and non-random patterning of distinct pairs of amino acid types with respect to one another.15–39 Molecular grammars of IDRs are useful to explain protein functions,22,23,39–43 quantify driving forces for condensation,18,21,27,28,30,31,35,38,44,45 and to rationalize compositions of condensates.18,31,36,44,46–48 The interplay between short, linear motifs49–51 and their modulation by sequence contexts afforded by IDRs can also be explained using molecular grammars.17,32,40,52–58 We used the NARDINI+31,40,44,46 algorithm to analyze individual IDRs and entire IDRomes. Using an unsupervised learning approach for clustering, we generated a basis set of IDRs spanning the entire human proteome, based on their molecular grammars. Within this IDRome-spanning basis set, we identified 30 distinct clusters, each with a unique set of standout grammars.

The grammars inferred using NARDINI+ (GIN) are a resource, supported by three Google Colab notebooks. We demonstrate the use of GIN by showing how distinct grammars contribute to distinct cellular localization preferences, functionalization, temporally organized biological processes, molecular functions, and to disruption in the context of human cancers. RESULTS Extraction of molecular grammars using NARDINI+ The NARDINI+ algorithm takes an IDR sequence as input and converts it into a 90-component Z score vector (ZSV) (Figure 1A). For an IDR sequence (Figure 1B), the fractions of each of the 20 naturally occurring residues are used to derive 34 compositional features34,45,46 (Figure 1C; STAR Methods). Each feature is compared with the distribution of that feature for all IDRs in the human IDRome (Figure 1D). 24,508 IDRs were predicted from MobiDB and used as the reference IDRome.59,60 For each compositional feature, a Z score is computed as the signed deviation of the feature from the mean of the distribution, referenced to the standard deviation of the distribution. prior. If |Z| ≥ 1, then the feature is non-random, and the extent of non-randomness is set by the magnitude of the Z score. In general, a negative Z score implies that the compositional feature is depleted in the IDR when compared with the reference IDRome, whereas a positive Z score implies that the feature is enriched when compared with the reference IDRome (STAR Methods). If the feature of interest is normally distributed, then the Z score also quantifies deviation from the median and mode because of the coincidence of the mean, median, and mode. We assessed the extent to which each of the 54 compositional features can be described as being normally distributed (Figure S1A). The computed Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) statistic61 was low relative to other distributions that we considered. Thus, across the distributions of different features, there is smaller maximum spread between the true distribution and the normal distribution compared to other distributions, thereby justifying the use of Z scores. Next, we assessed the impact of our choice of MobiDB to predict IDRs by using AlphaFold relative solvent accessibility (RSA) as an independent method62 (Figures S1B and S1C). This yielded a curated human IDRome comprising 28,864 IDRs. The AlphaFold RSA IDRome includes more hydrophobic regions, when compared with the MobiDB IDRome. Differences in the means of the feature-specific raw scores, which are a direct reflection of differences in compositional biases between the two methods, are essentially zero for 50 out of the 54 features (Figure S1C). The higher hydrophobicity generated by AlphaFold RSA is driven mainly by solvent accessible area calculations. This goes against the consensus regarding the defining signature of IDRs.7–10,59,60,62–65 Thus, we proceeded with MobiDB as the method for identifying IDRs because these predictions have gone through more thorough vetting than AlphaFold RSA predictions. The NARDINI+ algorithm also computes 36 distinct binary patterning features for a given IDR.16 These features are computed based on the grouping of residues into eight types, viz. polar, hydrophobic, positively charged, negatively charged, aromatic, alanine, glycine, and proline (Figure 1E). For each unique pair of residue types U and X, the NARDINI algorithm computes a parameter δUX that quantifies the extent to which U and X are mixed or segregated along the linear sequence15,17,19 (Figures 1F and 1G). To compute the Z score for each patterning feature, 105 randomly scrambled sequences are generated while keeping the IDR composition fixed. The scrambled sequences yield a distribution of realizable δUX values for a fixed composition. The mean and standard deviation for the

values of δUX are then used in conjunction with the actual value of δUX to compute the Z score for this feature. The Z score is set to zero if the fraction of U or X constitutes less than 10% of the IDR. If the Z score for a specific δUX lies in the interval − 1 < Z < +1, then the patterning of U and X with respect to one another is random. If |Z| ≥ 1, then the U and X residue types are patterned non-randomly in the linear sequence. The extent of non-randomness is set by the magnitude of the Z score. A negative Z score implies that U and X residue types are uniformly distributed along the linear sequence16 (Figure 1F). Conversely, a positive Z score implies that the U and X residue types are segregated into distinct blocks within the linear sequence, when compared with the ensemble of scrambled sequences16 (Figure 1G). Overall, for a given IDR, NARDINI+ generates 54 compositional Z scores and 36 patterning Z scores in order to convert every IDR into a 90-component ZSV (Figure 1H). A basis set of grammars spans the human IDRome ZSVs for every MobiDB-predicted IDR in the human IDRome were clustered to generate a basis set spanning the human IDRome via GIN (Figure 1I). We focused on IDRs of length 100 ≤ n ≤ 300 from the MobiDB-derived human IDRome to separate grammars into distinct clusters while minimizing signal-to-noise issues that crop up when analyzing short IDRs. This also minimizes biases that can arise when using fractional values for short versus long IDRs and focuses on IDR lengths for which grammars have been shown to modulate localization, function, and phase separation in the context of the full protein.44,46,47,66 ZSVs were extracted for 4,529 IDRs that met the stipulated length criterion. We used K-means clustering67 to cluster ZSVs (Figure 1I). Notably, 30 GIN clusters were found to span the 4,529 IDRs (Figures 1J and S1D). For each cluster, the signatures of ZSVs can be depicted using the median ZSV (Figure 1J). Similar GIN clusters were extracted when all IDRs were chosen, although the goodness of categorization of IDRs of length 100 ≤ n ≤ 300 decreased (Figures S1E and S1F). We mapped all remaining 19,979 IDRs from the human IDRome using the centroids of each of the 30 GIN clusters. Each GIN cluster is defined by its distinctiveness, quantified by the minimum intercluster distance (dmin) of each IDR (Figure 1K; STAR Methods). Large values of dmin imply stronger mappings. GIN can be used as a resource to extract and analyze IDR grammars We have created three Google Colab notebooks to facilitate the use of GIN as a resource (STAR Methods). With the first notebook, users can input a list of proteins using either UniProt gene names or accessions (Figures S2A and S2B).70 The analysis produces two outputs: a summary of GIN annotations (Figure S2C) and NARDINI+ ZSVs for each of the IDRs in the gene/accession list (Figure S2D). Additional options include sequence schematics of proteins from the gene/accession list (Figure S2E) and hierarchically clustered heatmaps of the ZSVs. The latter can be used for identifying exceptional grammars defined as features with absolute values of Z scores that are higher than a user-defined threshold. The second notebook allows users to input a specific IDR or a list of IDRs in FASTA format.71 This is helpful if users prefer to specify bespoke sequence regions for the IDRs of interest. For the given list of IDRs, this notebook also extracts GIN clusters and ZSVs. The third notebook enables evolutionary analysis of IDRs. GIN clusters with distinct grammars are in proteins with specific sub-nuclear localization preferences We asked if proteins featuring IDRs from specific GIN clusters are enriched within specific organelles or membraneless bodies. We extracted annotations of subcellular locations of proteins from the Human Protein Atlas (HPA).72 We focused on IDRs of length ≥70 and non-linker IDRs of length ≥50, which have high dmin values. This selects IDRs whose mapping onto GIN clusters is unique and strong. The length cutoff allowed us to analyze IDRs that unambiguously confer functional preferences within the context of full-length proteins with the chosen cutoff for the number of residues being equivalent to that of folded domains.1,44,46,47,66 IDRs from distinct GIN clusters are enriched in different subcellular locations (Figures 2A, 2B, and S1G). Previous studies uncovered distinct IDR grammars for proteins that localize to nucleoli and nuclear speckles.12,18,46 We tested whether these grammars were recovered in an unbiased analysis based on GIN clusters. Nucleoli are enriched in cluster 23 IDRs, which feature K blocks. All experimentally characterized nucleolar localization peptides map to this cluster73 (Figure 2B). Nuclear speckles are enriched in cluster 26 IDRs, which feature R patches; all speckles localizing IDRs18 map to this cluster (Figure 2B). Without accounting for mutational rates and drift, it appears that orthologous proteins in six different species showed similar sub-nuclear localization preferences, implying evolutionary conservation of grammars (Figure S2F). The results highlighted above suggest that IDRs annotated based on the GIN cluster might be useful for identifying grammars that predict localization of proteins.18,30,36,46,47,76 To test this hypothesis, we studied the spatial localization preferences of ambiguously mapped, full-length monomeric proteins containing cluster 23 and 26 IDRs using germinal vesicles (GVs)

GFP is excluded from nucleoli with a partition coefficient that is less than one.46 Likewise, the GFP-tagged N-terminal IDR of huntingtin, a cluster 11 Q-tract IDR, is also excluded from nucleoli.46 These systems served as negative controls for quantifying partitioning into nucleoli. Conversely, all four proteins containing cluster 26 IDRs partitioned into nuclear speckles (Figures 2E and S2K). Thus, grammars of cluster 23 and 26 IDRs appear to be determinants of localization to nucleoli versus nuclear speckles, respectively. To test the synergy between folded domains and IDRs as determinants of localization preferences, we investigated two naturally occurring proteins that are regulators of DEAH/RHA RNA helicases.77–79 GPatch3 and GPatch4 each contain a single partially folded GPatch domain. GPatch3 features a cluster 19 linker IDR, whereas GPatch4 contains a cluster 23 C-terminal IDR (Figure 2F). Measurements within GVs show that for proteins with similar folded domains, the IDR grammars are the determinants of localization preferences (Figures 2G and 2H). To query the context-specific contributions of folded domains, we swapped the GPatch domains between GPatch3 and GPatch4 (Figure 2F). In these swaps, the proteins housing the same IDRs showed the same degree of partitioning into nucleoli (Figures 2G and 2H). We note that in general, localization to membraneless nuclear bodies is influenced by the synergy between folded domains and IDRs.46 Distinct functions are enriched with IDRs from distinct GIN clusters We assessed the enrichment of each GIN cluster in high-level Gene Ontology (GO)80–82 Slim molecular function terms with at least 25 IDRs (Figure 3A). GIN clusters with IDRs annotated as being enriched in RNA binding show a bias toward a high fraction of charged residues (FCR). Two genes or gene products may be functionally linked if their fitness effects upon knockout across the cell lines are correlated. Using this DepMap dataset84,85 and the methods of Pan et al.,86 we asked whether proteins housing IDRs with specific GIN clusters show functional relationships with proteins housing IDRs of the same versus different GIN clusters (Figure 3D). An edge was drawn between two proteins if and only if the DepMap functional correlation rank for the two proteins housing the IDRs was ≤20. To determine the aggregate functional similarity between two

GIN clusters, edges were counted between proteins containing an IDR belonging to the first GIN cluster and IDRs belonging to the second GIN cluster, normalized to the total possible protein combinations between the two GIN clusters. The hypothesis is that clusters showing high numbers of edges between them either engage in IDR-IDR interactions or are involved in similar functions. Likewise, fewer edges between clusters would suggest that proteins housing these IDRs are not functionally similar. High-scoring fitness correlations between GIN clusters included those defined by shared subcellular localization preferences. This suggests that one of the contributors to fitness correlations might be subcellular localization preferences driven, in part, by IDRs.12,45–48,76,87–89 To test whether subcellular location alone can lead to high fitness correlations, we examined the correlations of proteins localized to the same or different subcellular locations (Figure S3A). Proteins localized to sub-nuclear regions or housing sub-nuclear specific IDRs have strong functional correlations. Additionally, the number of intra-cluster edges is not equivalent to the number of edges derived from localization considerations, suggesting that IDRs play roles in both localization and functionalization of proteins. GIN clusters enriched in sub-nuclear regions are associated with discrete processes Nucleoli are enriched in proteins with IDRs from GIN clusters 23, 7, and 18 (Figures 2A, 4A, and 4B). The nucleolus is multifunctional,90 and proteins with IDRs from clusters 7, 18, and 23 are enriched in proteins associated with ribosomal biogenesis and other functions88 (Figure 3C). These functions include DNA repair, DNA replication, and stress response.91–93 Ribosome biogenesis is a complex process whereby hundreds of proteins come together to organize 42 discrete and synergistic processes.95 Of the 42 discrete processes, 35 (83%) involve a protein with at least one IDR from cluster 7, 18, or 23 (Figure 4C). This is greater than an average of 21% for any other triplet of GIN clusters that excludes clusters 7, 18, and 23 (Figure 4D). Cluster 23 IDRs are most commonly found in proteins that are associated with discrete nucleolar processes

(71%) and are prevalent in proteins associated with early-stage ribosomal biogenesis. Conversely, cluster 18 IDRs are preferred in proteins that are involved in later-stage ribosome biogenesis. Thus, manipulating IDRs of clusters 23 and 18 might help uncover their functional contributions to early- and late-stage ribosome biogenesis. Nuclear speckles are membraneless compartments that function as storage and modification centers for pre-mRNA splicing factors,98 and they help couple transcription with splicing.99 IDRs localized to nuclear speckles are enriched in clusters 18 and 26, defined by the presence of Arg patches and a high fraction of Arg residues (Figures 1J, 2A, 4A, and 4B). We collated proteins involved in splicing70,96,97,98,100 and found that 18 of the 24 discrete splicing categories have proteins with at least one cluster 18 or cluster 26 IDR (75%) (Figures 4E and 4F). This contrasts with an average of 30% for any other doublet of clusters that does not include clusters 18 or 26. Proteins involved in early spliceosome assembly include the serine- and arginine-rich splicing factors, the U1 small-nuclear ribonucleoprotein, and other proteins that comprise the E complex.101 These proteins prefer cluster 26 IDRs, and they lack cluster 18 IDRs. The nucleoplasm is the key location for the regulation of gene expression, including RNA polymerase II (RNA Pol II)-regulated transcription.44,102–105 Nucleoplasmic proteins refer to proteins that are not unambiguously localized to nuclear bodies, and they include chromatin-bound proteins. The functional nucleoplasm houses many components of transcriptional regulation machineries including the Mediator complex, general transcription factors, chromatin-remodeling complexes, histone-modifying complexes, transcription factors, and transcriptional cofactors.103,106–112 Nucleoplasmic proteins are enriched in IDRs from clusters 10, 11, and 28 (Figures 2A, 4A, and 4B). Proteins that house cluster 10 and cluster 11 IDRs are enriched in DNAbinding proteins and proteins that regulate DNA-templated transcription (Figures 3A and 3C). In contrast, proteins that house cluster 28 IDRs are enriched in processes that involve RNA binding (Figures 3A and 3C). Cluster 10, 11, and 28 IDRs are defined, respectively by well-mixed hydrophobic residues with a preference for Met, Q tracts, and high fractions of aromatic residues with a preference for Tyr17,32,48 (Figures 1J and 4B). We examined the relevance of the nucleoplasm-specific GIN clusters in discrete complexes that regulate gene expression (Table S4).70,96,103,106–108,110–115 Most complexes that regulate gene expression house at least one IDR from clusters 10, 11, or 28 (89%) (Figure S3C). This is compared to an average of 70% for any other triplet set of clusters that does not include these clusters (Figure S3D). Of the multiprotein complexes found in the nucleoplasm, the canonical BAF (cBAF) complex houses IDRs from all three nucleoplasmic-specific GIN clusters. For GIN clusters defined by compositional features, we used the valence (number) because multivalence drives phase separation and the organization of protein interaction networks.17,21,30,48,105,118–123 Among nucleolar IDRs, the topscoring cluster 7 and cluster 18 IDRs were found within three key scaffolds, UBTF, NPM1, and NCL46,95,107,124–128 (Figures S4A and S4B). Additionally, two RNA polymerase I (RNA Pol I)-specific subunits, POLR1F and POLR1G, contain the highest-scoring cluster 23 IDRs (Figure S4C). Among nuclear speckle IDRs, the highest-scoring cluster 26 IDR was found in SRRM2 (Figure S4E). SRRM2 and SON, splicing factors that house top-scoring cluster 26 IDRs, are essential for nuclear speckle formation.129,130 Among nucleoplasmic IDRs, the highest-scoring cluster 28 IDR is the C-terminal domain of RNA Pol II—an important player in transcription104,107 (Figure S5C). Proteins involved in the earliest ribosome biogenesis processes, ribosomal DNA (rDNA) transcription and ribosomal RNA (rRNA) modification, house exceptional K block grammars, which is the defining feature of cluster 23 IDRs (Figure 5A). Specifically, rDNA transcription is enriched in proteins housing IDRs with top 80 pos-pos and pos-neg Z scores in the human IDRome (Figure 5B). These scores quantify the segregation of positively charged residues with respect to (1) one another (pos-pos) or (2) with respect to negatively charged residues (pos-neg).15 The transcription of rDNA has three main stages: initiation, elongation, and termination.95 Each stage involves proteins housing IDRs with top 80 pos-pos Z scores when sorted by the human IDRome (Figure 5C). These include POLR1F and POLR1G of the pre-initiation complex, CTR9 and RTF1 of the Paf1C elongation complex, and TTF1 of the termination complex.95 We analyzed the conservation of exceptional grammars and found that IDRs with high pos-pos and pos-neg Z scores are maintained across evolution within proteins involved in rDNA transcription (Figure 5D). Next, we analyzed specific complexes that are critical for chromatin and gene regulation. Only BAF and COMPASS-like MLL complexes131 contain IDRs that score in the top 80 for the defining features of nucleoplasm-specific GIN clusters (Figure 5E). These features include the number of Met residues, the cumulative length of Q patches, and the number of Tyr residues. These features define clusters 10, 11, and 28, respectively. Specifically, cBAF is enriched in top 80 IDRs that are sorted by the numbers of Met and Tyr residues (Figure 5F). IDR1 of the cBAF subunit, ARID1A, has the 4th highest valence of Met residues, the 5th highest valence

of Tyr residues, and the 26th longest cumulative length of Q patches in the human IDRome (Figures 5G and 5H). Other subunits, including SS18, also show exceptional grammars. We assessed the conservation of the exceptionality of these grammars across evolution and found that these grammars are well conserved even when the sequences themselves are poorly conserved12,132 (Figures 5H and S5D). In terms of the cumulative lengths of Q patches, IDRs within the MLL4 COMPASS-like complex stand out as being exceptional. Specifically, KMT2D, PAXIP1, and NCOA6 house the 1st, 2nd, and 10th longest cumulative Q patches in the human IDRome, respectively (Figures 5G and 5H). KMT2D and NCOA6 also feature IDRs with exceptional valence of Met residues. As with the cBAF proteins, many exceptional grammars are conserved across evolution (Figure 5H). ARID1A and KMT2D are among the 10 most commonly mutated driver genes in cancer.133,134 Removal of ARID1A IDR1 or truncation of KMT2D IDR10 does not affect the assembly of BAF and MLL4 COMPASS-like complexes, respectively.44,135 However, these IDRs, which house exceptional grammars, drive condensation and modulate heterotypic interactions of their respective complexes.131,134,135 RNA Pol I and II house exceptional and distinct grammars RNA Pol I catalyzes the transcription of rDNA in the nucleolus, whereas RNA Pol II catalyzes the transcription of mRNAs and regulatory noncoding RNAs in the nucleoplasm.107 RNA Pol I and RNA Pol II comprise 13 and 12 subunits, respectively, and share five core subunits111 (Figure 6A). From the RNA Pol I- and RNA Pol II-specific subunits, we extracted all IDRs of length ≥70 and non-linker IDRs of length ≥50. The subunit-specific IDRs of RNA Pol I and RNA Pol II have grammars with exceptional features that define clusters 23 and 28, respectively. The C-terminal POLR1F and POLR1G IDRs are 1st and 5th in the human IDRome in terms of pos-pos Z scores, a defining feature of cluster 23 IDRs (Figure 6B). Likewise, the C-terminal POLR2A IDR has the most well-mixed distribution of aromatic residues (most negative aro-aro Z score) and the 3rd highest number of Tyr residues in the human IDRome, which are features that define cluster 28 IDRs (Figures 5H and 6B). We analyzed the IDRome of seven species and calculated pos-pos Z scores for IDRs from orthologs of POLR1F and POLR1G and aro-aro Z scores for IDRs from orthologs of POLR2A. For POLR1F orthologs, high pos-pos Z scores are conserved from humans to zebrafish, while the percent identity with human POLR1F IDR1 decreases to ∼30% (Figure 6B). Similar results were observed for the C-terminal IDR of POLR1G (Figure 6B). Overall, our findings for POLR1F and POLR1G are concordant with the findings of Lee et al., who uncovered the importance of the K block richness of POLR1F.12

Cancer mutations disrupt exceptional IDR grammars within distinct GIN clusters Cancer driver genes139 encode proteins that are enriched in IDRs from clusters 10, 11, and 12 (Figure 7A). Organizing the cancer driver genes by their IDRs shows that many IDRs have exceptional features (Figure 7B). Specifically, 51 of the 619 cancer driver genes encode at least one of the top-scoring IDRs (top 80 out of 24,508) in terms of cumulative length of Q patches; number of Met, Tyr, or Pro residues; and linear segregation of polar residues with respect to one another (pol-pol Z scores), of oppositely charged residues (pos-neg Z scores), or of negatively charged residues (neg-neg Z scores). To determine if exceptional grammars are disrupted in cancer, we extracted COSMIC141 mutation data from all cancer driver proteins in Figure 7B. If we consider missense, nonsense, frameshift, and indel mutations, then the number of mutations within each of the exceptional IDRs is generally proportional to the length of the IDR within the full protein (Figure S7A). In contrast, 11 of the 25 exceptional IDRs defined by blocky patterns have an enrichment in indels, compared with what is expected based on the length of the IDR within the full protein (Figure 7C). For example, indels are enriched in the MAML2 IDR that has the 11th longest cumulative length of Q patches in the human IDRome (Figure 7D). MAML2 is a regulator of the Notch signaling pathway and is involved in Lynch-like syndrome tumors, with multiple tumors housing a deletion within the polyglutamine tract.142 This suggests that indels in cancers may be localized to exceptionally blocky IDRs, and this may change interaction networks through valence effects.143

In fusion oncogenes, chromosomal translocations give rise to hybrid genes117,140,144,145 that code for fusion oncoproteins (FOs), which often link a structured DNA-binding domain (DBD) of one parent protein with an IDR of another (Figure 7E). FOs can modify the recruitment of transcriptional and chromatin-remodeling complexes and lead to aberrant gene expression.140,146 Using patient-derived datasets,140 we identified IDR grammars and probed for the gain or loss of exceptional features upon fusion. Here, we focus on FOs with DBD(s) in a single parent protein that loses a cognate IDR and gains a novel IDR upon fusion (Figures 7E–7H). All 29 FOs examined lost or gained at least one non-random feature associated with IDRs in unmodified cancer driver proteins, thereby generating an apparently aberrant IDR grammar (Figure 7H). One such example is the MEF2D fusion, identified in B cell acute lymphoblastic leukemia (B-ALL),140,147 which replaces a Q-patch-rich IDR with a Tyr- and Pro-rich IDR (MEF2D::HNRNPUL1) or a Tyr-rich and blocky negative residue IDR (MEF2D::FOXJ2) (Figure 7G). This suggests that FOs may rewire gene regulation protein interaction networks by swapping IDR grammars. Next, we analyzed UBTF fusions, which are drivers of BALL148 and pheochromocytomas and paragangliomas (PCCs/ PGLs).149 In both cases, the exceptional C-terminal D/E-tract IDR of UBTF is replaced in the FOs (Figure S7B). Replacement of the D/E tract is likely to alter the scaffolding behavior of UBTF, interactions with POLR1F, and regulation of rDNA transcription.150 In B-ALL, UBTF forms an in-frame fusion with ATXN7L3. The ATXN7L3 portion encodes two IDRs, both of which are enriched in blocks of positively charged residues. Atomistic simulations show that the ATXN7L3 IDR2 interacts weakly with the POLR1F IDR, when compared with the UBTF IDR4 (Figure S6D). This points to a disruption of cognate interaction patterns upon alteration of the native IDR grammar. In PCC/PGL, UBTF forms a fusion with an IDR of the transcription co-activator MAML3 that is enriched in Q patches (Figure S7B). Thus, we asked if there is evidence for IDR grammar swaps in FOs modifying partner recruitment. We utilized the DepMap dataset85 to extract genes with correlated gene fitness effect profiles to UBTF and MAML3, the two FO parents for UBTF:MAML3. Here, 213 genes have a Pearson correlation ≥0.1 with UBTF, and 584 genes have a correlation ≥0.1 with MAML3 (Figure S7C). The UBTF- and MAML3-correlated gene sets are enriched for proteins with very different IDR grammars (Figure S7D). The UBTF set shows an enrichment in features such as high FCR and a preference for K blocks, when compared with the remaining human IDRome. Conversely, the MAML3 set shows a clear depletion of charged residues and enrichment in glycine, hydrophobic, and polar residues. The implication is that IDR-mediated interactions of UBTF and MAML3 are likely to be distinct. Thus, creation of the UBTF::MAML3 fusion likely engenders a loss of interactions for UBTF through its D/E tract and a gain of very different interactions through the MAML3 Q tract. Our analysis is focused on predicted IDRs that are unmodified by posttranslational modifications or the effects of changes to solvent quality induced by changes to temperature, pH, crowding, osmolyte concentrations, or salts. Zarin et al.66,151 adapted heuristics from studies of sequence-ensemble relationships and combined these with models for mutational rates to develop methods that enable evolutionary analyses of IDRs. The organization afforded by GIN complements the approaches of Zarin et al. and can be used to generate testable hypotheses regarding determinants of sequence-function relationships of IDRs. The organization of the IDRome afforded by GIN will potentiate proteome- or sub-proteome-wide assessments of sequence-function relationships. Overall, the current work makes it clear that considerable progress is likely to be made toward understanding and manipulating sequence-function relationships of IDRs, possibly bypassing the need for analyzing sequence-ensemble relationships.152,153

Limitations of the study We used a predictor to identify IDRs. To obviate the need for a predictor, we need in situ characterizations of IDRomes in live cells. Currently, we do not have access to data that provide insights on how two or more IDRs within the same protein, each derived from distinct GIN clusters, contribute to localization preferences. This will need larger-scale experiments.

METHOD DETAILS Analysis of IDR grammars using NARDINI+ NARDINI+16,44,46 is an IDR sequence grammar analysis tool that combines the NARDINI (Non-random Arrangement of Residues in Disordered Regions Inferred using Numerical Intermixing) analysis with analyses of compositional grammar features. Together this yields a sequence specific Z score vector (ZSV) of 90 unique sequence features: 36 from NARDINI16 and 54 from compositional analyses. For the NARDINI patterning analyses, a given sequence is compared to 105 random scrambles of its same composition to determine if a given patterning parameter is more well-mixed or blockier than by random chance. Here, residues are divided into eight groups: pol = (S, T, N, Q, C, H), hyd = (I, L, M, V), pos = (K, R), neg = (E, D), aro = (F, W, Y), ala = (A), pro = (P), and gly = (G). For two residue types U and X, we extract a U-X Z score. Z scores less than zero imply the input sequence is more well-mixed than expected, whereas Z scores greater than zero imply the input sequence is blockier than expected. For compositional features, Z scores are determined by comparing to the distribution from the species-specific IDRome. In this paper, we analyzed the IDRomes of eight different species: Human (Homo sapiens, ID: 9606), Mouse (Mus musculus, ID: 10090), Cow (Bos taurus, ID: 9913), Frog (Xenopus tropicalis, ID: 8364), Alligator (Alligator mississippiensis, ID: 8,496), Zebrafish (Danio rerio, ID: 7955), Fly (Drosophila melanogaster, ID: 7227), and Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae, ID: 559292). In all cases, proteomes were 24,508 IDRs for human, 23,460 IDRs for mouse, 22,409 IDRs for cow, 9,051 IDRs for frog, 14,168 IDRs for alligator, 26,351 IDRs for zebrafish, 16,413 IDRs for fly, and 5,555 IDRs for yeast. The tool localCIDER34 was utilized to calculate compositional features. The specific compositional features extracted were the fraction of each amino acid type (20 features); the fraction of polar (Asn, Cys, Gln, Gly, His, Ser, Thr), aliphatic (Ala, Ile, Leu, Met, Val), aromatic (Phe, Trp, Tyr), positive (K+R), negative (D+E), charged residues (FCR), chain expanding residues (FCE), disorder promoting residues (8 features); the ratios of Rs to Ks (R/K Ratio) and Es to Ds (E/D Ratio) (2 features); the net charge per residue (NCPR), the hydrophobicity, the isoelectric point, and the polyprolineII propensity (4 features). We also analyzed ‘‘Patch’’ features which denotes the fraction of the IDR comprising patches of a particular amino acid or arginine-glycine (RG) pairs (20 features). Specifically, a patch is defined by having at least four occurrences of the given residue or two occurrences of RG and could not extend past two interruptions. W patches are excluded since none exist in the human IDRome. For a given sequence, the specific compositional feature Z score is determined by utilizing the mean and standard deviation from the entire species specific IDRome. The compositional parameters are based on classification of IDRs7–10,158 and descriptions of conformational preferences or functions of IDRs.3,6,15,17,19–23,30–34,37,38,40,43,44,46,54,55,66,83,151–153,159–173 For features such as the

isoelectric point (pI), a negative Z score implies a more acidic pI, and a positive Z score implies a more basic pI than the mean of the human IDRome. Similar considerations apply to parameters such as net charge per residue (NCPR). We converted each IDR into a 90-component ZSV. Note that Z scores quantify the deviation of the feature of interest from the mean and it is normalized by the standard deviation. Across any distribution, a Z score quantifies the number of standard deviations that a feature is away from the mean in the prior, which is either the ensemble of scrambles or the reference IDRome. Formally, the calculation of a Z score does not require that the feature is normally distributed in the prior. The interpretation of the Z score as a measure of the deviation from the mean is valid no matter the nature of the distribution. However, if the feature of interest is normally distributed, then the deviation from the mean also quantifies the deviation from the median and the mode because of the coincidence of the mean, median, and mode. Furthermore, no additional statistic is needed for quantifying deviations because the features are symmetrically distributed about the mean, median, and mode of a normal distribution. Accordingly, we assessed the extent to which each of the 54 compositional features can be described as being normally distributed. For this, we first fit each feature distribution to four distributions including (i) a normal distribution; (ii) a log normal distribution; (iii) a gamma distribution; and (iv) an exponential distribution. For each of the fits, we quantified a Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) statistic61 and compared this statistic across the different fits. Fits and KS statistics were calculated using the python fitter package (https://fitter.readthedocs.io/en/latest/). In all cases, the KS statistic for the normal fit was low relative to the other distributions (Figure S1A). This suggests that across the distributions of different features there is smaller maximum spread between the true distribution compared to the normal distribution. In general, skewed distributions can affect the Z scores of extreme values. However, for the generation of GIN clusters, we set all Z scores ≥ 3 to 3 and all Z scores ≤ -3 to -3, thus counteracting this specific problem. However, users can always use the raw Z scores, and since the raw distributions are available, additional feature or IDR-specific analyses can be readily performed. Importantly, across all of the post-clustering analyses that we performed, such as the sorting by a single feature, we used the raw value rather than the Z score for compositional parameters. Comparing distributions from IDRomes extracted using MobiDB versus AlphaFold Similar mean and standard deviations were extracted if we used AlphaFold relative solvent accessibility (RSA) as the IDR predictor instead of MobiDB.62 Here, we used MobiDB to extract the IDRs. Specifically, IDRs were found by extracting prediction-disorderalphafold. If the protein accession had no AlphaFold prediction (often because of its long length), then the prediction-disorder-mobidb_lite was used instead to extract disordered regions. Specifically, 274 of 20,644 protein sequences did not have an AlphaFold prediction and had an average length of 2,776 residues. K-means clustering of IDRs based on NARDINI+ Z score vectors The Z score vectors from NARDINI+ were extracted for all human IDRs of length 100 ≤ n ≤ 300 (4529 IDRs, ∼18% of the human proteome). Seventy-nine percent of IDRs are below this cutoff and ∼2% of IDRs are above this cutoff. This length cutoff was used to minimize length dependent effects, particularly in the Z scores that depend on fractions of particular amino acids, as well as to focus on long IDRs that are more likely to play functional roles. In order to avoid large distances between Z score vectors mostly based on one or a few large Z score values, all Z scores > 3 were set to 3 and all Z scores < -3 were set to -3. A |Z score| cutoff of three was chosen since anything above three should be significant (p value < 0.01). To determine how many clusters the IDRs should be categorized into, we calculated the mean silhouette coefficient upon K-means clustering for cluster numbers ranging from 10 to 100 and five different initial random states. We found that the mean silhouette coefficient was generally flat regardless of number of clusters and initial random state chosen. Additionally, we found that the inertia started decreasing linearly around a cluster size of 30. Thus, we settled on a cluster size of 30 to allow for tractability. Next, we categorized all remaining 19979 human IDRs into one of the 30 clusters. This was done by first restricting the maximum Z score value to 3 and minimum Z score value to -3, as was done for the human IDRs of length 100 ≤ n ≤ 300. Then the distance between each IDR Z score vector and each cluster centroid vector was calculated. The given IDR was then assigned to the cluster corresponding to the centroid with the smallest distance. Furthermore, we calculated the squared Euclidean distance (dintra) between the ZSV of an IDR and the centroid of the cluster onto which the IDR was mapped. We also calculated the squared Euclidean distance between the IDR ZSV and centroids of all other clusters to quantify the next closest distance (dinter,min). The difference between these two values (dinter,min – dintra) is the Minimum InterCluster Distance (dmin). The median dmin for all human IDRs is 5. A large dmin value implies that the IDR is well mapped into its given cluster, whereas a small dmin implies that the IDR could be similarly mapped into another cluster. For analyses in which we only wanted to include well mapped IDRs, i.e., for Gene Ontology and Human Protein Atlas enrichment, a dmin cutoff of 1.5 was used. Lastly, we compared GIN types derived from all IDRs of length 100 ≤ n ≤ 300 to GIN types derived from all IDRs of length n ≥ 30. K-means clustering was performed on this set of IDRs for a range of cluster sizes. Similarly to the IDRs of length 100 ≤ n ≤ 300 set, a GIN type size of 30 seemed reasonable based on mean silhouette coefficients and inertia. To determine if similar GIN types were extracted for each of these two IDR sets, the Euclidean distance between median Z score vectors was calculated for all GIN types derived from all IDRs of length 100 ≤ n ≤ 300 versus all GIN types derived from all IDRs of length n ≥ 30. Two GIN types were said to be similar if the Euclidean distance was ≤ 3. Twenty-three of the thirty GIN types derived from IDRs of length 100 ≤ n ≤ 300 mapped well to at least one GIN type derived from all IDRs of length n ≥ 30 (Figure S1E). Thus, in a majority of cases similar GIN types were derived regardless of which set of IDRs were used for K-means clustering.

Extraction of functional relationships between GIN clusters using DepMap Information regarding functional relationships between GIN clusters was extracted from the DepMap 24q4 dataset.85 The dataset contains gene fitness effect scores for thousands of genes, generated by knocking out each gene one at a time using CRISPR and measuring the fitness effect of the knockdown on > 1000 cancer cell lines. Two genes are said to be functionally linked if their effects on cellular fitness are correlated. Genes were filtered to include only those in which valid gene effect scores were determined for at least 1000 cell lines (n=17787). Correlation was assessed by calculating the Pearson correlation coefficient of gene effect profiles between all gene pairs. For each gene, the correlation coefficient for every other partner gene was ranked, and this ranking was symmetrized by taking the minimum ranking for a given gene pair. An undirected edge was then constructed between two different gene nodes if the correlation rank was ≤ 20. To measure global associations between GIN clusters, all well mapped IDRs of length ≥ 70 and non-linker IDRs of length ≥ 50 were mapped to its gene node. Then, edges between genes were counted in order to convert between gene nodes and GIN cluster nodes. Here, edges were drawn between GIN clusters X and Y were weighted by first counting the number of edges between genes such that one gene encodes an IDR mapped to cluster X and the other gene encodes an IDR mapped to cluster Y. This is repeated for each pair of unique IDRs in the given gene pair. The total number of edges between two GIN clusters was then normalized by the number of combinations of member genes, producing an edge density metric. This procedure reduces the order of the original graph, defined by the number of genes, down to the number of GIN clusters. This is because multiple genes map onto a single GIN cluster. Each node in the reduced graph represents an agglomeration of gene nodes, and an individual gene contributes to multiple nodes in the lower order graph. To measure global associations between subcellular localizations, all proteins, regardless of whether they housed an IDR, were mapped to their main HPA location(s).72 Then, an edge was drawn between subcellular locations X and Y were again weighted in a manner consistent with GIN cluster edges, first counting the number of edges between genes such that one gene encodes a protein mapped to location X and the other gene encodes a protein mapped to location Y. The total number of edges between two subcellular locations was then normalized by the number of combinations of member genes to produce a comparable edge density metric.

Atomistic simulations All atom simulations were performed using the ABSINTH178 implicit solvation model and forcefield paradigm as implemented in the CAMPARI simulation engine (http://campari.sourceforge.net). The parameter set was based on abs3.2_opls.prm with the radius of the sodium ions increased to 1.81 Å to improve sampling with highly acidic sequences. Simulations were performed in spherical droplets of radii 350 Å at 340 K with explicitly modeled counterions179 and an excess of 2.5 mM NaCl. Two types of simulations were performed. First isolated IDR simulations were performed on RNA Pol I and II IDRs. For these simulations, five independent simulations were conducted with 107 equilibration steps and 5.15x107 production steps. Since, the POLR1A IDR1 is a linker IDR, a harmonic restraint was applied between the first and last Cα atoms to restrain this distance to the distance observed in the RNA Pol I structure (PDB: 7ob9).180 Here, the distance was set to 37 Å and the spring constant to 10 kcal mol-1 Å-2.
