Common cancer mutations R175H and R273H drive the p53 DNA-binding domain towards aggregation-prone conformations† Le Li, Xuhua Li, Yiming Tang, Zenghui Lao, Jiangtao Lei and Guanghong Wei

*

The p53 protein is a tumor suppressor and the most often mutated protein in human cancers. Recent studies reported that p53 mutants, including two of the common cancer mutants (R175H and R273H), are more prone to aggregation than wild type (WT) p53 and their pathological aggregation can lead to diverse cancers. However, the underlying molecular mechanism is poorly understood. Herein, we investigated the structural and dynamic properties of R175H and R273H mutants of the p53 core domain (p53C) by performing extensive all-atom molecular dynamics simulations. We found that both R175H and R273H mutants exhibit a well preserved b-sheet structure, but a larger hydrophobic surface area and higher loop flexibility than WT p53C. Introduction The p53 protein, a tumor suppressor, consists of 393 amino acids. It contains five domains including a N-terminal activation domain (residues 1–63), a proline-rich domain (residues 64–93), a DNAbinding domain (also known as the core domain, residues 94–297), a tetramerization domain (residues 298–355) and a C-terminal regulatory domain (residues 356–393).1–5 In response to cell stress, p53 regulates a series of gene expression patterns involved in cell cycle arrest, damaged DNA repair and cell apoptosis for the prevention of cancer development.6–9 For this reason, p53 is called the ‘‘guardian of the genome’’.10 When mutated, p53 loses its normal functions, which is linked to more than 50% of human cancers.11 According to the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) TP53 Database, more than 95% tumor mutations occur in the DNA binding domain (p53 DBD or p53C) and 75% of the tumor mutations are the single missense mutations.12,13 Among these single missense mutations, the six most frequent cancer-associated mutations include the four conformational mutations (R175H, R248Q, R249S and R282W)

However, the conformational properties of R175H and R273H mutants that lead to higher aggregation propensities are not well understood. In this work, we performed three independent 600 ns all-atom molecular dynamics (MD) simulations at 310 K for each of the three systems, including p53C of WT, of R175H mutant and of R273H mutant. The reasons that we chose p53C as a model system to study are as follows. First, p53C displays similar thermodynamic stability to that of full-length p53.38

Second, p53C generally behaves in the same way as individual monomers and when expressed as tetramers, such as sharing identical melting temperatures that are influenced in the same way by mutations.38 Our simulations show that common cancer mutants R175H and R273H exhibit a well preserved b-sheet structure as WT p53C, but a larger hydrophobic surface area and higher loop flexibility than WT p53C. These conformational properties are similar to the structural features of aggregation-prone molten-globule (MG) states that have higher propensities to aggregate.39 Furthermore, dynamic network analysis shows that R273H mutation allosterically increases the flexibility of the distant N-terminal region of loop2. These results provide mechanistic insights into the higher aggregation propensities of the two hot-spot R175H and R273H mutants with respect to WT p53.

Materials and methods WT, R175H and R273H mutants of p53C Three protein systems are studied and they are WT p53C (residues 94–297), and the corresponding R175H and R273H mutants. The initial coordinate of WT p53C was obtained from the NMR structure (PDB ID: 2FEJ) (the first structure).21 The two mutants were generated by mutating the residues at the corresponding sites of WT p53C using Pymol. The N-terminus

[Page 3]

and the C-terminus of all proteins were respectively capped by acetyl (ACE) and amine (NH2) in order to mimic the uncharged state of the two terminus residues in the full-length protein. In accordance with neutral experimental pH conditions, the side chains of Lys and Arg were protonated (Arg+ and Lys+), and the side chains of Glu and Asp were deprotonated (Glu and Asp).

Simulation details Three independent 600-ns MD simulations were performed for each system at 310 K using the GROMACS 5.1.4 software package in combination with the CHARMM36m force field.40,41 The reasons we chose CHARMM36m are as follows. First, it is an improved force field for folded and intrinsically disordered proteins. Second, the CHARMM force field has been used in recent p53 simulations to investigate the conformational dynamics of p53 isoforms,42 p53C–small molecule interaction,43 p53 peptide aggregation,44–46 and p53–MDM2 interaction.47,48 These studies provided evidence for the suitability of the CHARMM force field for p53 protein. Each protein system was placed in a cubic box filled with TIP3P water, with a minimum distance of 1.2 nm between protein atoms and the box edges. In order to keep the charge neutrality of each system, four Cl ions were added to WT p53 and three Cl ions were added to both R175H and R273H mutants. The distances between the Zn2+ ions and the four coordinated atoms were restrained using the piecewise harmonic/linear potential energy, where a flat bottom potential (Fig. S1, ESI†) was used when the distances between Zn2+ ions and the four coordinated atoms were within the reference bond length of 0.2 nm (r1 r0 = 0.2 nm). The force constants were adopted from the work by Luo et al.49 and the partial charges of atoms in the four coordinated residues were taken from the CHARMM36m force field. Table S1 (ESI†) gives the values of distance parameters (r0, r1, and r2) and the force constants used in the restraint potential. Before MD runs, the energy minimization was first carried out using the steepest descent for 20 000 steps and then using the conjugate gradient method for 10 000 steps. Subsequently, simulations were performed under an NVT ensemble followed by an NPT (1 bar) ensemble, both for 0.1 ns, to equilibrate the systems. After that, 600 ns production MD simulations were performed under an NPT ensemble. The pressure was kept at 1 bar by using the Parrinello–Rahman method and the temperature was maintained at 310 K by separately coupling the protein and non-protein groups to an external heat bath using a velocity-rescaling coupling method.50,51 The electrostatic interactions were calculated using the particle mesh Ewald (PME) method with a real space cut-off of 1.4 nm.52,53 The van der Waals interactions were treated using a cut-off of 1.4 nm. Analysis methods Conformational property analysis. We adopted the tools implemented in the GROMACS software package and in-house codes to analyse our trajectory data. Gromacs tools were used to analyse the following parameters: the backbone root-meansquare-derivation (RMSD), the backbone root-mean-squarefluctuation (RMSF), the radius of gyration (Rg), the total and

hydrophobic solvent accessible surface area (SASA), and the hydrogen bond (H-bond) number of the protein. Here, a H-bond was considered to be formed if the distance between donor D and acceptor A is r0.35 nm and the D–H A angle is Z1501. The secondary structures were calculated using the DSSP (Define Secondary Structure of Protein) method.54 Our in-house codes were used to calculate the residue–residue contact number, the salt-bridge probability, p–p stacking probability and contact number between water molecules and H-bonding groups (CO– and NH–) in p53C. Here, a contact was considered if the distance between two carbon atoms of nonsequential residues lies within 0.54 nm or the distance between any other two atoms of nonsequential residues lies within 0.46 nm. We first calculated the charge center of the charged group of the four residues, including Arg+, Lys+, Glu and Asp, and then calculated the distance of the charge center between two oppositely charged residues. A salt-bridge was formed if the above-mentioned distance is within 0.40 nm.42 Two aromatic rings were considered to form p–p stackings when their centroid distance is within 0.7 nm.55 The stacking patterns were roughly classified into three categories: parallel (01–301), herringbone (301–601) and perpendicular or T-shaped (601–901).56 The extent of solvent exposure of H-bonding groups in p53C was estimated by calculating the contact number between water molecules and H-bonding groups (CO– and NH–). Here, a contact was considered if the distance between water molecules and H-bonding groups in p53C is within 0.35 nm. Allosteric path analysis. Dynamic network analysis was performed using our in-house codes. Each C-alpha atom was selected as a representing node for the corresponding residue. Edges were added between two nodes if the corresponding residues have a contact probability 460%. An atomic contact was taken to be formed using the criteria defined above. The weight of each edge was defined as log|Cij|, where Cij stands for the dynamic cross correlation of two nodes (i and j).57 On the basis of the dynamic network, the shortest allosteric path between two residues was defined as the optimal network path between the two corresponding nodes. The optimal paths were calculated using codes developed by Eargle and Sethi.57 The sub-shortest allosteric path was defined as the sub-optimal path between two nodes when all edges presented along the optimal path were stripped away. Considering the relatively high flexibility of the terminal residues (N-terminal residues 94–108 and C-terminal residues 293–297) of p53 DBD, we used the residues 109–292 for analysis. Unless specified, the last 300 ns simulation data were used for analysis.

The native contact probability map of the WT (Fig. S2b, ESI†) displays a similar native contact pattern as that of the NMR structure (Fig. S2a, ESI†), indicating that the native contacts in WT mostly exist during simulations. This result also demonstrates that the CHARMM36m force field that we chose is suitable for p53C. The total number of native contacts in both R175H (8524) and R273H (8514) mutants is smaller than that in WT (8709), although the native contact probability map of R175H and R273H mutants is quite similar to that of WT (Fig. S2b, ESI†). The decrease of the total number of native contacts in R175H and R273H mutants implies that the two mutations disrupt the native structure. We next explored the effects of R175H and R273H mutations on the conformational properties of p53 DBD by calculating the average backbone RMSDs over the three MD runs for each system (WT p53C, R175H and R273H mutants) relative to their initial conformations (Fig. 1b). The average RMSD values of DBD in both R175H and R273H mutants are slightly larger than that in WT p53C (Fig. 1b), which is consistent with the decreased total number of native contacts in both R175H and R273H mutants. In order to find the regions that lead to larger RMSD values in R175H and R273H mutants, we calculated the average RMSDs of dominant regions (b-sheet including all of the ten b-strands, loop1, loop2 and loop3) of DBD (Fig. 1c and g–i). The average RMSD values of the b-sheet in the three systems are quite small (B0.13 nm) (Fig. 1c). In addition, during the full period of simulations, the proportion of b-sheet remains at around 35.63% (Fig. S3a–c, ESI†) and the b-sheet region in the DSSP profile remains almost unchanged (Fig. S3d–f, ESI†). These data suggest that the two single-site mutations (R175H and R273H) slightly affect the structural stability of b-sheet, which can be clearly seen from the all-atom superposition between the final and initial conformations of the three systems (Fig. 1d–f). In the three systems, we see that both loop1 and loop2 deviate significantly from their initial structure (larger RMSD values), while the conformational change of loop3 is less pronounced (relatively smaller RMSD values). Moreover, the average RMSD values of the three

loops (loop1, loop2 and loop3) in both R175H and R273H mutants are larger than those in WT p53 (Fig. 1g–i). These results indicate that both loop1 and loop2 are less stable than loop3, and the two mutations reduce the stability of the three loops. Qualitatively similar results are observed in individual backbone RMSDs for each simulation (Fig. S4, ESI†). Taken together, our data reveal that R175H and R273H mutations cause a large conformational change in the three loops, while they slightly change the structural stability of the b-sheet. We then investigated the influence of the two mutations on the conformational flexibility of p53 DBD by calculating the average backbone RMSFs over the three MD runs for each system (Fig. 2a). In the three systems, four loops (i.e. loop1, loop2, loop3 and the loop between b7 and b8) have relatively large RMSF values (Fig. 2a). This observation reveals the high flexibilities of the four loops, especially loop2. Moreover, the overall RMSF values of loop2 in R175H and R273H mutants are larger than that in WT p53C. Specifically, in R175H mutant, it is the C-terminal region (residues 179–194) of loop2 that has a larger RMSF, while in R273H mutant it is the N-terminal region (residues 163–178) of loop2. The enhanced flexibilities of both the C-terminal region of loop2 in R175H mutant and the N-terminal region of loop2 in R273H mutant can be clearly seen in Fig. 2b. In addition, the other three loops in the two mutants exhibit similar flexibilities to those in WT p53 (Fig. 2b). RMSFs are given for each simulation in the three systems (Fig. S5, ESI†). These results demonstrate that R175H and R273H mutations result in an increased flexibility of loop2. To examine whether the two mutations impact the volume and hydrophobic surface of p53 DBD, we calculated the probability density function (PDF) of the radius of gyration (Rg), the solvent accessible surface area (SASA) of all residues and the SASA of hydrophobic residues (hydrophobic SASA). Fig. 3a–c show that the average Rg, SASA and hydrophobic SASA in R175H and R273H mutants are larger than those in WT p53, indicating that the two mutations cause an increased protein volume and solvent exposure of hydrophobic residues. These physical and conformational properties of the two mutants show structural features of aggregationprone MG states,39,62 which enable R175H and R273H mutants to have higher aggregation propensities than WT p53. The decreased H-bonds of the two mutants (Fig. 3d) demonstrate that the two mutations reduce the stabilities of the two mutants and may facilitate their unfolding and aggregation. Recently, Cino et al.63 showed that p53C enhances the incidence of exposed backbone H-bonds compared to p63C and p73C by MD simulations, in support of their experimental observation of the high aggregation propensity of p53C relative to p63C and p73C.63 Similarly, we examined the exposed backbone H-bonds of the three systems by calculating the contact number between water molecules and backbone H-bonding groups (CO– and NH–) in p53. Considering that the exposure of side chain H-bonding groups also affects aggregation of p53C, we calculated the contact number between water molecules and side chain H-bonding groups of polar residues. The probability density function (PDF) of the contact number between water molecules and all H-bonding groups is shown in Fig. 3e. It can be seen that the water-exposed H-bond number in the two mutants is larger than that in WT, although the increase of exposed H-bonds in R175H mutant is not obvious. The overall increase of exposed H-bonds in the two mutants (Fig. 3e) suggests that the two mutations increase the incidence of exposed H-bonds, which may enhance the aggregation tendencies of the two mutants. The exposure of the amyloidogenic segments triggers the p53 mutants to form an intermolecular b-sheet-like structure, leading to amyloid aggregation.64 A recent experiment by Wang et al.65 demonstrated that there are multiple aggregation

sites in p53C and five (including residues 109–113, 194–203, 230–238, 251–25735,44 and 271–276) of them are most effective for p53 aggregation. To investigate the effect of R175H/R273H mutation on the solvent exposure of the five amyloidogenic sequences, we calculated the SASA of each amyloidogenic sequence in each system. Fig. S6a (ESI†) shows that the total SASA of the five regions in the R175H mutant is larger than that in the WT. Fig. S6b–f (ESI†) reveals that the three segments (residues 194–203, 230–238 and 271–276) contribute to the increase of total solvent exposure in R175H mutant. Although the total SASA in R273H mutant is similar to that in WT (Fig. S7a, ESI†), there are three segments (residues 109–113, 194–203 and 251–257) in R273H mutant that have larger SASAs than those in WT (Fig. S7b–f, ESI†). These results indicate that the amyloidogenic segments that trigger the aggregation of R175H and R273H mutants are different. Next, we used dynamic network analysis57 to identify the optimal allosteric paths from the mutation site 273 to residue H168, which has the highest flexibility in the N-terminal region of loop2 of R273H mutant. As shown in Fig. S8(a) and (b) (ESI†), the optimal allosteric path (R(H)273–E271–K164–S166–H168) in R273H mutant is the same as in WT p53. In addition, the path lengths in WT p53 (144) and in R273H mutant (148) are quite similar. These results indicate that R273H mutation has little effect on the optimal allosteric path. Therefore, we turned to the sub-optimal allosteric path and found that a sub-optimal allosteric path (R273–M133–V272–I251–Y163– E171–H168, path length: 169) exists in WT p53 (Fig. 5f). However, the path in R273H mutant is broken at Y163–E171 (Fig. 5g). The enhanced H273–F134 and H273–P250 interactions result in an increase of correlation strength (Fig. 5g) and contact number (Fig. S8c, ESI†) for residue pairs H273–M133, M133–V272 and V272–I251 as M133, I251 and V272 are the nearest neighbors of F134, P250 and R273, respectively (Fig. 5b). Residues M133, I251, V272 and H273 locate in the b-sheet. Though the correlation I251– Y163 is slightly reduced by R273H mutation, the overall increase of correlation stabilizes the b-sheet structure (Fig. S8d, ESI†). In R273H mutant, the increase of correlation strength and stability of the b-sheet may cause the decrease of H-bonding interaction of Y163–E171 because E171 is located on loop2. This is confirmed by the time evolution of the Y163–E173 H-bond in the two systems, where the Y163–E173 H-bond is maintained in all of the three simulations of the WT (Fig. S8e, ESI†), while it disappears in two MD runs (MD-1 and MD-2) of R273H mutant (Fig. S8f, ESI†). The destruction of the Y163–E171 H-bond may trigger a reduction of interactions for residue pairs within the N-terminal region of loop2, thus leading to an increased flexibility of this region. To check this hypothesis, we calculated the contact number map for residues within the N-terminal region of loop2 in both WT p53 and R273H mutant (Fig. 5h). In WT p53, relatively strong interactions are observed for H168–Y163/Q165, M169– Y163/Q165/S166, E171–Y163/H168 and V173–Y163 residue pairs (marked by white rectangles), while those interactions in R273H mutant are significantly reduced or even disappeared, confirming our hypothesis. Overall, R273H mutation enhances the correlation of b-sheet via aromatic stacking interactions of H273–F134/P250, disrupts Y163–E171 H-bonds and weakens the interactions of residue pairs within the N-terminal region of loop2, leading to an increased flexibility of this region.

by performing multiple all-atom explicit solvent MD simulations. R175H and R273H mutants display similar b-sheet stability with WT p53, while they exhibit larger Rg and hydrophobic SASA values than WT p53. Loop2 in the two mutants shows larger RMSD and RMSF values than those in WT p53. These results indicate that the two mutants possess structural properties of aggregation-prone MG states. Our data also provide more details on the increased flexibility of loop2 in R175H and R273H mutants. Specifically, R175H mutation weakens the interactions between R175 and four residues (R181, C182, P190 and P191) in the middle part of the C-terminal region of loop2 because of the shortened side chain of residue 175, thus increasing the flexibility of this region. R273H mutation enhances the H273–F134 and H273–P250 interactions by aromatic stacking. The interaction enhancement increases the correlation strength of residue pairs H273–M133, M133–V272 and V272–I251 in the b-sheet region, but disrupts the sub-optimal path at Y163–E171 by breaking the Y163–E171 H-bond. The disruption of the Y163–E171 H-bond attenuates the interactions for residue pairs within the N-terminal region of loop2, thus leading to an increased flexibility of the N-terminal region of loop2. Taken together, our data offer mechanistic insights into the p53C aggregation induced by the common cancer mutations of R175H and R273H.
