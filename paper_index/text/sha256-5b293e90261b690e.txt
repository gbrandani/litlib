A universal polymer signature in Hi-C resolves cohesin loop density and supports monomeric extrusion Kirill Polovnikova,b,1 ID and Dmitry Starkova

Edited by Job Dekker, University of Massachusetts Chan Medical School, Worcester, MA; received December 1, 2025; accepted February 25, 2026

The genome is folded by molecular motors, yet how many motors are required to generate a single chromatin loop has remained unknown. In vertebrates, cohesin extrudes loops along interphase chromosomes, but the linear density of these loops— and therefore the stoichiometry of extrusion—has not been directly measurable in living cells. A fundamental challenge is that Hi-C contact maps, the primary experimental probe of genome organization, are shaped not only by chromatin folding but also by the physics and biochemistry of contact detection itself. Here, we develop an analytical polymer theory of short-scale chromosome folding that explicitly incorporates the steps of Hi-C detection protocol. The theory predicts a characteristic dip in the logarithmic derivative of the contact probability, arising from the interplay between cohesinmediated looping and contact capture. This dip is a universal feature of Hi-C data and provides a direct, protocol-aware readout of loop density. Applying the theory to human and mouse datasets reveals a conserved density of approximately six loops per megabase. Independent measurements of chromatin-bound cohesin quantitatively match this value, providing strong evidence that loop extrusion in vivo is predominantly mediated by single cohesin motors. cohesin | chromatin organization | polymer physics | Hi-C | loop extrusion

The three-dimensional organization of chromosomes is central to gene regulation, replication, repair, and segregation (1). In mammalian interphase nuclei, submegabase genome organization is dominated by dynamic chromatin loops and domains (2, 3). A major driver of this architecture is loop extrusion by cohesin, a ring-shaped SMC complex that reels in DNA to form transient loops and thereby structures genome-wide contact patterns (4–6). A central unresolved question concerns the stoichiometry of cohesin during loop extrusion in vivo—how many cohesin complexes extrude a single chromatin loop. Single cohesin complexes can extrude DNA in vitro (7, 8), yet superreBiophysics and Computational Biologysolution imaging and biochemical studies in living cells have reported both monomeric and dimeric assemblies (9–11). This ambiguity persists for three reasons. First, loop extrusion is highly dynamic, and clustered or stalled cohesins can appear as “dimers” in microscopy even when acting as monomers (11). Second, interphase loops form and dissolve rapidly, making them difficult to visualize directly. Third, cohesin copy-number measurements alone cannot resolve extrusion stoichiometry because the key structural parameter—the linear density of loops—remains unknown. Resolving cohesin stoichiometry therefore requires a quantitative framework that connects protein copy-number measurements to chromatin structure. Chromosome conformation capture (Hi-C) provides rich genome-wide information on chromatin organization by measuring how often pairs of genomic loci are found in close spatial proximity in a cell population (12). By pooling all locus pairs separated by a genomic distance s, one obtains the contact probability P(s), a widely used physical fingerprint of chromatin folding (6, 13–17). Polymer theory has identified several largescale chromatin features in P(s), including crumpled state of chromatin and folding into loops (6, 12, 13, 18). In particular, a universal peak in the slope of P(s) around s ≈ 100 kb has been routinely used as an estimate of the typical size of cohesin-mediated loops (6, 19). A key challenge, however, is that P(s) does not directly reflect intrinsic features of genome organization. Instead, it represents a convolution of chromatin folding with the Hi-C detection protocol, which involves a sequence of biochemical steps: crosslinking, restriction digestion into short fragments, and subsequent ligation. Despite the widespread use of Hi-C, a quantitative physical understanding of how this detection process shapes the measured contact signal, and thereby influences the features of P(s), has remained underdeveloped. Significance Loop extrusion is believed to dynamically fold meter-long DNA inside a micrometer-sized nucleus, yet the density of these loops has remained unknown. A central obstacle is that Hi-C contact maps encode chromatin structure through a biochemical detection process that intrinsically reshapes the measured signal, analogous to measurement backaction in physics. Here we develop a protocol-aware polymer theory of Hi-C that explicitly integrates chromatin looping with the detection steps. The theory explains a universal signature in Hi-C maps and reveals a conserved loop density of six loops per megabase, implying that 60 to 70% of the genome is organized into loops. Measurements of chromatin-bound cohesin quantitatively match this value, providing strong evidence for single-motor loop extrusion in living cells.

Here we develop a polymer theory that explicitly quantifies Hi-C contact detection and shows how the experimental protocol interplays with chromatin looping, generating a universal behavior of the Hi-C signal at short genomic scales. Remarkably, the short-scale P(s) profile reduces to just two interpretable parameters: a structural one (the linear density of cohesinmediated loops) and a protocol-dependent one (the Hi-C capture radius). We validate the theory using molecular-dynamics simulations and two independent experimental perturbations. Applying this approach to published mammalian datasets reveals a conserved loop density of ≈6 ± 1 loops per megabase. This Hi-C–based inference agrees with independent microscopy and mass-spectrometry measurements of the cohesin subunit RAD21, supporting predominantly monomeric loop extrusion in living interphase nuclei. Chromosome Folding Across Scales Interphase chromatin is a canonical exemplar of a multiscale soft matter system where different physical mechanisms dominate at different genomic separations s (Fig. 1 A–C). Below, we outline the main polymer regimes and their characteristic length scales, that eventually leads us to a short-scale window where chromatin folding simplifies, paving the way for the short-scale Hi-C theory. Large-Scale Crumpled Organization. At megabase scales, Hi-C

and imaging experiments support a crumpled organization of chromatin (12, 15, 22–24) (Fig. 1A). In this regime, Hi-C typically yields a power-law contact probability P(s) ∼ s−γ with γ ≈ 1.1 over 0.5 ≲ s ≲ 5 Mb, substantially shallower than the ideal-chain expectation γ = 3/2 (25, 26). From a polymerphysics perspective, this behavior is consistent with equilibrium entangled melts of unknotted, nonconcatenated rings above an entanglement length s > Ne (6, 27–30). Physically, topological constraints crumple segments for s > Ne , slowing the decay of P(s); below Ne , segments remain topologically relaxed and exhibit nearly ideal statistics. Biologically, such slow decay of contact statistics—predicted and argued theoretically early on (22)—is thought to facilitate gene expression and target-search processes in the nuclear environment (14, 31, 32). However, the physical origin of crumpling in linear interphase chromosomes, and the role of nonequilibrium effects therein, remain incompletely understood and difficult to quantify (15, 18, 28–30, 33–37). An interplay between topological constraints and megabase-scale compartmentalization—partitioning the genome into transcriptionally active and inactive domains (38–45)— further complicates robust extraction of folding parameters from large-scale Hi-C data. Ideal Chain Subject to Active Extrusion. At submegabase scales,

superimposed on the crumpled background, cohesin actively extrudes chromatin loops, converting ATP hydrolysis into mechanical work (5, 7, 8). In Hi-C, loop extrusion produces a characteristic shoulder in P(s) and a peak in its log-derivative near s ≈ 100 kb (Fig. 2A), reflecting loop-induced contact enhancement with a typical loop size λ ≈ 100 kb (6, 19). Consistently, cohesin depletion removes this shoulder and yields an extended γ ≈ 1.1 regime from ≈50 kb to several megabases in Micro-C experiments (6, 46), indicating that large-scale crumpling persists and becomes more evident in the absence of extrusion, while loop extrusion adds a distinct layer of submegabase organization.

Recent theoretical work (6, 47) shows that loop formation can strongly dilute topological entanglements, increasing the effective entanglement length Ne by up to ∼50-fold relative to cohesin-free chromatin [NeΔcoh ≈ 30 to 50 kb (15, 48)]. As a result, loop extrusion maintains a largely unentangled organization of interphase chromosomes across several megabases, consistent with limited intermingling of compartmental domains independently observed in multicontact (MC-3C) experiments (49, 50). This suppression of entanglements implies that, for s ≲ 1 Mb, chromosomes can be treated as effectively ideal chains subject to active loop extrusion (Fig. 1B). Equilibration Length Scale. The interplay between extrusion and thermal relaxation produces a fundamental crossover scale s∗ , below which activity becomes effectively irrelevant. Because loop extrusion proceeds linearly in genomic length, whereas Rouse relaxation time scales quadratically in s, chromatin segments shorter than s∗ (s < s∗ ) remain equilibrated subject to the loop constraints (6, 47). Short-Scale Redemption. By contrast, at short genomic separations below the loop size λ chromatin folding simplifies dramatically (Fig. 1C). Entanglements are negligible, unlooped segments obey ideal random-walk statistics, and polymer relaxation occurs much faster than loop extrusion dynamics at these scales (s  s∗ , Ne ). As we show below, the effect of loops in this regime enters primarily through a single structural parameter—the linear density of loops. Together, these simplifications render short-scale folding amenable to an accurate analytical description. At the same time, the Hi-C protocol becomes the dominant factor shaping the measured contact statistics. In what follows, we exploit this short-scale simplification to develop a protocol-aware polymer theory of the Hi-C signal that explicitly incorporates experimental contact detection. This provides a direct route to infer the linear density of chromatin loops and, in turn, resolve cohesin stoichiometry.

A Short-Scale Hi-C Theory Quantifies the Dip Across Hi-C datasets, the slope of the contact probability P(s) exhibits a characteristic short-scale behavior, including a reproducible local minimum (a “dip”) at genomic separations of a few tens of kilobases (Fig. 2A). This fingerprint is remarkably robust across cell types and species, yet its physical origin has remained unclear. As we show below, the dip arises from the interplay of two effects: the finite spatial resolution of Hi-C and chromatin folding into cohesin-mediated loops. Only their combination generates the dip and enables robust inference of the linear density of cohesin-mediated loops along chromatin. We start with a physical model of how the Hi-C protocol shapes contact statistics, apply it for a linear polymer and then show how folding into loops modifies this baseline to produce the universal short-scale dip. An Eective Model of Hi-C Contact Detection. Hi-C measures

how often genomic loci are found in close spatial proximity (12, 56), but a “contact” is neither point-like nor instantaneous. Briefly, in the Hi-C protocol, chromatin is fixed with a crosslinker, then digested into short randomly sized fragments, and finally nearby fragment ends are ligated and sequenced to register interactions. From a physical viewpoint, two genomic loci are registered as a Hi-C “contact” only if the restriction fragments on which they reside overlap in space at the moment of ligation (Fig. 1D).

[Page 4]

Accordingly, the spatial distance R between the two loci should be shorter than the spatial size of their fragments, R ≲ r(v) = b v1/2 (SI), where v is the fragment length measured in kilobases. Restriction digestion can be modeled as an approximately Poisson process due to weak correlations of the enzyme binding motifs along the DNA sequence (57, 58). Thus, fragment lengths v follow an exponential distribution with mean v0 , which is on the order of a few kilobases for standard cutters such as HindIII (12). Two loci separated by distance R are therefore captured as a contact only if their restriction fragments are longer than v(R) ∼ (R/b)2 to span that distance, which occurs with probability K (v) = exp(−v(R)/v0 ). Here, the capture radius rc is set by 1/2 the size r(v0 ) = bv0 of the typical restriction fragment. The derivation above implicitly assumes that the timing between cross-linking and digestion is sufficiently short so that chromatin structure is not substantially perturbed in response to cross-linking (59), which seems to be the case for the modern protocol design (see Discussion). We further assume that restriction fragments have sufficient time before ligation to allow their ends to explore contacts within a spatial volume set by r(v0 ). In practice, however, unrestricted polymer relaxation necessarily involves diffusion of the chain as a whole over distances comparable to its own size (25, 54, 60). Such a global diffusion of fragments would clearly affect the spatial specificity of a “contact,” effectively increasing the capture radius rc beyond the nominal value r(v0 ) by a factor of two or more. The extent of this diffusion-induced blurring depends on several experimental factors, including fragment mobility, the time interval between digestion and ligation, nonsimultaneous digestion events, and, most critically, the efficiency of chromatin cross-linking. With moderate cross-linking, restriction fragments remain largely unconstrained and can freely diffuse prior to ligation, broadening the effective capture radius rc . In contrast, excessive cross-linking (more than one cross-link per fragment) can immobilize fragments altogether and hinder efficient contact sampling within the nominal capture distance set by digestion frequency. In our theoretical description, we absorb these dynamical effects into an effective fragment length v0eff ≳ v0 . This phenomenological parameter accounts for fragment diffusion prior to ligation by treating the detection process as if it involved slightly longer, but nondiffusing fragments. Active-Extrusion Simulations. To further test the validity of

the equilibrium treatment at short genomic scales (Eq. 2), we compared the short-scale theory (Eq. 8) with molecular-dynamics simulations of chromatin undergoing active, two-sided cohesin extrusion (5). The simulations are parameterized by the mean cohesin spacing d, the extrusion processivity l, and the extrusion rate r (see Methods). Here, the processivity l denotes the average genomic length a single cohesin can extrude in the absence of constraints, which is generally larger than the typical steady-state loop size λ. For phantom (random-walk) polymers, the simulated logderivatives at short genomic separations were quantitatively described by the theory, provided the cohesin spacing d was identified with the loop period T . In this regime, the signal depended only on loop density and the effective fragment length v0eff , while the processivity l affected only larger scales near the loop size λ (Fig. 3 A and B and SI Appendix, Fig. S6). Importantly, nested and overlapping loops that can arise naturally in cohesin dynamics (64) did not modify the short-scale behavior of the curve (SI Appendix, Fig. S6). We next tested a more realistic crumpled-chromatin model with intrinsic persistence using melts of unknotted ring polymers calibrated to Micro-C data (46). Here, the theory again matched the short-scale simulation results, provided the fragment length in simulations was reduced by the chromatin persistence length lp ≈ 1.8 kb (Fig. 3D and SI Appendix, Fig. S5). This correction accounts for the semiflexibility of chromatin, reflecting the fact that segments shorter than lp behave as rigid impermeable units and contribute weakly to contact detection. Crucially, the inferred loop period T remained unchanged. These results confirm that short-range Hi-C signatures of actively extruding chromosomes are well captured by an equilibrium polymer theory with flexible fragments, with chromatin semiflexibility absorbed into a renormalized effective fragment length v0eff . We first examined a recent Hi-C dataset in mouse embryonic stem cells (mESCs) from Shah et al. (65), in which the chromatinbound cohesin fraction was systematically reduced using an auxin-inducible RAD21 degron system. The small molecule dTAG-13 was titrated at concentrations of 20 and 45 nM to induce partial degradation of RAD21 and thereby reduce cohesin occupancy on chromatin. Flow cytometry confirmed that approximately 50% of cohesin remained chromatin-bound at 20 nM and about 30% at 45 nM dTAG. We asked whether these measured reductions in cohesin abundance could be independently reproduced by our theoretical inference. For each degron concentration, we computed the short-scale logarithmic derivative of the contact probability P(s) (Fig. 4A). Increasing dTAG concentration produced a systematic rightward and downward shift of the dip—corresponding to

larger smin and smaller ymin —consistent with an increased loop period T and reduced loop density T −1 . Fitting the data using Eq. 8 revealed a decrease in loop density from 10 loops/Mb in wild type to 5.6 loops/Mb (56% remaining) at 20 nM and 4.5 loops/Mb (45% remaining) at 45 nM, closely matching the cohesin retention from flow cytometry (50% and 30%, respectively) (65). Notably, the fitted effective fragment length, v0eff ≈ 1.6 kb, remained nearly constant across all concentrations, confirming that the observed changes in P(s) are explained solely by altered cohesin abundance. We further analyzed another perturbation targeting the cohesin loader NIPBL in the same system (65). NIPBL promotes cohesin loading onto DNA and may also facilitate its extrusion rate. Fitting this profile against the theoretical model (SI Appendix, Fig. S4A) yielded a loop period of T ≈ 160 kb, corresponding to a loop density of 6.3 loops/Mb (63% of wild-type levels). This reduction mirrors the effect of partial RAD21 degradation and agrees with independent flow cytometry measurements showing that ≈50% of cohesin remains DNA-bound after NIPBL degradation (65). Together, these analyses demonstrate that our framework quantitatively captures changes in loop density arising from either direct cohesin degradation or impaired loading efficiency. To assess an orthogonal perturbation affecting experimental resolution rather than cohesin levels on chromosomes, we analyzed Hi-C data from Oksuz et al. (20). In this study, cross-linking and fragmentation conditions were systematically varied while maintaining identical chromatin organization. We compared standard in situ Hi-C 2.0 (FA cross-linking, DpnII digestion) (66) to the enhanced Hi-C 3.0 protocol employing dual cross-linkers (FA+DSG, DpnII digestion) (20). As shown in Fig. 4B, the dual cross-linking condition caused a leftward and downward shift of the dip in HFF cells, reflecting a reduction in v0eff consistent with suppressed fragment diffusion and a smaller capture radius rc (Eq. 5). Theoretical fits reproduced both curves accurately, revealing a threefold reduction in v0eff upon addition of DSG, while the loop period remained nearly unchanged. A similar ∼threefold reduction in v0eff was observed in the hESC system for the equivalent addition of cross-linker (SI Appendix, Fig. S4B). Together, these two complementary perturbations—one altering cohesin density and the other contact capture resolution— provide strong experimental validation of our theoretical framework and demonstrate its ability to quantitatively extract key physical parameters from the Hi-C signal. We find that while the inferred effective fragment lengths v0eff vary substantially across experiments (from ≈2 to ≈11 kb), reflecting differences in the experimental protocol (SI Appendix, Table S1), the loop periods are narrowly distributed around

The modest (≈20%) uncertainty reflects striking consistency across cell types, organisms, and experimental conditions. Importantly, the inferred loop density is reproducible across biological and technical replicates (SI Appendix, Fig. S9). This result suggests that loop density is a fundamental and conserved feature of mammalian interphase chromatin. Our inference complements earlier modeling efforts that relied on fitting large-scale Hi-C features—such as TAD structures or the global P(s) curve (6, 17, 38, 55, 67–69). At these larger genomic scales, chromosome organization reflects a complex interplay between loop architecture, topological entanglements, and nonequilibrium extrusion dynamics, rendering parameter inference inherently degenerate and model-dependent. In contrast, at short genomic separations chromatin folding simplifies and depends on loop periodicity as the single structural parameter, allowing us to bypass these confounding effects and extract loop density in a robust and well-controlled manner. Stoichiometry of Extruding Cohesin. The progressive shift of the dip upon partial RAD21 degradation (Fig. 4A) highlights that this short-scale Hi-C feature is directly linked to the abundance of cohesin motor. This enables a direct comparison between the loop densities inferred from Hi-C and the densities of chromatin-bound cohesin measured independently by quantitative fluorescence spectroscopy and mass spectrometry (Table 1). We find that the reported densities of 5 to 7 cohesins per megabase in human and mouse cells (9, 70) fall within the range of 6 ± 1 loops/Mb revealed by the short-scale Hi-C theory. Across systems, the two densities agree closely, yielding an average of 0.98 ± 0.39 cohesins per inferred loop (Fig. 5B). This correspondence suggests that, on average, a single cohesin complex is sufficient to extrude a loop under interphase conditions, arguing against widespread dimeric extrusion. As an important caveat, a recent superresolution imaging study reported substantially higher apparent cohesin copy numbers on HeLa cells (11 to 12 per Mb) (11), which—if taken at face value—would imply dimeric extrusion. However, as argued by the authors of the same study, these elevated figures, roughly twice those obtained previously in the same system (70), arise from concentration-dependent clustering of the abundant STAG2 isoform in the course of interphase progression, which was overlooked previously. Such clustering increases local cohesin encounters and can contribute to the appearance of paired complexes (10, 11) without true stoichiometric dimerization. Consequently, these inflated counts likely reflect aggregation of unbound cohesin with loop-extruding molecules rather than genuine extrusion dimers. We therefore caution against using these values directly in stoichiometric estimates. Overall, the close quantitative correspondence between inferred loop numbers and absolute cohesin counts across datasets and organisms provides genome-wide support for predominantly monomeric cohesin extrusion during interphase. Notably, our conclusion constrains the average extrusion stoichiometry to be predominantly monomeric, but cannot exclude rare or transient dimeric interactions that may occur locally or under specific conditions.

Dynamics of Chromatin Loop Reorganization During Mitotic Exit We next applied the framework to a dynamic biological transition—mitotic exit—during which condensins dissociate and cohesins reload to progressively establish interphase chromatin architecture (11, 50). To this end, we analyzed synchronized Hi-C time courses from Abramo et al. (HeLa) (71) and Zhang et al. (mouse erythroblasts GE1) (72) (Fig. 6 and SI Appendix, Figs. S10 and S11). Tracking the positions of log-derivative minima revealed pronounced temporal dynamics in loop periodicity in both datasets. In the HeLa system (Abramo et al.), loop spacing peaked at telophase, indicating a transient reduction in loop density during the condensin–cohesin handoff, consistent with the existence of an intermediate folding state at this stage (71). In contrast, in the Zhang dataset, the largest loop period occurred later, at early G1, with the highest loop density detected in anaphase. This pattern is consistent with the slower binding of cohesin in the mouse system: RAD21 ChIP–seq profiles indicated minimal levels of cohesin occupancy until early G1 (72). These complementary trends align with recent superresolution imaging of HeLa cells (11), which revealed rapid loading of STAG1isoform of cohesin immediately after telophase, followed by gradual STAG2-isoform recruitment. Together, these results suggest that the precise kinetics of cohesin accumulation differs modestly between human and mouse systems, reflecting speciesor cell-type specificities. The observed trajectories in both systems capture the sequential disengagement of condensin and loading of cohesin, providing an independent validation of the framework’s applicability to dynamic chromatin reorganization in the cell cycle. Yet, despite a decade of experimental and theoretical work, key quantitative parameters of this organization have remained difficult to extract from populationaveraged data. A central advance of this work is the explicit integration of the Hi-C contact detection protocol into a physical model of chromatin organization. We show how a sequence of biochemical steps in Hi-C imposes a well-defined perturbation to the measured signal that must be taken into account to interpret conformation contact capture data quantitatively. Universal Short-Scale Organization of Chromatin. Although

chromosome folding across scales reflects a complex interplay of topological entanglements, loop-gap architecture, and nonequilibrium extrusion dynamics, chromatin statistics simplify dramatically at genomic separations from a few to ≈100 kb. In this regime: i) topological constraints are irrelevant because the entanglement length is much larger [Ne ≳ 0.5-1 Mb (6, 47)]; ii) chromatin persistence does not enter, as the Kuhn length is at most a few kilobases (51, 73); iii) polymer segments relax thermally faster than they are reorganized by extrusion, rendering local conformations effectively equilibrated; and iv) looped-chain statistics depend on a single architectural parameter, the loop period T . Combined with the effective restriction fragment length v0eff , this yields a simple two-parameter description of the short-scale Hi-C signal. Within this regime, the theory recapitulates a universal local minimum (a “dip”) in the logarithmic derivative of P(s). The dip position is set by thegeometric mean of the two characteristic

length scales, smin ∼

Tv0eff , and physically reflects a competition

between protocol-induced smearing at short distances s ∼ v0eff and chromatin compaction due to cohesin-mediated loops at larger separations s ∼ T . In practice, this feature provides a direct and interpretable readout of loop density: Shifts in the dip position and depth quantitatively track changes in cohesin abundance across cell types and perturbations (Fig. 1F and Fig. 4 A and B), analogous to how the peak at s ≈ 100 kb has been used to monitor loop size (6, 19). More broadly, the ubiquity of this dip across Hi-C datasets provides protocol-aware evidence of loop extrusion as a dominant organizing mechanism of interphase chromosomes in vivo. Notably, loop-induced compaction—the mechanism responsible for the right branch of the log-derivative curve—persists even when loops are assumed to be out of equilibrium (47). When combined with the protocol-induced smoothing generating the left branch, the emergence of a dip becomes inevitable. Thus, while chromatin is likely equilibrated below the loop size under typical conditions, full equilibration of cohesin-mediated loops is not required for dip formation. We therefore expect the predictions of our theory to remain

valid even at higher extrusion rates or reduced chromatin mobility. Why the Hi-C Dip Is Absent in Micro-C. An important pillar of the present ideal-chain theory is that the capture radius rc exceeds the chromatin Kuhn length lK ≈ 0.05 μm (51). We find that this condition is readily satisfied in Hi-C experiments (SI Appendix, Table S1). Simulations of semiflexible polymers suggest that chromatin persistence renormalizes the inferred v0eff due to the reduced accessibility of rigid segments. Therefore, as the capture radius approaches the spatial scale of the persistence length lp ≈ lK /2 (25), the dip is predicted to disappear. Consistent with this prediction, Micro-C experiments (20)— where fragment lengths are on the order of linker DNA between nucleosomes (≈0.01 μm)—exhibit no dip in the log-derivative (SI Appendix, Fig. S12). Instead, the log-derivative demonstrates a monotonic decay over 10kb < s < 100 kb, in agreement with the theoretical limit of point-like contact detection (Fig. 2B) (6). At shorter genomic separations (s < 10 kb), the MicroC signal shows a characteristic downturn due to intrinsic chromatin persistence, which is well reproduced by simulations of a semiflexible polymer (SI Appendix, Fig. S5D). By contrast, in Hi-C these persistence effects are smeared out by the larger capture radius, generating the left branch of the logderivative and, together with loop-induced compaction at larger s, giving rise to the dip. These observations show that the dip is a specific consequence of a capture radius exceeding the chromatin persistence length and is therefore absent in Micro-C by design. Consistent with this estimate, livecell imaging at the Fbn2 locus reports 57 to 61% looped chromatin (52), in close agreement with our genome-wide inference. The Physics of Contact Detection in Hi-C. Although Hi-C in-

volves a complex sequence of biochemical steps, we show that its essential detection physics can be captured by a simple and physically grounded model. Random digestion, followed by spatial sampling of contacts by restriction fragments and their ligation, naturally gives rise to an effective Gaussian detection kernel. While Gaussian kernels have previously been introduced ad hoc in modeling studies (75), here we derive them directly from the Hi-C protocol itself. The effective fragment length v0eff —the central protocol parameter in our framework—sets the spatial resolution of the experiment. It phenomenologically summarizes protocoldependent aspects of Hi-C dynamics beyond restriction cutting frequency, most notably chromatin cross-linking efficiency. Moderate cross-linking permits restriction fragments to sample contacts within their own volume and to diffuse as a whole prior to ligation, effectively broadening the capture radius beyond the nominal fragment size. Consistent with this polymer-physics picture, introducing an additional cross-linker in the Hi-C 3.0 protocol (20) reduces v0eff by nearly a factor of three (Fig. 4B and SI Appendix, Fig. S4B), corresponding to a decrease in capture radius by a factor of ≈1.7. This shift reflects suppressed fragment diffusion, driving v0eff toward the true fragment length v0 set by digestion. Across datasets, inferred values of v0eff span 2 to11 kb (mean 6.4 kb; SI Appendix, Table S1). Using rc = b 3v0eff /2 with

b ≈ 0.03 μm kb−1/2 yields a typical Hi-C capture radius rc ≈ 0.09 μm. Strikingly, this scale is comparable to the chromatin mesh size ξ ∼ (V /L)1/2 , where V ∼ (10 μm)3 is the nuclear volume and L ∼ 0.1 m is the total chromatin length, assuming a compaction ratio of ∼ 60 bp/nm (15). This correspondence supports a physical picture in which the surrounding chromatin network constrains fragment diffusion and thereby sets the effective spatial resolution of Hi-C under standard cross-linking conditions. Excessive cross-linking (exceeding roughly one cross-linker per fragment), however, can impede efficient contact sampling by fragment ends. In this regime, ligation would occur over distances smaller than the nominal fragment size r(v0 ), introducing systematic, position-dependent capture biases. In particular, loci located near fragment ends and those near fragment centers would equally register ligation events while having markedly different spatial distances. Thus, the cross-linking step must balance suppression of fragment diffusion against unbiased sampling of contacts within the intrinsic spatial volume of restriction fragments. Cross-Linking–Induced Chromatin Collapse. The timing between cross-linking and digestion imposes an additional constraint on Hi-C measurements. Prolonged cross-linking prior to digestion can, in principle, reorganize chromatin itself, inducing a polymer collapse analogous to the formation of a Lifshitz globule by saturating bonds (59, 76, 77). This can generate artificial contacts that were not present in the native chromatin structure (78). Simulations show that such a collapse produces a short-scale shoulder in the contact probability (59, 77), superficially similar to the broadening caused here by finite fragment size and fragment diffusion (Fig. 2). However, several observations argue against cross-linking– induced collapse as the reason for the short-scale Hi-C shoulder of P(s) . If short-scale broadening in Hi-C (Fig. 2A) was driven by the collapse, increasing cross-linker concentration would be expected to extend the shoulder and increase the inferred capture radius rc . Instead, we observe the opposite trend: Introducing an additional cross-linker enhances spatial resolution and reduces rc (Fig. 4B and SI Appendix, Fig. S4B). Moreover, switching from Hi-C to Micro-C with comparable cross-linking conditions leads to dramatic changes in the signal (SI Appendix, Fig. S12). Both observations are inconsistent with a collapse-driven mechanism and are naturally explained by suppressed fragment mobility and fragment length. Consistent with this interpretation, experiments in yeast show that cross-linking–induced collapse requires extended exposure times of approximately 10 min at 1% formaldehyde (59). Standard Hi-C protocols operate near or below this threshold (20, 66), suggesting that these effects are likely weak under typical conditions. Comparison of collapse times with Rouse relaxation times of individual restriction fragments (≈0.1 to 1 s) additionally indicates that, at the fragment scale, chromatin remains largely unconstrained. Fragment diffusion therefore constitutes a major contribution to the effective capture radius in Hi-C. We suggest that systematic variation of cross-linking strength, digestion conditions, and interstep timing (20, 78) would provide direct experimental access to distinct dynamical regimes of contact formation, informing both interpretation of existing data and the rational design of next-generation chromosome conformation capture protocols.
