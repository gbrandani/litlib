SUMMARY

Transforming the vast knowledge from genetics, biochemistry, and structural biology into detailed molecular descriptions of biological processes inside cells remains a major challenge—one in sore need of better imaging technologies. For example, transcription involves the complex interplay between RNA polymerase II (Pol II), regulatory factors (RFs), and chromatin, but visualizing these dynamic molecular transactions in their native intracellular milieu remains elusive. Here, we zoom into single tagged genes using nanoscopy techniques, including an active target-locking, ultra-sensitive system that enables single-molecule detection in addressable subdiffraction volumes, within crowded intracellular environments. We image, track, and quantify Pol II with single-molecule resolution, unveiling its dynamics during the transcription cycle. Further probing multiple functionally linked events—RF-chromatin interactions, Pol II dynamics, and nascent transcription kinetics—reveals detailed operational parameters of gene-regulatory mechanisms hitherto-unseen in vivo. Our approach sets the stage for single-molecule studies of complex molecular processes in live cells. INTRODUCTION Our knowledge of biological processes has vastly expanded over the past few decades. Genetics; biochemistry; and molecular, cellular, and structural biology studies identified key components and interactions, provided snapshots of macromolecules in action, and began to elaborate on the dynamic and interconnected nature of biological pathways. A major challenge is transforming this vast knowledge base into a

complete description of how biological processes take place inside live cells. Developing tools to visualize complex molecular transactions at exact intracellular locales is a pressing unmet need. Gene control is fundamental across biomedical fields. To transcribe eukaryotic protein-coding genes, the RNA polymerase II (Pol II) machinery coordinates with multiple RFs: DNA-specific transcription factors, chromatin remodelers, histone modifying enzymes, chromatin readers, and Mediator. How this intricate biochemical system works in vivo remains obscure. Visualizing single molecules at specific intracellular 3D loci, such as enhancers and promoters, is technically very challenging. Thus, little is known at the single-gene level about the spatiotemporal Pol II and RF organization, the mechanisms that dictate assembly of transcription complexes and direct enhancer-promoter communication, and how the various molecular interactions and biochemical activities control the progression of the transcription cycle. A technology capable of imaging single Pol II and RFs at a single gene, concomitant to nascent transcription kinetics, would greatly advance our understanding of these hitherto-unseen molecular processes in vivo. Visualizing transcription at single genes in live cells presents unique challenges. First, single-molecule tracking experiments often detect the behavior of transcription factors at random nuclear locations and without extra functional readouts. How observations at unspecified locations relate to the function of these factors at specific genes remains unclear. Second, real-time single-molecule detection at specific loci requires discriminating the signal of individual molecules functionally engaged at the gene of interest from an overwhelming background from molecules transiently diffusing through the optical detection volume, as well as from molecules bound to loci nearby. High nuclear Pol II and RF concentration—hundreds of nanomolars to low mM—is a formidable barrier. Finally, addressing functional consequences of observed molecular dynamics requires imaging multiple functionally linked events at the transcription site and promptly analyzing the effects of perturbations to capture primary effects and test causal relationships. transcription at the single-molecule and single-gene level requires new, improved optical imaging approaches. Advances in single-molecule tracking, fluorescence spectroscopy, and sub-diffraction microscopy were steps toward these goals. Such techniques reach single-molecule resolution by sparse labeling or stochastic photo switching, multi-copy signal amplification, or multi-event averaging via temporal-correlation approaches. In natural or artificial multi-copy tandem gene arrays, the signal from hundreds to thousands of recruited fluorescent molecules enables robust quantitative analyses (Dundr et al., 2002; McNally et al., 2000; Stasevich et al., 2014; Yao et al., 2006). However, imaging single-copy genes remains elusive. Setups with minimal out-of-focus background (Chen et al., 2014a; Zhao et al., 2014) could only detect single molecules at (10-nM concentrations by under-labeling or stochastic photo switching. Because such approaches detect only a small percentage (0.01%–0.1%) of molecules at any given time, the effective sensitivity during dynamic imaging is dramatically decreased. Live-cell photo-activated localization microscopy (PALM) could detect interpretable signals only when an estimated 100 Pol II molecules were simultaneously present in the same sub-diffraction volume (Cisse et al., 2013). As PALM traces contain mostly an un-interpretable absence of signal, fewer molecules (e.g., 2–20 possible elongating Pol IIs at a gene of interest), as well as real-time dynamic changes involving such small Pol II numbers, could not be detected in live cells (Cho et al., 2016). Also, multiple on-off transitions of the same molecule (‘‘blinking’’) seriously complicate counting exact numbers of molecules by PALM. Thus, despite remarkable technical advances, methods to directly visualize the organization, dynamics, and relation to transcription activity of down to 1–10 Pol II and RF molecules at the general and physiological setting of endogenous single-copy genes are currently lacking. Here, we achieve real-time, single-gene and single-molecule, multi-faceted interrogation of transcription in live cells: zooming into individual tagged gene loci, detecting real-time nascent transcription kinetics, and simultaneously tracking Pol II and RF dynamics with down-to-single-molecule resolution. To overcome optical detection challenges, we show that spatially targeted optical nanoscopy techniques efficiently suppress background at high concentrations. We further develop an active target-locking system to maximize signal from detected molecules while eliminating time-consuming and signal-wasting point-by-point scanning. At single transcription sites, point scanning provides 3D spatial perspective, while target locking maximizes detection signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), allows long-term tracking with minimal photobleaching, and by suppressing background via stimulated emission depletion (STED), enables single-molecule detection sensitivity. With the new capabilities, we elucidate the nanometer-scale Pol II and RF organization at single gene loci, visualize Pol II dynamics during the transcription cycle, dissect RF hierarchies and interdependencies at the enhancer, and link these complex molecular transactions to control of promoter kinetics. Beyond transcription, these improved technologies can be applied broadly to elucidate the molecular processes that underlie many important cellular functions in situ.

RESULTS 3D STED Enables Single-Molecule Detection and Nanometer Localization at High Background Concentrations Spatially targeted fluorescence suppression in the periphery of a focused excitation beam using STED appeared promising for single-molecule detection in the presence of high background concentrations. In 1D and 2D geometries, STED could visualize dense molecules (Eggeling et al., 2009; Heller et al., 2013; Hua et al., 2014). However, pioneering (Kastrup et al., 2005) attempts to detect single molecules at high 3D bulk concentrations were less fruitful: STED increased 3D resolution (Harke et al., 2008) and achieved nano-sized detection volumes, but inefficiently depleted out-of-focus background molecules overwhelmed the signal from molecules transiting through the center, resulting in SNR that degrades with increasing depletion intensity. Whether such approaches can break the high concentration barrier thus remained unclear. We set out to explore the fundamental limits of single-molecule detection using 3D STED (Figures 1A and S1A). Conceptually, two distinct types of noise emerge in different experimental systems and molecular concentration and/or brightness regimes (STAR Methods): (1) Poisson-distributed shot-noise, due to the stochastic nature of fluorescent photon emission, and (2) super-Poisson noise from particle number fluctuations, as background molecules randomly transit the detection volume. To identify the dominant noise source under single-molecule detection conditions, we first characterize the background properties in solutions of freely diffusing molecules. Our results reveal hallmarks of super-Poisson noise (Figures S1B–S1D), indicating that particle-number fluctuations hinder singlemolecule detection at high concentrations. Intriguingly, measurements of fluorescent Pol II in live cells recapitulate results from freely diffusing molecules in solution (Figures S1E and S1F), which shows that super-Poisson number fluctuations can also dominate in certain crowded intracellular environments. Theoretical calculations and numerical simulations reveal that STED should be particularly effective in suppressing super-Poisson noise from number fluctuations (STAR Methods; Video S1). Experimental results confirm the predicted efficiency both for freely diffusing molecules in solution (Figures 1B and S1D) and Pol II in live cells (Figure S1E). To exploit this pristine background suppression by STED for single-molecule detection at high concentrations, we reason that the maximum SNR increase is achieved for molecules exactly at the center point of zero STED (STAR Methods). We thus center surface-immobilized Cy3-labeled single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) targets at the STED zero and measure the realtime on-off binding of short Atto647N-labeled complementary probes from the solution (Figure 1C). Real-time traces of directly excited Atto647N reveal noisy intensity fluctuations, while STED enables unambiguous identification of binding events (Figure 1D), markedly improving SNR (Figure 1E). This proof-of-principle experiment shows that target-locking STED achieves single-molecule detection at up to 0.6–1 mM solution concentrations.

If the 3D coordinates where molecules reside are not predefined, spatial information is often obtained imaging point by point. As the beams are raster scanned over the sample, the molecules do not always reside in the highest-intensity peak, trading some signal for spatial information. Even so, point-scanning imaging is improved: single molecules obscure in confocal-only images become visible with STED (Figures 1F, 1G, and S1G–S1I) at up to 0.3–0.6 mM background in solution. Beyond detecting the presence of a molecule in an image, position information at length scales much shorter than the spatial resolution can also be extracted. (Sub-)nanometer precision is achievable at low background (Pertsinidis et al., 2010), but performance dramatically deteriorates due to super-Poisson back-

ground noise (STAR Methods). STED recovers the lost precision, enabling nanometer measurements at elevated background concentrations (Figures 1H and 1I). Both increased detection SNR and improved localization precision rely on suppressing super-Poisson noise from number fluctuations—a previously uncharacterized property of STED, distinct from the known inversesquare-root spatial resolution law (Westphal and Hell, 2005) pffiffiffiffiffiffiffiffiffi ð1= 1 + z; z : depletion saturationÞ. Pol II Accumulates at Active Transcription Sites We first establish a synthetic ‘‘mini-gene’’ system (Figure 2A) to simultaneously image (1) Pol II dynamics via an O6-alkylguanineDNA alkyltransferase derivative (SNAP-tag) fused to the Rpb1subunit (SNAP-Rpb1) and labeled with silicon rhodamine (SiR), (2) nascent transcription kinetics via phage PP7-derived stem loops (24 3 PP7) bound by a tandem-dimer phage coat protein EGFP fusion (tdPCP-EGFP), and (3) genomic location via tetracycline-repressor RFP (TetR-RFP) bound at an upstream tetracycline operator array (28 3 TetO). Single mini-gene copies are stably integrated in U-2 OS cells (Figures S2A–S2C), and use of the highly active Cytomegalovirus immediate-early (CMV-IE) promoter-enhancer results in transcription sites that contain multiple nascent RNAs appearing as bright tdPCP-EGFP and TetR-RFP co-localized nuclear foci (Figure 2B; Video S2). Fast-scanning imaging in a small region of interest (ROI) around the transcription site reveals SiR-Rpb1 foci that colocalize with tdPCP-EGFP (Figures 2C and 2D). Rpb1-tdPCP co-localize better in STED versus confocal-only images, indicating that background suppression by STED increases localization precision. Unlike Pol II, TetR does not strictly overlap with tdPCP, indicating resolvable nanometer separations between 30 and upstream regions. Notably, the brightness and gradual SiR-Rpb1 photobleaching within these foci (Figure S2D) indicate multiple Pol II molecules clustered at the mini-gene. It was unclear if typically, transcription is carried out by single, solitary Pol II molecules (Zhao et al., 2014) or if it involves multiple Pol II molecules (Jackson et al., 1993). When Pol II clustering occurs, as typically, little information on genomic position and promoter activity is simultaneously available, it has been hard to establish whether and how this phenomenon relates to transcription. Our observations reveal that focal Pol II accumulation and transcription activity from a specific promoter at a specific genomic locus are spatially and temporally linked. Target Locking Probes Nanometer Pol II Organization at the Transcription Site Motion blur and bleaching during scanning distorts the 3D images, limits the observation times, and hinders quantifying and tracking Pol II at the transcription site with single-molecule sensi-

tivity and higher spatiotemporal resolution (Figure S2D). To eliminate scanning, we develop a target-locking system that obtains single-shot spatial information and actively stabilizes the transcription site at the excitation maximum and STED zero (Figure S1A; Video S1). Feedback loop speeds up to 30 fps cancel Brownian motion of the transcription site (Figure S2E) and lock tdPCP-EGFP within 30 nm root mean square (r.m.s.) from the desired set point (Figures 2E and S2F). Target locking at the excitation maximum provides the same total photon counts as scanning but at orders-of-magnitude lower power, improving photostability. Importantly, target-locking STED reduces background more efficiently than scanning (STAR Methods) and is ultimately essential for single-molecule detection with optimum SNR at a mobile locus, like the transcription site. The improved capabilities of target-locking enable further quantifying the spatial Pol II distribution at the transcription site. The Pol II cluster center—obtained from background-subtracted images immediately after the red laser turns on and before appreciable photobleaching—is within 33 nm r.m.s. from the tdPCP-EGFP set point (Figures 2F–2H), improving on the less precise scanning data (Figures 2C and 2D). Target-locking STED can also measure Pol II cluster size by increasing STEDxy doughnut-beam intensity and monitoring depletion of Pol II molecules at progressively smaller radii from the center. We obtain an r.m.s. cluster size of 85 ± 11 nm (mean ± SD) for SiR-Rpb1 by target-locking STEDxy in live cells (Figure S2G), consistent with 85 ± 13 nm (mean ± SD) radius of gyration obtained by tdPCP-EGFP super-resolution imaging (Figures S2H–S2J) and in rough agreement with 40–50 nm r.m.s. size from SiR-Rpb1 confocal and STEDxy imaging (Figures S2K–S2M), in fixed cells. Nascent RNA and Pol II thus co-occupy the same sub-diffraction volume (Rg 45–85 nm) at the transcription site—smaller than z10 kb active chromatin domains (Rg R 100 nm; Boettiger et al., 2016) but significantly larger than a single 15 nm diameter Pol II molecule. transcription cycle at two stages: (1) initiation, using triptolide (TRP) (Titov et al., 2011), and (2) promoter-proximal pause release and transition to elongation, using flavopiridol (FVP) (Chao and Price, 2001) (Figure 3A). Upon adding either inhibitor, but not DMSO, tdPCP-EGFP gradually disappears from transcription sites (Figures 3B and 3C; Video S3; decay times 130–180 s). This result is consistent with completion of elongation and release of nascent transcripts by Pol IIs already engaged and complete inhibition of new productive initiation events. Pol II follows the fate of nascent transcripts, gradually decreasing upon inhibition (Figures 3B, 3C, and S4F; decay times 160–240 s). Thus, Pol II accumulation at the mini-gene does not persist in the absence of de novo transcription (Jackson and Cook, 1985; Mitchell and Fraser, 2008). Since Pol II loss follows kinetics similar to elongation completion and RNA release, Pol II likely accumulates at the mini-gene at a step after pause release, i.e., elongation and/or termination. This contrasts models proposing transient Pol II clusters forming before initiation and persisting upon transcription inhibition (Cho et al., 2016; Cisse et al., 2013). SiR-Rbp1 traces at target-locked mini-genes (with and without STED and from 1.6 kW/cm2 down to an ultra-low 6.4 W/cm2 average excitation) are absent of features such as large intensity ‘‘spikes’’ that would have indicated transient clustering of 100 Pol IIs. Chromatin immunoprecipitation (ChIP) experiments show near-complete Pol II loss from the mini-gene body and terminator within 5 min of inhibition (Figures S4G and S4H). The good agreement with decay rates measured optically further indicates that clustering reflects multiple elongating/terminating Pol IIs. During multi-round transcription, Pol II could be locally recycled by spatial restriction (Yao et al., 2007; Zobeck et al., 2010) or termination-initiation coupling (Ansari and Hampsey, 2005; Mapendano et al., 2010). We devised target-locking fluorescence recover after photobleaching (FRAP) experiments to probe Pol II dynamics at the transcription site under steady-state activity. Bleached SiR-Rpb1 signal initially recovers linearly, reaching a plateau over a few minutes (Figure 3D), while the Pol II cluster reappears (Figure 3E). The initial recovery rate, 2 ± 1 Pol II/min (Figure 3F), is close to the estimated 2.4 ± 0.5 RNA/min initiation rate (Figure S4C). The population-averaged recovery has a non-linear concave shape due to site-to-site variability: slower-recovering sites dominate the rate at longer times. With an (empirical) singleexponential fit, we estimate 290 s recovery time and 60% recovery efficiency (Figure 3G). The recovery time agrees with an estimated 310 s needed to complete one minigene transcription cycle (STAR Methods; Figures S4I and S4J). The recovery amplitude is consistent with close-to-full recovery of the Pol II population accumulated at the minigene (70%, S), as the immobile background population (30%, Bi) is expected to recover at a much slower rate (1,000–1,500 s; Figures S4K and S4L). Taken together, our results indicate that a Pol II molecule is recruited to the minigene from the nucleoplasm, enters productive elongation, and upon termination after one transcription round, is predominantly released back into the nucleoplasm rather than being retained and recycled over multiple rounds.

Pol II and RFs Form Spatially Distinct Foci at the Endogenous Pou5f1 Locus in Mouse Embryonic Stem Cells We next focus on Pol II and RFs at Pou5f1 in mouse embryonic stem cells (mESCs). This essential gene for early embryo development is controlled by enhancers (Figure 4A) (Yeom et al., 1996) bound by several transcription factors (TFs), including Sox2, Oct4, and Nanog (Chen et al., 2008). Enhancer clusters that regulate genes with cell-type-specific roles (Parker et al., 2013; Whyte et al., 2013) show high ChIP signals of TFs, certain regulatory complexes, and histone acetylation. However, steadystate occupancies, binding kinetics, and links to transcription dynamics are not well characterized. Pou5f1 transcription sites, imaged via 30 UTR-integrated 24 3 MS2 and an MS2 phage coat protein fused to mNeonGreen (MCP-mNeonGreen), appear as bright nuclear foci in live cells, indicating multiple present nascent RNAs. These foci persist over 30 min (Video S4), an on time significantly longer than one transcription round (see below). Strikingly, zooming onto active Pou5f1 loci we discover subdiffraction clusters of Pol II (SNAP-Rpb1), as well as four distinct RFs (SNAP-tagged Sox2, Cdk9, Brd4, and Med22; Figure 4B). Our results further reveal that Rpb1-MCP colocalize but that MCP does not strictly overlap with any of the four RFs (Figures 4C and 4D). This nanometer-scale separation between Pol II/30 UTR-tagged RNA and upstream-bound factors markedly resembles the organization of Pol II, tdPCP, and TetR at the CMV mini-gene. Canceling Pou5f1 Brownian motion (Figure S5A) by target locking enables single-molecule Pol II quantification (Figures S5B and S5C). We obtain 9.2 ± 1.6 detected SiR-Rpb1 molecules (Figure S5D; mean ± SD, n = 10 experiments), which reflects at least 6–7 molecules at the Pou5f1 transcription site and immobile background contributions equivalent to up to 2–3 molecules (72%, S and 28%, Bi; Figures S5E–S5H). By comparing the brightness of Sox2, Cdk9, Brd4, and Med22 foci with Rpb1, we estimate 14–22, 4–6, 9–15, and 3–5 Sox2, Cdk9, Brd4, and Med22 detected molecules, respectively (Figures S5I and S5J). These results indicate focal accumulation of multiple Pol IIs at the gene body and multiple Sox2, Cdk9, Brd4, and Med22 molecules at the enhancer region. Target Locking Reveals Pol II Dynamics during the Pou5f1 Transcription Cycle Transcription inhibition with FVP or TRP results in gradual Pol II and nascent Pou5f1 RNA loss (Figures 5A and 5B; Video S5) but with slower kinetics than the CMV mini-gene, consistent with a longer transcription cycle (6.3 kb versus 3 kb gene length). Pol II decay measured optically agrees with kinetics measured by ChIP (Figures S6A and S6B). Without inhibitors, under steady-state Pou5f1 transcription, target-locked FRAP experiments show Pol II recovery (Figures 5C and 5D), with 330 s and 66% averaged recovery time and efficiency, respectively (Figure 5E). The recovery time agrees with an estimated 270–743 s or a more precise z338 s needed to complete one Pou5f1 transcription cycle (STAR Methods). Brd4 Controls Pol II Pause Release at Pou5f1 by ‘‘Seeding’’ Rather than ‘‘Scaffolding’’ Cdk9 Clustering The shorter Brd4 residence time (2 s) versus Cdk9 (7 s) at the Pou5f1 locus is inconsistent with on-off binding of binary Brd4:P-TEFb complexes or a canonical ‘‘scaffold-client’’ relationship and indicates distinct or partially overlapping recruitment and retention mechanisms (Figure S7D). Brd4 clustering at Pou5f1 is more sensitive (K = 28 nM; Figure S7B) to JQ1 than both Cdk9 clustering and nascent transcription amplitude (K = 97 nM and 63 nM; Figure 6E). Thus, Cdk9 clustering and Pou5f1 nascent transcription amplitude can be sustained with limited amounts of accumulated Brd4 (Figure S7C). Tuning total nuclear Brd4 levels by targeted degradation further corroborates these findings: consistent with the JQ1 titration results, nascent Pou5f1 transcription output quickly saturates after the onset of Brd4 clustering (Figures S7E and S7F). These observations do not favor a model where Brd4 clustering creates a ‘‘local trap,’’ as Cdk9 accumulation should have been highly sensitive to trap size, but rather hint toward a ‘‘seeding’’ mechanism. Brd4 is crucial for initial Cdk9 recruitment (Figures 6D and 6E), but retention of clustered Cdk9 at the transcription site likely involves additional interactions. Sox2 (Arter and Wegner, 2015) and Mediator (Donner et al., 2010) both interact with P-TEFb and cluster at Pou5f1. These results suggest that Brd4 initiates or seeds and TFs and/or co-activators further stabilize Cdk9 clustering. Interplay between p300 and/or CBP HAT Activity and Sox2 Clustering Maintains the Active Pou5f1 Enhancer State The p300 and CREB-binding protein (CBP) histone acetyltransferases (HATs) are essential for early development (Yao et al., 1998) and co-occupy mESC enhancers with Sox2, Oct4, and Nanog (Chen et al., 2008). To understand how p300 and/or CBP HAT activity relates to transcription, we use a potent and selective small-molecule inhibitor, A-485 (Lasko et al., 2017). At 1mM, A-485 Pou5f1 shuts down within 20 min (Figure S7G). The rapid kinetics indicates a primary A-485 effect and causal relationships between p300 and/or CBP HAT activity and Pou5f1

transcription. The decay time upon A-485 treatment is slower than JQ1, FVP, or TRP (635 s versus 340–380 s; Figure S7H versus Figure 5B). Although these other three inhibitors act within seconds, transcription shutdown by A-485 involves minute-long processes, consistent with histone acetylation turnover times (Katan-Khaykovich and Struhl, 2002; Weinert et al., 2018). Strikingly, although at the population level titrating A-485 results in gradual Pou5f1 transcription attenuation (Figure 6G), individual transcription sites exhibit all-or-none responses (Figures 6H and S7I). At intermediate [A-485], transcription either persists or completely shuts down, with [A-485] controlling shutdown probability. This is in stark contrast with Pou5f1 response to Brd4 inhibition, where at intermediate [JQ1] individual transcription sites show attenuated output without complete shutdown (Figures 6H and S7A). This all-or-none behavior could reflect positive feedback mechanisms, as in some ‘‘switch-like’’ biological responses (Thomas and D’Ari, 1990). Histone acetylation can modulate TF-chromatin association (Lee et al., 1993; Vettese-Dadey et al., 1996), providing an underlying biochemical basis. Consistent with this hypothesis, upon A-485 addition, Sox2 clusters at Pou5f1 gradually shrink (Figure 6I), with similar kinetics to transcription shutdown (12.5 versus 10.5 min). Brd4 clusters at Pou5f1 also gradually shrink (8 min; Figure S7J), with kinetics similar to H3K27ac loss (7 min; Figure S7K). Our results are consistent with a positive feedback mechanism that maintains and/or stabilizes the active Pou5f1 enhancer state, whereby Sox2/TFs recruit p300 and/or CBP (Chen et al., 2008; Nowling et al., 2000) and p300 and/or CBP HAT activity further reinforces Sox2/TF accumulation. Visualizing RF-nascent transcription interplay in single cells uncovers phenomena otherwise masked when ensemble averaging, providing detailed views of transcription mechanisms in vivo (Figure 6J). DISCUSSION Elucidating Transcription at Single Genes, In Situ Visualizing transcription at single-molecule and single-gene level in live cells addresses questions that could not be answered

[Page 14]

otherwise. First, imaging, tracking, and quantifying Pol II with single-molecule resolution reveals its spatial organization, its participation into functional assemblies, and its dynamics during the transcription cycle. Since early descriptions of mammalian nuclear ultra-structure (Monneron and Bernhard, 1969) and continuing to date, such questions have attracted great interest. The methods we establish pave the way for studying different genes, cell types, or growth conditions to deepen our understanding of the diversity of gene regulation mechanisms. Second, we discover that focal RF accumulation at the Pou5f1 and Nanog enhancers controls target promoter kinetics. Perturbing and modulating these high-local-concentration foci and monitoring effects on nascent transcription establishes the functional consequences of RF clustering. Cis-elements had been extensively analyzed at the DNA sequence level, e.g., number and/or type of TF binding sites. Probing how steady-state RF occupancies and on-off binding kinetics relate to nascent transcription dynamics provides a biophysical basis and unveils detailed, context-specific kinetic mechanisms (e.g., control of promoter amplitude, burst frequency) that are only accessible by single-gene, live-cell imaging. Third, our results expand our understanding of the interdependencies and intertwined activities of the multiple RFs at active gene loci. Traditional in vitro reconstitution portrays step-by-step assembly mechanisms (Orphanides et al., 1996). Live-cell experiments reveal highly dynamic interactions that argue against formation of stable complexes and ordered progression through well-defined stable and static intermediates (Hager et al., 2009; Liu and Tjian, 2018). Such data are often interpreted in probabilistic frameworks (Coulon et al., 2013; Hager et al., 2009; Misteli, 2007) whereby assembly of large complexes occurs through random interactions of individual components without strict hierarchy. Some nuclear bodies form via such stochastic self-assembly mechanisms (Dundr and Misteli, 2010), and weak and transient Pol II and RF interactions are proposed to drive formation of cooperative molecular assemblies (Hnisz et al., 2017; Liu and Tjian, 2018; Shin and Brangwynne, 2017), but the specific scaffold-client (Banani et al., 2016) relationships remain uncharacterized. The range of models proposed—from ordered to random—contrasts the lack of tools to visualize these phenomena in live cells. Probing assembly pathways and RF synergies and interdependencies, exemplified by the Sox2-Brd4-Cdk9-Pol II hierarchy, the seeding mechanism of P-TEFb recruitment by Brd4, and the positive Sox2-p300 and/or -CBP feedback we discovered at Pou5f1, are critical strides toward reconciling these conundrums. Fourth, we provide glimpses of functional genome organization at kilobase scales, shedding light onto active gene ‘‘microenvironments.’’ Nuclear compartments were associated with distinct genome regulation processes (Carmo-Fonseca, 2002), and how such membrane-less organelles form is a fascinating subject (Shin and Brangwynne, 2017). Although micrometer-sized nuclear structures containing 103–105 molecules have been amenable to light-microscopic investigations, nuclear organization at 10–100 nm scales, relative to transcription, has been hard to probe in live cells. At Pou5f1 and Nanog, we discovered 100 nm foci containing 10 Pol II and RF molecules stabilized by specific RF-chromatin interactions. These finite system size effects and spatial constraints of the chromatin scaffold provide

quantitative frameworks to synthesize statistical-mechanics and soft-matter physics concepts into models of transcription control (Hager et al., 2009; Hnisz et al., 2017; Misteli, 2007). Fixed-cell super-resolution fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) imaging revealed genome folding at tens of kilobase to megabase scales (Boettiger et al., 2016). Live-cell localization down to single fluorescent molecules bound at specific genomic sites will provide even higher resolution views at %1 kb levels, e.g., elucidating real-time promoter-enhancer interactions. The resolvable spatial separation between upstream-bound factors and 30 UTR-tagged nascent RNA suggests that real-time tracking of individual Pol II molecules moving along the gene through a complete transcription cycle (Wang et al., 2016) in live cells might be within reach. Future Developments of Target-Locking 3D STED Target-locking 3D STED can be further improved in speed, photon efficiency, background reduction, and photobleaching. Fast electro-optic beam deflectors, single-photon counting electronics, and real-time hardware-based control systems will extend temporal resolution to %100 ms, enabling locking onto fast-moving targets, like single freely diffusing molecules. Optimal localization algorithms (Balzarotti et al., 2017) and 3D interferometry (Wang et al., 2016) will enhance 3D target locking onto dim targets. Near-isotropic, tightly focused excitation and depletion profiles (Schmidt et al., 2008) will increase background suppression at reduced STED intensities. Triplet- or dark-state relaxation and time-gated detection can further reduce photo-bleaching and background. Spatially targeted fluorescence background suppression can be achieved by photo-physical and/or -chemical means beyond STED (e.g., photo-switching or -activation, expanding the usable fluorophores and the possible applications). Further Dissemination to Understand Complex Molecular Processes in Cells Our tools can broadly transform studies of molecular processes in live cells. The new capabilities allow probing stoichiometry, nanoscale organization, and dynamic molecular transactions in a variety of important pathways. We anticipate exciting adaptations of target-locking nanoscopy for real-time single-molecule detection in size-tunable volumes at specific intracellular activity sites (e.g., a single DNA lesion, single mRNAs and translating ribosomes, sub-cellular organelles, presynaptic active zones and signaling complexes and clusters on the membrane). These technological innovations henceforth enable single-molecule approaches to fulfill their unrealized potential to elucidate molecular mechanisms in vivo. METHOD DETAILS Detailed 3D single-molecule STED Optical Setup Microscope Optics The setup (Figure S1A) was built around an inverted optical microscope base (Olympus, IX-71) with a two-tier multi-port design to accommodate selectable confocal/STED and wide-field light paths. We used a silicone oil immersion objective lens (Olympus UPLSAPO60XS, 60 3, NA = 1.30). For fluorescence excitation we used two pulsed laser diodes operating at 490nm and 640nm (PicoQuant, LDH-P-C-485B and LDH-P-C-640B, controlled by Sepia 828-S 2 channel driver) and a 561nm CW solid-state laser (Cobolt, Jive 500). For STED we used a beam from a Titanium-sapphire oscillator (Coherent MIRA HP-D, pumped by an 18W 532nm Verdi G-18 laser or Spectra Physics Tsunami 3941D, pumped by a 10W 532nm Millennia Xs laser) operated in CW mode. The MIRA beam was additionally passed through an Electro-optic Modulator (Conoptics, model 350-80) that enabled fast modulation of laser intensity. The STED beam was split into two beams that were independently phase modulated using a vortex phase plate (RPC Photonics, VPP-1a) with a 0-2p ramp that created the STEDxy doughnut profile and a liquid-crystal spatial light modulator (Hamamatsu, LCOS-SLM X10468-02) programmed with a circular 0-p phase step that created the STEDz profile. The STEDxy and STEDz beams were combined with the excitation beams, passed through an achromatic quarter-waveplate (Edmund Optics) and delivered to the upper-tier right-side port of the microscope. Fluorescence emission collected by the objective lens was back-propagated though the same optical path and separated from the excitation and STED beams using dichroic mirrors (Chroma/Semrock). Different emission wavelengths were then separated using additional dichroics and band-pass emission filters and focused onto 3 separate avalanche photo-diode (APD) detectors (Pico-Quant, Tau SPAD and Perkin-Elmer, SPCM-AQRH). The physical size of the APD active area corresponded to 1 Airy disk unit. Alternatively, the fluorescence was separated using a quad-view device (Photometrics, QV2) and GFP, RFP and SiR were simultaneously imaged onto different quadrants of a back-illuminated EM-CCD detector (Andor iXon3 897). The CCD was operated either in Electron-Multiplication (EM) mode (running at 30fps) or in conventional CCD mode (at 10fps). For astigmatismbased axial localization, a weak cylindrical lens was inserted before the quad-view. Final magnification was 67nm/pixel. For inspecting cellular samples using wide-field epi-fluorescence, 4 laser beams (405nm and 642nm diodes, Thorlabs; 488nm, Coherent Sapphire HP 500; 561nm, Cobolt Jive 500) were combined and delivered through the back port of the IX-71 microscope using a multi-band dichroic mirror (Semrock). Fluorescence emission transmitted through the dichroic was imaged at the lower-tier left side-port of the microscope using appropriate relay optics and band-pass filters for BFP, GFP, RFP and SiR onto a back-illuminated EM-CCD (Andor Ixon 860). Selecting between wide-field and confocal/STED light paths was achieved by rotation of the IX-71 fluorescence cube turret. Control Electronics and Software The specimen holder was mounted on a direct drive, high-dynamics 3D nanopositioning stage equipped with capacitive sensors (Physik Instrumente, P-561.3DD), interfaced to a digital controller (Physik Instrumente E-710 or E-712). For 2D and 3D imaging, the built-in wave generator of the controller was programmed to raster-scan the specimen in a pre-defined trajectory over the stationary excitation and STED beams. The actual trajectory achieved was monitored using the stage capacitive sensors and the position data obtained were used to reconstruct the final images. A typical 2mm 3 2mm, 8-second scan consisted of 80 lines and 8000 points were sampled to obtain the actual trajectory of the stage (100 points/line). Individual fluorescence photons detected by the APDs were registered using time-tagging single-photon counting electronics (PicoHarp 300, PicoQuant) with 4psec temporal resolution. Instrument control, including communication with the nanopositioning stage controller and the photon-counting electronics was implemented in LabVIEW 2012 & 2014 (National Instruments). The wave generator ran under internal timing of the nanopositioning controller which served as the master clock for synchronization. Trigger pulses were sent to the photon-counting electronics to mark each raster line. Offline analysis and image reconstruction was performed in MATLAB 2010b (MathWorks). We extracted the arrival time of each photon from the PicoHarp 300 data and, using the markers from the wave generator, we binned the detected photons in 1ms intervals starting at the beginning of each line. We then performed a 2D interpolation of the xy stage position and corresponding photon counts at each time-bin to a regular 200 3 200 grid (10nm interval). The final image was then binned to a 40nm pixel size. For active feedback control experiments, we adopted a custom LabVIEW program, optimized for fast CCD acquisition, real-time image analysis and active microscope stabilization using nanopositioning equipment (Wang et al., 2016). The images obtained from the Ixon3 897 detector were analyzed on the fly by non-linear least-squares fitting of a 21 3 21 pixel ROI to a 2D Gaussian peak function implemented in LabVIEW. Axial localization was achieved either though the degree of defocus or based on astigmatism. The coordinates returned by the 2D Gaussian peak fit were passed onto a PID controller that calculated the desired position correction

Setup operation For imaging live cells at 37 C we built a stage incubator with a temperature-controlled brass insert that can accommodate chambered coverglass specimens. Single-molecule in vitro experiments Glass coverslips and slides were passivated with Poly-ethylene-glycol (PEG) based on our previous work (Pertsinidis et al., 2010), but using 4-arm instead of linear PEG (Wang et al., 2016). Coverslips and slides were cleaned in a 70:30 v:v H2SO4:H2O2 mixture, rinsed with Milli-Q water, cleaned in a 0.5M KOH solution, rinsed with Milli-Q water and then soaked in a 98:2 v:v acetone-(3-aminopropyl)triethoxysilane (Sigma 440140) solution for 10 min and washed with Milli-Q water. A 10% w/v solution of 4arm 10k PEG, Succininidyl carbonate (Laysan Bio, 4arm-PEG-SC10K) prepared at 4 C in 0.55M K2SO4 was applied for 90 min at RT. Coverslips also contained 1:100 mixture of biotin-PEG:PEG (mPEG-Biotin, MW 5,000, Laysan Bio MPEG-Biotin-5000) for immobilizing single molecules through biotin-streptavidin interactions. The high resistance to non-specific adsorption was critical for successful single-molecule experiments at up to 1mM concentration, especially using Atto647N, one of the best-performing fluorophores under STED, for which previous attempts using surfactant-passivated surfaces had failed (Hua et al., 2014). Flow cells were assembled with a PEG-coated glass slide that contained drilled holes and a PEG-coated glass coverslip, sandwiched with 100-mm-thick double-sticky tape. Channels were block with 10% w/v BSA for 10 min, incubated with 50ng/mL streptavidin for 10 min and rinsed with 1 3 PBS. 20-100pM Cy3/biotin-labeled 15nt target oligo (IDT, 50 Cy3-ACTCTCTTCCTCATC-30 Biotin-TEG, HPLC purified, 8nt matching to the probe underlined) was then bound to the surface for 10 min, followed by 1 3 PBS rinse. Depending on the concentration of the probe oligo, the imaging buffer also contained a variable concentration of NaCl (750, 500 and 100 mM NaCl for 300, 600 and 1000 nM probe respectively) in order to achieve 50:50 on-off binding equilibrium of the probe to the target. A 2mm 3 2mm region was first scanned in 8 s with 200mW 561nm excitation to localize immobile Cy3 molecules. Subsequently the nanopositioning stage was moved to place a selected Cy3 molecule at the zero of the STED beams. The STED beams were off for the first 20 s and on for the last 20 s. Time-tagged single photon detection events were simultaneously recorded from the Cy3 and Atto647N APDs. QUANTIFICATION AND STATISTICAL ANALYSIS Types of background noise in single-molecule detection The signal of a molecule at a predefined 3D coordinate is masked by intensity fluctuations due to an ultimately overwhelming number of n background molecules within the detection volume (Figure 1A). Two main types of intensity fluctuations are expected to dominate for different experimental systems and measurement regimes (Chen et al., 1999; Kask et al., 1999): shot noise and particle number fluctuations. Shot noise For a closed system, n remains constant during the measurement, and shot noise due to the stochastic nature of fluorescence emission dominates. For shot noise the intensity p I, given ffiffiffiffiffiffiffi by I = nε (ε: molecular brightness), follows a Poisson distribution, characterized by a variance dI2shot noise = I = nε. Thus SNR ε=n and single-molecule detection (SNR > 1) is in principle achievable at high molecular brightness (ε >> n). Particle number fluctuations A fundamentally different situation emerges in open systems, where n can fluctuate. In the simplest case of freely diffusing molecules pffiffiffi in a 3D solution, n is Poisson-distributed ðdn = n Þ, thus giving rise to intensity fluctuations dI2number fluctuations = nε2 . For ε>1, the intensity fluctuations exceed the Poisson limit ðFano number = dI2number =I = ε > 1Þ. Thus above a certain molecular brightness, and particularly under conditions relevant to single-molecule detection pffiffiffiffiffiffiffiffi (ε >>1), particle number fluctuations give rise to super-Poisson intensity noise. Importantly, for number fluctuations SNR 1=n, thus, irrespective of molecular brightness, single-molecule detection becomes impossible above a certain concentration (SNR < 1 for n >> 1).

Experimental determination of dominant noise type under single-molecule detection conditions Under low brightness conditions (0.5 < ε < 3), often used in fluorescence correlation spectroscopy, small deviations from Poisson were seen in the tails of photon-counting (intensity) histograms at 1-50nM molecules in solution (Chen et al., 1999). The small deviations from Poisson were deemed characteristic of number fluctuations. It was further intuitively proposed that the relative effect of number fluctuations diminishes at high concentrations, with the largest deviations from Poisson expected at increasing ε and decreasing n. However, the molecular concentration and brightness regimes where (Poisson) shot noise and (super-Poisson) number fluctuations respectively dominate, and how these relate to conditions relevant to single-molecule detection (ε >> 1), have not been fully characterized. To understand which noise type dominates under single-molecule detection conditions, we measure background level (mean of intensity) and noise (standard deviation of intensity) in solutions of Atto647N-labeled streptavidin, over a range of 15nM-1mM concentrations. Our results show that noise exceeds several-fold the Poisson limit (Figures 1B and S1D), while the Fano factor (variance/ mean) increases proportionally with excitation power (i.e., molecular brightness ε), independent of concentration (Figures S1B and S1C). Also, we characterize the background in live HeLa cells that stably express a SNAP-tag fusion of the Rpb9 Pol II sub-unit, labeled with SNAP-silicon-rhodamine (SiR). Background level and noise measurements over a range of SiR-Rpb9 levels reveal super-Poisson characteristics, reminiscent of number fluctuations of freely diffusing molecules in solution (Figure S1E). Hence, superPoisson number fluctuations hinder single-molecule detection at up to 1mM solution concentrations and can also dominate in certain cellular systems. Contributions of other sources of noise When measuring the fluorescence signal of a single molecule, additional noise sources can play a role: the intrinsic Poisson noise of the molecule’s fluorescence emission, the background from cellular autofluorescence, and noise sources dependent on parameters of the detector in use, such as (effective) Quantum Efficiency (QE), as well as readout and dark noise contributions. Here we provide a detailed account of the relative contributions of these various sources of noise: Poisson emission statistics Under the experimental conditions used in this paper, single-molecule brightness corresponds to 100-1000 photons per time-bin or camera exposure (e.g., Figures 1D, 1G, 2I–2K, S1F, S1G, S3G, S3H, S5B, and S5C). The intrinsic Poisson noise (10-30 photons) is a negligible contribution to the overall noise, providing in this ideal case SNR1:10-1:30. Cellular autofluorescence, laser scattering and other sources of background The total background in the SiR detection channel when imaging an unstained sample is merely 1% of the background level when imaging a fully SiR-stained sample. This shows that the combined effects of cellular autofluorescence, laser scattering, cross-talk from GFP/mNeonGreen fluorescence and detector dark counts are only minor contributions to the total noise under our experimental conditions. Detector QE and readout noise In our experiments we use a back-illuminated (EM)-CCD or a far-red-optimized APD, providing > 90% and 70% QE at 670nm (Cy5/ SiR emission) respectively. When operating the CCD in electron-multiplication mode, the effective QE is 45%, while the readout noise is negligible, while under conventional CCD operation, readout noise is 15 photo-electrons. For the (EM)-CCD, dark noise is 0.001 photo-electron/sec, and for the APD < 20 cps. Thus, there are only minor differences in the performance of the EM-CCD versus CCD versus APD detectors: %2-fold QE difference, contributing only %1.4-fold difference in noise, while readout/dark noise are similar to or less than the intrinsic Poisson noise of the fluorescence signal under our conditions. We note that none of these detector-related noise sources fundamentally prohibit single-molecule detection: limitations in the detector can be merely overcome by increasing molecular brightness with higher excitation intensity. This not the case for super-Poisson number fluctuations at high concentrations, which require radically different approaches to overcome. Thus there is a fundamental reason why QE and readout noise detector specifications are mostly irrelevant under our experimental conditions. At low brightness conditions when Poisson shot noise dominates, the detector specifications might be important. However, at the laser power used for all our single-molecule detection experiments, shot-noise is negligible and super-Poisson number fluctuations dominate. In fact, intensity traces in solutions of 10nM-1mM concentrations show that increasing laser power (and thus molecular brightness) has little effect on achievable SNR (Figures S1B and S1C). As detailed in the previous section on Types of background noise in single-molecule detection, this is a hallmark of super-Poisson number fluctuations. To further illustrate that number fluctuations dominate and detector noise specifications are mostly irrelevant under our experimental conditions, we split the fluorescence signal 50:50 between an APD and an (EM)-CCD. When we focus in the nucleus of a U-2 OS cell with SiR-Rpb1, we discover that both detectors exhibit the same noise magnitude and, moreover, show d-correlated intensity fluctuations (Figure S1F). This clearly indicates that number fluctuations (from the sample) are the dominant source of noise while detector noise is of secondary importance under our conditions. Promoter kinetics and transcription noise Certain multi-step kinetic processes that are characterized by finite wait times (‘‘memory’’) between individual events can exhibit reduced noise. Suppression of intrinsic noise is theoretically predicted in models of e.g., transcription, translation or RNA decay characterized by several small steps between individual birth or death events (Pedraza and Paulsson, 2008). Applied to transcription kinetics, if the promoter cycles between multiple (inactive) states, there is effectively a memory between productive initiation events: after one Pol II escapes into elongation, a new Pol II has to wait a certain time for the promoter cycle to be completed. The end result for such a simple kinetic scheme (Figure S6H) is that individual initiation events would be more regularly spaced in time, thus reducing the transcription noise. This feature is also recapitulated in numerical calculations based on this simple multi-step kinetic scheme for promoter activation (Figures S6I–S6K). Interestingly, if release of RNA and Pol II upon termination occurs in a single step, characterized with a release time constant that is comparable to the total elongation time, the autocorrelation functions of transcription traces of a Poisson promoter (n = 1) or promoters that cycle through multiple steps (n > 1) closely resemble each other (Figure S6J). When the auto-correlation functions are dominated by the RNA/Pol II release time, additional experimental parameters (e.g., mean and variance of RNA/Pol II numbers) allow discriminating between different promoters (n = 1 versus n > 1).
