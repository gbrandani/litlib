Nucleated transcriptional condensates amplify gene expression Ming-Tzo Wei1, Yi-Che Chang1,2, Shunsuke F. Shimobayashi1, Yongdae Shin1,3,4, Amy R. Strom1 and Clifford P. Brangwynne 1,5 ✉ Membraneless organelles or condensates form through liquid– liquid phase separation1–4, which is thought to underlie gene transcription through condensation of the large-scale nucleolus5–7 or in smaller assemblies known as transcriptional condensates8–11. Transcriptional condensates have been hypothesized to phase separate at particular genomic loci and locally promote the biomolecular interactions underlying gene expression. However, there have been few quantitative biophysical tests of this model in living cells, and phase separation has not yet been directly linked with dynamic transcriptional outputs12,13. Here, we apply an optogenetic approach to show that FET-family transcriptional regulators exhibit a strong tendency to phase separate within living cells, a process that can drive localized RNA transcription. We find that TAF15 has a unique charge distribution among the FET family members that enhances its interactions with the C-terminal domain of RNA polymerase II. Nascent C-terminal domain clusters at primed genomic loci lower the energetic barrier for nucleation of TAF15 condensates, which in turn further recruit RNA polymerase II to drive transcriptional output. These results suggest that positive feedback between interacting transcriptional components drives localized phase separation to amplify gene expression. Transcriptional condensates and other nuclear bodies are enriched in nucleic acid-binding proteins containing large intrinsically disordered proteins or intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs), which numerous studies have shown to promote phase separation14–18. The effective valence of IDR-rich proteins is often enhanced through binding to nucleic acids16,17, or through direct oligomerization19. Of particular interest are members of the FET protein family, which is comprised of the proteins FUS, EWS and TAF15. These proteins have roles in transcription as well as in pre-messenger RNA splicing, DNA repair and mRNA transport in neurons20. FUS, EWS and TAF15 all contain an N-terminal IDR enriched in uncharged polar amino acids and aromatic amino acids (Extended Data Fig. 1), an RNA-recognition motif and an arginine- and glycine-rich domain (Fig. 1a). Recent in vitro studies have begun to explore the roles of these amino acids in defining molecular interactions and specificity18,21–24, but whether and how sequence-encoded phase behaviour can influence functional transcriptional kinetics25,26 remains to be tested. To address the sequence-dependent transcriptional function of FET-family proteins, we took advantage of the recently developed optoDroplet system27, which uses blue-light-controlled oligomerization of the cryptochrome 2 (Cry2) protein to control IDR

oligomerization, and thus potentially drive phase separation. Using the IDRs rather than the full-length proteins of interest enables elucidation of the contribution of these protein regions within a tractable experimental platform. In agreement with earlier work, we found that blue light drives phase separation of an optoFUS construct into liquid-like droplets in both the cytoplasm and nucleoplasm of NIH 3T3 cells (Fig. 1b). Both nuclear and cytoplasmic localization occurs because these constructs include only the N-terminal IDRs, and thus exclude the nuclear localization signals located at the C terminus in the endogenous proteins (Fig. 1a,c). This dual localization enables us to compare phase behaviour, which is intrinsic to the IDR sequence in the cytoplasm, with regulated phase behaviour in the nucleus, including the role of post-translational modifications and the presence of endogenous nuclear interaction partners. Consistent with the physics of classical phase separation, when cells expressing optoFUS at different concentrations are illuminated with blue light, the cytoplasmic concentration (Ccyto) began to plateau at a saturation concentration (C*) of roughly C*Cyto = 2.4 ± 0.4 μM (Fig. 1d). The concentration outside of the optoIDR condensates was relatively insensitive to bulk concentration, suggesting that these IDRs drive phase separation primarily through homotypic interactions28. We observed similar behaviour with an optoEWS construct and an optoTAF15 construct, which consist of Cry2 fused to the IDR regions of EWS and TAF15, respectively (Fig. 1c,d). Interestingly, previous studies have reported that in HeLa cells, full-length FUS, EWS and TAF15 are at physiological concentrations of 1.4 μM, 0.2 μM and 0.05 μM, respectively18,29, indicating that the saturation concentration for each optoIDR construct is slightly higher than these reported physiological levels. However, the result does not imply that the full-length proteins cannot phase separate at the lower physiological concentrations. Indeed, other protein domains could make additional contributions, through increased effective interaction strength, as well as conferring additional valency through oligomerization, both of which can drive phase separation at lower protein concentrations30,31. Previous studies have highlighted the importance of aromatic residues, particularly tyrosine, in IDR-mediated phase separation18,21,32. To address the role of tyrosines, we mutated varying numbers of tyrosines to serines in the FUS IDR, resulting in optoFUS(5Y>S), optoFUS(9Y>S) and optoFUS(27Y>S). Consistent with previous studies, the cytoplasmic saturation concentrations of these constructs exhibit an inverse dependence on the number of tyrosine residues (Fig. 1e and Extended Data Fig. 2a,b). Interestingly, although the TAF15 IDR has a similar number of tyrosine residues (27) as the FUS IDR (27) and EWS IDR (29), the optoTAF15 construct has a weaker tendency to phase separate in the cytoplasm compared with optoFUS and optoEWS, with a significantly higher saturation concentration, C*Cyto = 8.0 ± 0.6 μM

(Fig. 1d). The TAF15 IDR has more charged amino acids than the other FET-protein IDRs (total number of charged residues: TAF15 IDR, 35; FUS IDR, 6; and EWS IDR, 7). To test the contribution of these charged amino acids to the phase behaviour of optoTAF15, we mutated optoTAF15 to reduce its charge by replacing arginine with

glutamine, and aspartic acid with serine, results in the construct optoTAF15(10R>Q/15D>S) (Extended Data Fig. 1) with a similar IDR charge distribution as the EWS IDR (Fig. 1c). The cytoplasmic saturation concentration (C*Cyto) of optoTAF15(10R>Q/15D>S) decreased significantly to 1.3 ± 0.2 μM, which is similar to the C*Cyto of optoEWS (1.0 ± 0.5 μM) (Fig. 1d). Thus, the large number of charged residues in the TAF15 IDR weaken its tendency to phase separate in the cytoplasm. However, a TAF15 IDR construct with fewer charge mutations, optoTAF15(10R>Q/10D>S), exhibited a slightly higher C*Cyto (9.5 ± 0.8 μM) than that of the wild-type optoTAF15, emphasizing the importance of the specific sequence patterning in IDRs, consistent with a growing body of experimental and computational work15,33,34. We next sought to understand the phase behaviour of TAF15 within the nucleus, where its endogenous regulation and interaction partners could influence its phase behaviour. We found that the saturation concentration for optoTAF15 in the nucleoplasm (C*Nuc = 2.6 ± 0.5 μM) is roughly threefold lower than in the cytoplasm (Fig. 1f); optoFUS, optoEWS and optoDDX4 each exhibits only moderate differences between the cytoplasmic and nucleoplasmic saturation concentration (Fig. 1f, inset and Extended Data Fig. 2a). Thus, nucleoplasmic optoTAF15 has an enhanced tendency to phase separate in the nucleoplasm compared to the cytoplasm. To examine the biophysical origins of this surprising phase behaviour of TAF15, we used fluorescence correlation spectroscopy to estimate the strength of molecular interactions. is fitted, where D is protein diffusivity, D0 is the diffusivity at infinite dilution and C is the protein concentration32,35. We note that these experiments are performed in the absence of global light activation, in order to measure the interaction strength of TAF15 IDR with itself, without input from light-dependent Cry2. We find that optoTAF15 diffusivity, in both the cytoplasm and the nucleoplasm, decreases with increasing protein concentration (Fig. 1g and Extended Data Fig. 3a,b), indicating attractive biomolecular interactions. However, optoTAF15 exhibits significantly stronger interactions in the nucleoplasm (βNuc) compared with the cytoplasm (βCyto) (that is, βNuc is more negative than βCyto). OptoFUS also exhibits slightly stronger interactions in the nucleoplasm, but the difference is smaller than for optoTAF15. OptoEWS and a control construct optoDDX4 both exhibit no significant difference between their cytoplasmic and nucleoplasmic interaction strengths (Fig. 1g, inset and Extended Data Fig. 2d). Our data reveals a general relation between the saturation concentration and the apparent interaction strength (in the absence of blue-light illumination), yielding a common intracellular phase boundary for our optoIDR constructs (Fig. 1h). These findings suggest that IDR-driven phase separation exhibits remarkably universal features, and that the enhanced tendency for optoTAF15 to phase separate in the nucleoplasm compared with the cytoplasm reflects its correspondingly stronger intermolecular interactions in the nucleoplasm. We next sought to understand the origin of the increased interaction strength and preferential condensation in the nucleoplasm of TAF15. TAF15 is a key transcriptional regulator and has been shown to interact with the C-terminal domain (CTD) of RNA polymerase II (Pol II) (Fig. 2a and Extended Data Fig. 1), which strongly localizes to the nucleus25,36,37. To examine the interaction between IDRs from the FET-family proteins and the CTD of Pol II, we co-expressed each optoIDR along with an enhanced green fluorescent protein (eGFP)-tagged CTD construct. We characterized the colocalization between CTD and optoTAF15 as a function of time after the onset of blue-light illumination, and found that optoTAF15

condensates steadily recruit CTD (Fig. 2b,c), ultimately achieving a nearly fourfold preferential partitioning over the nucleoplasm (Fig. 2d,e). OptoFUS condensates exhibited a twofold enrichment of CTD, but notably, optoEWS condensates showed no visible CTD recruitment. As expected, there was no preferential partitioning of eGFP–CTD to induced optoDDX4 condensates (Fig. 2d,e). The CTD of Pol II is known to undergo cycles of post-translational modifications to regulate transcriptional activity, including phosphorylation25, which alters charge-mediated interactions. Pol II interacts with transcription factors in its unmodified state and is released from its promoter-proximal stalled state upon being phosphorylated (Fig. 2a). To test whether phosphorylation affects interactions between the IDRs of FET-family proteins and Pol II CTD, we depleted ATP, a blunt method of inhibiting phosphorylation. Compared with untreated conditions, all three FET optoIDR condensates exhibited a significant increase in preferential partitioning of CTD in ATP-depleted conditions (Extended Data Fig. 3c,d). As expected, there was no preferential partitioning of CTD into induced optoDDX4 condensates. Together, these data suggest that each member of the FET family is capable of recruiting Pol II CTD, with TAF15 IDR being the most potent recruiter of CTD, followed by FUS IDR and EWS IDR; these interactions with Pol II CTD can be disrupted by phosphorylation, consistent with a previous study25. We next examined the contribution to CTD partitioning of the many charged residues distributed along the TAF15 IDR, notably arginine, which is thought to interact with tyrosine residues on CTD, and negatively charged aspartic acid, which electrostatically interacts with lysine on CTD37. Consistent with the importance of charge for CTD partitioning, in charge-reduced optoTAF15(10R>Q/10D>S) condensates, CTD partitioning is significantly diminished, from 4-fold to only 2.5-fold (Fig. 2d,e). The optoTAF15(10R>Q/10D>S) construct also has a weakened tendency to phase separate, as indicated by its higher saturation concentration in nucleoplasm (C*Nuc = 4.5 ± 0.5 μM; Fig. 2f). Of note, the ratio of C*Cyto to C*Nuc decreases as charged amino acids in optoTAF15 are mutated (Extended Data Fig. 2b). Together, these data on the ratio of C*Cyto and C*Nuc, as well as CTD partitioning, are consistent with a possible role for charge-mediated interactions between TAF15 and CTD contributing to the tendency of TAF15 to condense more readily in the nucleoplasm compared with the cytoplasm. Because ATP depletion affects all phosphorylation and might affect many functions throughout the nucleus, we sought to more specifically test whether the phosphorylation state of CTD influences the propensity of optoTAF15 to phase separate. Cyclin-dependent kinase 7 (CDK7) is a key kinase that phosphorylates CTD on serine 5, which triggers Pol II to move from transcriptional initiation to elongation by disrupting its interactions with initiation-associated binding partners38. We used the CDK7-inhibiting drug THZ1 and measured the cytoplasmic and nucleoplasmic saturation concentrations of optoTAF1539,40. We found that THZ1 treatment decreased the nucleoplasmic saturation concentration of optoTAF15 (from 2.6 ± 0.5 to 1.3 ± 0.2 μM; Fig. 2f and Extended Data Fig. 3e), suggesting stronger interaction of unphosphorylated CTD with optoTAF15 to promote its phase separation. Consistent with this interpretation, there was no significant change in the optoTAF15 saturation concentration in the cytoplasm, where CTD is absent (Fig. 2f). Moreover, the partition coefficient of CTD into nuclear optoIDR condensates strongly correlated with the ratio of nucleoplasmic saturation concentration over cytoplasmic saturation concentration (Fig. 2g). Thus, the degree of CTD phosphorylation modulates phase separation of transcription factor IDRs in the nucleus, and this effect correlates with the apparent interaction between CTD and the transcriptional IDRs. To examine whether condensation of optoTAF15 constructs exhibits a similar pattern, we performed blue-light-cycling experiments in which optoTAF15 condensates are induced to repeatedly phase separate, dissolve and then phase separate again. The optoTAF15 condensates reformed

in the same physical locations for 2–3 cycles, but these repetitively forming condensates were transient, and after several cycles their localization no longer correlated with the first cycle (Fig. 3a,b). This behaviour is in marked contrast to that of control optoDDX4 condensates, which appeared at locations where they were not found in previous cycles (Extended Data Fig. 4a). To test whether stabi-

[Page 5]

lizing endogenous Pol II clusters would alter optoTAF15 behaviour in cycling experiments, we treated cells with actinomycin D or α-amanitin, drugs that block transcription elongation, thereby locking Pol II at its promoter-proximal pausing stage43,44. Cells treated with actinomycin D or α-amanitin show repeated localization of optoTAF15 to the same locations in all subsequent light illumination cycles (Fig. 3c,d and Extended Data Fig. 4b). To quantify this repeated localization, we used Pearson’s correlation coefficient (PCC) to determine colocalization on frames of optoTAF15 condensates from successive activation cycles; untreated cells show correlation for 2–3 cycles, whereas cells treated with actinomycin D or α-amanitin show high correlation persisting through at least 8 cycles (Fig. 3e). This is consistent with optoTAF15 condensates nucleating at transient endogenous Pol II transcriptional clusters in untreated cells and nucleating at ‘locked’ clusters in drug-treated cells. To demonstrate that seeds with high concentration of CTD are sufficient to nucleate TAF15-rich condensates, we used an engineered system that stably localizes CTD to telomeres, by fusing the telomere associated protein TRF1 to eGFP–CTD (TRF1–CTD). As expected, TRF1–CTD appears as bright puncta, localized at telomeres. In light-cycling experiments, the optoTAF15 condensates are seeded at telomeres and always assemble and dissolve at the same locations (Fig. 3f,g and Extended Data Fig. 4c,d); unlike the dynamic endogenous Pol II clusters, these engineered CTD foci provide long-term stable nucleation sites for optoTAF15 (Extended Data Fig. 4e). Classical nucleation theory describes how localized seeds can decrease the energetic barrier to nucleation, and thus increase nucleation rate (or decrease the delay time until nucleation onset)45. To quantitatively test this prediction of nucleation kinetics, we measured the nucleation delay time (dt), and found that optoTAF15 at TRF1–CTD clusters nucleates faster than optoTAF15 condensates that form elsewhere in the nucleus (Fig. 3h,i). The delay time of optoTAF15 condensates formed in these cells away from the TRF1– CTD clusters (dt = 60 ± 9s ) is similar to that of condensates forming in non-TRF1–CTD-expressing cells (dt = 52 ± 13 s) (Fig. 3j). By contrast, the delay time distribution around TRF1–CTD clusters is shifted to lower times, dt = 32 ± 9 s. Moreover, consistent with classical nucleation theory, the delay time is inversely correlated to the concentration of TRF1–CTD clusters, with the brightest clusters nucleating optoTAF15 condensates in 25 ± 5 s (group I, Fig. 3k); the dimmest TRF1–CTD clusters nucleate optoTAF15 condensates in 41 ± 11 s (group III, Fig. 3k), approaching the long delay times measured for non-TRF1–CTD seeding. These results provide further quantitative evidence that clusters of Pol II can modulate and localize the kinetic nucleation of TAF15 condensation (Fig. 3l). To examine whether optoTAF15 condensates recruit endogenous proteins, we performed immunofluorescence on blue-light-activated cells expressing optoTAF15. We used a TAF15 antibody that recognizes an epitope that is not present in the optoTAF15 construct, to ensure that it binds only to the full-length endogenous protein. We found that endogenous TAF15 was strongly recruited into optoTAF15 condensates (Fig. 4). Moreover, using antibodies for markers of Cajal bodies (coilin) or paraspeckle (TDP43), we show that the optoTAF15 condensates do not colocalize with either of these nuclear bodies. To further study the recruitment of endogenous Pol II to optoTAF15 condensates, we performed antibody staining of three functional states of the Pol II CTD: (1) all CTD, (2) elongating CTD with phosphorylated Ser5 (CTD(pS5)), and (3) elongating CTD with phosphorylated Ser2 (CTD(pS2)). The results show that the Pol II CTD colocalizes into optoTAF15 condensates, whereas CTD(pS5) and CTD(pS2) encircle optoTAF15 condensates but are not enriched within them (Fig. 4). Additionally, BRD4—which regulates CDK9 phosphorylation of CTD Ser2—does not colocalize with optoTAF15 condensates. These colocalization results

(Fig. 4 and Extended Data Fig. 5a) indicate that optoTAF15 condensates recruit endogenous TAF15 as well as Pol II CTD, but not the elongating CTD(pS5) or CTD(pS2). Therefore, our optoTAF15 condensates appear to reflect transcription-initiation clusters, with the elongating species accumulating concentrically as they are released from initiation. TAF15 is a transcriptional activator, and we questioned whether condensation of optoTAF15 condensates and associated partition of additional Pol II would affect transcriptional activity. To test this, we used 5-ethynyl uridine (EU, a uridine analogue) to label nascent RNA transcripts and quantify their spatial location and abundance. In cells expressing optoTAF15, but kept in the dark, few nuclei exhibited detectable EU puncta; instead, the EU signal was distributed evenly throughout the nucleoplasm (Fig. 5a,b). By contrast, an enriched EU signal was detected within optoTAF15 condensates in blue-light-illuminated optoTAF15 cells. This enhanced EU signal is due to nascent transcription and is not an artefact of the optoIDR constructs, as no localized EU puncta were detected in cells treated with actinomycin D or α-amanitin to inhibit transcription elongation. Moreover, no puncta were observed in control blue-light-induced optoDDX4 condensates. OptoEWS and optoFUS showed only a weak EU signal, consistent with our measurements of their weaker CTD recruitment (Fig. 2e). Interestingly, this ability of optoTAF15 condensates to enhance local transcription was also observed in cells expressing TRF1–CTD (Extended Data Fig. 5b,c); although telomeres are generally considered transcriptionally quiescent, telomeric repeat-containing RNA (TERRA) is known to be transcribed at telomeres46, and this enhanced EU signal at telomeres could reflect local enhancement of TERRA transcription (Extended Data Fig. 5d,e). To further examine whether optoTAF15 condensates amplify gene expression, we co-expressed the optoTAF15 constructs along with the MS2–MCP live-cell transcription readout system47. Here, MS2 is not integrated in the genome but instead directly from the transfected plasmid, resulting in many copies of the MS2 transcriptional unit throughout the nucleus. Plasmids containing MCP–eGFP and 24 repeats of the MS2 stem-loop construct were introduced into cells by transient transfection, and the colocalization between MS2– MCP and optoTAF15 was monitored as a function of time from the onset of blue-light activation. Consistent with our findings that optoTAF15 nucleates at existing sites of localized Pol II CTD (Fig. 3), we found optoTAF15 condensates initially appearing at actively transcribing MS2–MCP puncta (Fig. 5c). Additionally, optoTAF15 condensates amplified MS2 gene expression by nearly twofold over 30 min (points 1 and 2 in Fig. 5d,e). Acting as a control, an MCP punctum without a localized optoTAF15 condensate maintained constant MCP levels over similar timeframes (point 3 in Fig. 5d,e). Moreover, optoTAF15 condensates were able to nucleate away from MCP puncta, but MCP did not accumulate into these condensates (point 4 in Fig. 5d,e). The time-dependent intensity of optoTAF15 condensates was strongly correlated with the MCP intensity at MS2 loci, whereas there was no correlation in MCP puncta without a localized optoTAF15 condensate or in optoTAF15 condensates away from MCP puncta (Fig. 5f,g). Thus, optoTAF15 condensates do not accumulate nascent transcripts from other sites, but instead specifically enhance transcriptional output when nucleated at an actively transcribing locus. Using super-resolution Zeiss Airyscan microscopy to further observe the colocalization between optoTAF15 condensate and MCP puncta, we find that the MCP signal surrounds the optoTAF15 condensates (Fig. 5h). These findings indicate that nascent transcripts localize immediately adjacent to the optoTAF15 condensate, consistent with our finding that CTD(pS5) and CTD(pS2) exhibit a similar pattern around optoTAF15 condensates (Fig. 4). Our findings reveal that transcriptional condensates form through the underlying driving forces of liquid–liquid phase separation, which are themselves modified by non-equilibrium biological activity. In the endogenous system, nucleation of nascent TAF15 condensates may be facilitated by interactions with DNA-bound factors such as TFIID, to which Pol II is also recruited through favourable interactions with its CTD, to further stabilize the condensate and drive transcription. The charge-based interactions of TAF15 with Pol II CTD are key to their regulation by phosphorylation, as CTD can be phosphorylated on Ser2 and Ser5, thereby releasing it from the condensate to perform its elongation activity, ultimately contributing to condensate destabilization. These dynamics of nucleated phase separation followed by phosphorylation and destabilization probably underlie previous reports of intermittent bursts of transcriptional activation26,48 followed by periods of quiescence (Fig. 5i). Such dynamics underscore how the equilibrium thermodynamic driving forces underlying phase separation are subjected

to highly regulated, non-equilibrium activity characteristic of living cells, which extensive future theoretical and experiment work will be required to fully understand49–52. An interesting apparent consequence of this non-equilibrium activity is our finding that elongating phosphorylated versions of Pol II CTD accumulate concentrically around optoTAF15 condensates (Fig. 4). This finding was corroborated by visualizing live transcriptional output with MS2–MCP system, which also appears as a concentric enrichment around optoTAF15 condensates (Fig. 5h). This concentric structure is reminiscent of nascent pre-ribosomal RNA sorting and processing in the multi-phase-separated nucleolus53, consistent with the nucleolus representing a ‘hypertrophied’ example of the nanoscale transcriptional amplification found throughout the nucleus. Indeed, while the scale of our optogenetically induced FET-family protein condensates is probably substantially larger than that of the endogenous analogues, we interpret these structures as being amplified versions of endogenous ‘transcription factories’ or ‘hubs’10,41,48, which may similarly release elongating polymerase and nascent RNA in a concentric fashion.

Methods

Mammalian cell culture. NIH 3T3 (ATCC, CRL-1658) and 293T Lenti-X (Takara Bio, 632180) cells were cultured in growth medium (DMEM, GIBCO) supplemented with 10% FBS (Atlanta Biological) and penicillin and streptomycin (GIBCO) at 37 °C with 5% CO2 in a humidified incubator. Plasmid construction. DNA fragments encoding human TAF15 IDR (IDR of TAF15, residues 1–208), human TRF1 (both from our previous study54), the CTD of the largest subunit of human RNA polymerase II (RPB1, Addgene 35175) and eGFP (Addgene 122147) were amplified by PCR using Phusion High-Fidelity DNA Polymerase (NEB). DNA fragments for the IDR of EWS (residues 47–266), FUS IDR(5Y>S), FUS IDR(9Y>S), FUS IDR(27Y>S), EWS IDR(5Y>S), DDX4 IDR(4F>S), DDX4 IDR(9F>A), TAF15 IDR(10R>Q/10D>S), TAF15 IDR(10R>Q/15D>S) were synthesized (Integrated DNA Technologies). Plasmids for OptoFUS containing IDR of human FUS (residues 1–214) and optoDDX4 containing IDR of DDX4 (residues 1–236) were generated in our previous study27 and are available through Addgene (101223 and 101225). The sequences of IDRs used in this study are shown in Extended Data Fig. 1. Additionally, MS2 stem-loops were subcloned from Addgene plasmid 31865 into a Tet-Off lentiviral vector (Diamond laboratory, UTSW Medical Center). The Tet-Off vector also contains the transactivator under a ubiquitin promoter. A plasmid expressing MCP–eGFP (Addgene plasmid 40649) was used to visualize MS2 stem-loops. The constructs for optoIDRs, TRF1–CTD and CTD were cloned into a pHR-based vector. For optoIDR constructs (IDR-fusion Cry2 plasmids), DNA fragments encoding the IDRs were inserted into the linearized pHR-mCh-Cry2WT (from our previous study27, Addgene 101221) cut with MluI-HF (NEB) and treated with Quick CIP (NEB). All DNA constructs were assembled using the In-Fusion HD cloning kit (Clonetech) following the manufacturer’s instruction. Assembled products were transformed into Stellar competent cells (Clontech), from which single colonies were picked, grown in LB growth medium supplemented with ampicillin overnight, and isolated (QIAprep spin miniprep kit, QIAGEN) following the manufacturer’s instructions. All cloning products were confirmed by Sanger sequencing (Genewiz). Construction of stable cell lines. To produce stable cell lines (to express optoIDRs, eGFP–CTD and MCP–eGFP), lentivirus was produced by transfecting the transfer plasmids, pCMV-dR8.91 and pMD2.G (9:8:1 mass ratio) into Lenti-X 293T cells grown to approximately 70% confluency in 6-well plates. A total of 3 μg plasmid and 9 μl of FuGENE HD Transfection Reagent (Promega) were delivered into each well following the manufacturer’s instructions. After 2 d, supernatant containing viral particles was harvested and filtered with a 0.45 μm filter (VWR). NIH 3T3 cells plated at ~30% confluency in the 12-well plates were infected by adding 1 ml of filtered viral supernatant directly to the cell medium. Virus-containing medium was replaced with fresh growth medium 24 h after infection. Transient transfection. Cells were grown to approximately 70% confluency in 35 mm dish before being transfected. Nine microlitres of FuGENE HD Transfection Reagent (Promega) was diluted with OPTI-MEM (GIBCO). Each dish received 150 μl transfection mixture containing a total of 2.5 μg MS2 plasmid DNA. Transiently transfected cells were typically imaged between 24–48 h after transfection. Sample preparation for live-cell imaging. Cells were plated on 35 mm glass-bottom dishes pre-coated with Poly-d-Lysine (MatTek) and grown typically overnight in normal growth medium to reach ~50% confluency. For transcription inhibition experiments, actinomycin D (Sigma-Aldrich) and α-amanitin (Sigma-Aldrich) were dissolved in DMSO at 0.5 mg ml−1 and 1 mg ml−1, respectively. Both inhibitors were further diluted in growth medium to a final concentration of 5 μg ml−1. Actinomycin D would be added to NIH 3T3 cells followed by a 2 h incubation before imaging and α-amanitin would be added for 24 h before imaging. For ATP depletion, 2 mM sodium azide and 10 mM deoxyglucose were dissolved in growth medium before being applied to NIH 3T3 cells followed by a 0.5 h incubation before imaging. For CDK7 inhibition experiments, THZ1 (Med Chem Express) was dissolved in DMSO at 1 mM and further diluted with growth medium to a final concentration of 1 μM before being applied to NIH 3T3 cells followed by a 1.5 h incubation before imaging. Immunofluorescent staining. NIH 3T3 cells expressing optoTAF15 were fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS pH 7.4 for 10 min at room temperature. Cells were washed twice with PBS and permeabilized with 0.25% Triton X-100 in PBS for 20 min. Non-specific epitopes were blocked for 30 min with blocking buffer (PBS, 0.1% Triton X-100, 10% normal goat serum (Vector Laboratories)). Primary immunostaining was performed with the following antibodies overnight at 4 °C in blocking buffer: anti-TAF15 (rabbit monoclonal, AbCam, ab134916, 1:1,000), anti-CTD (mouse monoclonal, AbCam, ab817, 1:1,000), anti-CTD pS5 (mouse monoclonal, AbCam, ab5408, 1:1,000), anti-CTD(pS2) (rabbit monoclonal, AbCam, ab193468, 1:1,000), anti-BRD4 (rabbit monoclonal, AbCam, ab128874, 1:1,000), anti-coilin (rabbit polyclonal, Santa Cruz, sc32860, 1:1,000) and anti-TDP43 (rabbit polyclonal, ProteinTech, 10782-2-AP, 1:100). The antibodies Nature Cell Biology | www.nature.com/naturecellbiology

were validated for use in immunofluorescence to recognize endogenous CTD, transcription factors and others nuclear bodies. Cells were then washed three times with a blocking buffer. Secondary immunostaining was performed with the following antibodies from Invitrogen in a blocking buffer at room temperature for 2 h: Alexa Fluor 488 goat anti-rabbit (AbCam, ab150077, 1:1,000), Alexa Fluor 488 goat anti-mouse (AbCam, ab150113,1:1,000). Cells were washed three times with a blocking buffer. Finally, a blocking buffer was removed and replaced with PBS before imaging. Nascent RNA transcripts labelling of cultured cells. To label nascent RNA transcripts in live cells, Click-iT RNA Alexa Fluor 488 Imaging Kit (ThermoFisher) was used following the protocol from the manufacturer with Alexa Fluor 647 azide from Click-iT EdU Alexa Fluor 647 Imaging Kit (ThermoFisher). Cells seeded in a 35 mm glass-bottom dish were first illuminated with blue light for 15 min before EU was added and incubated for 15 min under blue-light illumination. Eight microlitres of 100 mM EU stock solution was added to each dish containing 2 ml growth medium to obtain a final concentration of 0.4 μΜ. After EU labelling, cells were first washed with PBS, and then fixed and permeabilized using 3.7% formaldehyde and 0.5% Triton X-100 in PBS, respectively, for 15 min each at room temperature, and washed again with PBS. Next, Alexa Fluor 647 conjugation was carried out by adding 500 μl of Click-iT reaction cocktail containing Alexa Fluor 647 azide to each dish; samples were then incubated for 30 min at room temperature without blue light before being washed with Click-iT reaction rinse buffer and PBS. Finally, DNA was stained with Hoechst 33342 (10 μg ml−1 in PBS) for 15 min at room temperature and samples were washed twice with PBS prior to imaging. RNA fluorescence in situ hybridization. RNA fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) experiments were performed following protocols from Biosearch Technologies with some modifications. Cells were incubated at 37 °C for 30 min with or without blue-light illumination and fixed with 4% paraformaldehyde in PBS at room temperature for 10 min. Cells were then permeabilized with 70% ethanol at room temperature for 1 h and pre-equilibrated with buffer A (0.2× Stellaris RNA FISH Wash Buffer A and 10% formamide in PBS) at room temperature for 5 min. To probe TERRA RNA, hybridization was carried out using 0.125 μM DNA probe with the sequence 5′-TAACCCTAACCCTAACCCT AACCCTAACCCTAACCCTAACCC-/3Cy5Sp/-3′, where (/3Cy5Sp/ indicates 3′-end Cy5 modification) in Stellaris RNA FISH Hybridization Buffer with 10% formamide in a humidified chamber incubated at 37 °C overnight. Samples were then washed twice with buffer A at 37 °C for 30 min; 5 μg ml−1 DAPI was included during the second wash. Cells were then washed with Stellaris RNA FISH Wash Buffer B at room temperature for 5 min. Finally, anti-fade reagent Vectashield was added and the samples were covered with a cover-glass and sealed with nail polish. Samples were stored in 4 °C for 1 d before imaging. Airyscan microscopy. The super-resolution fluorescence images in Figs. 4 and 5h were taken with a 63x oil-immersion objective (Plan Apo 63×/1.4, Zeiss) on a Zeiss Airyscan LSM 980 microscope. Imaging was performed using Zeiss Zen 3.0 software. An imaging incubator was maintained at 37 °C with 5% CO2. Laser scanning confocal microscopy. All the other fluorescence images were taken using a 60x oil-immersion objective (Plan Apo 60×/1.4, Nikon) on a Nikon A1 laser scanning confocal microscope. Images were performed using the NIS-Elements Version 4.40. An imaging chamber (Okolab) was maintained at 37 °C with 5% CO2. For activation, cells were usually imaged with 440-nm laser with a dichroic filter for the 488 nm laser in order to attenuate the blue laser intensity at the specimen plane below 0.4 μW. For studying light-activated phase behaviour, the activation protocol was performed with a 440 nm laser every 1 s and fluorescent image of mCherry was acquired with a 568 nm laser every 10 s, with pin hole size set to 33.2 μm, frame size to 512 × 512 pixels and dwell time to 2.2 μs. For each cycle of a multi-cycle activation, the above activation protocol was applied for 1 min, followed by a 9 min deactivation (with acquisition of mCherry fluorescent image every 10 s); this cycle was repeated 8 times. For delay time of initial nuclearization measurement, activation protocols were performed with the 440 nm laser every 10 s and with the 568 nm laser every 1 s to observe the mCherry channel with pin hole size set to 33.2 μm, 512 × 512 pixel frame sizes and 2.2 μs dwell time. To observe multi-channel image, 667-nm, 568-nm, 488-nm, and 405-nm lasers were used to excite Alexa Fluor 647, Alexa Fluor 488, mCherry, eGFP and DAPI (or Hoechst 33342) in cells, respectively. Fluorescence correlation spectroscopy. We used a laser scanning confocal microscope (Nikon A1 with a Plan Apo 60×/1.4 oil-immersion objective (Nikon)) combined with Fluorescence Correlation Spectroscopy (FCS) Upgrade Kit for Laser Scanning Microscopes (PicoQuant). FCS measurements were performed using the SymPhoTime Software (PicoQuant). Data for diffusion and concentration of proteins were obtained with 30 s of FCS measurement time. For each measurement, the resulting autocorrelation functions were fitted with the pure diffusion model. The measurement volume was determined using fluorophore

Here, in living cells, the refractive index mismatch that can distort the FCS measurement volume as a non-Gaussian profile as well as other optical artefacts would lead to an absolute error of ~20% in diffusion coefficient and concentration56. The dependence of the diffusivity can be written as in equation (1). The measurements show the diffusion as a function of protein concentration, which is lower than saturation concentration. Here we considered the thermodynamics of binary Flory–Huggins theory, in which the strength of molecular interactions does not change between molecules inside or outside of the condensate. β can be written as (2B2M − ks − υ) × Mc, where M is the molecular weight, B2 is the second virial coefficient, ks is the sedimentation interaction parameter, υ is the partial specific volume and c is protein weight concentration35. To compare the contributions from IDR and Cry2, we systematically analysed the molecular interactions with the different constructs (that is, mCherry, mCherry-tagged Cry2, mCherry-tagged FUS IDR, optoFUS and optoFUSx2) in 3T3 fibroblasts. Here, optoFUSx2 is a construct consisting of two repeat FUS IDRs fused to mCherry (mCh) and Cry2. The results showed that in the absence of blue-light illumination, the attractive forces are mainly contributed by the IDR (Extended Data Fig. 3a,b). Correlation analysis for the MS2–MCP experiment. An ROI that contains an optoTAF15 condensate, an MCP focus or both was used as the basic unit for the following analyses. The ROIs were identified and tracked using ImageJ, and the maximal fluorescence intensity values (for both channels) within each ROI at each time point were recorded and further binned every three frames. These ROIs were characterized as three classes on the basis of whether they contained an optoTAF15 condensate and/or MCP focus in the first three frames. To visualize the relationship between signals of MCP–eGFP and optoTAF15 for each ROI, scatter plots were generated with a pair of fluorescence intensities from both channels. To aggregate movies for ROIs of the same class, the following transformations were made within each ROI dataset: the fluorescence intensity was mean-subtracted and divided by the standard deviation; and the time in seconds was linearly rescaled to the range from 0 to 1. To further quantify the correlation between fluorescence intensity changes from the two channels, the first-order derivatives of fluorescence intensity (as a function of time) were used for the following analysis. For each ROI, the PCC was calculated for the pairs of the resulting derivatives from the two channels; +1 indicates perfect correlation, 0 indicates no correlation, and −1 indicates

perfect anticorrelation. Welch’s t-tests were then performed to test if the means of correlation coefficient from different classes of ROI are equal. Spatial analysis for the MS2–MCP experiment with AiryScan microscopy. Images of each ROI were first manually cropped and then analysed with custom Python scripts (https://github.com/icheo2l5/optoIDR-MCP) using the standard library as well as other open-source libraries including NumPy and scikit-image. Condensates in the images were detected using a blob detector based on the Laplacian of the Gaussian. Each detected blob was then fit to a circular Gaussian function, and the full width at half maximum (FWHM) was calculated for the next step. Each condensate was cropped out as a square with sides of length 2 × FWHM and a centre overlapping the centre of the condensate. The resulting images were then downsampled and resized to the dimension of the smallest image before being averaged. Statistics and reproducibility. All statistical procedures performed are indicated in the figure captions. P values are calculated using Fisher’s exact tests.
