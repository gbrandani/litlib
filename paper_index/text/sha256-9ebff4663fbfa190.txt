Mechanism of DNA translocation underlying chromatin remodelling by Snf2 Meijing Li1,2,3,4,6, Xian Xia1,2,4,6, Yuanyuan Tian1,2,3,4,6, Qi Jia5,6, Xiaoyu Liu1,2,3,4,6, Ying Lu5, Ming Li5*, Xueming Li1,2,3,4* & Zhucheng Chen1,2,3,4*

Chromatin remodellers include diverse enzymes with distinct biological functions, but nucleosome-sliding activity appears to be a common theme1,2. Among the remodelling enzymes, Snf2 serves as the prototype to study the action of this protein family. Snf2 and related enzymes share two conserved RecA-like lobes3, which by themselves are able to couple ATP hydrolysis to chromatin remodelling. The mechanism by which these enzymes couple ATP hydrolysis to translocate the nucleosome along the DNA remains unclear2,4–8. Here we report the structures of Saccharomyces cerevisiae Snf2 bound to the nucleosome in the presence of ADP and ADP-BeFx. Snf2 in the ADP-bound state adopts an open conformation similar to that in the apo state, and induces a one-base-pair DNA bulge at superhelix location 2 (SHL2), with the tracking strand showing greater distortion than the guide strand. The DNA distortion propagates to the proximal end, leading to staggered translocation of the two strands. The binding of ADP-BeFx triggers a closed conformation of the enzyme, resetting the nucleosome to a relaxed state. Snf2 shows altered interactions with the DNA in different nucleotide states, providing the structural basis for DNA translocation. Together, our findings suggest a fundamental mechanism for the DNA translocation that underlies chromatin remodelling. To uncover the remodelling reaction, we determined the cryoelectron microscopy (cryo-EM) structures of the Snf2–nucleosome complex in the presence of ADP or ADP-BeFx. The motor domain of Snf2 from S. cerevisiae was used and nucleosome core particles (NCPs) were assembled with the ‘601’ sequence (167-NCP) as described previously9. As in the apo state9, Snf2 binds the nucleosome at the SHL2 or SHL6 sites in the ADP-bound state (Fig. 1a, Extended Data Figs. 1 and 2, and Extended Data Table 1). The ADP-bound SHL2 complex was achieved at an overall resolution of 3.62 Å. SHL2 is well established as the strategy site for Snf2 binding and chromatin remodelling10,11, and is discussed in detail below. The biological relevance of the SHL6 complex is not clear, and it was not observed on mouse mammary tumour virus (MMTV) DNA (see below). Snf2 binds the 167-NCP equally well at SHL2 and SHL−2 sites in the presence of ADP-BeFx, yielding maps at resolutions of 3.91 Å and 3.64 Å, respectively (Fig. 1b and Extended Data Fig. 3). The two complexes are basically identical (Extended Data Fig. 4c), and we use the SHL2 complex for the detailed discussions below. The maps of the ADP-bound and ADP-BeFx-bound complexes clearly resolve the individual phosphate groups of the DNA except those near the entry side (Extended Data Figs. 2, 4 and Supplementary Video 1). Most of the bases, which are under geometry restraints during model refinement, fit reasonably well into the maps, with the detailed twisting and tilting not fully resolved. The backbone phosphate groups are the key for binding to Snf2 and the histones9,12, central to chromatin remodelling, and hence the main focus of this study. Comparison with the unbound nucleosome13 indicates that both the 5′- and 3′- DNA strands are distorted by Snf2 in the ADP-bound state

(Fig. 2a, b). The DNA distortion can be analysed in a tripartite manner. At SHL1.5 and further inside (from positions ±60 to the distal end), the phosphate groups do not show notable displacement, serving as reference points to determine the register of the rest of the molecule. At SHL2, where the distortion is initiated owing to Snf2 binding, the phosphate groups at positions 56, 54 and 52 of the 5′-strand bulge out from the normal path on the surface of the free nucleosome, moving towards the dyad with increasing displacements of 2 Å, 4 Å and 7 Å, respectively. The DNA from SHL5 to SHL2.5 (positions 20–50) does not interact with Snf2. Yet, the DNA distortion initiated at SHL2 propagates towards the proximal end because of the fluidity of the phosphate backbone5,14. As a consequence, the phosphate groups at positions 50 (SHL2.5), 40 (SHL3.5), 30 (SHL4.5) and 20 (SHL5.5) of the 5′-strand slide in one-nucleotide register (approximately 7 Å) and are translocated to positions 51, 41, 31 and 21 of the free nucleosome, respectively (Fig. 2c–f). Thus, the bulging out of one nucleotide at SHL2 leads to long-range corkscrew-like DNA translocation of the 5′-strand from the entry side. The DNA translocation induced by Snf2 is consistent with the notion that DNA is pulled in from the proximal end rather than from the distal end2,6. To validate the direction of DNA pulling, we compared the nucleosome electron microscopy density with the models that have the 5′-strand pulled from the proximal or distal ends. The data showed that the former resulted in a better fitting (Extended Data Fig. 2h), supporting DNA pulling from the proximal end. As a result of the DNA translocation, the DNA shows altered interactions with the histone core (Fig. 2c–f). Arg72 of histone H3 binds to the phosphate group at position 51 of the nucleosome in the absence of Snf2, whereas it contacts the phosphate group at position 50 within the ADP-bound complex. Analogously, the histones at SHL3.5, SHL4.5 and SHL5.5 remain interacting with the 5′-strand DNA, but with an altered register of the phosphate groups. Notably, the 3′-DNA strand shows a less marked movement, maintaining base pairing and stacking with the 5′-strand (Extended Data Fig. 2a). The phosphate backbone of the 3′-DNA from SHL5 to SHL2.5 shows little change and keeps its register. The larger translocation of the 5′-strand than the 3′-strand suggests base twisting, in line with the idea of the ‘twisting diffusion’ model of chromatin remodelling6,7,15. Whereas localized out-of-sync DNA translocation has been observed previously16, the long-range twisted translocation from the entry to SHL2 induced by Snf2 was unexpected. To validate the staggered DNA translocations, we developed single-molecule fluorescence resonance energy transfer (smFRET) assays (Fig. 3a, b and Extended Data Fig. 5a, b). The phosphate backbone of the nucleosome was labelled at position 33 of the tracking strand with Cy5 dye, and a mutated histone protein (H2A(R71C)) was labelled with Cy3. Translocation of the tracking strand by one nucleotide register towards SHL2 is expected to increase the Cy3–Cy5 distance by approximately 6 Å, and hence decrease the FRET value. The strandspecific DNA translocation induced by Snf2 is also consistent with a recent cross-linking study15. It is also supported by the related study of ISW1–nucleosome complexes19, which achieved even higher resolutions. To test whether the DNA translocation is specific to the ‘601’ sequence, we collected data using a nucleosome with a different DNA fragment (MMTV-A)20. Snf2 in the ADP-bound state binds to the MMTV-A nucleosome at SHL2 and SHL−2, with the structures determined at resolutions of 3.47 Å and 3.67 Å, respectively (Extended Data Figs. 6 and 7). The SHL2 and SHL−2 complexes adopt essentially identical structures (Extended Data Fig. 7d). The SHL−2 complex was not found with the ‘601’ nucleosome in the presence of ADP. This may be due to the asymmetric nature of the ‘601’ nucleosome21. The map of the MMTV-A nucleosome complex resolves the positions of the DNA phosphate groups (Extended Data Fig. 7b). The bases at SHL2, which remain paired and stacked together, are under-twisted to various degrees owing to the sliding in of 1-base-pair (1-bp) of DNA (Extended Data Fig. 7e). Notably, the phosphate backbone of the MMTV-A nucleosome was induced to adopt the same type of DNA conformation as that found with the ‘601’ nucleosome bound by Snf2 (Extended Data Fig. 7f). Snf2 adopts the same structure in the ADP-bound and apo states (Extended Data Fig. 8d), thus prompting re-examination of the previous data. With a new image processing protocol, we obtained a map of the apo complex at a higher resolution of 4.31 Å (Extended Data Fig. 8a). Using the structure of the ADP complex as the initial model, the improved electron microscopy map of the apo complex supported the 1-bp DNA movement towards SHL2. By contrast, lobe 2 shows a rotation of around 13° towards the dyad of the nucleosome after ADP-BeFx binding. The importance of the small perturbation of the DNA structure in the ADP-BeFx-bound state is not clear, and we could not exclude the possibility that it might represent part of the reaction cycle for driving DNA from the entry to the exit side of the nucleosome. The domain rotation of lobe 2 alters its binding register relative to the tracking strand, and allows the 5′-strand to slide in upon ATP hydrolysis and phosphate release (Fig. 4b and Extended Data Fig. 9d, e). Specifically, α13—which is inserted at the SHL2–SHL2.5 junction and packs against the 5′-tracking strand (Extended Data Fig. 9f)—moves 4 Å away from the DNA upon Snf2 opening, allowing the phosphate group at position 52 to slide in 7 Å, and binding to Lys1057 in the ADP-bound state. The guide strand makes fewer interactions with lobe 2, displaying a smaller movement upon the domain rotation. Therefore, it is the distinct interactions with lobe 2 that cause the tracking strand to be repositioned and the guide strand to remain relatively unchanged upon Snf2 opening. To explore the importance of the interaction between lobe 2 and the tracking strand, we introduced α13 mutations. The N1050G/M1053G mutations did not interfere notably with ATP hydrolysis, but severely reduced the remodelling activities (Fig. 4c, d). Similarly, the K1057A mutant also showed a markedly reduced remodelling activity. These data support a key role of α13 in chromatin remodelling, particularly in coupling ATP hydrolysis to DNA translocation. Together, our work indicates that Snf2 in the ADP-bound and apo states adopts similar open conformations, inducing a 1-bp bulge at SHL2 and long-range DNA translocation of the tracking strand from the entry to SHL2 but less perturbation of the guide strand. ADP-BeFx binding triggers a closed conformation, which does not markedly perturb the structure of the nucleosome. Together with previous findings10,11,17,18,25, our analyses suggest a two-step translocation model, and provide a mechanism for the nucleosome sliding mediated by Snf2 (Fig. 4e, f and Supplementary Video 2). The opening of the primary DNA-binding cleft of Snf2 after ATP hydrolysis and phosphate release, which leads to the sliding in of the

DNA from the entry side to SHL2, is only the first part of the remodelling cycle. During this transition step, lobe 2 moves away from the dyad of the nucleosome, whereas the 5′-strand is accordingly drawn in (Fig. 4e, orange arrows). As a result, lobe 2 shifts its DNA-binding register, with a 1-bp DNA bulge at SHL2. ATP binding triggers a closed conformation, which constitutes the second part of the conformational cycle and completes the 1-bp translocation to the exit side (Fig. 4f, blue arrows). During this transition step, lobe 2 tightly grips the 5′-tracking strand and pushes the DNA distortion forward. The translocation of the tracking strand at SHL2 by one nucleotide register leads to the conjugated movement of the guide strand, resetting the nucleosome to the largely unstrained state. The tracking strand translocation at the first step was well detected by cryo-EM and smFRET, but the movement of the guide strand at the second step was not directly observed. To consolidate our model, we detected the guide strand translocation using smFRET. The nucleosomes with guide strand labelled at position −30 were first loaded with Snf2 in the apo state, and then ADP-BeFx was infused into the system. Our model suggests little perturbation of the guide strand after Snf2 binding, but DNA translocation after Snf2 closing. Specifically, translocation induced by Snf2 at SHL2 is expected to decrease the FRET value upon Snf2 closing (Fig. 3b and Extended Data Fig. 5b), whereas propagation of the DNA distortion induced by Snf2 at SHL−2 is expected to move the Cy3–Cy5 pair closer, increasing the FRET value. Consistent with our model, no notable FRET change was detected after the addition Snf2. Notably, a decrease of the FRET value was found after charging Snf2 with ADP-BeFx, supporting the notion of guide strand translocation after Snf2 closing (Fig. 3i, left, and Extended Data Fig. 5n). In some cases, no FRET change was followed by an increase of the FRET value (Fig. 3i, middle, and Extended Data Fig. 5p), which is consistent with DNA translocation by Snf2 at SHL−2. As expected from our model, the probe placed at position 33 of the tracking strand was translocated by Snf2 at SHL2 in the apo state, but there was no further movement after ADP-BeFx binding (Extended Data Fig. 5m). Thus, the structural and smFRET analyses indicate that the trackingstrand DNA from the entry to SHL2 is translocated after ATP hydrolysis and Snf2 opening (Fig. 4e), and the guide-strand catches up with the movement after ATP recharging and Snf2 closing (Fig. 4f). The DNA strands from SHL1.5 to the exit move synchronously, showing little change after Snf2 opening but translocating 1 bp after Snf2 closing. The complementary movements in a complete ATPase cycle maintain the structural integrity of the nucleosome while altering its position. These findings suggest a simplified remodelling cycle (Extended Data Fig. 10). Our model of DNA translocation suggests rationales for directional DNA movement. Because of enzyme binding at SHL2, the longer path and the tighter histone–DNA interactions at the dyad position would disfavour pulling in of the DNA from the distal end after ATP hydrolysis26. Instead, lobe 2 releases the 5′-tracking strand and permits the DNA sliding in from the entry side (Fig. 4e). After ATP binding and Snf2 closing, lobe 2 grips the tracking strand to deliver the DNA distortion to the exit side (Fig. 4f). In particular, α13 pushes behind the tracking strand and prevents the DNA from sliding back. In line with this idea, α13 is found to have a specific role in coupling ATP hydrolysis to chromatin remodelling. We term it the ‘gating helix’. The gating helix opens the gateway for the DNA to enter SHL2 after Snf2 opening; it closes the gateway to ensure that the DNA distortion propagates towards the exit side after Snf2 closing. A ‘DNA loop/wave’ model6,11 and a ‘twist diffusion’ model7,15 have been proposed to explain the propagation of DNA distortion along the histone surface. Our model provides an answer to the central question of these models, in that the DNA wave or twist is first induced at SHL2 by Snf2 in the ADP-bound state. The findings that Snf2 in the ADPbound state induces DNA translocation without losing the bulk of the DNA–histone contacts probably provide a solution to the previous

concern of disruption of the many histone–DNA contacts along the way from the entry to SHL2 of the nucleosome7,27,28. The energy cost of bases twisting associated with the DNA translocation is unknown, and more studies are needed. The DNA-binding elements of Snf2 are highly conserved among the chromatin remodellers, such as the Chd1 and ISWI subfamilies in particular19,24, suggesting that the DNA-translocation model we illustrated may be a general mechanism used by these enzymes.

Methods

No statistical methods were used to predetermine sample size. The experiments were not randomized, and investigators were not blinded to allocation during experiments and outcome assessment. Protein expression and purification. The 167-bp ‘601’ nucleosomes and yeast Snf2 (residues 666–1400) were prepared as described previously9. The MMTV-A nucleosomes were constituted with the DNA fragment CCACCAAC GTGCGTGGAACCATCTGCAACAGTCCTAACATTCACCTCTTGTGTGTTT GTGTCTGTTCGCCATCCCGTCTCCGCTCGTCACTTATCCTTCACTTTCC AGAGGGTCCCCCCGCAGACCCCGGCGACCCTCAGGTCGGCCGACTGCG GCACAGTTTTGAT (the MMTV-A sequence is underlined). Sample preparation and electron microscopy data collection. The yeast Snf2 (666–1400)–NCP complexes were obtained by mixing 20 μM protein with 5 μM NCP-167 in different cofactor conditions. We purified and stabilized the complex using the GraFix method similarly as described before9. To prepare the samples in the ADP-bound state, the top solution contained 50 mM NaCl, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.0, 1 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM ADP, 0.01% glutaraldehyde (Polysciences) and 10% glycerol (Sigma). The bottom solution contained 50 mM NaCl, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.0, 1 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM ADP, 0.15% glutaraldehyde (Polysciences) and 30% glycerol (Sigma). After ultracentrifugation, the best fraction was dialysed to 50 mM NaCl, 10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 2 mM MgCl2, 1 mM ADP and 3 mM dithiothreitol (DTT). For the samples in the ADP-BeFx-bound state, the top solution was 50 mM NaCl, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.0, 1 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM ADP, 8 mM NaF, 1 mM BeSO4, 0.01% glutaraldehyde (Polysciences) and 10% glycerol (Sigma). The bottom solution was 50 mM NaCl, 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.0, 1 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM ADP, 8 mM NaF, 1 mM BeSO4, 0.15% glutaraldehyde (Polysciences) and 30% glycerol (Sigma). After ultracentrifugation, the best fraction was dialysed to 50 mM NaCl, 10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 2 mM MgCl2, 1 mM ADP, 16 mM NaF, 2 mM BeSO4 and 3 mM DTT. Then the samples were concentrated for electron microscopy analyses. Collection of cryo-EM data was carried out as previously described9. UCSFImage4 and eTas (developed by B. Shen at Xueming Li’s laboratory) was used to acquire images automatically29. The micrographs of the Snf2–NCP complex in distinct states were taken with a Titan Krios microscope (FEI company) operated at 300 kV under super-resolution mode of K2 camera (Gatan Company), which were 2×2 binned to yield image stacks with 1.32 Å pixel size. The datasets were collected under a defocus range of 1.7–3.2 μm and magnification of ×22,500. Each micrograph was dose-fractionated to 32 frames with 250 ms exposure time per frame. With a dose rate of 8.2 counts per physical pixel per second, the total dose was approximately 50 electrons per square angstrom. MotionCor2 program was used to correct the beam-induced motion, and generated dose-weighted motion-corrected integrated images for further processing30. Defocus parameters were determined with Gctf program31. Image processing, model building and video making. For the ADP-bound state samples, 2,238 images were collected with UCSFImage4. About 515,000 particles were automatically picked with RELION32. All subsequent 2D and 3D image analysis and refinement were carried out using RELION 1.4. After screening with three rounds of 2D classification, 420,000 high-quality particles of NCP–Snf2 complexes were selected for further 3D classifications. The previous apo NCP–SHL2 density map was low-pass-filtered to 40 Å and used as initial model for the first round of 3D classification. Two classes corresponding to the SHL2 and SHL6 complexes were selected and subjected to further 3D classifications, separately. For the 245,000 particles of the SHL2 complex, the first round of 3D classification was carried out with apo NCP-SHL2 as the initial model, which resulted in five different classes. All 245,000 particles were also read back with the re-centred coordinates and manual picking33. These re-centred particles were performed another round of 3D classification with the second class obtained above as the initial model (yellow one boxed with red square in Extended Data Fig. 1d). Then the boxed classes in the two rounds of 3D classification were combined to get 146,723 particles (the duplicated ones were removed), which were refined to the 3.62-Å-resolution map. The SHL6 complex was classified similarly as above. After refinement, SHL6 complex achieved a 3.57-Å-resolution map. The resolution was estimated using the gold-standard Fourier shell correlation (FSC) with 0.143 criterion34. For the sample in the ADP-BeFx-bound state, 6,816 images were collected with eTas automated software. About 1,125,000 particles were automatically picked. After particle sorting and three rounds of 2D classification, 950,000 NCP–Snf2 complex particles were selected for 3D classifications using RELION 1.4. The first round of 3D classification was performed with the same initial model mentioned above. Two classes corresponding to the SHL2 and SHL−2 complexes were selected for further processing, separately. About 332,000 SHL2 complex particles were subjected to another round of 3D classification. Finally, around 190,000 SHL2 complex particles were selected and subjected to 3D refinement with RELION, which resulted in a 3.91 Å map. About 212,000 SHL−2 particles were finally selected and 3D refined with RELION to reach a final 3.64 Å map.

To re-examine the previous apo SHL2 complex, the micrographs were reprocessed with MotionCor2. The CTF parameters were re-measured using Gctf. All the 42,383 particles selected in previous work were re-extracted for further 3D refinement and the final resolution was improved from 4.69 Å to 4.31 Å as determined by RELION. These particles were then refined to get the higher-resolution map of 4.22 Å with THUNDER23. The program THUNDER is a new program for cryo-EM 3D construction, which includes CTF refinement and implements a particle-filter algorithm to obtain a complete solution for the parameter estimation problem in 3D alignment. This new algorithm improved the accuracy and robustness of cryo-EM 3D reconstruction so that we were able to achieve the better resolution. All the computations were based on the experimental images. For the MMTV-A datasets, 5,878 images were collected with eTas. Particles were auto-picked with RELION. After particle sorting and three rounds of 2D classification, 980,000 particles were selected for future processing. Three rounds of 3D classification, 162,726 particles of the SHL2 complex were finally refined to 3.47 Å. With a similar procedure, 154,645 particles of SHL−2 complex were selected and a 3.67 Å map was obtained. The models were built using the published structures of the ‘601’ nucleosome (PDB code 3MVD)13, MMTV-A nucleosome (PDB code 5F99)20 and Snf2 (PBD code 5X0Y)9. In particular, the bulging out of the tracking strand at SHL2 in the ADP-bound state was modelled by pulling the DNA from the proximal end. The phosphodiester backbone of the DNA from the proximal end to SHL2.5 largely follows the normal path as that found in the high-resolution crystal structures of free nucleosomes, but with an altered register of the tracking strand. The DNA from SHL1.5 and further inside (from positions 60 to the distal end), the bulk part of the nucleosome, does not show much change in different nucleotide states. Docking the high-resolution crystal structures of the nucleosomes fits the maps reasonably well. The models were then refined using phenix.real_space_refine with geometry restraints to ensure base pairing, base stacking and planarity35. The hypothetical model with DNA pulled from opposite direction in the ADPbound state was built by shifting 1 nucleotide register of the tracking strand from the distal end into SHL2, and locally refined against the experimental density map with geometry restraints as described above. FSCs were calculated between the corresponding models and the segmented DNA density inside the nucleosome, in which the first 20 bp from the entry side and the last 5 bp from the exit side were not included due to the weak electron microscopy densities of these segments. The morphing video is made with Chimera36 according to the model proposed in Fig. 4b. The models of stages 1, 2 and 3 are based on the structure determined in this study, and the structure of stage 4 is based on its counterpart in the previous ATPase cycle (stage 1) with 1 bp DNA translocated to the exit side. ATPase and remodelling activities. ATPase and remodelling activities were measured as described before9. To measure the ATPase activity, an EnzCheck Phosphate Assay Kit (Life Science) was used. Proteins (25 nM) were preincubated with 125 nM 147-bp dsDNA or NCP147 in 50 mM Tris-HCl, pH 7.5, 1 mM MgCl2 and 50 mM NaCl at room temperature. Then 2 mM ATP was added to initialize the reaction and the absorbance at 360 nm was measured. To measure the remodelling activities, 1 nM protein was incubated with 5 nM Cy5-labelled 347-bp mononucleosomes, 3 mM ATP and 40 U of HhaI in remodelling buffer (20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 20 mM potassium acetate, 30 mM NaCl, 5 mM MgCl2 and 0.1 mg ml−1 bovine serum albumin). Samples were taken at time points, stopped by 2× stop buffer (10 mM Tris, pH 8.0, 0.6% SDS, 40 mM EDTA and 0.1 mg ml−1 proteinase K) and then resolved by native PAGE. dsDNA constructs and nucleosomes preparation for smFRET. Doublestranded DNA (dsDNA) was made by annealing and ligating a set of overlapping, complementary oligonucleotides used for single-molecule nucleosome remodelling experiments, varying FRET acceptor Cy5-labelled positions on the DNA phosphate. Both slides and coverslips were washed with acetone, methanol,and a mixture of sulfuric acid and hydrogen peroxide with a volume ratio of 7:3, and sodium ethoxide, treated with aminosilane, and then coated with a mixture of 99% mPEG (m-PEG-5000, Laysan Bio) and 1% of biotin-PEG (biotin-PEG-5000, Laysan Bio). The imaging buffer was composed of 50 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 50 mM KCl, 5 mM MgCl2, 5% glycerol, 1 mM DTT and an oxygen scavenging system (0.8% d-glucose; 1 mg ml−1 glucose oxidase; 0.4 mg ml−1 catalase; 1.5 mM Trolox). The experiments were performed on an objective-based total-internal-reflection fluorescence microscope (IX71, Olympus) at room temperature. Samples containing Cy3 and Cy5 are excited by a 532 nm laser. The signal of Cy3 and Cy5 channels were separated by a 630 nm dichroic mirror and detected by an EMCCD camera. 16-bit video with time resolution of 300 ms was recorded and further analysed. 100 pM nucleosomes were anchored to coated quartz slides via a streptavidin-biotin linkage and washed away with imaging buffer. After a few seconds (~10 s) of imaging, 100 nM Snf2, 100 nM Snf2 with 0.5 mM ADP (10 U ml−1 hexokinase converted contaminating ATP) and 100 nM Snf2 with 0.5 mM ADP-BeFx were added respectively. In two-step reactions, after nucleosomes were added, 100 nM Snf2 was added and recorded for about 60 s, and then 0.5 mM ADP-BeFx was added subsequently. FRET time traces were analysed with alternative step-finding algorithm38,39. In brief, we first fit a single large step to the data, finding the size and location based on a chi-squared calculation. We then search for new steps by fitting to the plateaus found in the previous cycles, each time selecting the most prominent one. This eventually leads to a series of ‘best’ fits that differ only by one step. We then compare each best fit in the series to a ‘counter fit’ that has an equal number of steps as the original one but with step locations displaced randomly. A ‘step-indicator’ S defined as the ratio between the chi-squared of the counter fit and the chi-squared of the best fit was then used to evaluate the quality of the step fits. The ‘step-indicator’ S is large when the number of steps in the best fit is very close to the real number of steps.
