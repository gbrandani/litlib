Chromatin remodellers are pivotal in the regulation of nucleosome dynamics in cells, and they are important for chromatin packaging, transcription, replication and DNA repair1. Here we show that the human chromatin remodeller SMARCAD1 exhibits a substrate preference for subnucleosomal particles over the canonical nucleosome. Cryo-electron microscopy structures of SMARCAD1 bound to the nucleosome and hexasome provide mechanistic insights into the substrate selectivity. SMARCAD1 binds to the hexasome through multiple family-specific elements that are essential for the functions in vitro and in cells. The enzyme binds to the canonical nucleosome in an inactive conformation, which accounts for its diminished activity towards the nucleosome. Notably, the histone chaperone FACT complex acts synergistically with H2A–H2B to promote the activity of SMARCAD1 in nucleosome remodelling. Together, our findings reveal an avenue for chromatin regulation, whereby subnucleosomes are remodelled through an ATP-dependent process.

Nucleosomes are the basic repeating unit of chromatin in eukaryotic cells. Canonical nucleosome core particles (NCPs) contain around 147 bp of DNA wrapped ~1.7 turns around the histone octamer, which is composed of two H2A–H2B dimers and a core H3–H4 tetramer2. Hexasomes and tetrasomes miss one and two copies of the H2A–H2B dimer, respectively, and are believed to be the intermediates for the assembly of full canonical nucleosome3–5. During replication, transcription and DNA repair, nucleosomes undergo disassembly and reassembly, and form nucleosome intermediates. The histone chaperone FACT complex has a key role in manipulation of the nucleosome intermediates6. A recent study identifies a histone hexamer associating with the replication machinery7. RNA polymerase II elongation also leads to the formation of nucleosome intermediates8,9. In fact, hexasome-sized particles are repeatedly found in vivo8,10,11. Yet, the subnucleosomal particles are generally believed to appear transiently in cells. The mechanism of how these subnucleosomal particles are remodelled and regulated represents a fundamental question. Nucleosomes are mainly remodelled by the Snf2-like ATP-dependent chromatin remodellers, which slide and edit the nucleosome substrates12,13. The motor domains of these enzymes are highly conserved14, and share a common fundamental mechanism of DNA translocation in chromatin remodelling13. Human SMARCAD1 is an understudied chromatin remodeller15. SMARCAD1 and the homologues in yeasts (Fun30 in Saccharomyces cerevisiae and Fft3 in Schizosaccharomyces pombe) are localized at the sites of replication and are important for the maintenance of heterochromatin and gene silencing16–20. In fission yeast, Fft3 has a major role in removing nucleosome barriers during transcription elongation21. In mouse embryonic stem (mES) cells, deregulation of Smarcad1 expression resulted in a loss of pluripotency19,22. In human cells, SMARCAD1 is important for the repair of

DNA damage, making it a potential target for cancer treatment23–26. Despite the critical functions, the mechanism of action of SMARCAD1 remains unclear. The yeast protein Fun30 shows a modest activity in nucleosome sliding27,28. Recent studies reported a low-resolution structure of SMARCAD1 bound to the nucleosome, and suggest a role in nucleosome assembly29,30. However, the observed enzymatic activities are notably low, raising concerns about the enzyme’s functional efficacy and suggesting that SMARCAD1 is not the type of remodeller that slides the nucleosome. Here we report that human SMARCAD1 exhibits robust ATPase and remodelling activities towards the subnucleosomal particles, rather than the canonical nucleosome. To elucidate the underlying mechanism of substrate selectivity, we conducted cryo-electron microscopy (cryo-EM) studies and determined the structures of SMARCAD1 bound to the nucleosome and the hexasome, respectively. Our findings identify an avenue of chromatin regulation through remodelling nucleosome intermediates in mammalian cells particularly.

To ensure the protein quality, we subjected the FL protein to multiangle light scattering (MALS) analysis, and the result showed that the protein was mainly a monomer (Extended Data Fig. 1b). Considering that SMARCAD1 is regulated by N-terminal phosphorylation29, we dephosphorylated the FL protein (Extended Data Fig. 1c). The measured rates of ATP hydrolysis by the phosphorylated and dephosphorylated SMARCAD1 towards the NCP were consistent with the values reported previously29. To completely remove phosphorylation, we purified the motor region from Escherichia coli. The protein also displayed selective activation by the hexasome and tetrasome (Extended Data Fig. 1e). The data indicate that the preference for nucleosome intermediates is an intrinsic property of the SMARCAD1 motor. The preferential activation by the hexasome and tetrasome suggests that SMARCAD1 may selectively remodel these substrates. To validate this notion, we used restriction site accessibility assays to evaluate the chromatin-remodelling activity. Consistent with the ATPase activity, the Snf2 motor exhibited no marked preference for the hexasome (Fig. 1c–e). Notably, the SMARCAD1 motor showed a great preference for hexasome over nucleosome in chromatin remodelling. Similar to the motor construct, FL SMARCAD1 displayed a negligible remodelling activity towards the canonical nucleosome but remodelled the hexasome efficiently (Extended Data Fig. 1g). The inactivity towards the canonical nucleosome is not because of the short linker, as FL SMARCAD1, and the motor as well, barely remodelled the 100N100 nucleosome, which carries 100 bp linker DNA at both ends (Extended Data Fig. 1h). By contrast, the Snf2 motor is highly active towards the 100N100 nucleosome under the same conditions. The preference of SMARCAD1 for the hexasome over canonical nucleosome was unexpected. We consolidated this finding with additional assays. Using native gel migration assays, both the motor and the FL SMARCAD1 are inactive towards the nucleosome but remodelled the hexasomes efficiently (Fig. 1f and Extended Data Fig. 1i). Likewise, using histone dimer-exchange assays, SMARCAD1 is inert towards the canonical nucleosome, but displayed a robust activity towards the hexasome substrate (Fig. 1g and Extended Data Fig. 1j). Together, these data indicate that SMARCAD1 loses the activity towards the canonical nucleosome but is active towards the hexasome. The substrate preference of SMARCAD1 is different from the yeast INO80 complex, which efficiently remodels hexasomes and nucleosomes with long linker DNA11,32. SMARCAD1 is different from the yeast homologue Fun30, which shows a modest activity in repositioning the canonical nucleosome27,28.

Structural features of SMARCAD1 SMARCAD1 comprises several structural features, including the motor domains, NPD, HBD and CTH, which are collectively tailored for subnucleosome remodelling (Fig. 3a,b). The two RecA-like motor domains form a central DNA-binding cleft (Fig. 3c) and adopt a closed conformation analogous to the active state of Snf2 (Extended Data Fig. 3a,b), suggesting that SMARCAD1 engages the hexasome in the activated configuration. Key DNA-binding residues (Ser555, Arg909, Ser928, Thr880, Lys780, Lys634) are conserved between SMARCAD1 and Snf2 (Fig. 3c and Extended Data Fig. 3c), yet SMARCAD1 exhibits notable divergence in its DNA-interaction surface. For example, residues Lys878, Lys905 and Trp1185 in Snf2—which are critical for DNA binding and remodelling activity40—are substituted by Ser609, Ile639 and Cys951 in SMARCAD1 (Extended Data Fig. 3b). Although Ser609 retains interactions with the DNA phosphate backbone, the side chains of Ile639 and Cys951 diminish the contacts observed in Snf2 (Fig. 3c). These substitutions render the central motor domains of SMARCAD1 functionally distinct, featuring a degenerated DNA-binding interface that probably underpins its unique remodelling activity. The HBD is a defining structural feature of SMARCAD1. The HBD adopts a globular fold with a pronounced negatively charged surface that mediates extensive interactions with the histone H4 tail (Fig. 3d). Analogous to the elongated loop of the Ino80 motor (Extended Data Fig. 3d), the HBD projects from the RecA2 domain at a position accommodating sequence variations among Snf2-family ATPases41. Notably, the SMARCAD1 HBD orchestrates a U-shaped conformation of the H4 tail (Extended Data Fig. 2j). This binding mode involves the insertion of H4 Leu10 into a hydrophobic pocket of the HBD, while H4 Lys16 engages the adjacent RecA2 domain through an electrostatic interaction with Asp884—a conserved residue in Snf2 homologues (Fig. 3d and Extended Data Fig. 3c). Further stabilizing this interface, H4 Arg19 packs against the periphery of the HBD, whereas H4 Arg17 and Arg23 form salt bridges with Asp821 at the domain’s core. In contrast to Snf2, which binds to a short basic motif of the H4 tail39,42 (Extended Data Fig. 3e), SMARCAD1 establishes a multifaceted interaction network spanning multiple H4 residues, underscoring a distinct mechanism for histone engagement. The NPD, which forms an elongated structure (Fig. 3a (right)), binds to the hexasomal DNA at the N terminus through a positively charged surface (Fig. 3e), and interacts with the CTH at the C-terminal end (Fig. 3f). The CTH–NPD interface is located at a pivot position equivalent to the regulatory hub of Snf240,43,44 (Extended Data Fig. 3f). Yet, the regulatory hub of SMARCAD1 shows specific interactions different from Snf2. The brace II helix of Snf2 is not found in SMARCAD1, which is instead compensated by the presence of the CTH (Extended Data Fig. 3f). Likewise, the arm helix of SMARCAD1 packs against RecA1, equivalent to the post-HSA domain in Snf2, but orients differently. In brief, relative to the prototypic Snf2 motor, SMARCAD1 loses several canonical DNA-binding residues in the major domain cleft, but is compensated by additional interactions through the NPD domain. The HBD of SMARCAD1 makes the most extensive interaction with the H4 tail among the Snf2-like remodellers. The CTH reinforces the Lobe1–Lobe2 contact, which is accompanied by substantial alternation of the regulatory hub interface.

Importance of the SMARCAD1’s features Given that the motor domains of SMARCAD1 show variations in the DNA-binding elements, we tested the impacts by reverting them into the canonical counterparts individually and in combination (S609K/ I639K/C951W, 3M). The mutations did not change the ATPase much (Extended Data Fig. 4a). Relative to the wild-type (WT) motor, the I639K and C951W mutations increased the remodelling activities, whereas the S609K mutation slightly reduced the activity (Fig. 3g and Extended Data Fig. 4b–d). In combination, the 3M revertant increased the remodelling activities by around threefold. The data indicate that SMARCAD1 evolves a unique set of DNA-binding elements that are tuned to be less efficient in DNA translocation. The HBD is a unique feature of the SMARCAD1-family proteins (Extended Data Fig. 3c). To consolidate the H4 recognition mode by HBD, we measured the binding affinity using isothermal titration calorimetry (ITC). The WT H4 tail peptide binds to Lobe2 of SMARCAD1 with a dissociation constant (Kd) of around 7 μM (Fig. 3h). The R17A and R23A mutations decreased the binding affinity by around fourfold, whereas the R19A mutation modestly decreased the affinity by a factor of two, consistent with Arg17 and Arg23 of the H4 tail having central roles in SMARCAD1 binding (Fig. 3d). To validate the functional importance of H4 recognition, we tested the activities of two H4-binding mutants, D884K and D821K. Both mutations weakened the remodelling activity, with D821K mutation showing a considerable effect (Fig. 3i and Extended Data Fig. 4e,f), consistent with the central role of Asp821 in H4 binding (Fig. 3d). The tight H4 tail-dependency of SMARCAD1 is different from Snf2, which remains active in the absence of H4-binding40. To validate the functional relevance of H4 binding in cells, we used short-hairpin RNA (shRNA) to knockdown (KD) the expression of endogenous Smarcad1 in mES cells. Subsequently, we rescued the cells with WT and D821K-mutant GFP-tagged SMARCAD1. KD of endogenous Smarcad1 resulted in its reduction to approximately 25%, and the rescued proteins were expressed at comparable levels (Extended Data Fig. 5a,b). Consistent with the previous studies19,22, Smarcad1 KD led to a loss of pluripotency of the mES cells, as evidenced by the changes in cell morphology and diminished alkaline phosphatase (AP) staining (Fig. 3j and Extended Data Fig. 5c). These phenotypes were largely rescued by reintroducing the WT Smarcad1, whereas introduction of the D821K-mutant Smarcad1 did not rescue the phenotype. Similarly, Smarcad1 KD resulted in the derepression of the silent retroviral marker genes Bglap3 and Cml2, consistent with earlier reports19 (Extended Data Fig. 5b). The WT protein restored the gene silencing, but the D821K mutant failed to do so. These results support the critical role of H4 binding for the function of SMARCAD1 in mES cells. To evaluate the importance of the NPD, we truncated the DNA-binding region (ΔNPD). The ΔNPD truncation reduced the ATPase activity by less than 3-fold (Extended Data Fig. 4g) but substantially diminished the remodelling activity by over 30-fold (Fig. 3k and Extended Data Fig. 4h–j). Likewise, a single point mutation of the positively charged surface (K410E) markedly reduced the hexasome-remodelling activity (Fig. 3e,k). By contrast, replacement of a nearby negatively charged residue with a positively charged one (D440K) increased the remodelling activity. The data indicate that, besides the central DNA-binding cleft, the NPD provides additional grasp to the DNA and enables SMARCAD1 to efficiently remodel the hexasome. Importantly, a truncated isoform (isoform-3) of human SMARCAD1 is specifically expressed in the skin, and is associated with Basan syndrome and autosomal-dominant adermatoglyphia when mutated in patients45–47. This short isoform lacks the N-terminal 430 residues, including the DNA-binding region of the NPD. The data suggest that the short isoform of SMARCAD1 has reduced chromatin-remodelling activity, consistent the deregulation of very few genes caused by the mutations in patients45. To assess the importance of CTH, we deleted CTH (ΔCTH) and introduced the point mutation V473D into the NPD to disrupt the CTH– NPD interaction (Fig. 3f). The ATPase activities of the two mutants were similar to the WT motor (Extended Data Fig. 4k), consistent with the periphery location of CTH. Notably, the two mutants substantially lost remodelling activity towards the hexasome (Fig. 3l and Extended Data Fig. 4l–n). The data suggest that CTH works through a mechanism after ATP hydrolysis, probably through stabilization of the interaction between the two ATPase core domains to provide the mechanic force to drive DNA translocation, analogous to the brace II helix of the Snf2 motor40. In alignment with our structural and biochemical analyses, introduction of a CTH-deletion mutant in Smarcad1-KD cells did not rescue the mES cell phenotypes (Fig. 3j and Extended Data Fig. 5c), supporting an essential role of the CTH in SMARCAD1 function in mES cells. Together, the biochemical and cellular analyses are fully consistent with the structural model, and support the notion of subnucleosome remodelling by SMARCAD1.

The maps of the complex in ADP-BeFx-bound and apo states have overall resolutions of 3.7 Å and 3.1 Å, respectively (Extended Data Figs. 7 and 8). The histone and DNA were clearly detected. The motor was more flexible, with the molecular envelopes resolved, which fit the structures of Lobe1 and Lobe2 unambiguously. A structure of SMARCAD1 in a complex with the canonical nucleosome was reported previously29, in which only the SMARCAD1 motor was found, although the FL protein was used. Both the early structure and the one reported herein resolved only low-resolution maps, suggesting that the SMARCAD1 motor binds to the nucleosome in an inactive, or non-specific, manner. Consistent with this idea, we found particles of SMARCAD1 motor bound to variable positions of the nucleosome (Extended Data Figs. 7c and 8c) that account for only a small fraction of the dataset and probably arose due to non-specific interactions. In the structure of SMARCAD1 bound to the nucleosome, Lobe1 of SMARCAD1 wedges into the space between the two DNA gyres, interacting with the DNA through the positively charged surface (Fig. 4c,d). Notably, Lobe2 does not closely bind to the nucleosomal DNA, with a noticeable gap to the histone surface (Fig. 4c). Consistent with the loose binding, the DNA bound by SMARCAD1 followed the canonical path, and was not distorted in the apo state (Extended Data Fig. 8g). This is in sharp contrast to an active motor39,40, which tightly embraces the nucleosomal DNA and induces a DNA bulge at the site of binding (Fig. 4e). The data indicated that the SMARCAD1 motor cannot actively engage the nucleosome, which provides a structural basis for the weak ATPase and remodelling activity towards the nucleosome. The dislodging of Lobe2 from the nucleosomal DNA can be rationalized by the protruding HBD. Assuming SMARCAD1 adopts the canonical nucleosome binding mode39, the H4 tail-bound HBD would clash with the histone surface of the nucleosome (Fig. 4f,g). By contrast, when engaging the hexasome, Lobe1 and Lobe2 make a coordinated rotation along the DNA towards the side of the unwrapping DNA (Fig. 2c). By doing so, the HBD and the bound H4 avoid clashing with the histone surface, enabling the enzymes to bind in the active conformation. Thus, DNA unwrapping turns the hexasome into the preferred substrate for SMARCAD1.

Our findings suggest that SMARCAD1 remodels hexasomes through a unidirectional sliding mechanism. The motor domain is active towards the substrate with unwrapped exit DNA (Fig. 3b), whereas it is inactive to the substrate with wrapped exit DNA, a characteristic of intact nucleosomes. To validate this model, we engineered DNA gap substrates using the asymmetric 601 sequence33 (Extended Data Fig. 6c). A single-nucleotide gap was introduced at SHL + 2 (AT-poor/unwrapped face) and SHL − 2 (AT-rich/wrapped face). Consistent with our model, the SMARCAD1-mediated hexasome sliding was impeded by the SHL − 2 gap but was unaffected by the SHL + 2 gap (Fig. 4h,i). By contrast, Snf2 demonstrated comparable remodelling activity on both substrates (Fig. 4i and Extended Data Fig. 6d). These findings consolidate our model of hexasome remodelling by SMARCAD1.

SMARCAD1–FACT cooperation in remodelling Subnucleosomal particles are intimately bound by the histone chaperone FACT complex6,7,9. AlphaFold predicts with high confidence that the highly acidic surface of the CUE2 domain of SMARCAD1 interacts with the HMG domain of SSRP1 subunit of the FACT complex (Extended Data Fig. 9a). To validate the SMARCAD1–SSRP1 binding, we expressed Flag-tagged SMARCAD1 in HEK293 cells, and examined the interaction with SSRP1 using co-immunoprecipitation (co-IP) analysis. Consistent with the structural prediction, WT SMARCAD1 interacted with SSRP1 in cells, and deletion of the CUE2 domain disrupted the interaction (Fig. 5a). Likewise, endogenous SMARCAD1 co-immunoprecipitated with SSRP1 in mES cells (Extended Data Fig. 5d). We next tested their direct interaction using purified proteins in vitro. GST-tagged HMG domain of SSRP1 pulled-down SMARCAD1, and the mutations R562D and K579D of SSRP1 at the predicted binding interface interrupted the interaction (Fig. 5b and Extended Data Fig. 9a). In agreement with the structural and biochemical analyses, the SSRP1-binding mutants of SMARCAD1 (ΔCUE2 and F292D) did not co-immunoprecipitate with SSRP1 in mES cells (Extended Data Fig. 5e) and also could not restore the Smarcad1 KD phenotypes (Fig. 3j and Extended Data Fig. 5c). These data indicate that SMARCAD1 interacts with FACT in cells.

Notably, structural superposition of SMARCAD1 and FACT bound to the hexasome reveals that they can engage the same particle without steric hindrance (Extended Data Fig. 9b), suggesting a cooperative mechanism of SMARCAD1 and FACT in chromatin remodelling. To investigate this hypothesis, we pre-incubated FACT with H2A–H2B and introduced the resulting complex into the remodelling assays using the canonical nucleosome as a substrate. While SMARCAD1 and the FACT–H2A–H2B complex individually exhibited minimal remodelling activity in sliding the canonical nucleosome (Extended Data Fig. 9c), their combined application showed robust remodelling activity (Fig. 5c), as well as marked ATPase activity (Extended Data Fig. 9d). FACT and H2A–H2B alone barely stimulated SMARCAD1 remodelling activity (Extended Data Fig. 9e). To rule out the possibility that unknown contaminants contributed to nucleosome remodelling, we mutated the HBD and CTH, which disrupted hexasome remodelling by SMARCAD1 (Extended Data Fig. 9f). The mutations noticeably abolished the FACT-facilitated remodelling towards the nucleosome, indicating that the observed activity depends on SMARCAD1 (Fig. 5c and Extended Data Fig. 9c). Given that FACT destabilizes the nucleosome integrity48–50, we propose that its interaction with H2A–H2B may act synergistically to promote DNA unwrapping, thereby enabling SMARCAD1 to remodel the unwrapped nucleosome. These findings establish a functional interplay between FACT–H2A–H2B and SMARCAD1, although future studies will be required to elucidate the mechanistic and regulatory implications in chromatin dynamics.

Discussion Together, our biochemical, structural and cellular data reveal that human SMARCAD1 preferentially remodels subnucleosomes rather than the fully assembled nucleosome. Hexasomes in cells have been detected for a long time10,51–53. The discovery of a remodelling enzyme showing a strong specificity for hexasomes and tetrasomes indicates that processing these nucleosome intermediates are of biological importance. The subnucleosome preference of SMARCAD1 is encoded in the structure of the motor. Although located similarly at SHL2, the SMARCAD1 motor engages the hexasome and canonical nucleosome in different modes. The motor loosely binds to the nucleosome, with Lobe2 detaching from the nucleosome surface and probably being tethered through the interaction with the adjacent H4 tail (Extended Data Fig. 10). By contrast, when binding to the hexasome, the motor rotates into the open space left by the unwrapped DNA, so that Lobe2, together with the protruding HBD, closely contacts the substate. Non-canonical nucleosomal particles wrapped by single-stranded DNA were reported to activate the ATPase activity of Fun30 in budding yeast54, in which the remodelling activity remains unclear. SMARCAD1 is well known for heterochromatin maintenance during replication through mechanisms that remain incompletely understood18,19,55. Our findings propose a model in which SMARCAD1 facilitates the propagation of epigenetic memory by coordinating chromatin reorganization at replication forks (Fig. 5d). Parental H3–H4 tetramers, which carry epigenetic signatures that are critical for sustaining silencing56, may rely on SMARCAD1-mediated repositioning of subnucleosomal particles to propagate heterochromatin signals. Specifically, SMARCAD1-driven hexasome sliding could promote the spatial redistribution of silencing marks, enabling their inheritance across replicated chromatin. Structural constraints after replication may further necessitate SMARCAD1 activity. A hexasome closely packed against a nucleosome may not have enough linker DNA for the incorporation of H2A–H2B dimer for canonical nucleosome assembly. SMARCAD1 resolve this by translocating the hexasome to increase the exit DNA length, and therefore allows the reassembly of an intact nucleosome. Similarly, the resolving of closely packed tetrasomes by SMARCAD1 may have a role in nucleosome de novo assembly. Notably, SMARCAD1

interacts with KAP131,57, a scaffold for heterochromatin-associated enzymes, suggesting collaborative regulation of silencing. Furthermore, functional synergy with the FACT complex may enhance the chromatin-remodelling efficiency. This interplay is supported by studies in S. pombe, in which both Fft3 and FACT are essential for heterochromatin maintenance20,58. More studies of subnucleosome remodelling by SMARCAD1 to fully delineate its contributions to genome stability and epigenetic regulation are warranted in the future.

Sample preparation and data collection of cryo-EM analysis Hexasome used for cryo-EM were reconstituted with 157 bp dsDNA, which was the same as DNA used in the ATPase assay. Different constructs of SMARCAD1, including motor (residues 375–1026), ΔCUE2 (residues 300–1026) and ΔCUE1 (residues 200–1026), were used for structural determination, and handled similarly. In brief, 12.5 μM SMARCAD1 was mixed with 2.5 μM hexasome and dialysed to 50 mM NaCl, 20 mM Tris, 5 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM ADP, 8 mM NaF, 1 mM BeSO4, pH 7.5. The sample was then subjected to Grafix purification similarly to as described previously40. To prepare the SMARCAD1–nucleosome complex in the ADPBeFx-bound state, SMARCAD1 motor (residues 375–1026) was used. The cryo-EM samples were prepared using the similar method as described above. The sample in apo state was prepared in the buffer 50 mM NaCl, 20 mM Tris, 5 mM MgCl2, pH 7.5. For cryo-EM grid preparation, Quantifoil gold R2/1 grids with 200 mesh size were subjected to glow discharge in air for 30 s using a PDC-32G-2 plasma cleaner set to a low power. The samples (4 μl at 0.5 μM) were blotted for 3.5 s at −2 force before being plunge-frozen into liquid ethane using the FEI Vitrobot IV at 8 °C and 100% humidity. Grids were examined and screened using an FEI Talos Arctica or Tecnai Arctica operated at 200 kV. Cryo-EM data were collected using an FEI Krios G3i operated at 300 kV equipped with a K3 direct electron detector and GIF Quantum energy filter (Gatan), at a nominal magnification of ×81,000 for a final pixel size of 0.54125 Å px−1 with the defocus values ranging from −1.4 to −1.8 μm. The total electron dose was 50 e−Å−2 fractionated in 32 frames (exposure time 2.56 s). Image processing The initial 3D model of the SMARCAD1–hexasome complex was constructed using Relion3.059. In the dataset of SMARCAD1–hexasome sample, 5,376 micrographs of SMARCAD1(ΔCUE1, residues 200–1026)– hexasome (dataset 1), 5,466 micrographs of SMARCAD1(ΔCUE2, residues 300–1026)–hexasome (dataset 2) and 3,566 micrographs of SMARCAD1 motor (residues 375–1026)–hexasome (dataset 3) were collected for further processing. Datasets with 11,451 micrographs of the SMARCAD1–nucleosome sample in the apo state, and 11,129 micrographs of the SMARCAD1–nucleosome sample in the ADP-BeFx-bound state were collected. All image stacks were dose-fractionated, and aligned using MotionCor2 with twofold binning60. The CTF parameters were estimated using CTFFIND461. Particle picking, two-dimensional (2D) classification and three-dimensional (3D) classification were performed in Relion3.0 or Relion4.0. The initially picked particles were extracted with fourfold binning to increase the signal to noise ratio. In dataset 1, a subset of 354,776 particles was processed for multiple rounds of 2D and 3D classification to remove broken particles. The selected 178,999 particles as seeds were

[Page 13]

then combined with each subset to identify more intact particles using methods described previously. The selected 573,439 particles after seed-facilitated 3D classification were combined for further processing62. After focused 3D classification with a soft mask of motor, the best 303,679 particles were re-extracted without binning and refined to 3.3 Å. Similar processing was applied to dataset 2 and dataset 3, resulting in overall resolutions of 4.0 Å and 3.3 Å, respectively. According to these reconstructions, the SMARCAD1–hexasome complex in the three datasets has essentially the same conformation, with the structure of CUE2 undetected. The three datasets were merged to improve map quality. The combined 894,646 particles were further processed for 3D classification using cryosparc v.4.4, the 563,964 particles with clear NPD density were selected for local refinement. These particles were further performed post-processing and yielded a 3.1 Å reconstruction of the SMARCAD1–hexasome complex. To improve the map quality around the motor, the combined particles were performed focused 3D classification with a soft mask of motor. The best 303,679 particles were processed for Bayesian polishing in Relion without binning, yielding an overall resolution of 2.9 Å for the SMARCAD1 motor. In the dataset of ADP-BeFx-bound state SMARCAD1–nucleosome, 7,596,361 particles were selected after 2D classification. A subset of 637,811 particles was selected for 3D classification and multiple conformations were found. The particles with weak density around SHL0–1 or SHL3–5 of the nucleosome account for only a minor fraction compared with SHL2, possibly resulting from non-specific cross-linking. Most of the SMARCAD1–nucleosome particles are near the SHL2 of the nucleosome and only the SHL2 conformation had a clear motor feature with two lobes. To separate more motor-bound particles from noisy particles, guided multireference 3D classification was performed. The resulting 920,887 particles were refined, and focused 3D classification was then performed with a soft mask of the nucleosome part. The 199,055 particles with clear nucleosome features were selected for 3D classification. The 52,972 particles with clear motor-bound density were refined to an overall resolution of 3.7 Å. The refined map was processed for local filtering in cryosparc using a lanczos kernel. In the dataset of apo state SMARCAD1–nucleosome, 7,140,541 particles were selected after 2D classification. A subset of 1,172,660 particles was selected for 3D classification. The broken particles and free nucleosomes were discarded, and representative classes with a clear motor density at SHL2 were chosen as references for subsequent guided multireference 3D classification. The junk particles were removed after multireference 3D classification and the 735,394 particles were processed for 3D classification with local search alignment. Finally, the 103,023 particles with a clear motor-bound density were refined to an overall resolution of 3.1 Å.
