Turner4,5,6, Sissel Juul 4,5,6, Eoghan Harrington4 and Marcin Imieliński 1,2,8,9,12 ✉ High-order three-dimensional (3D) interactions between more than two genomic loci are common in human chromatin, but their role in gene regulation is unclear. Previous high-order 3D chromatin assays either measure distant interactions across the genome or proximal interactions at selected targets. To address this gap, we developed Pore-C, which combines chromatin conformation capture with nanopore sequencing of concatemers to profile proximal high-order chromatin contacts at the genome scale. We also developed the statistical method Chromunity to identify sets of genomic loci with frequencies of high-order contacts significantly higher than background (‘synergies’). Applying these methods to human cell lines, we found that synergies were enriched in enhancers and promoters in active chromatin and in highly transcribed and lineage-defining genes. In prostate cancer cells, these included binding sites of androgen-driven transcription factors and the promoters of androgen-regulated genes. Concatemers of high-order contacts in highly expressed genes were demethylated relative to pairwise contacts at the same loci. Synergies in breast cancer cells were associated with tyfonas, a class of complex DNA amplicons. These results rigorously link genome-wide high-order 3D interactions to lineage-defining transcriptional programs and establish Pore-C and Chromunity as scalable approaches to assess high-order genome structure.

fundamental question in human epigenetics is to understand how three-dimensional (3D) chromatin organization drives cell identity-defining transcriptional programs1. Although gene regulation is understood to depend on the 3D proximity of enhancers and promoters, such interactions occur in a background of DNA folding patterns that are not directly linked to transcription, including cohesin-mediated loop extrusion (for example, topologically associated domains (TADs) and CTCF loops)2,3. More fundamentally, it is unknown whether the 10–20 enhancers associated with a typical gene contact their targets individually or simultaneously to activate gene expression. Cooperativity is a hallmark of classic biochemical models of gene regulation, where allosteric conformational changes increase the probability of high-order interactions between DNA and transcription factors (TFs)4. The genome-wide distribution of gene regulatory cooperativity has not, however, been assessed in mammalian cells. The key roadblocks to such investigations have been technical. Over the past two decades, the workhorse of 3D genomics has been chromatin conformation capture (3C), which enables quantification of interaction frequencies between pairs of DNA loci. 3C libraries are composed of DNA sequences called concatemers that

result from the ligation of sets of DNA fragments that are proximal (within a 200-nm contact radius) in the sample5. However, most 3C-based assays (for example, Hi-C) use short Illumina reads and thus rarely detect high-order contacts (<1% of interactions), limiting their insight into gene regulatory cooperativity. Assays using targeted (Tri-C6, TM3C7, COLA8), molecular barcoding (C-walks9) and long-read (MC-4C10)-based profiling of 3C libraries have been shown to detect high-order interactions at select loci but do not scale to whole genomes. Recently developed genome-wide approaches for the study of high-order 3D interactions bypass 3C by directly applying molecular barcodes to chemically fixed nuclear fragments (SPRITE11 and ChIA-Drop12) or sections (GAM13,14). SPRITE molecularly tags nuclear fragments from a wide size distribution produced through sonication; as a result, many high-order SPRITE clusters reflect origin in a larger nuclear fragment and represent interactions across a substantially larger contact radius than 3C11. In GAM, each barcode marks sequences that originate in the same thin (220-nm) nuclear cryosection; as a result, sequences that share barcodes can be distant (up to a nuclear diameter that is 5–10 μm) despite sharing the same thin plane. Beyond assay limitations, a key conceptual challenge is how to identify cooperativity among patterns of high-order 3D chromatin interactions4,14–16. Biochemical intuition suggests that the contact frequency of three or more cooperatively interacting genomic loci should exceed that which would be expected on the basis of their pairwise interaction rate. Concrete computational challenges include how to identify statistically significant high-order interaction frequencies in the presence of this considerable pairwise background and how to uncover foci of cooperativity in a large combinatorial space of locus sets. To address these gaps, we sought to develop (1) a genome-wide assay to deeply and efficiently profile high-order 3D chromatin interactions within a small (<200-nm) contact radius and (2) computational methods to uncover genome-wide hotspots of synergy or statistically significant high-order interactions above pairwise background. We reasoned that the contact radius (<200 nm) of 3C libraries would be sufficient4,5 to detect functional regulatory element interactions with specificity, as has been validated for many mammalian cell types1 and cancers that undergo enhancer hijacking17. We also surmised that recent step changes in the throughput of Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) sequencing would render genome-wide ONT profiling of 3C libraries practical and enable rigorous genome-wide analyses of 3D cooperativity. Results. Nanopore sequencing of chromatin concatemers. To scalably assay genome-wide high-order chromatin interactions, we developed Pore-C, which applies nanopore sequencing to size-selected (>1.5-kilobase (kb)) DNA libraries obtained through in situ restriction digestion and proximity ligation of cross-linked nuclei (Fig. 1a). To segment each Pore-C read into a concatenation of restriction fragments (concatemer), we developed an algorithm to identify the minimal set of high-quality alignments that maximally covers the sequence by solving a graph optimization problem (Methods). Each of these filtered alignments is then assigned to a restriction fragment (monomer) based on midpoint position (Fig. 1a and Extended Data Fig. 1a). We refer to the number of fragments associated with a concatemer as its cardinality and refer to concatemers with cardinality of >2 as high order. We optimized the Pore-C assay on the Ebstein–Barr virus (EBV)-transformed B lymphocyte cell line GM12878 (Methods and Supplementary Tables 1 and 2), generating 397 gigabases (Gb) of sequence data (132× genome-wide read depth) and detecting 135 million concatemers, of which 78.59% were high order. To assess library quality and permit comparison with Hi-C pairwise contacts, we first decomposed each Pore-C concatemer into all ( ) possible monomer pairs, yielding 2k virtual pairwise contacts (VPCs) per read, where k is the concatemer cardinality. Comparing different restriction enzymes, we found that library preparation using the four-cutter NlaIII consistently had a higher density of VPCs among total bases sequenced (5.67 million VPCs per Gb) and lower monomer N50 (391 bp, N50 is the shortest sequence length in a library such that the summed length of all longer sequences comprises less than 50% of the total library length) than a protocol based on a second four-cutter (DpnII; 2.1 million VPCs per Gb and 733 bp) or a six-cutter (HindIII; 0.68 VPCs per Gb and 6,249 bp) enzyme (Extended Data Fig. 1b,c). Based on these results, we concluded that NlaIII was a superior reagent for human genomic Pore-C library preparation. Pore-C concatemers faithfully capture 3D and linear human genome structure. To assess the fidelity of Pore-C data, we compared contact maps computed from Pore-C VPCs and previously published ‘gold

standard’ Hi-C data18. Indeed, Pore-C contact maps visually recapitulated features of GM12878 Hi-C maps (Fig. 1b-d) and derivative values (compartment scores, insulation scores; Extended Data Fig. 1e,f) reflecting A/B compartment and TAD structure. Unbiased analysis of Pore-C VPCs with state-of-the-art 4DN tools19,20 uncovered TAD boundaries (Extended Data Fig. 1g) and structural loops (Extended Data Fig. 1h–k) with higher accuracy than SPRITE, although with less sensitivity than Hi-C. Overall, we found high values of stratum-adjusted correlation coefficients (SCCs21; for Pore-C VPC contact maps at five resolutions; Fig. 1e, 10 kb–1 Mb), comparable to Hi-C replicates and higher than for SPRITE11 or a negative control (HCC1954 Pore-C). This correlation was maintained at the level of individual PromethION runs (P1 or P2, mean of 16.8 million concatemers, 22× genome-wide coverage, mean SCC of 0.89, 50 kb; Extended Data Fig. 1d) but was reduced with MinION runs (1–2 million concatemers, 2–4×, mean SCC of 0.77). These results indicated that two PromethION flow cells of Pore-C are sufficient to capture major pairwise features of 3D chromatin structure. We also assessed the ability of Pore-C VPCs to faithfully reflect linear genome structure by scaffolding de novo human genome assemblies, a common Hi-C application22–26. We profiled the HG002 genome in a bottle reference sample27 with a PromethION flow cell of a shotgun whole-genome sequencing (WGS) library (40× coverage, read N50 of 20 kb) and a second flow cell of an NlaIII-digested Pore-C library (15× coverage, 201 million VPCs). Application of the Shasta assembler28 to WGS nanopore reads yielded an initial draft human genome assembly with 8,843 contigs and an NG50 (the length of the shortest scaffold in the assembly such that the length of all longer scaffolds is less than 50% of the reference genome length) of 7.2 Mb (Extended Data Fig. 2a). Applying the 3D-DNA and SALSA2 algorithms to merge proximal contigs using the 3D data in Pore-C VPCs (Methods24,25 and Extended Data Fig. 2a,b) yielded an assembly with median scaffold NG50 of 125 Mb, representing a 17-fold improvement over the initial draft assembly and approaching the GRCh38 reference assembly in size (Extended Data Fig. 2c). The final assembly merged all chromosomes into one or two scaffolds that covered >80% of their respective genomic territory, with some scaffolds approaching 200 Mb (Extended Data Fig. 2b) and joining chromosome arms (Extended Data Fig. 2d). These results, which are comparable to results obtained for previous Hi-C-based studies22–26, demonstrate that Pore-C VPCs enable chromosome-scale de novo human genome assembly. High-order concatemers are abundant and reflect proximal chromatin interactions. Having validated the pairwise signal implicit in Pore-C concatemers, we investigated the potential of Pore-C libraries to reveal high-order chromatin structure. Analyzing distributions of GM12878 Pore-C contact cardinality revealed a smooth unimodal distribution with a median cardinality of 4 (Fig. 1f). While GM12878 SPRITE clusters also had a median cardinality of 4, a smaller fraction represented high-order interactions (11.69% versus 78.59% for Pore-C). Taken together, these results revealed a marked enrichment (odds ratio (OR) = 27.72 for >two way) of high-order contacts in Pore-C libraries relative to SPRITE. Investigating SPRITE distributions further at the read level (Fig. 1f, right) rather than the cluster level (Fig. 1f, left) revealed a bimodality in the histogram showing the fraction of reads associated with a given cluster cardinality. The majority (58%) of reads belonging to high-order SPRITE clusters were associated with a very high cardinality (cardinality > 25) mode distinct from a lower mode that included pairwise contacts (Fig. 1f, right). By contrast, the histogram of Pore-C monomer membership was unimodal. Although the relative fidelity of Pore-C and SPRITE against the Hi-C gold standard was comparable overall, SPRITE’s fidelity was reduced to background levels when virtual contact maps were derived only from high-order SPRITE interactions. We hypothesized that this difference in fidelity may be driven by differences in contact radius between high-order contacts in Pore-C and SPRITE. To address this, we analyzed the decay of interactions as a function of reference distance in high-order and pairwise Pore-C and SPRITE contacts. High-order and pairwise Pore-C contacts showed the same reference distance decay as Hi-C; by contrast, high-order SPRITE clusters showed a markedly slower decay than Hi-C, pairwise SPRITE clusters and high-order Pore-C concatemers (Fig. 1g). These results indicate that both high- and low-order Pore-C concatemers arise from the same ligation process assayed by other 3C-based methods and thus sample the same contact radius (<200-nm) previously associated with other 3C assays5; by contrast, high-order SPRITE clusters may reflect larger chromatin fragments in the assay and thus represent more indirect and long-range interactions in the nucleus.

Pore-C concatemers reveal cooperativity at active enhancers and promoters. Given the abundance, fidelity and proximity of high-order contacts in Pore-C data, we next asked whether some of these proximal interactions arose from cooperative biological processes distinct from those generating pairwise interactions. We reasoned that the statistical enrichment of high-order contacts relative to what would be expected given the pairwise contact frequencies across a set of bins would provide a readout of synergistic or cooperative 3D interactions. To detect this signal, we developed a background model predicting the count of concatemers overlapping an arbitrary set of genomic bins (bin-set) as a function of covariates (including reference genomic distance between bins, GC content, restriction fragment count and bin-set cardinality) using a gamma-Poisson generalized linear model (GLM; Fig. 2a, left). To assess a bin-set B of interest, we analyze the observed and expected concatemer counts across pairwise and high-order subsets of B (up to a cardinality of 5). In the output of this algorithm, which we call Synergy, the magnitude (relative risk, RR) of this deviation is returned as the effect size along with its P value (Fig. 2a, right, and Methods). We interpret this effect size as a measure of bin-set cooperativity and also refer to each synergistic bin-set (that is, one that harbors a statistically significant positive deviation in cooperativity) as a synergy. We next asked whether enhancers and promoters were significantly enriched in cooperativity. To address this, we annotated non-overlapping 10-kb genomic bins as intersecting one of 64,070

strong enhancers or 15,274 active promoters in GM12878. For each 2-Mb sliding window (1-Mb stride) in the genome (Fig. 2b and Methods), we assessed cooperativity for the set of bins in the window that overlapped a promoter (or enhancer; Methods). This approach revealed that a subset of windows in the enhancer (3%; Fig. 2c, top row, left) and promoter (5%; Fig. 2c, bottom row, left) analysis harbored statistically significant cooperativity (false discovery rate (FDR) < 0.1). For comparison, we applied Synergy to random bin-sets of identical cardinality in each window (Extended Data Fig. 3b).

Chromunity uncovers synergies at cell identity-specific loci. Having demonstrated significant cooperativity at enhancers and promoters, we sought to uncover specific synergies through genome-wide analysis of Pore-C data. High-order chromatin interactions reside in large combinatorial space that is intractable to exhaustive search. To enable de novo discovery of synergistic bin-sets, we combined community detection of concatemers with the Synergy model into an algorithm we call Chromunity (Fig. 3a). Chromunity begins with a graph of concatemers whose weighted edges quantify genomic overlap between concatemer pairs. This graph is used to build a second k-nearest neighbor concatemer graph, which is clustered using a standard community detection algorithm (Methods). Each concatemer community above a minimal size is evaluated to identify its genomic footprint and derive a candidate bin-set, which is evaluated by Synergy on an independent (leave-out) set of concatemers. Chromunity can be applied across the genome across a sliding window of genomic tiles (for example,

10-kb bins tiling the genome, called ‘sliding window Chromunity’) or to a set of user-defined regulatory element (RE) targets (for example, enhancers and promoters, called ‘RE Chromunity’; Fig. 3a). We first applied sliding window Chromunity to 10-, 25- and 50-kb genome-wide tiles. Given the computational complexity of each analysis (≈85 million concatemers across 60,000–300,000 bins), we limited concatemer community detection to 2-Mb sliding windows (1-Mb stride) to uncover 3,120 candidate bin-sets, of which 21% showed cooperativity in a leave-out concatemer set. As in the previous analyses, this rate of cooperativity was significantly higher than for random bin-sets with the same characteristics and shuffled Pore-C concatemers (Extended Data Fig. 3f,g). We next asked whether synergistic bin-sets were epigenetically distinct from background. Analyzing histone mark chromatin immunoprecipitation assays with sequencing (ChIP–seq) overlap with Chromunity output (Fig. 3b and Supplementary Table 4), we found that synergies were enriched in regions associated with

[Page 7]

transcriptional elongation and were depleted in repressed chromatin and heterochromatin. Synergies colocalized with elongating (S2p), initiating (S5p) and total isoforms of RNA polymerase IIA. They also overlapped with active enhancer and promoter histone marks (Fig. 3b and Supplementary Table 4) and were enriched in genes in the top two quartiles of expression (highly expressed genes) and depleted in unexpressed genes. Synergistic bin-sets were preferentially bound by B cell-associated TFs, including PAX5, IKZF1 and EBF1. These results indicate that 3D cooperativity is concentrated at transcriptionally active and cell identity-specific loci. EBV-induced overexpression of MYC has previously been shown to be a hallmark of lymphoblastoid cell line (LCL) transformation29. Indeed, Chromunity analysis identified a nominally synergistic bin-set at the MYC locus (RR = 3.4, P = 0.016; Fig. 3c, left) overlapping a set of superenhancers previously associated with EBV lymphocyte transformation29. These superenhancers were located (~525 kb and ~428 kb) upstream of the MYC gene, which were brought together with the MYC promoter. This interaction has been previously shown to be essential for MYC expression in EBV-transformed LCLs29–31. This bin-set included an additional H3K27ac enhancer peak (black interval) not previously implicated in the EBV-driven activation of MYC. We also identified a nominal synergy linking EBV-associated enhancers to the promoter and gene body of CDK6 (RR = 5.09, P = 2.14 × 10–3; Fig. 3c, right), another known overexpressed target of EBV transformation29. These results suggest that cooperative 3D interactions may play a role in viral transformation. Significant cooperativity across reference-distal regulatory elements. To better understand cooperativity between more reference-distal (>2 Mb) regulatory elements, we applied RE Chromunity to a previously annotated set of GM12878-associated enhancers and promoters. The dimensions of the analysis (79,344 promoters and enhancers overlapping 5,394,087 concatemers) enabled genome-wide concatemer community detection without the need for sliding windows, allowing for the study of reference-distal (including interchromosomal) interactions (Methods). RE Chromunity analysis yielded 647 candidate bin-sets, of which 207 (32%) demonstrated significant cooperativity (FDR < 0.1) in the leave-out concatemer data. This represented a profound enrichment relative to shuffled concatemers (Fisher’s exact test, OR = 12.65, P < 2.2 × 10–16) and compared to random bin-sets with similar characteristics (Extended Data Fig. 3h,i and Methods). Among these 207 synergies, 94 spanned multiple chromosomes (Extended Data Fig. 3j). An example of an interchromosomal enhancer–promoter (E–P) synergy discovered by RE Chromunity is shown in Fig. 3d. The supporting concatemers spanned multiple interchromosomal and also reference-distal intrachromosomal active compartments and superenhancers on chromosomes 8, 12 and 20, including the promoters of several highly expressed and B cell-specific genes (IRAG2, CD72 and BLK32). Pathway analysis33 of the 207 synergies revealed enrichment of biological processes related to viral infection, immune response and B cell differentiation,

consistent with GM12878’s B cell origins and EBV-transformed cell identity (Fig. 3e). Taken together, these results support the existence of cooperative and reference-distal E–P interactions at lineage-specific loci in GM12878 cells. Concatemer base modifications link synergy with hypomethylation at highly expressed genes. Because Pore-C libraries are composed of unamplified cellular DNA, the resulting concatemers retain base modifications such as DNA methylation, which can be detected by nanopore sequencing. Indeed, we found a close correlation between genome-wide DNA methylation fractions obtained from Pore-C and gold standard whole-genome bisulfite sequencing34 (WGBS; r = 0.92; Extended Data Fig. 4a) or nanopore WGS data (r = 0.94; Extended Data Fig. 4b), exceeding interreplicate correlations of WGBS ENCODE data (r = 0.86; Extended Data Fig. 4c). As biological validation of the Pore-C methylation signal, we investigated X chromosome inactivation (XCI) in GM12878 cells35,36. After assigning X chromosome Pore-C concatemers to known GM12878 single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) haplotypes27, we found a clear methylated (Xi) and unmethylated (Xa) haplotype in regions subject to XCI (two-sided Wilcoxon rank sum test, P < 2.22 × 10–16) but not in regions previously shown to undergo X inactivation escape (two-sided Wilcoxon rank sum test, P = 0.26; Extended Data Fig. 4d). Xi-specific methylation enrichment was concentrated in CpG islands (CGIs) at promoters but absent from random genomic regions or CGIs in promoters of genes that escape XCI (Extended Data Fig. 4e). Xa-specific concatemers demonstrated robust A/B compartmentalization, while the Xi contact map demonstrated two megadomains lacking TADs but harboring a 16-Mb superloop linking the microsatellite repeat DXZ4 and long non-coding RNA gene FIRRE, consistent with previous findings8,37,38 (Extended Data Fig. 4f). Taken together, these results validate the ability of Pore-C to simultaneously assess phase, methylation and 3D structure. Having established the validity of the Pore-C methylation signal, we sought to explore the methylation footprint of 3D cooperativity. Specifically, we posited that loci engaging in cooperative high-order interactions may be preferentially bound to TFs and thus harbor demethylated promoters. To address this, we compared transcription start site (TSS)-proximal CGI methylation between synergistic and non-synergistic bin-sets and between high- and low-order (≤two-way) interactions in the same loci. Synergistic bin-sets uncovered by Chromunity were significantly demethylated at TSS-proximal CGIs compared to non-synergistic Chromunity candidates (P = 0.0045, two-sided Wilcoxon rank sum test; Fig. 4a), consistent with expression differences at the associated genes (Fig. 4b). We found that, in aggregate, high-order concatemers had significantly lower TSS-proximal CGI methylation than low-order (≤2) contacts at the same synergistic loci (two-sided Wilcoxon rank sum test, P = 1.6 × 10–5), an effect that was attenuated at non-synergistic loci (P = 0.051; Fig. 4c). Androgen stimulation induces high-order chromatin interactions. The androgen receptor (AR) is the primary transcriptional effector of hormone stimulation in prostate cells, triggering growth and differentiation gene expression programs after binding distal regulatory elements in complex with other TFs (for example, FOXA1) in an androgen-dependent fashion39. To better understand the role of 3D cooperativity in androgen stimulation, we starved LNCaP cells of androgen for 72 h and treated with either vehicle (0.1% ethanol) or the androgen dihydrotestosterone (DHT; 10 nM) for 18 h, generating Pore-C profiles across two biological replicates for each condition (two PromethION flow cells per condition). Applying a sliding 2-Mb window (1-Mb stride) Chromunity analysis across 25-kb bins uncovered 213 and 66 synergies in DHT+ and DHT– conditions, respectively (Extended Data Fig. 5a). Bins contributing to DHT+ synergies significantly overlapped DHT+ AR ChIP–seq peaks (OR = 1.39, P = 0.0015, logistic regression; Fig. 5a and Methods). DHT– synergies displayed no AR enrichment (P = 0.23). Extending this analysis to additional publicly available TF ChIP–seq data (21 LNCaP TF profiles, 121 consensus TF profiles across 129 cell lines from ENCODE), we uncovered significant overlaps between DHT+ synergies and the binding sites of lineage-specific AR cofactors (HOXB13, ETV1 and FOXA1; Fig. 5a). Most of the remaining non-prostate lineage TF profiles did not show significant overlap with DHT+ or DHT– synergies, with the exception of ubiquitously expressed structural chromatin factors (SMC3, ZNF143 and TRIM22; Fig. 5a). To uncover additional reference-distal (2-Mb) synergies around AR binding, we applied RE Chromunity to 19,671 DHT+ AR enhancers and 19,886 genome-wide promoters (Extended Data Fig. 5b). Jointly analyzing 692 concatemer communities uncovered in either experimental condition with Synergy in independent leave-out concatemer sets uncovered 33 DHT+-specific, 16 DHT–-specific and 23 shared AR E–P synergies (Fig. 5b). Among these, we found many (23) interchromosomal AR enhancer synergies in the DHT+ condition that were absent in DHT– cells (Fig. 5c). We next tested these sets of loci for their proximity (10 kb) to 289 known androgen-responsive genes (ARGs)40. We found that DHT+-specific synergies were significantly enriched in ARG overlap relative to DHT– (Fisher’s exact test OR = 11.36, P = 0.006; Fig. 5d).

As an example, we identified a DHT+-specific synergy spanning four chromosomes and 18 distinct loci linking reference-distal (>1 Mb) AR binding sites in the proximity of nine ARGs, including the prostate-specific genes FKBP5, KLK3 and KLK4, along with high density of relevant TFs (AR, FOXA1 and HOXB13; Fig. 5e). Taken together, these results implicate high-order chromatin interactions in hormone-dependent AR binding and gene regulation. Complex synergies induced by a breast cancer tyfonas. Cancer structural DNA variants create complex loci that harbor multiple copies of genomic intervals and rearrangement junctions in cis and trans41,42. The linear and 3D allelic structure at highly rearranged and amplified loci may have important biological consequences, including enhancer activation43–47. Tyfonas are a recently identified class of highly rearranged and chromosomally integrated amplicons comprising clusters of high-copy rearrangements, including fold back inversions42. To probe synergies associated with tyfonas, we analyzed publicly available short-read Illumina WGS data42,48 to identify a tyfonas in a breast cancer cell line HCC1954, which we then profiled with Pore-C and sequenced with one PromethION flow cell, yielding 160 million pairwise contacts and 13× genome-wide coverage. Applying RE Chromunity using genome-wide promoters and HCC1954 enhancers as targets, we uncovered five synergies spanning the majority of the tyfonas subgraph (Fig. 6a), spanning seven chromosomes and 30 amplified Cancer Gene Census (CGC) genes, including MYC, CCND1, TERT and so on. We next postulated that some of the observed synergies may be the direct consequence of altered allelic structure in this cancer genome. To explore this, we used a greedy traversal of the genome graph (Methods) to identify a walk that maximally overlapped the observed synergies. This analysis yielded a single continuous 202.5-Mb haplotype overlapping 3,655 concatemers spanning all five synergies (Fig. 6b). To validate whether linear and 3D proximities were the result of this rearranged allele, we performed three-color metaphase fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) on HCC1954 cells and the diploid ANA cell line. We designed probes against three discontiguous genomic regions overlapping the reference genomic location of three segments on the inferred tyfonas allele (Fig. 6c,d, highlighted walk, top). As predicted from our joint genome graph and Pore-C analysis, these three probes were proximal and amplified in the HCC1954 cell line and were distinct and diploid in ANA cells (Fig. 6d, bottom). These results confirmed the presence of a complex and amplified allele connecting chromosome 9, 12 and 20 enhancers in a chromosomally integrated locus. We concluded that the observed enhancer synergies were the direct result of altered linear contiguity and 3D proximity induced by this complex, amplified and rearranged allele. Discussion. The scalability of Pore-C can be attributed to recent increases in nanopore sequencing throughput, which enables the generation of 40–200 Gb of sequence with a comparable per gigabase reagent cost to that of a standard Illumina run. By capturing the same proximal (<200 nm) interactions as other 3C-based assays5, Pore-C may be the ideal method to study functional high-order 3D interactions at the genome scale. Pore-C may also provide insight into linear genome structure, where future algorithms that directly use the high-order contiguity information in Pore-C concatemers may further improve the scale of phased de novo assembly, particularly for polyploid and aneuploid genomes. Our results provide the first statistical evidence of widespread 3D cooperativity in proximal (<200 nm) 3D chromatin interactions across a mammalian genome. While previous methods (GAM13 and SPRITE11) have reported high-order interactions in 3D human genomes, these data represent long-range 3D proximities and thus are less likely to specifically reflect the direct binding events associated with genome function. By applying new analytics (Chromunity) to Pore-C data, we show across the genome that cooperativity is concentrated at enhancers and promoters around active chromatin regions and highly expressed lineage-defining genes (for example, B cell TF and EBV oncoprotein targets in LCLs and ARGs in LNCaP cells). The selective demethylation of CGIs at high-order, but not pairwise, contacts at highly expressed genes suggests that Pore-C synergies reflect a cooperative, activated and TF-bound 3D chromatin conformation. These findings validate long-held biological intuition about cooperativity in mammalian gene regulation4. Previous studies of gene regulatory cooperativity have been focused on the dynamics of TF aggregation at specific genes, both within and outside of the context of phase-separated transcriptional condensates4,15,49–53. A key question going forward is whether the enhancer synergy detected by Pore-C is linked to phase separation16 or other modes of cooperativity between enhancers, promoters and TFs4. Future Pore-C-guided superresolution microscopy-based investigations of 3D regulatory element cooperativity and phase separation will be needed to better delineate the relationship between these possibly distinct phenomena, particularly at steroid hormone receptors, including AR53,54. Our data demonstrate that high-order interactions with very significant cooperativity can show subtle deviations from background on a pairwise contact map (Fig. 3c). Indeed, we show that statistical signals of synergy disappear when concatemers are scrambled to generate high-order interactions with nearly identical pairwise contact frequencies (Extended Data Fig. 3c–e). The interaction patterns (TADs, CTCF loop domains) that dominate Hi-C contact maps at this scale are thought to be statistical echoes of a rapid and constant process of cohesin-mediated loop extrusion, whose role in gene regulation is still unclear3. A key question is how cooperative and regulatory high-order interactions nominated by Pore-C and Chromunity might physically coexist with loop extrusion. The mechanics of this interplay may be directly resolved by Pore-C-informed physical models that are constrained by concatemer alignment patterns. Our analysis of the aneuploid cancer cell line HCC1954 demonstrates that rearranged and amplified somatic alleles can create substantial synergies. Rearrangement-associated 3D interactions have been previously shown to give rise to new E–P interactions in cancer via enhancer hijacking17,55,56 or the mobilization of extrachromosomal circular DNA43,44. An outstanding conceptual and analytic challenge, highlighted by our analysis of a breast cancer tyfonas locus, is to understand which synergies are trivially explained by rearrangement-induced departure from reference contiguity and which reflect new transcriptional cooperativity induced by rearrangement. While such models have been developed for predicting

Hi-C interactions in the context of simple (for example, constitutional) rearrangements55,56, the nomination of new and functional structural variant (SV)-induced high-order interactions in highly rearranged and copy number-altered genomes will likely require additional algorithmic development. The application of such models to joint long-molecule and Pore-C profiling of malignant cells may help identify new and recurrent noncoding SV cancer drivers, which may be particularly relevant to the biology of advanced and therapy-resistant cancers57.

Methods

Cell culture. Human B lymphocytes (GM12878; Coriell Institute, RRID:CVCL_7526) and HG002 cells (Coriell Institute, NA24385, RRID:CVCL_1C78) were each maintained in suspension at a concentration greater than 200,000 cells per ml in RPMI-1640 (ATCC, 30-2001) supplemented with 15% fetal bovine serum (FBS) and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. HCC1954 (ATCC, CRL-2388) human breast cancer cells were maintained using a subculture ratio of 1:4 every 3 d in RPMI-1640 (ATCC, 30-2001) supplemented with 10% FBS and 1% penicillin/streptomycin. LNCaP cells were purchased from ATCC (CRL-1740), grown on poly-l-lysine-coated plates in 5% FBS containing RPMI-1640 and incubated at 37 °C and 5% CO2. Cells were passaged twice weekly or once cultures reached 80% confluency. LNCaP cells were starved of hormone for 72 h and stimulated with vehicle or 1 nM DHT for the indicated time points (0 or 18 h) before fixing with 1% formaldehyde. ANA cells were obtained from peripheral blood mononuclear cells (ATCC, PCS-800-011) by immortalization using EBV. Cross-linking. Ten million cells were washed three times in chilled 1× PBS in a 50-ml centrifuge tube and pelleted by centrifugation at 500g for 5 min at 4 °C between each wash. Cells were resuspended in 10 ml of room temperature 1× PBS and 1% formaldehyde (EMD Millipore, 818708) by gently pipetting with a wide-bore tip and incubating at room temperature for 10 min. To quench the cross-linking reaction, 527 μl of 2.5 M glycine was added to achieve a final concentration of 1% (wt/vol) or 125 mM in 10.5 ml. Cells were incubated for 5 min at room temperature followed by 10 min on ice. The cross-linked cells were pelleted by centrifugation at 500g for 5 min at 4 °C. Restriction enzyme digest. Each cell pellet was resuspended in a mixture of 50 μl of protease inhibitor cocktail (Sigma-Aldrich, P8340) in 500 μl of cold permeabilization buffer (10 mM Tris-HCl pH 8.0, 10 mM NaCl, 0.2% IGEPAL CA-630) and placed on ice for 15 min. Cells were centrifuged at 500g for 10 min at 4 °C, after which the supernatant was aspirated and replaced with 200 μl of chilled 1.5× digestion reaction buffer (NEB) compatible with the restriction enzyme used. Cells were centrifuged again at 500g for 10 min at 4 °C and aspirated and resuspended in 300 μl of chilled 1.5× digestion reaction buffer. To denature the chromatin, 33.5 μl of 1% (wt/vol) SDS (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 15553027) was added to each cell suspension and incubated for exactly 10 min at 65 °C with gentle agitation and placed on ice immediately afterward. To quench the SDS, 37.5 μl of 10% (vol/vol) Triton X-100 (Sigma-Aldrich, 93443) was added for a final concentration of 1%, followed by incubation for 10 min on ice. Permeablized cells were then digested with a final concentration of 1 U μl–1 of DpnII, NlaIII or HindIII (NEB) brought to volume with nuclease-free water to achieve a final 1× digestion reaction buffer in 450 μl. Cells were then mixed by gentle inversion. Cell suspensions were incubated in a thermomixer at 37 °C for 18 h with periodic <1,000 r.p.m. rotation (<30 s every 15 min) to prevent condensation inside the lid. Proximity ligation and reverse cross-linking. DpnII and NlaIII restriction digests were heat inactivated at 65 °C for 20 min with 300 r.p.m. rotation. HindIII digests were chemically inactivated with a final concentration of 0.1% (wt/vol) SDS at 65 °C for 20 min with 300 r.p.m. rotation and quenched with a final concentration of 1% (vol/vol) Triton X-100 (Sigma-Aldrich, 93443). Proximity ligation was set up at room temperature with the addition of the following reagents: 100 μl of 10× T4 DNA ligase buffer (NEB), 10 μl of 10 mg ml–1 bovine serum albumin and 50 μl of T4 ligase (NEB, M0202L) in a total volume of 1,000 μl with nuclease-free water. The ligation was cooled to 16 °C and incubated for 6 h with gentle rotation. Protein degradation and DNA purification. To reverse cross-link, samples were treated with 100 μl of 20 mg ml–1 Proteinase K (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 25530049), 100 μl of 10% SDS (Thermo Fisher Scientific, 15553027) and 500 μl of 20% (vol/vol) Tween-20 (Sigma-Aldrich, P9416) in a total volume of 2,000 μl with nuclease-free water. Samples were incubated in a thermomixer at 56 °C for 18 h with <1,000 r.p.m. rotation (<30 s every 15 min) to prevent condensation inside the lid. To purify DNA, the sample was transferred to a 5-ml centrifuge tube. The original tube was rinsed with an additional 200 μl of nuclease-free water to collect any residual sample, bringing the total sample volume to 2.2 ml. DNA was then purified from the sample using a standard phenol–chloroform extraction and ethanol precipitation. Nanopore sequencing. Purified DNA was SPRI size selected for fragments >1.5 kb and prepared for sequencing using ONT SQK-LSK109 and sequenced on ONT’s MinION, GridION or PromethION platforms. In total, 31 sequencing runs were conducted, generating a total of 730 Gb of raw sequence from different tissue sources, including the human cell lines GM12878 and HG002 and the human breast cancer cell line HCC1954 and human prostate cancer cell line LNCaP. The runs produced a total of 4.9 billion pairwise contacts. Runs were conducted using DpnII, HindIII or NlaIII for restriction enzymes to optimize the Pore-C methodology. The complete details for the runs are available in Supplementary Tables 1 and 2.

Pore-C pipeline. We developed a reproducible analytic pipeline for deriving high-order chromatin contacts from Pore-C concatemers (https://github.com/ nanoporetech/pore-c/) and a workflow that can be found at (https://github.com/ nanoporetech/Pore-C-Snakemake/), which uses the snakemake framework58. Briefly, the workflow involves alignment of Pore-C reads to a reference genome using bwa59 with alignment parameters that we adapted from another chromatin confirmation capture method, MC-4C10. The resulting alignments are filtered to identify a minimal alignment set that covers each Pore-C read. Each of the resulting filtered alignments is then annotated with the set of reference genome restriction fragments with which it overlaps based on an in silico restriction digest. From those fragment sets, each alignment is assigned to a single fragment based on the position of the alignment midpoint, which also serves to remove direct non-chimeric pairs that are caused by either incomplete digestion of the sample or ligation of cognate-free ends. With the assignment of each alignment along a Pore-C read to a single restriction fragment, a high-order contact is constituted, and this high-order contact can then be decomposed into pairwise contacts. Decomposition to pairwise contacts involves enumerating the number of high-order contacts containing a given pair of restriction fragments for every possible pair of restriction fragments in the genome. The fractions of alignments falling a given reference genomic distance from the predicted in silico restriction sites in the GRCh38 reference genome were computed for DpnII, NlaIII and HindIII Pore-C datasets. The pairwise contacts were assigned to genomic bins using conventional Hi-C workflows to generate a contact map, such as Hi-C explorer60 and cooler61. The workflow also includes an optional step to output the contact data in the bed format compatible with SALSA2 (ref. 25) for genome assembly correction and scaffolding as well as the ‘medium text format’ for conversion to the binary .hic format generated by the juicer-tools pre command (https://github.com/aidenlab/juicer/wiki/Download/) for use with the juicebox toolkit. Graph-based concatemer alignment. We developed an algorithm for Pore-C read alignment with the goal of identifying the most likely set of monomers comprising a given concatemer. Because each Pore-C read is a chimera of (potentially) distant monomeric sequences, the processing of these reads requires performing a multiway split alignment. The goal of our split alignment algorithm was to identify a set of minimally overlapping alignments that maximally cover the read sequence. In this algorithm, raw sequencing data are initially aligned with bwa bwasw with parameters –b 5 –q 2 –r 1 –T 15 –z 10, allowing supplementary and secondary alignments for each read. We remove low mapping quality (mapping quality = 0) alignments. In addition, for each set of alignments with identical positions on the read, we take the single highest scoring alignment. The remaining alignments are used to build a weighted directed acyclic graph whose optimal traversal determines the set of final, filtered alignments (that is, monomers) associated with the read (that is, concatemer). The nodes of this directed acyclic graph G = (V, E) are composed of the alignments as well two additional nodes (START, END) representing the start and end, respectively, of the read. Each node a ∈ V is associated with a start s(a) and end e(a) position on the read. Each alignment a ∈ V is also associated with an alignment score AS(a). Directed edges (a, b) ∈ E connect an alignment pair a, b ∈ V if e(a) < e(b) or otherwise if e(a) = e(b) and s(a) < s(b). Additional directed edges connect START to each alignment node and each alignment node to END. The resulting graph structure has a topological ordering from the START to the END node. Each edge (a, b) ∈ E is assigned a weight w((a, b)) as a function of the alignment score and start and end properties of the associated nodes. For edges (a, b), where a is the START node, w((a, b)) = s(b) − AS(b). For edges (a, b), where b is the END node, w((a, b)) = s(b) − e(a). Finally, for all remaining edges (a, b), where both a and b are alignments, w((a, b)) = ∣e(a) − s(b)∣ − AS(b). The Bellman–Ford algorithm is then applied to this weighted graph (G, w) to identify the minimal scoring path from the START to END node. In this formulation, edges between overlapping or distant alignment pairs are penalized. Similarly, edges connecting to low-scoring alignments will receive a higher weight. As a result, the minimally weighted path, as computed by the Bellman–Ford algorithm, will identify a set of minimally overlapping high-quality alignments that maximally cover the read. These are returned as the set of monomers associated with the given read/concatemer. Pore-C and Hi-C comparisons. We developed the Synergy model to test whether a set B of genomic intervals (also called a bin-set) shows statistical evidence of cooperativity. Our goal is to identify sets B whose high-order (cardinality > 2) subsets show a higher frequency of simultaneous interactions than would be expected on the basis of their pairwise interaction frequency and genomic covariates (including GC content and distance). We approach this inference through a two-stage process. The first involves training a background model to predict the count of Pore-C concatemers overlapping the pairwise and high-order subsets of B. The second involves identifying sets B whose high-order subsets are specifically enriched in concatemer counts relative to their pairwise subsets. Training of the synergy background model. Given a collection of hypotheses B = {B} each representing a set of bins B = {r ∈ N3 } (where each bin r represents the chromosome, start and end of a genomic range), we would like to train a linear function f(X(b)) to predict the count y(b) of concatemers overlapping each subset b ⊆ B, 2 ≤ ∣b∣ ≤ K given a vector of subset covariates X(b) ⊂ Rm. These covariates comprise m features consisting of ‘core’ covariates XC(b), which comprise cardinality and set-wise aggregations (minimum, median, or maximum) of bin widths and bin-bin distances as well as additional genomic covariates XG(b), including mean GC content as well as other (user specified) reference genomic features associated with the interval set b, such that X(b) = [XC(b) XG(b)]. Next, we construct a data matrix to train the Synergy background model. To provide examples for model training, we generate a large collection B̂ = {B̂} of random bin-sets based on B such that the empirical distribution of the core covariates XC (b̂) of subsets b̂ ∈ B̂, 2 ≤ |b| ≤ K matches the empirical distribution of core covariates XC(b) associated with B. This is achieved by the SampleRandomBinsets procedure (Algorithm 1), which randomly samples sets of intervals that match the input collection B with respect to their cardinality, interval widths, bin-to-bin distances and interchromosomal jumps. The output of this algorithm is the bin-set collection B̂, which is used for model training. B̂ is then used to populate a data matrix whose rows represents each size 2 to K subset b̂ of each bin-set B̂ ∈ B̂. The count of concatemers y(b̂) overlapping each subset b̂ is predicted from the covariates X(b̂) by fitting a gamma-Poisson GLM with m + 2 parameters (including the dispersion parameter θ): E[ln y(b̂)] = f(X(b̂))

Given a universe of genomic intervals across the genome U = {r ∈ N3 } (for example, representing fixed-width genomic tiles or regions of interests, such as enhancers and promoters) and a concatemer dataset D = {d ⊂ U} (where each concatemer d is represented by a subset of U), our goal is to identify sets B ⊂ U with significant cooperativity under the Synergy model. The de novo discovery of high-cardinality sets from a large (for example, ∣U∣ > 1,000) universe of items presents a combinatorial challenge analogous to the search for frequent item sets and association rules from market basket data, a classic machine learning problem63. To overcome these combinatorial challenges, we developed the Chromunity framework, which (1) identifies candidate synergies de novo using a graph community detection approach in a subset of concatemer data D̃ ⊂ D and then (2) tests them for cooperativity by applying the Synergy model to the remaining concatemers D \ D̃. Concatemer community detection. Given a subset of concatemers D̃, we build a weighted graph G = (D̃, E, w) of bin-wise concatemer overlap, where the weight w(e) ∈ [0, 1] on each edge e = {d1, d2} ∈ E represents the Jaccard similarity of concatemer pair d1 , d2 ∈ D̃. A second weighted graph Ĝ = (D̃, Ê, ŵ) is then built with weights ŵ(e) that represent the number of k = 25 nearest (that is, most similar) neighbors that are shared by each concatemer pair e ∈ Ê . Only edges with weight above a certain cutoff (kmin = 5) are included in Ĝ . We then apply a community detection algorithm to Ĝ (greedy optimization of modularity64) to assign each concatemer a community label (Fig. 3a, middle). On the basis of these labels, we retain a collection of concatemer communities C = {C ⊂ D | |C| ≥ smin } supported by smin = 5 or more concatemers. We then associate each community C with a bin-set B by first associating bins B̃ = {∪d∈C d} that belong to at least one concatemer in C with a concatemer count y(u) ∈ N, u ∈ B̃. A bin-set B ⊂ B̃ is then defined as the bins having concatemer counts y(b) ≥ quantile({y(u) | u ∈ B̃}, qmin ) at or above some threshold qmin = 0.85 (Extended Data Fig. 3a). From communities to synergies. Bin-sets B in the collection B = {B} obtained through concatemer community detection each represent a ‘guess’ or hypothesis that a set of regions are cooperatively interacting. To statistically evaluate and validate these hypotheses, we apply Synergy using the left-out set of concatemers D \ D̃. Applying this analysis to B allows us to calculate cooperativity effect sizes and assess statistical significance (P values and FDRs) on an independent data sample. The Chromunity algorithm thus represents the composition of concatemer community detection with Synergy evaluation. Indeed while the majority of bin-sets nominated by community detection are not synergistic, the frequency of statistically significant cooperativity is much higher in Chromunity output (Extended Data Fig. 3) than when analyzing random bins or even enhancers and promoters within a sliding window (Fig. 2 and Extended Data Fig. 2). Scaling and optimization. Because concatemer community detection involves building a O(|D̃|2 ) graph, computational complexity can be limiting for the analysis of very large concatemer datasets (for example, |D̃| > 107). This may limit the portion of the dataset D that can be used for community detection and hence limit the power for de novo synergy discovery. We apply several approaches in practice to mitigate this complexity and scale the analysis:

[Page 15]

Capitalize on the fact that D is a multiset, namely, that there are multiple concatemers that represent identical subsets of U. As a result, nodes and edges in G can be collapsed around the unique concatemers in D̃, reducing dimension. This can especially reduce graph dimension when using wider bins (50-kb versus 10-kb tiles). Remove concatemers d ∈ D̃ that only target two or fewer locations in U, because these are unlikely to provide support for cooperative interactions across distinct bins in U. Limit U to regions that are spaced far apart (for example, enhancers and promoters, which are on average 100 kb apart). Combining this optimization with the previous two will reduce the number of nodes because fewer unique sets in D̃ will overlap three or more bins in U. Remove bins from U that overlap fewer than smin concatemers and remove concatemers that hit two or fewer bins in U (see 2). Repeat until a fixed point is reached. Limit U to a local region of the genome (for example, a 2-Mb window) and discard concatemers D that do not overlap with U. Community detection can then can be applied across the genome through a sliding window approach to generate B. The sliding window approach is most useful for the analysis of high-resolution (for example, 10-kb) genomic tiles, where the density and spacing of bins may generate large and densely connected concatemer graphs that prevent the analysis of very wide (or chromosome-scale) windows.

In practice, we combine these strategies across two Chromunity modes, including (1) ‘Sliding window’ Chromunity where U is defined around (10-kb, 25-kb and/or 50-kb) tiles in each 2-Mb width, 1-Mb stride sliding window to study regional cooperativity. The benefit of this approach is that it enables a relatively unbiased discovery of cooperativity (that is, without needing to know specific annotations); however, it does not scale to the analysis of reference-distal or interchromosomal cooperativity. (2) Regulatory element (RE) Chromunity where U is defined around genome-wide enhancers, promoters, and TF binding sites. RE Chromunity is useful for the study of reference-distal and interchromosomal cooperativity but requires a hypothesis set of regulatory elements. In RE Chromunity, we combine the concatemer community detection results from a genome-wide U with additional analyses limiting U to a single chromosome. We then assess the joint collection of candidate bin-sets B for Synergy. Across each of the sliding window and RE Chromunity modes, we use about half of concatemers in D for concatemer community detection (|D̃| ≈ 15 million concatemers for two PromethION flow cells). Both the Chromunity framework and Synergy model are provided in the R package Chromunity (https://github.com/mskilab/Chromunity). GM12878 Synergy and Chromunity analyses. We applied Synergy to assess cooperativity across promoters and enhancers in the GM12878 cell line. Fifteen-state ChromHMM annotations for the GM12878 cell line were obtained from the hg19 ENCODE data resource (http://genome.ucsc.edu/ENCODE/downloads.html) and lifted to hg38 via the University of California Santa Cruz (UCSC) liftOver utility. We used the ChromHMM annotations ‘strong enhancer’ and ‘active promoter’ to define enhancers and promoters, respectively. For each enhancer (or promoter) analysis, we partitioned the genome into 10-kb tiles and applied a 2-Mb width and 1-Mb stride sliding window across the genome. Within each sliding window, we randomly sampled a set B of tiles overlapping an enhancer such that all pairs of tiles in B were at least 50 kb apart, and B had a maximum cardinality of 5. If no sets in the window satisfied this constraint, that window was not considered in the analysis. We then took all bin-sets across all windows to generate B, which was then tested using the Synergy model. The same analysis was performed for promoters. As a negative control, we also generated random bin-sets B̂ based on each of the enhancer and promoter collections, respectively, applying the SampleRandomBinsets procedure (Algorithm 1) to the corresponding Synergy input B. Sliding window Chromunity was applied to the GM12878 cell line across three separate analyses comprising distinct universes U of 10-, 25- and 50-kb-resolution tiles, respectively. Bin-sets passing FDR < 0.1 in any of the three Chromunity analyses were reported in the final results. We further analyzed sliding window Chromunity synergies (10-kb resolution) for overlap with epigenetic annotations. For this analysis, GM12878 A/B compartments, histone marks and TF binding site annotations were obtained from ENCODE34. To assess the specificity of Synergy and Chromunity results, we developed a procedure to generate negative-control data composed of scrambled concatemers with preserved pairwise interaction frequencies. Given a collection of concatemers D defined as subsets of bins U, we defined a weighted complete graph with nodes U whose edge weight w(u1, u2) for bin pair u1 , u2 ∈ U represents the frequency that a concatemer in D that contains u1 also has u2. Given this graph, we set up a Markov chain traversing bins U with conditional probability P(u2∣u1) = w(u1, u2). XCI analyses. Using the phasing approach, concatemers were assigned to their constituent haplotypes. High-order contacts were melted into pairwise contacts using GxG (http://github.com/mskilab/GxG), a suite of GenomicRanges-based classes for representing, querying and manipulating matrices of genomic data. For the DXZ4–FIRRE loop, concatemers intersecting both genomic regions were filtered and plotted. For XCI-dependent methylation comparative analysis between genes subject to XCI and those that escape it, XCI status was determined from Cotton et al.68. Genes with variable XCI status were excluded. CGIs were obtained from the UCSC Genome Browser69. DNA methylation calls were aggregated across CGIs within 1 kb of the TSS, and a mean aggregate score was calculated for each gene. The difference between DNA methylation between active X (Xa) and inactive X (Xi) was tested by two-sided Wilcoxon rank sum test. For promoter-centric analysis, promoter annotations were obtained from H3K4me3 peaks. The first two bands of the X chromosome coincide with the pseudoautosomal region and invariably escape XCI70. The coordinate system was reoriented to place the promoter at coordinate 0 and the gene body in positive coordinates. CGI methylation calls were averaged at 1-kb windows within 50 kb of the promoters.

HCC1954 cell line complex amplicon and Chromunity analysis. Short-read Illumina WGS data for the breast cancer cell line HCC1954 and matched normal HCC1954BL data were obtained from the genomic data commons (https://gdc.cancer.gov/). DNA rearrangement junctions were detected using SvABA71, and binned read depth was assessed using fragCounter (http://github. com/mskilab/fragCounter). Junction and read depth data were input into a junction-balanced genome graph inference algorithm JaBbA (https://github.com/ mskilab/JaBbA)42. JaBbA integrates read depth and SV junctions and produces a junction-balanced genome graph by solving a constrained optimization problem42. The junction-balanced graph allows annotation and querying of complex SVs via the R package gGnome (https://github.com/mskilab/gGnome). The resulting graph was queried for known SV classes using the gGnome events function, identifying a tyfonas42 spanning seven chromosomes (5, 8, 9, 11, 12, 20 and 22). Concatemers from Pore-C overlapping the nodes of the tyfonas subgraph were obtained. RE Chromunity was then run on promoters and 50-kb padded HCC1954 H3K27ac peaks (obtained from ENCODE34) overlapping the tyfonas nodes, which yielded five synergies (FDR < 0.1). We then searched the genome graph for a contiguous allele that maximally overlapped the observed synergies using a constrained optimization on the tyfonas subgraph (gGnome peel procedure; see ref. 42 for additional details). A 202.5-Mb allele was constructed using this approach and queried for overlap with CGC72 genes (https://cancer.sanger.ac.uk/census). A part of this walk connecting three loci on chromosomes 9, 12 and 20 was identified and validated by FISH.

CGI methylation analyses. CGI annotations for hg38 were obtained from UCSC. FISH. We validated the complex amplicon-associated interchromosomal synergy in HCC1954 cells using three-color DNA FISH. FISH probes were designed against synergistic and tyfonas-associated loci on chromosomes 9, 12 and 20 using the BAC clones (https://bacpacresources.org) RP11-341L12 (directly labeled green), RP11-476D10 (directly labeled red) and RP11-762A13 (directly labeled magenta). Prepped slides were placed in a denaturation solution (70% formamide/2× SSC) for 15 min at 73 °C followed by ethanol dehydration.
