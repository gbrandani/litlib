The thermodynamic stability of a protein plays an important role during evolution and adaptation in order to maintain a folded and active conformation. p53 is a tumour suppressor involved in the regulation of numerous genes. Human p53 has an unusually low thermodynamic stability and is frequently inactivated by oncogenic missense mutations. Here, we examined the thermodynamic and kinetic stability of p53 DNA binding domains from selected invertebrate and vertebrate species by differential scanning calorimetry and equilibrium urea denaturation. There is a correlation in the apparent melting temperature of p53 with the body temperature of homeotherm vertebrates. We found that p53 from these organisms has a half-life for spontaneous unfolding at organismal body temperature of 10–20 min. We also found that p53 from invertebrates has higher stability, bearing more resemblance towards p63 and p73 from humans. Using structure-guided mutagenesis on the human p53 scaffold, we demonstrated that the amino acid changes on the protein surface and in the protein interior lead to the elevated stability of p53 orthologs. We propose a model in which the p53 DNA binding domain has been shaped by the complex interplay of different selective pressures and underwent adaptive evolution leading to pronounced effects on its stability. p53 from vertebrates has evolved to have a low thermodynamic stability and similarly short spontaneous half-life at organismal body temperature, which is related to function.

Introduction The p53 tumour suppressor is an important transcription factor involved in the transcription of a wide range of genes that control cell cycle arrest, DNA repair or apoptosis upon cellular stress.1 The p53 protein consists of a folded central DNA binding domain (DBD) and a tetramerization domain, flanked by flexible, intrinsically unstructured Nand C-terminal ends. It functions as a tetramer in vivo.2 The human DBD (residues 94–312), which determines the overall stability of the full-length protein, is thermodynamically unstable, making it susceptible to denaturation by mutations.3 p53 is also kinetically unstable at 37 °C, leading to spontaneous unfolding in vitro.4 Mutation of the DBD is

found in ∼50% of all human cancers with up to 50% prevalence due to oncogenic mutations5,6 that either alter DNA-binding residues or lower the overall thermodynamic stability.3 p53 is a member of a family that in vertebrates includes two additional paralogs, p63 and p73. Besides the three domains they share with p53, both p63 and p73 contain an additional protein– protein interaction domain, the so-called SAM domain. While some invertebrate p53-like sequences share similar domain organization to vertebrate p53 (e.g., Caenorhabditis elegans, Drosophila melanogaster, Entamoeba hystolytica, Ciona intestinalis, Nematostella vectensis, etc.), others such as p53-like sequences from molluscs resemble more closely the vertebrate paralogs p63 and p73. The full-length sequences of p53 homologs exhibit a high degree of divergence. The most conserved across all p53 orthologs and paralogs is the region comprising the DBD. Most p53 missense mutations found in human cancers are located in the DBD, suggesting a correlation between the degree of evolutionary constraint and the structural or functional importance of this region.

[Page 2]

It remains a question whether there is any evolutionary selective advantage for the low thermodynamic stability of p53 in humans. p53 is normally kept at low levels in the cell and only accumulates rapidly upon cellular stress to activate downstream genes. It is also quickly targeted for degradation by ubiquitination through various feedback loops such as Hdm2.7 It is thought that the low intrinsic thermodynamic stability of p53 can also help to facilitate rapid degradation due to the requirement for a tight regulation of this protein in the cell. The thermodynamic stability of human p53 is correlated to the level of folded protein in vivo in Escherichia coli.8 The p53 paralogs, p63 and p73, are much more stable thermodynamically, further suggesting that the low thermodynamic stability of p53 is linked to its unique function.9,10 Both rational design11 and directed evolution12 have been used for the generation of p53 mutants of higher thermodynamic stability, one of which, a quadruple mutant (M133L/V203A/N239Y/N268D), has been used extensively for biophysical studies.11,13 The quadruple mutant has provided important insights into how p53 mutants lead to inactivation in cancer.14 Over the years, various organisms such as D. melanogaster, C. elegans, Mus musculus and Danio rerio have been used as model organisms to study the role of p53 in cancer and ageing. These simpler model systems have made an important contribution to understanding the molecular functions of p53. In this study, we investigated the thermodynamic stability of p53 core domain from selected invertebrate and vertebrate species that can be gathered into the two large groups of homeotherms and poikilotherms. We observed that in homeothermic species, the thermal unfolding of the p53 DBD appears to correlate with the organismal body temperature. We found that the p53 DBDs from these organisms are kinetically unstable with a half-life between 10 and 20 min at the optimal body temperature. Our results suggest that the p53 DBDs from homeotherm vertebrates have higher stability than poikilotherm vertebrates. The p53 DBDs from poikilotherm invertebrates are more stable, bearing more resemblance towards human p53 paralogs, p63 and p73. We used structure-guided mutagenesis in order to gain insight into the intrinsic factors responsible for these stability differences and showed that amino acid changes in the protein interior and on the protein surfaces lead to the elevated stability of p53 orthologs. We propose an evolutionary model in which p53 was subject to selective pressure and underwent adaptive evolution leading to pronounced effects on its stability.

Equilibrium urea denaturation The equilibrium free energy of denaturation (ΔGD-N) can be measured and compared accurately at low temperatures.17 Global unfolding of the p53 DBDs was monitored by either intrinsic tryptophan fluorescence at 352 nm or tyrosine fluorescence at 302 nm, and the fraction of protein unfolded was plotted against urea concentration (Fig. 3). Table 1 shows the midpoints of unfolding ([urea]50%) and their corresponding m values (m = ∂ΔGD-N/∂[urea]). The p53 DBDs show similar trend in their stability, in which DBDs from C. elegans and D. melanogaster are most stable, followed by mammals and lower vertebrates. C. elegans p53 DBD is significantly more stable with a urea midpoint of unfolding of 5.21 M. D. melanogaster p53 has a relatively high urea midpoint of unfolding at around 3.41 M. Mammalian p53 DBDs have very similar midpoints of unfolding between 3.04 M and 3.13 M. The p53 DBDs from lower vertebrates are the least stable, with midpoints between 2.56 M and 3.06 M urea. The free energy of unfolding of the p53 DBDs was calculated by approximating to a two-state transition of unfolding, using an average m value for all species,17 with the exception of C. elegans p53, which had a significantly lower m value (0.99 ± 0.17). The lower m value from C. elegans might be due to residual structure at high urea concentrations or an accumulation of intermediates during unfolding. Values of ΔΔGD-N calculated for the rest of the proteins with respect to human wild-type (WT) p53 are shown in Table 1.

Kinetics of unfolding of p53 DNA binding domains The kinetic stability of p53 is correlated to its thermodynamic stability with human p53.4 We observed that p53s from homeothermic species (G. gallus, H. sapiens, B. taurus, M. musculus and M. domestica) have apparent melting temperatures that correlate with their body temperatures (Table 1). We further investigated the unfolding kinetics of p53 DBDs from these species by monitoring intrinsic tyrosine fluorescence at 305 nm at their corresponding body temperatures (Table 2). The p53 thermal denaturation involves a complex process of unfolding and aggregation. We fitted our data to an exponential decay with linear drift to account for aggregation in order to estimate the halflife of denaturation as previously described4 (Fig. 5). The half-lives of denaturation are shown in Table 2. The p53 DBDs from homeothermic organisms are kinetically unstable with a half-life between 10 and 20 min at the optimal body temperature. M. domestica p53 DBD has a half-life of around 23.9 min at 33 °C, which is slightly longer than the rest of the analysed p53 DBDs. We also compared the unfolding kinetics of these homologs at the human body temperature of 37 °C. Interestingly, we observed a much faster denaturation for M. domestica p53 with a t1/2 of 1.2 min. G. gallus p53, on the other hand, is stable at 37 °C. Homology modelling of p53 DNA binding domains The known structures18–20 of the DBDs from three p53 orthologs (H. sapiens, M. musculus and C. elegans) and the human p53 paralog p63 provide a framework for an analysis. The p53 core domain has a βsandwich fold of two long twisted antiparallel βsheets of four (S1, S3, S5 and S8) and five (S4, S6, S7, S9, S10) strands. The β-sandwich is flanked on one side by a loop–sheet–helix motif harbouring strands S2′ and S2 and the DNA binding helix H2. The structural variations amongst the p53 homologs of known structure are largely confined to the peripheral elements (e.g., loops and turns; Fig. 6a).

The sequence identity between p53 orthologs analysed in this study ranges between 14% and 90% (Fig. 1). The vertebrate p53 DBDs share a relatively high degree of sequence similarity. The modest similarity between vertebrate and invertebrate sequences may reflect their evolutionary distance and/or their functional divergence. Figure 7 shows the physicochemical properties of the analysed proteins such as aliphatic and hydropathy (GRAVY) indexes and amino acid composition. Multiple sequence alignment of homologous p53 DBDs is shown in Fig. 6b. Many positions in the alignment favour a particular type of amino acid, although only a few core positions show absolute conservation. We built homology models for those p53 DBDs whose structure is unknown, and compared models and known structures in order to understand the intrinsic factors for the observed stability differences. D. melanogaster and C. elegans p53 contain amino acid substitutions that improve the hydrophobic packing in the interior of the DBD. The buried residues that constitute their hydrophobic

core have higher aliphatic and hydropathy indexes (data not shown). D. melanogaster p53 DBD has the highest aliphatic and hydropathy indexes that can be attributed to its high thermodynamic stability. In contrast, C. elegans p53 DBD has the lowest hydropathy index but an unusually high content of Phe residues, most of which are deeply buried in the interior of the protein domain. H. sapiens and B. taurus p53 DBDs share 90% sequence identity, 16 amino acid substitutions, most of which are located on the surface and only 3 buried in the interior. The latter include Met133→Leu, Val203→Ala and Tyr236→Phe. Each of these has a slightly stabilizing effect on the p53 core domain stability and can account for the slightly increased thermodynamic stability. Despite the van der Waals volume occupied by Met being the same as for Leu, the substitution of Met with Leu within the interior of the protein is expected to increase stability because of the more favourable solvent transfer and the reduced entropic cost of holding a Met side chain in a defined position. This substitution causes only minimal structural change in the protein interior as observed in the quadruple mutant structure.13 The stabilizing effect of the substitution Val203→Ala in human p53 has been recently described.13 Val203 is located at the β turn between β-strands S5 and S6. The structural analysis shows that the cavity created by the decrease of the aliphatic side chain is compensated by the rotation of the side chain of Leu188 in a position suitable for hydrophobic contacts with Ala203 and Tyr205. Mutation of Tyr236 to Phe causes a loss of buried hydrogen bond, which is expected to cost ∼0.5– 1.5 kcal mol− 1.21 The loss of buried hydrogen bond is, however, neutral11 because it is compensated by increase of the hydrophobicity on mutation Tyr to Phe. A similar effect upon mutation of Tyr to Phe has been observed for barnase.22 The pairwise sequence identity between G. gallus and H. sapiens p53 DBDs is 70% (Fig. 8a). Most of the amino acid substitutions are located on the protein surface. Some of these involve hydrophobic-tocharged or neutral-to-charged residue changes. G. gallus p53 DBD displays slightly higher hydropathy (GRAVY) and aliphatic indexes that can be also indicative of elevated thermostability. p53 DBDs from poikilotherm vertebrates, X. laevis, D. rerio and R. marmoratus, have the lowest stability amongst the p53 domains. Thus, our results suggest that changes in the surface composition account for the thermal stability of chicken p53. Particularly interesting are N268R, Q165K and Q167E substitutions. The guanidinyl group of Arg268 is within close distance to the main-chain carbonyl groups of Leu111 and Ser269 and could potentially engage in stabilizing hydrogen bonds. As such, it could act similarly to the N268D mutation, the structural effect of which has been recently described.13 The side chains of Lys165 and Glu167 could participate in a salt-bridge formation.

Discussion We examined the thermodynamic stability of orthologous p53 DBDs using DSC and equilibrium urea denaturation. The p53 DBDs analysed in this study fall broadly into the two large groups of poikilotherms and homeotherms. Poikilotherms are sensitive to the environmental temperature and migrate to warmer environments in order to regulate their body temperature. In contrast, homeotherms such as birds and mammals are less sensitive to the environmental changes and maintain a relatively constant body temperature at varying ambient temperature. Our data suggest that the thermal unfolding of the p53 DBDs from homeothermic species correlates with the organismal body temperature. Birds have higher body temperature than marsupials and eutherians. Correspondingly, we find that p53 DBD from G. gallus has a higher thermal stability than mammals, with a Tm of 52.6 °C. Mammalian (eutherians) p53 has Tms within a narrow range of 45.2 °C to 47.1 °C. Their Tms are only ∼7–8 °C higher than the organismal body temperature that ranges between 37 °C and 40 °C. The only exception is M. domestica (marsupial) p53 DBD, which has a melting temperature of 45.6 °C, but a body temperature around 33 °C. Despite the differences in their

The p53 DBDs from poikilotherm vertebrates (X. laevis, D. rerio and R. marmoratus) are less stable than those from homeotherm vertebrates, with melting temperatures from 39.1 °C to 42.6 °C. Amongst these, the X. laevis p53 DBD shows the lowest stability under both thermal and urea denaturation and has a melting temperature at around 39.1 °C. This temperature-sensitive behaviour of X. laevis p53, being inactive at 37 °C and active at 33 °C in human cells, has been previously reported.29 The temperature-sensitive behaviour of p53 has also been demonstrated for another amphibian, urodele.30 p53 DBDs from homeothermic organisms are kinetically unstable with a half-life for denaturation between 10 and 20 min at the optimal body temperature. Our results correlate well with the measurements of in vivo half-life of WT p53 in human and mouse fibroblast cells, which is between 15 and 30 min.31–33 p53 has probably evolved to be kinetically unstable at the body temperature of these organisms. An increase in body temperature of a few degrees might lead to p53 becoming too unstable for any biological function, whereas a

decrease in body temperature could result in increased p53 in vivo stability. The body temperature of an organism is a balance between the rate at which heat is supplied and the rate at which it is lost, and hence it reflects the metabolic rate. The metabolic rates for mammals and birds are much higher than those for invertebrates, fish and amphibians, and increase in the following sequence [invertebrates, fish, amphibians, reptiles] b [marsupial] b [eutherian] b [birds]. 34,35 There is a direct correlation between the extent of oxidative damage and the metabolic rate of organisms; similarly, typical life span is correlated with its metabolic rate.36 Lowering the ambient temperature of worms and flies, for instance, slows the metabolic rate but also results in concomitant extension of life span.37 Since the rates of oxidative DNA damage are proportional to specific metabolic rate,38,39 species with higher metabolic rate may have high levels of DNA repair. The oxidative DNA damage is a natural inducer of p53, and as such, it may act as a selective force responsible for the evolution of the p53 function and stability. Our study shows that the p53 DBDs from invertebrates (e.g., D. melanogaster and C. elegans) are more stable than the other species analysed in this study. Thus, they closely resemble the p53 paralogs, p63 and p73, which have apparent Tms of 60.1 °C and 52.1 °C, respectively, with correspondingly higher stabilities from urea denaturation.9,10 Under both thermal and urea denaturation, the most stable is p53 from C. elegans with apparent Tm of 53.8 °C and a urea midpoint of unfolding of around 5.21 M. In contrast to vertebrates, D. melanogaster and C. elegans contain a single p53 gene. Both organisms are composed largely of postmitotic cells once they reach adulthood. p53-deficient models in D. melanogaster and C. elegans do not show increased tumour phenotypes and lack aspects of p53 responses.30 Worms and flies have a very short lifespan and do not develop cancer as adults. The current data from both fly and worm suggest that their p53 function is related to control of apoptosis.40 There is no evidence supporting a role for p53 as tumour suppressor in these organisms, suggesting that this function is a late acquisition in vertebrates. Typically, the differences in stability between homologous proteins are determined by the difference in the amino acid sequence. There is a broad agreement that both surface-exposed and buried amino acid residues can be important for the stability of proteins.41–46 Numerous studies comparing homologous mesophilic and thermophilic proteins reinforced the notion for their key contribution to the stability.47,48 Here, we used a structure-guided mutagenesis in order to understand the intrinsic factors responsible for the elevated stability of some p53 orthologs. Several mutants were designed to test the contribution of buried residues to the p53 stability by introducing the corresponding nonconserved residues from more stable p53 homologs into human p53. These mutations increase the aliphatic and hydropathy indexes of the buried residues and are expected to improve the hydrophobic packing in the protein interior by providing favourable van der Waals interactions. Mutation of the surface residues of human p53 to residues in equivalent positions in the chicken p53 results in increase of the thermal stability. This stabilization is probably due to specific side chain–side chain interactions and multiple interactions with water molecules. According to the current sequence data, the p53 family diverged from a common ancestor that was more similar to p63. This is evident from the invertebrate p53-like sequences that are more similar to p63 and p73 than to p53. There is strong evidence that two gene duplication events probably have occurred early in the metazoan evolution after the divergence of the two bilaterian lineages: protostomes and deuterostomes. While the vertebrate p53 diverged earlier after the first gene duplication, the second gene duplication resulted in the divergence of p63 and p73. Evolutionary processes, either neutral or adaptive, involve acquisition of mutations that may affect the protein stability. Most mutations in the course of protein evolution are thought to be incorporated through a random drift and, therefore, unless there is a selective pressure for changing the stability of protein, the stability of two homologous proteins should be similar. Under selective pressure, however, there will be accumulation of amino acid substitutions that will result in a change in the stability of the protein. The overall trend in the stability of p53 homologs analysed here strongly suggests that p53 was probably a subject of selective evolutionary pressure. We propose a model in which p53 evolved from a more stable ancestral protein. The invertebrate p53like gene, as a single copy, was under strict evolutionary pressure and probably retained the stability of its ancestor. Following the gene duplication event, one of the copies may have accumulated mutations that otherwise would have been counterselected. Moreover, the functions of the ancestral p53/p63 duplicates were initially redundant, but due to the absence of pressure to maintain the particular function, one of the gene copies may have acquired a novel function. The acquisition of novel function has probably imposed a new requirement for a protein turnover and stability within the cell. Thus, in the process of divergence, the poikilotherm vertebrate p53 was under different selective pressure than its paralogs, p63 and p73, and evolved to be less stable. The emergence of homeothermy demanded a higher metabolic rate that correlates with the extent of oxidative damage. Consequently, the p53 function and stability underwent profound specialization because of the increase in the rate of tissue renewal leading to increased risk of cancer formation. In the course of vertebrate evolution, p53 has probably evolved to be kinetically unstable at the organismal temperature with a short half-life in the cell to allow a mechanism for spontaneous degradation in addition

to regulatory pathways such as Hdm2. This rapid turnover of p53 ensured that the protein will remain no longer in the cell than required, unless when it is in complex with DNA or other proteins.

The aliphatic index of a protein was calculated according to the following formula: Aliphatic Index = xA + axV + b (xI + xL), where xA, xV, xI and xL are mole percent (100 × mol fraction) of alanine, valine, isoleucine and leucine.53 The coefficients a and b are the relative volumes of aliphatic side chains to that of alanine side chains. These values were a = 2.9 ± 0.1 and b = 3.9 ± 0.1, based on the volume of amino acid residues in proteins. The GRAVY index was calculated as the sum of hydropathy values (Kyte and Doolittle scale54) of all the amino acids, divided by the number of residues in the sequence. Solvent accessibility was calculated with NACCESS55 using a probe radius of 1.4 Å. The residue accessibility was determined as a sum of the atomic accessible surface areas over each residue. The relative accessibility of each residue was computed as the percent accessibility compared to the accessibility of that residue type in an extended Ala-X-Ala tripeptide.
