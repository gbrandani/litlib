SUMMARY

In eukaryotic genomes, transcriptional machinery and nucleosomes compete for binding to DNA sequences; thus, a crucial aspect of gene regulatory element function is to modulate chromatin accessibility for transcription factor (TF) and RNA polymerase binding. Recent structural studies have revealed multiple modes of TF engagement with nucleosomes, but how initial ‘‘pioneering’’ results in steady-state DNA accessibility for further TF binding and RNA polymerase II (RNAPII) engagement has been unclear. Even less well understood is how distant sites of open chromatin interact with one another, such as when developmental enhancers activate promoters to release RNAPII for productive elongation. Here, we review evidence for the centrality of the conserved SWI/SNF family of nucleosome remodeling complexes, both in pioneering and in mediating enhancer-promoter contacts. Consideration of the nucleosome unwrapping and ATP hydrolysis activities of SWI/SNF complexes, together with their architectural features, may reconcile steady-state TF occupancy with rapid TF dynamics observed by live imaging. INTRODUCTION Eukaryotic genomes are packaged into nucleosomes, where DNA is tightly wrapped around histone octamers.1 Although nucleosomes are necessary for packaging DNA into the small space of a nucleus and for mitotic condensation of chromosomes, the arrangement of these beads on a chromosomal string limits access to almost three-quarters of the DNA sequence of a genome. Thus, nucleosomes are the predominant structures blocking transcription factors (TFs) from binding to genomic DNA, and so their positions along the genome must be controlled in gene promoters and enhancers to allow for transcriptional regulation.2 A critical aspect of gene regulation is to clear nucleosomes from gene promoters and enhancers; thus, active regulatory elements appear as ‘‘open’’ where exposed DNA sequences can be bound by TFs. Open chromatin was first described for promoters using DNase I as an accessibility reagent.3,4 With advances in DNA sequencing technologies, the use of DNase I and other accessibility reagents, such as DNA methyltransferases5 and Tn5 transposase,6 have provided powerful tools for the high-resolution identification of functional regulatory elements. The popularity of assays for identification of open chromatin in bulk and single-cell and single-molecule contexts, and the abundant evidence that open chromatin correlates with gene regulatory processes, belies how little we truly understand about how open chromatin is established and maintained. Structural studies have confirmed that TFs can stably bind to nucleosomal surfaces in a variety of configurations and that nucleosomebound TFs can cooperate with one another.7 But just how these TF-pioneering processes result in open chromatin requires understanding of the dynamics of nucleosome eviction. Nucleo-

some eviction requires torque and energy, and this focuses our attention on nucleosome remodelers—ATP-dependent DNA translocases that have evolved to mobilize nucleosomes—and, in particular, the SWItch/sucrose non-fermentable (SWI/SNF) family.8 RNA polymerase II (RNAPII) also plays an active role in maintaining open chromatin, as paused RNAPII is found in both orientations at promoters and enhancers9 and is itself a DNA translocase. Here, we will focus on evidence for the roles of SWI/SNF family complexes in establishing and maintaining open chromatin at active regulatory elements and ask whether these processes can shed light on the as-yet unresolved question of how enhancers can act on promoters at a distance. These considerations suggest targets for upstream and downstream processes catalyzed by histone-modifying enzymes and histone chaperones that may promote gene activation through facilitating remodeling. According to this view, pioneering is driven by both sequence-specific (i.e., genetic) TFs and nonsequence-specific (i.e., epigenetic) machineries, such as SWI/ SNF remodelers, RNAPII, and other chromatin regulators. TF PIONEERING Although physical properties of DNA sequences—such as their stiffness—contribute to generating open chromatin by antagonizing wrapping of DNA around a histone octamer, such properties are relatively rare in eukaryotic genomes. Instead, the binding of TFs themselves sterically excludes histone octamers across the same DNA sequence,10,11 thereby maintaining open chromatin. More perplexing is how TFs initially bind on a chromatinized template. It has long been recognized that there must be at least some TFs that can pioneer the reorganization of chromatinized regulatory elements,12 and further elaboration of this idea has recognized that such pioneer TFs should engage with histones and with nucleosomal DNA.13 The structural characterization of exactly such compound moieties in the past three years has revealed, with exquisite detail, how multiple pioneer factors engage chromatin, proving the nucleosome-binding model of pioneer factor action.14 Indeed, a number of the characterized factors are biologically crucial for triggering developmental programs, underlining the importance of nucleosome-binding activities for accessing chromatinized regulatory elements. Surveys of where TFs can bind nucleosomal DNA have shown a wide diversity along the length of wrapped DNA, from the edge to the center of nucleosomes.7,15 The effect of nucleosomes on TF binding is determined by how a DNA-binding domain interacts with its cognate motif and how that motif is presented around the wrapped nucleosome.16 The turn of the DNA double-helix prevents factors that bind long motifs from accessing the length of their site when the motif is wrapped around a histone octamer; this is what limits binding of many factors to DNA that is dynamically unpeeling. Thus, most nucleosome-binding factors must bind to short motifs. A factor need not have only one kind of interaction with chromatin when it has alternative potential binding motifs. For example, the pluripotency TF Sox2 binds to a 7-bp motif, allowing it to recognize DNA wrapped around the nucleosome, with its motif rotationally positioned facing out and away from the histone-DNA interface.17 However, factor binding is also influ-

enced by synergistic interactions with other factors and antagonistic structural clashes. Structural determination of the nucleosome complexed with the Sox2 and Oct4 pluripotency factors identified that these two factors bind a composite 12-bp motif located within the breathing portion close to the edge of the nucleosome (near superhelical location 6 [SHL 6]; Figure 1). Because of the longer length of the composite motif, these two factors trap unpeeled DNA, potentiating further unwrapping. Sox2 bound within a nucleosome preferentially binds near the dyad center of the nucleosome across one DNA helix because around the rest of the nucleosome it would clash with the second wrapped helix. However, Sox2 binding near the nucleosome dyad axis (close to SLH 2) also distorts the wrapped DNA and, in this position, has a dramatic effect on nucleosome stability because loosening DNA in this region can rapidly convert to loss of the histone octamer from DNA or eviction of the nucleosome. A core idea that emerges from these structural studies is the importance of some unwrapped DNA in the engaged structures. Although this explains how factor binding itself can contribute to further unwrapping of a nucleosome,15 it begs the question of how DNA gets unwrapped in the first place to allow stable engagement. This problem was addressed by Widom and colleagues, who used a restriction enzyme accessibility assay to demonstrate spontaneous unwrapping at the nucleosomal edge or the entry-exit sites that allow TF binding.18 In vivo evidence for site-exposure was our demonstration that high levels of yeast GAL4 sufficed to open chromatin silenced by classical heterochromatin in both mitotically active and differentiating cells in Drosophila.19 As yeast GAL4 lacks pioneering activity in mammalian cells,20 it seemed possible that pioneering in Drosophila does not require a pioneer factor. However, in the first step of Drosophila zygotic genome activation the Zelda pioneer TF finds its binding sites in an undifferentiated chromatin landscape, followed by the actions of other pioneer TFs that regulate developmental processes.7,21 Recent work has shown that FOXA1 and HNF4 (respectively a pioneer and a non-pioneer factor active during liver development) are equivalent in being able to open chromatin sites defined by assay for transposaseaccessible chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-seq) in a nonliver cell type (human K562 cells).22,23 In contrast, a different study showed that in human fibroblasts, FOXA1 prefers DNase I occluded sites 2:1, whereas HNF4 prefers DNase I hypersensitive sites 6:1 (Lerner et al.24). In many situations, sites of pioneer factor binding are tissue-specific, implying that they must cooperate with tissue-specific factors for stable binding.25 Further, in some settings, accessibility precedes pioneer factor expression and binding.26 These studies support the notion that even for nucleosome-binding pioneer factors, nucleosome dynamics in vivo must contribute to the formation of open chromatin and activation of regulatory elements.27 For most nucleosomes, the entering and exiting DNA at its edges are in a constant state of transient thermal DNA unwrapping and rewrapping of a histone octamer. Thus, a major mechanism of promoting transcriptional activation is to modulate the dynamics of nucleosome unpeeling. Such unpeeling can be enhanced by the activities of chromatin remodelers28 and by the effects of various DNA sequences on nucleosome stability.

THE SWI/SNF-RELATED RSC REMODELER MAINTAINS PROMOTER ACCESSIBILITY IN YEAST The catalytic subunit of the first nucleosome remodelers to be characterized, yeast Swi2/Snf229 and Drosophila Brahma,30 are components of multi-megadalton complexes, collectively referred to as SWI/SNF complexes. In mammals, three distinct SWI/SNF complexes have been biochemically characterized: cBAF for canonical Brahma-associated factor, PBAF for polybromo BAF, and ncBAF for non-canonical BAF.31 The subunit composition of the yeast Swi2/Snf2 containing SWI/ SNF complex32,33 is similar to that of mammalian cBAF,34,35 and the SWI/SNF-related remodeling the structure of chromatin (RSC) complex,36,37 is similar to that of PBAF.38 RSC is the only essential remodeler in yeast, and depletion of RSC reduces the distance between the +1 and 1 nucleosomes flanking transcription start sites (TSSs) of most yeast genes.39 This observation has been interpreted as RSC pushing nucleosomes apart to open chromatin at TSSs,40 opposed by the nucleosome spacing action of the other three classes of conserved nucleosome remodelers, ISWI, INO80, and CHD1.41 Consistent with its role in maintaining promoters free of nucleosomes, RSC is a powerful DNA translocase with the potential of evicting nucleosome cores.42 The cryoelectron microscopy (cryo-EM) structure of RSC bound to a nucleosome shows it to consist of 16 subunits, where the ATP-dependent DNA translocase subunit, Sth1, binds tightly to the nucleosome, making both DNA and histone contacts.36,37 Two additional subunits, Rsc3 and Rsc30, contain DNA interaction modules that bind poly(A) and GC-rich motifs often found in yeast gene promoter sequences.43,44 These interactions bring RSC into close proximity with the first promoter nucleosome, where it works together with essential sequence-specific general regulatory factors (GRFs), including Abf1 and Reb1, to maintain promoter accessibility.45,46 Although GRFs resemble pioneer factors in their action, they do not ‘‘recruit’’ RSC in that loss of a GRF from a promoter does not result in displacement of RSC from flanking 1 and +1 nucleosomes, where RSC is most en196 Molecular Cell 84, January 18, 2024

SWI/SNF-RELATED BAF COMPLEXES MAINTAIN OPEN CHROMATIN IN MULTICELLULAR EUKARYOTES In mammals, the counterparts of yeast Swi2/Snf2 and Sth1 and Drosophila Brahma are the BRG1 and BRM DNA translocases, which are found in all three biochemically characterized mammalian SWI/SNF family complexes. These translocases, together with three additional proteins, form a central scaffold for alternative subunits; some of these alternatives are different isoforms and others are tissue-specific components. cBAF and PBAF are the most abundant and predominantly localize to enhancers and promoters, with multiple tissue-specific variant complexes that may confer specific regulatory roles in those cells.31 Cryo-EM structures of cBAF34,35 and PBAF38 show high structural concordance, respectively, with yeast SWI/SNF32,33 and RSC complexes.36,37 In both mammalian complexes, BRG1 binds across the DNA gyres just after they start to wrap around a nucleosome and twists that DNA off the surface of the histone octamer near SHL 2, while multiple other subunits of the complex hold on to the histones like a vise (Figure 2). Binding of ATP by BRG1 further cranks on the distorted DNA, and subsequent ATP hydrolysis and release pumps the DNA across the surface

of a histone octamer, thereby altering nucleosome conformation. The result is that simple binding of BAF can contribute to unwrapping nucleosomal DNA, and repeated cycles of ATP binding and hydrolysis can completely unwrap a histone octamer, leading to its eviction from chromatin.39 Thus, chromatin remodeling plays a central function in making DNA accessible and, indeed, BAF binds predominantly at transcriptional enhancers where it is required for their activation. Although these translocases are constitutively expressed in all cells, their remodeling activity promotes the binding of TFs and thereby can assist the pioneering of new binding sites by developmental TFs. An important difference between yeast RSC and animal BAF complexes is that there is no sequence-specific counterpart in any of the mammalian structures to the DNA-interaction module of RSC. As pointed out above, the DNA interaction module can account for the ability of RSC to bind yeast promoters in the absence of a GRF47 and partially unwrap nucleosomes.48,53,54 There are multiple DNA interaction domains in the various BAF complexes that might serve an analogous role, but in fact the lack of sequence-specific binding by BAF is probably a functional necessity in that these complexes must bind to essentially every active enhancer in the cell. So, if the function of the RSC DNA interaction module is to keep yeast RSC in place during remodeling cycles, what keeps mammalian BAF in place? BAF subunits interact with multiple TFs, histone modifications, and non-coding RNAs, and these may serve to stabilize binding at active target regulatory elements.55 BAF binding is also enhanced by RNAPII in vivo, particularly its promoter-paused isoform.56 Although these interactions are recognized to stabilize active chromatin, the first establishment of binding is often considered to be recruitment by some new factor. However, an alternative is that factors such as BAF and RNAPII may be transiently sampling the genome. This would futilely increase nucleosome dynamics but without stable binding. The apparent specificity of accessible chromatin sites would thus result when cooperatively binding TFs interact, producing stable nucleosome-depleted regions at active promoters and enhancers. Support for this model comes from the observation that druginduced increase of paused RNAPII stabilizes the binding of BAF in silenced chromatin regions, where neither is present without drug treatment.56 The appearance of these proteins implies that transient binding of BAF (and RNAPII) occurs broadly across the genome but is undetectable. This model explains how newly expressed pioneer factors rapidly bind distinctive collections of sites in a tissue-specific manner. Maintaining high concentrations of BAF at regulatory elements is critical in light of evidence that BAF is continuously required to maintain open chromatin.57,58 These observations lead us to ask whether the presence of BAF at enhancers and promoters, and its requirement for maintaining DNA accessibility, might provide a clue to the mechanisms whereby enhancers activate promoters.

Also, super-resolution and live-cell imaging studies show that contacts are transient64,65 and how close two sites come to each other is not clear.66 Thus, stable enhancer-promoter contacts are unlikely to be responsible for transcriptional stimulation. Finally, there are cases in which direct contact between an enhancer and a promoter does not appear to be involved at all.67,68 These problems with contact models have led to the idea that enhancers may radiate a diffusible component that crosses over to nearby promoters and stimulates them.59 In the authors’ TF activation gradient model, the lysine acetyltransferases CBP and P300, which are abundant at enhancers, acetylate TFs that diffuse to promoters and activate transcription. However, CBP/P300 inhibition only slowly reduces open chromatin, and only at a small subset of sites, despite rapid global inhibition of transcription,69 which implies that promoter nucleosomes have already been evicted by the time that acetylated pioneer TFs are on the scene. An alternative possibility is that even a transient contact between an enhancer and a promoter is enough to stimulate transcription.70 Widespread transient unwrapping likely occurs as abundant chromatin remodelers sample the genome, but cooperative interactions between chromatin proteins would initiate regulatory events.28 In support of this idea, in the Drosophila early embryo where the timing of activation has been resolved, enhancers become accessible before their promoters.21 Very recent studies suggest that a ‘‘kiss-and-run’’ mechanism applies to the vast majority of enhancer-promoter contacts. Very deep sequencing of Hi-C libraries, together with heuristics to solve the otherwise intractable computational challenge, has increased the resolution of Hi-C contact mapping to 500 bp.71 This revealed that the alternating higher-order A/B compartment structure in fact consists of large, featureless B compartments punctuated by multiple open chromatin sites that are in close contact with each other. A complementary approach used Micro-C with oligonucleotide capture of large regions of the human genome to obtain nucleosome resolution, also observing small punctate A compartments.72 In the latter study, such ‘‘little-a’’ microcompartments included enhancer-promoter interactions. These interactions were robust to loss of loop extrusion and of transcription, leaving open the question of just what an enhancer might provide to a promoter to activate transcription. BAF is ideally suited to provide the missing link between enhancers and promoters. Direct evidence comes from a study in which increased levels of SMARCB1, a central nucleosomebinding subunit of cBAF and PBAF, increases remodeling and accessibility at enhancers.73 Strikingly, increased levels of SMARCB1 also caused a dramatic increase in loop density, direct evidence that BAF is a limiting factor governing promoter-enhancer contacts.

We consider two models for how BAF present on an enhancer might rapidly activate a promoter, one based on thermodynamic considerations and the other based on direct hand-off. Using a restriction site exposure assay, one study estimated that a single round of human BRG1 remodeling hydrolyzes 1,000 ATPs to ADP, with similar estimates for purified BAF.75 Perhaps the heat generated by enhancer-bound BAF undergoing futile cycles of ATP hydrolysis is directly transferred to RNAPII stalled at the promoter pause-release site and that would be sufficient to increase the probability of a transcriptional burst. A limitation of this model is that a back-of-the-envelope calculation estimates that hydrolysis of 10,000 ATPs is needed to increase the temperature of a volume the size of BAF by 1 C, and it is difficult to envision how this thermodynamic model could be experimentally falsified. An alternative model is motivated in part by the observation that Drosophila BAF hydrolyzes ATP to release from chromatin in vivo.76 Contact with a promoter-flanking nucleosome would trigger ATP hydrolysis and release of an enhancer-bound BAF (Figure 3). Hand-off would be mediated by the large overall C-shaped structure of the non-catalytic BAF domains, with many non-specific DNA-binding surfaces that loop out from the DNA translocase.34,35,38 The BAF non-catalytic C-shaped core would reach out, anchor onto a nearby promoter nucleosome, followed by ATP hydrolysis to release the DNA translocase module from the enhancer nucleosome. Because BAF subunits contain many intrinsically disordered regions that promote condensate formation within the nucleus,77,78 bridging might be assisted by high local concentrations of TFs near interacting promoters and enhancers.79 The frequency of bursting and the observed stability of a contact would thus depend on the number of BAF hand-off cycles while the enhancer and promoter are in continuous contact. The specificity of such contacts would be enhanced by cooperatively binding TFs and histone modifications that stabilize specific enhancer-promoter connections.37,39 This hand-off model is parsimonious in that it does not require any unknown diffusible component and is potentially an ancestral mechanism for gene activation in that it only requires machineries that are common to all eukaryotes. PERSPECTIVE AND CONCLUSIONS Our current view of TF binding has been enriched by structural studies showing a variety of ways that different pioneer TFs can engage a nucleosome16 and by genomic studies documenting cooperativity across a nucleosome without requiring direct contact.80 However, remodeling of the nucleosome core to open chromatin at enhancers and promoters requires the energy and torque provided by the SWI/SNF family DNA translocases. In vitro, the BRG1 catalytic subunit by itself can remodel a nucleosome as well as the full BAF complex.75 Yet, the cryo-EM structure of BAF shows that the bulk of the 185-kDa BRG1 protein is bound to the nucleosome core particle, with a long alpha-helical extension connecting it to a 1.25-MDa C-shaped core, and other SWI/SNF complexes display a similar architecture.35 The noncatalytic bulk of the BAF core is critical for in vivo function and some of the subunits, such as ARID1A, are among the most frequently mutated proteins in human cancers.8,81–83 These observations raise the question of how the non-catalytic bulk of the BAF complex might contribute to its molecular function at enhancers and promoters. Intriguingly, a recent study reports that intrinsically disordered regions of the ARID1A subunit are crucial for cBAF activity, linking condensate formation to efficient chromatin remodeling.78 Our hand-off model provides a testable rationale for this conserved feature of SWI/SNF complexes that

is consistent with the transience of contact interactions,64 with the ubiquity of contacts between open chromatin sites,71,72 and with the role of BAF in mediating functional enhancer-promoter contacts.73 BAF remodeling and hand-off also provides a mechanism for epigenetic memory of gene expression, which persists through chromatin disruption during DNA replication and during mitosis. In Drosophila, Brahma is conspicuously abundant behind the replication fork, whereas Ino80, Iswi, and Chd1 are not, so that it is poised to return to newly replicated DNA.84 Brahma opposes Polycomb silencing,30 as does human BAF,85 and the relatively slow trimethylation of H3K27 post-replication86 means that BAF is likely to load before epigenetic silencing can be re-established.87 Finally, the BAF non-catalytic core has recently been shown to occupy gene regulatory elements during mitosis, providing a general ‘‘bookmark’’ that mediates cellular memory when one cell divides into two.88 Taken together, these observations establish SWI/SNF family remodelers as the general currency of epigenetic inheritance, maintaining open chromatin continuously for enhancer and promoter function.
