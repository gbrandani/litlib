Structures of the ISWI–nucleosome complex reveal a conserved mechanism of chromatin remodeling Lijuan Yan1,2,7, Hao Wu2,3,7, Xuemei Li4, Ning Gao5* and Zhucheng Chen

*

1,2,6

Chromatin remodelers are diverse enzymes, and different models have been proposed to explain how these proteins work. Here we report the 3.3 Å-resolution cryogenic electron microscopy (cryo-EM) structures of Saccharomyces cerevisiae ISWI (ISW1) in complex with the nucleosome in adenosine diphosphate (ADP)-bound and ADP-BeFx-bound states. The data show that after nucleosome binding, ISW1 is activated by substantial rearrangement of the catalytic domains, with the regulatory AutoN domain packing the first RecA-like core and the NegC domain being disordered. The high-resolution structure reveals local DNA distortion and translocation induced by ISW1 in the ADP-bound state, which is essentially identical to that induced by the Snf2 chromatin remodeler, suggesting a common mechanism of DNA translocation. The histone core remains largely unperturbed, and prevention of histone distortion by crosslinking did not inhibit the activity of yeast ISW1 or its human homolog. Together, our findings suggest a general mechanism of chromatin remodeling involving local DNA distortion without notable histone deformation.

hromatin remodelers are helicase-like proteins that include diverse enzymes1, such as Snf2, ISWI, Ino80 and Chd1. These enzymes share two conserved catalytic core domains, which by themselves are able to couple adenosine triphosphate (ATP) hydrolysis to chromatin remodeling. Association of the catalytic cores with auxiliary domains and other subunits within the remodeling complexes regulates the activities of the remodeling enzymes1–5. The mechanism of how these enzymes overcome the many histone-DNA contacts to slide nucleosomes has been extensively studied. Based on the well-studied helicase proteins, a ‘DNA wave’ model of chromatin remodeling was proposed2,6–10. Recent cryoEM structures of the Snf2–nucleosome complex illustrate the DNA-Snf2 interactions in different nucleotide states, providing insights into the mechanism of DNA translocation underlying chromatin remodeling11,12. Different from Snf2, ISWI slides but does not disassemble the nucleosome and its activity is highly regulated by the auxiliary domains and nucleosomal epitopes13. The ATPase motor of ISWI is active in chromatin remodeling14, but it is tightly auto-inhibited in the absence of nucleosomes15. The N-terminal AutoN domain functions as a negative element, competing with the H4 tail and inhibiting the activity of the enzyme. The enzyme is also inhibited by the NegC domain, which is in turn antagonized by the C-terminal DNA-binding domain (DBD)13,15,16. A recent study reported that binding of human ISWI (Snf2h) induces marked histone deformation, which is essential for enzyme activity17. It was proposed that diverse remodeling enzymes exploit the plasticity of the histone octamer in different ways, and mobilize the nucleosome through distinct mechanisms17–19. However, this idea is controversial as the catalytic motors of chromatin remodelers are highly conserved and histone deformation is not required for

the activities of related chromatin remodelers, such as the SWI/SNF, Ino80 and RSC complexes17,20–22. To provide insight into how ISWI interacts with the nucleosome, we report the cryo-EM structure of yeast ISWI (ISW1) in complex with the mononucleosome in different nucleotide states and biochemical analyses performed. Our findings suggest a conserved mechanism, but distinct regulation, of chromatin remodeling.

Structural determination of the ISW1–nucleosome complex

The structures of the ISW1–nucleosome complexes were determined using single-particle cryo-EM (Fig. 1, Table 1 and Supplementary Figs. 1–5). ISW1 (residues 69–1129) from S. cerevisiae formed a complex with the nucleosome in the presence of a stable ATP analog (ADP-BeFx) using two methods of sample preparation. Initially, to avoid glutaraldehyde fixation, we mixed the enzyme with the nucleosome and subjected the samples to direct vitrification. However, most of the complex disassociated. To compensate for auto-inhibition by AutoN15, the inhibitory L3 loop was removed which resulted in a highly active ISW1 mutant (ISW1ΔL) (Supplementary Fig. 1a). To further increase the stability of the complex, EV71 virus was added as supporting material before the samples were subjected to vitrification. Stable complexes were obtained and ISW1 was found to bind the nucleosome at SHL +2 or SHL –2, which adopt essentially the same structures (Supplementary Fig. 2c,d). To increase the effective number of particles in map reconstruction, we combined these data and achieved a final map at an overall resolution of 3.87 Å, with a higher resolution of the nucleosome at 3.56 Å. The resulting high-resolution map clearly resolves the positions of the bound co-factors, the backbone phosphate groups, some bulky sides of ISW1 and the histones (Supplementary Fig. 3). Activation of ISW1 by nucleosome engagement

ISWI is locked in an inhibited state in the absence of the nucleosome15. Nucleosome binding activates ISW1 by triggering a dramatic

conformational change of the enzyme (Fig. 2a). Superimposition of the structures of lobe1 in the ADP-BeFx–nucleosome-bound state and the free state indicates that lobe2 rotates ~148°, leading to the formation of a new lobe1–lobe2 cleft as the primary DNA-binding site (Supplementary Fig. 7a). AutoN was implicated in histone deformation17 and binding to the acidic patch of the H2A-H2B24. Different from these hypotheses, our data show that AutoN packs against SuppH in the nucleosomebound state, distal to the histone core (Fig. 2b). Following ISW1 activation, SuppH, which is key to maintaining the active conformation of the enzyme11,15, displays an obvious movement and the helical bundle (α1–α3) of AutoN shows a coordinated adjustment (Supplementary Fig. 7b). To demonstrate the significance of the AutoN-SuppH interaction, we mutated this binding interface (F119D). The data show that disruption of the AutoN-SuppH interaction slightly increased ATPase activity but markedly reduced chromatin remodeling activity (Fig. 2c,d). Thus, AutoN plays a dual role in ISWI regulation. It not only holds ISWI in auto-inhibition in the absence of nucleosome, but also supports remodeling activity through interaction with SuppH in the activated state. The critical function of AutoN in packing against SuppH would probably make improbable any dissociation from the catalytic core to interact with histones during chromatin remodeling. Lobe2 also undergoes some local conformation adjustments following nucleosome binding (Fig. 2e). In particular, due to interaction with nucleosomal DNA, the insertion domain (core2i) becomes partially melted. Intact histone core in the presence of ISW1

Based on nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) data, it was proposed that ISWI in the ADP-BeFx state induces distortion of the histone octamer, particularly at the binding site17. However, the structure of the histone octamer of the nucleosome bound by ISW1 based on cryo-EM data retains a largely identical structure to that of the unbound state29, with a root mean squared deviation of 0.92 Å over the entire histone octamer (~5,500 atoms, Fig. 5a). Neither the H3–H4 tetramer nor the H2A–H2B dimer shows obvious displacement following ISW1 binding. Specifically, deformation of the histone octamer at the dyad (SHL0) of the nucleosome was proposed to be critical for Snf2h activity17. However, our structure of the ISW1–nucleosome complex indicates that the two copies of H3 in the bound nucleosome maintain their structures as in the unbound form (Fig. 5b and Supplementary Fig. 3e). Previous data also suggest that the environment of several hydrophobic residues buried in the H4 core is altered following ISWI binding17, although our cryo-EM structure indicates that the histone core is not altered markedly, with the main chains of the helices well defined and some bulky side chains clearly resolved (Figs. 5c,d and Supplementary Fig. 3f). To rule out the possibility that the data are compatible with the type of conformational changes that could lead to the NMR measurements, we used Rosetta to refine the structure. Excepting the flexible H4 tails, the regions predicted to undergo conformational

change converged to a unified model that has no marked change relative to the native nucleosome (Supplementary Fig. 8). Marked chhanges in backbone conformation are thus limited, but changes in the side chains remain a possibility. The reason for the discrepancy in histone distortion detected by NMR and cryo-EM is unclear. It is possible that the changes detected by NMR may have resulted from induced protein dynamics on a millisecond–microsecond timescale17, which is associated with different rotation conformers of the side chains, but would not be detected in the frozen samples used in the cryo-EM studies.

No requirement for histone deformation in chromatin remodeling by ISWI

In contrast to a previous report17, human Snf2h and yeast ISW1 efficiently remodeled the crosslinked nucleosome, with activities ~70% of that towards the uncrosslinked nucleosome (Fig. 6a,b,e). Moreover, reverting the S–S crosslinkage with dithiothreitol (DTT) did not lead to marked increase in activity. Comparable results were obtained using a substrate oxidized by Cu-phen (Fig. 6e and Supplementary Fig. 9d–f). To avoid potential H3–H3 crosslinking, we repeated the experiments using crosslinked nucleosomes carrying the H3–C110A mutation (Supplementary Fig. 9g)30. Interestingly, the enzymes indeed showed markedly reduced activity towards the crosslinked H3–C110A mutant nucleosome, by a factor of 4 (Fig. 6c–e). Discussion

The mechanism of chromatin remodeling has been pursued for a long time, and different models involving DNA and histone distortion were proposed. A potential requirement for the flexibility of the histone core represents a change in perspective from the DNA distortion model18,19. Our findings argue against the idea of histone deformation during chromatin remodeling and suggest that a general mechanism governs different chromatin remodelers. Snf2h was proposed to deform the histone octamer through its family-specific regulatory domains17. Our structure suggests that it lacks extra auxiliary elements to enforce histone deformation. The N-terminal AutoN does not contact the nucleosome directly. Instead, AutoN interacts with SuppH of lobe1 in a manner analogous to the binding of postHSA to SuppH in Snf2 (ref. 11), playing a key role in regulating the remodeling activity of the enzyme. The structure of the C-terminal DBD could not be solved, presumably because of its dynamic interaction with the linker DNA. Consistent with the lack of histone deformation, H3–H4 crosslinking in the context of the nucleosome has minimal impact on the activities of both human Snf2h and yeast ISW1. While we found no obvious histone deformation, DNA distortion was clearly detected in the ADP-bound state, which is consistent with the model of DNA translocation mediated by Snf2 (ref. 12). The similar structures of the ISW1–NCP and Snf2–NCP complexes, and the comparable mode of DNA interaction, suggest a conserved mechanism of coupling the conformational switch of the enzymes to drive DNA translocation. Specifically, following ATP hydrolysis, the enzyme in the ADP-bound state opens the nucleosome by inducing a 1 bp bulge at SHL +2 and DNA translocation from the entry; the DNA distortion is then delivered to the exit side following ATP binding and closing of the nucleosome-binding cleft of the enzyme. Chd1 also binds the nucleosome at SHL +2 via an analogous mechanism31, suggesting that Chd1, and probably other related chromatin remodelers, evolved the same mechanism to achieve nucleosome sliding. Interestingly, Ino80 binds the nucleosome near SHL6 (refs. 32–34). Given the conservation of the motor domain, it might also apply a similar mechanism to initiate DNA translocation but at a different position of the nucleosome. The ISWI-containing ACF complex was found to act as a dimer23. Nevertheless, a recent study showed that a single WT motor is sufficient for remodeling activity and that dimerization is not essential16. Our findings are consistent with the idea that monomeric ISWI is competent in chromatin remodeling, and suggest that the diverse activities of enzymes Snf2 and ISWI may not result from a distinct remodeling mechanism35, but rather from distinct regulation1,2. The weaker activities of ISWI in catalyzing H2A–H2B dimer loss and nucleosome disassembly may be regulated by its auxiliary domains. Snf2 lacks a DBD, and hence it is unable to sense the linker DNA length. In a simple scenario, Snf2 is able to slide a nucleosome into a neighboring one, which, in turn, ‘peels off ’ the proximal DNA end, thus exposing the H2A–H2B dimer36. The dependence of ISWI on the linker DNA would prevent this.

Methods

Protein expression and purification. We cloned the gene of ScISW1 from S. cerevisiae genomic DNA and the gene of HsSnf2h from Homo sapiens complementary DNA (cDNA), respectively, then inserted them into a modified pET-28b vector containing a SUMO tag using NotI and BamHI restriction sites. We chose ScISW1 (residues 69–1,129) and its mutant ISW1ΔL by replacing the H4-like L3 loop (residues 145–160) with a flexible linker (GGASGS) for the structural and biochemical studies. Point mutants were generated by Quickchange mutagenesis. All constructs were confirmed by DNA sequencing. The proteins ISW1 and Snf2h were overexpressed in the Escherichia coli expression strain Rosetta (DE3) induced with 0.5 mM isopropyl-beta-Dthiogalactoside at OD600 ~ 0.8, and cultured at 18 °C overnight. Cells were harvested by centrifugation at 4,000 r.p.m. (Beckman, Rotor JA4.2) for 20 min, resuspended in 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 1 M NaCl and 1 mM phenylmethanesulfonyl fluoride and then disrupted by a high-pressure homogenizer (ATS). After high-speed centrifugation, the supernatant was loaded on an Ni–NTA column and allowed to flow through by gravity. After a washing step, the protein was eluted with 20 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.0, 150 mM NaCl and 200 mM imidazole, and the His6-SUMO tag was cleaved by SUMO Protease (Ulp) at 4 °C. The protein was further purified on an ion-exchange column (Source-15Q, GE Healthcare) with Tris-HCl buffer, pH 8.0, and then subjected to gel-filtration chromatography (Superdex-200, GE Healthcare) in buffer containing 10 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 150 mM NaCl and 5 mM DTT. The purified protein was concentrated to 10 mg ml–1 and stored at −80 °C. H3–H4 crosslinking and nucleosome assembly. The WT histone octamer was assembled from purified histones as described37. Histone variants (H3L82C and H4V81C) were made by site-directed mutagenesis. Cysteine variant histone proteins were purified in the presence of excess DTT during all steps of protein purification. To purify the cysteine variants, only the gel-filtration step was carried out. As described previously11, the ‘601’ DNA fragment with 20 bp linker DNA at one end was used to assemble the nucleosome (167-NCP) used for structure determination in the presence of ADP-BeFx without fixation. Similarly, the nucleosome with 5 bp linker DNA (152-NCP) was used for structure determination under the conditions of glutaraldehyde fixation in the presence of ADP or ADPBeFx. The 207-NCP (601 sequence with 60 bp linker DNA) at one end used for the remodeling assays was made as described previously15. To crosslink nucleosome (H3L82CH4V81C) with the S-S disulfide bond, we adopted two strategies. The first was to catalyze the reaction by Cu-phen38,39. Octamer (H3L82C–H4V81C, 5 μM) was dialyzed against buffer (10 mM Hepes, pH 7.25, 2 M NaCl) for at least 4 h, following which the sample was oxidized with 25 μM:100 μM Cu-phen (CuSO4 and o-phenanthroline were freshly mixed to obtain the Cu-phen solution) for 5–10 min at 25 °C in the dark. The reaction was terminated by 10 mM EDTA. The histone octamers were then purified using Superdex 200 chromatography. The alternative strategy was to oxidize the octamer by dialysis into the crosslinking buffer (20 mM Tris-HCl (pH 8.8), 2 M NaCl, 1 mM EDTA, 1 mM benzamidine, 0.1 mM leupeptin, 1 mM oxidized glutathione) for 24 h at 22 °C in the dark, before Superdex 200 chromatography as described30. The nucleosomes assembled from the crosslinked histone octamer were purified on a 10–30% glycerol gradient to remove free DNA and subnucleosomes. To reverse the S-S crosslinkage, the crosslinked nucleosomes were reduced by DTT (100 mM) at 37 °C for 1 h. Nucleosome centering assays. Nucleosome centering assays were performed basically as described15 with 40 nM Cy5-labeled nucleosomes containing WT and mutant histones, and 80 nM ISW1 and Snf2h, whereas 40 nM ISW1ΔL was used because of its higher activities. Reactions were performed in buffer (50 mM KCl, 20 mM HEPES, pH 7.5, 5 mM MgCl2, 0.1 mg ml–1 bovine serum albumin, 5% glycerol, 1 mM DTT) with 2 mM ATP, and stopped with 150 ng sperm DNA at the indicated time points. The products were resolved with 8% native acrylamide gels, 0.25 × TBE at 4 °C and 150 V for 120–180 min. The positions of Cy5-labeled DNA were detected using a Typhoon 9410 imager (GE Healthcare). Band intensities were quantified in Quantity One software. The fraction of nucleosomes centered at a given time point was determined by the ratio of slow-migrating nucleosomes to total nucleosome intensity. The reaction rate constants were fit to a single exponential decay using Origin9.2. ATPase assays. Measurement of ATP hydrolysis was performed as previously described15, using ISW1 (0.1 μM) and 177-NCP (0.2 μM). Structure refinement by Rosetta. Rosetta was performed to calculate 100 models and obtain the ten with the lowest energy levels, using an experimental 3.87 Å map of the ISW1–NCP complex in the ADP-BeFx-bound state. We implemented a fragment-based refinement function of Rosetta to rebuild H4 (residues 4–101) guided by the map density, and refined the structures using the Rosetta force field augmented with fit-to-density energy as previously described40. We aligned and analyzed the Rosetta-refined ten top-scoring structures with the native 601– nucleosome structure (PDB code, 3MVD), and we refined the structure in three regions predicted to undergo conformational change in histone H4 (residues 24–32, 45–50 and 55–70)17. Root mean square deviations, which include side

chains, were calculated between the Rosetta-refined ten top-scoring structures and native structure. Sample preparation for EM analysis. The fixed ISW1–NCP complexes were obtained by mixing 30 μM protein with 5 μM 152-NCP in the presence of ADPBeFx or by mixing 60 μM protein with 5 μM 152-NCP in the presence of ADP. The resultant mixture was then stabilized and purified using the GraFix method as described11. In the ADP-bound complex, Virus (EV71) was added at a final concentration of 1 mg ml–1 for the following EM sample preparation. The ISW1ΔL–NCP complexes were obtained by mixing 1.5 μM protein with 0.5 μM 167-NCP in the presence of ADP-BeFx. The complex was dialyzed to buffer containing 50 mM NaCl, 10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.5, 1 mM MgCl2, 0.5 mM ADP, 1 mM BeSO4 and 8 mM sodium fluoride. Virus (EV71) was then added to the mixtures to a final concentration of 1 mg ml–1 to prevent dissociation of the complexes for the following EM sample preparation. Electron microscopy. Negative staining of the ISW1–NCP complex was performed with 2% uranyl acetate. Grids were examined using an FEI T12 microscope operated at 120 kV, and images were recorded using a 4 x 4 k charge-coupled device camera (UltraScan 4000, Gatan). For cryo-grid preparation, 4 μl aliquot of sample was applied to a glowdischarged holy carbon grid (Quantifoil R1.2/1.3, 300 mesh), and cryo-freezing was performed with an FEI Vitrobot IV (8 °C and 100% humidity). Grids were examined using an FEI Tecnai Arctica operated at 200 kV. Cryo-images of the fixed ISW1– NCP complexes were recorded using a 300 kV FEI Titan Krios with Cs corrector on an FEI Falcon II camera. Micrographs were collected at a magnification of ×75,000, which renders the pixel size of 0.88 Å at the object scale, and with the defocus ranging from –1.5 to –2.5 μm. Data collection was done under low-dose conditions by a semi-automatic software, AutoEMation II. For each micrograph stack, 27 frames were collected with a total dose of 45 electrons per pixel. Images of ISW1ΔL–NCP complexes were recorded using a 300 kV FEI Titan Krios with a K2 Summit direct electron detector and GIF Quantum energy filter (Gatan) in super-resolution mode, at a nominal magnification of ×29,000, which renders a final pixel size of 0.535 Å at object scale after post-magnification calibration, and with the defocus ranging from −1.5 to −2.5 μm. Images were collected under low-dose conditions using an energy-processing unit and Leginion41. For each micrograph stack, a total of 40 frames were collected at a dose rate of 5.5 counts per physical pixel s–1 for an exposure time of 12 s. Image processing. The initial three-dimensional (3D) model from negatively stained particles was calculated with RELION42 using the nucleosome structure as reference. For cryo-EM data of the ISW1ΔL–NCP complexes, a total of 1,408 dose-fractionated image stacks were subjected to beam-induced motion correction using MotionCor2 suit43, and contrast transfer function (CTF) parameters for each micrograph were determined by CTFFIND4 suit44. Particle selection, twodimensional (2D) classification and 3D classification were performed using RELION on a binned dataset with a pixel size of 1.07 Å. A total of 619,386 particles were initially selected, and multiple rounds of 2D classification were used to discard those in poorly defined classes, resulting in a set of 450,169 particles for further processing. The initial model gained from negative staining was used as a reference for the first round of 3D classification, from which we obtained structures for both bound and unbound nucleosomes. One class with well-resolved structural features (~239,689 particles) was subjected to the second round of 3D classification, which finally resulted in three effective classes: free NCP (53,439 particles), ISW1–NCP complex (102,160 particles) and (ISW1)2–NCP complex (73,364 particles). Subsequently, the set of ISW1–NCP particles were subjected to a third round of 3D classification into five classes using an artificial reference with a protruding linker DNA, which allowed the separation of two alternative binding modes: with ISW1 binding to either SHL +2 or SHL -2. Given the pseudosymmetric nature of the nucleosome, we combined the particles from the two modes (62,121 particles) to achieve a high-resolution structure. The final round of refinement was performed with a soft-edged mask applied, resulting in a 4.05 Å map. After correction for the modulation transfer function of the K2 detector and map sharpening using the post-processing options of RELION, the overall resolution of the final density map within the region defined by the soft mask was 3.87 Å for the overall map. When only the histone core was included for FSC calculation the nominal resolution was 3.62 Å, with many regions higher than 3.5 Å (Fig. 1b and Supplementary Fig. 2e). Similarly, an additional round of 3D classification of (ISW1)2–NCP particles retained 38,258 particles (stable association of two copies of ISW1) for final refinement, resulting in a map at 4.42 Å resolution. A similar strategy was applied to process the fixed ISW1–NCP complexes in the presence of ADP-BeFx (4,118 movie stacks). Briefly, a total of 2,473,226 particles were initially selected and multiple rounds of 2D classification were performed, which resulted in a set of 2,070,652 particles. After the third round of 3D classification using the above-mentioned non-symmetrical reference with a protruding linker DNA, the particles were distributed into five classes. Two of these (316,230 particles) were combined to obtain a final density map at 3.37 Å resolution, which could be improved to 3.25 Å when particles were refined with a soft-edged NCP mask.

[Page 11]

To process the dataset (4,500 movie stacks) of the fixed ISW1–NCP complexes in the presence of ADP, a total of 2,516,969 particles were selected for subsequent 2D and 3D classification. After the third round of 3D classification using the reference mentioned above, 168,430 particles with a defined SHL +2 binding feature were subjected to further 3D refinement, where we gained a map of 3.40 Å density. The resolution was improved to 3.29 Å when the particles were refined with a soft-edged NCP mask. Model building. The structural model was first built by fitting the crystal structures of the NCP (PDB code 3MVD)29 and the individual core domains of MtISWI (PDB code 5JXR)15 to the EM density map using UCSF Chimera45. The model was further refined using phenix.real_real_space46 with secondary structure constrains, and rebuilt manually in Coot. Reporting Summary.
