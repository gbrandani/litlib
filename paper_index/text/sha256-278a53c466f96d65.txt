Phase separation of p53 induced by its unstructured basic region and prevented by oncogenic mutations in tetramerization domain Chen Chen , Gaohong Fu , Quanqiang Guo , Song Xue *, Shi-Zhong Luo * Beijing Key Laboratory of Bioprocess, College of Life Science and Technology, Beijing University of Chemical Technology, Beijing 100029, China

A B S T R A C T

Keywords: p53 Liquid-liquid phase separation Unstructured basic region Tetramerization domain Oncogenic mutations

Liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) drives the formation of extensive membrane-less compartments to regulate various cellular biological activities both physiologically and pathologically. It has been widely accepted that LLPS is closely related to amyloid diseases and increasing reports have linked this phenomenon to cancers. Mutations of tumor suppressor protein p53 exist in more than half of malignant tumors, making the protein vitally important in cancer research. Recently, p53 was reported to undergo phase separation, which may regulate the function of p53. The molecular mechanism of p53 phase separation and how this process relates to cancer remains largely unclear. Herein, we find that the disordered unstructured basic region (UBR) plays a crucial role in p53 LLPS, driven by electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions. Mutations in the tetramerization domain (TD) disrupt p53 phase separation by preventing the tetramer formation. Furthermore, our results have revealed that, in response to DNA damage in cell, the wild type (WT) p53 undergoes LLPS, while LLPS in oncogenic mutations is diminished or eliminated. The expression of the target gene of p53 decreased significantly with the mutations and cell survival increased with the mutations. Thus, we propose a novel mechanism of p53 carcinogenesis, whereby oncogenic mutations in TD impair the formation of p53 condensates, decreasing the activation of target genes and promoting cancer progression. This study helps to understand the behavior and function of p53 in a different aspect and may provide insights into cancer therapies targeting p53.

1. Introduction Liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) regulated cellular biological activities via assembling into extensive membrane-less compartments, such as nucleoli, Cajal bodies, P granules, stress granules, and cell signaling clusters [1,2]. These dynamic condensates formed by LLPS play critical roles in cellular processes like transcription regulation, genome organization regulation, stress response, and signal trans duction [3]. Despite the functional LLPS, the phenomenon of phase separation was also observed in pathological processes, mainly related to neurodegenerative diseases [4–7]. Numerous reports revealed that dysregulation of LLPS drove amyloid and aggregation formation [8,9]. Recent studies have extended the concept in cancer research. Diversities of condensates with various cellular functions have been found in cancer [10–13]. For instance, m6A(N6-methyladenosine)-modified mRNAs and their bounding motifs formed compartmentalized condensates by phase separation, involved in m6A-relevant tumor genesis and progression [14,15]. SPOP (speckle-type BTB/POZ protein) was reported to undergo LLPS with its substrates to inhibit tumorigenesis [16]. In addition,

53BP1 (p53-binding protein 1) was found to assemble at the DNA damage sites, where the DNA repair compartments showed liquid-like properties [17]. It was also reported that transcriptional regulators could undergo a phase separation that improves transcriptional activ ities in cancer, including transcription factors, RNA polymerase II, super-enhancers, and co-activators [18–21]. For example, co-activators BRD4 (bromodomain-containing protein 4) and MED1 (mediator com plex submit1) were condensed in super-enhancers to form liquid-like condensates through phase separation [22,23]. Consequently, the transcriptional apparatus was concentrated to regulate gene expression. Tumor suppressor p53 is a transcription factor with pivotal roles in multiple processes, including DNA repair, cell cycle arrest, senescence, genomic stability, and apoptosis [24–26]. >50 % of human cancers are involved in mutant variants of p53 [27]. Considering that p53 has the tendency of self-assembling, whether this protein undergoes LLPS and how this process regulates its function has drawn increasing attention to researchers. Indeed, it was reported long ago that p53 could be taken up Cajal and promyelocytic leukemia protein (PML) bodies, which existed as droplets in cells [28–30]. 2.2. LLPS of UBR is driven by electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions A detailed characterization of the LLPS of UBR was performed for a better understanding of its behaviors under different conditions and optimization for further experiments. Droplets were observed at the UBR concentration as low as 5 μM when using 5 % PEG. Further increase of protein concentration led to an increase in the size of the droplets (Fig. 2A). The solution turbidity of UBR also increased as protein or PEG concentration increased (Fig. 2B), which is consistent with the fluores cence image results, suggesting that the LLPS of UBR is dependent on protein and PEG concentration. However, UBR formed irregular droplets at PEG up to 10 % and protein concentration up to 30 μM (Fig. S2), and the corresponding turbidity dropped. FRAP was performed to examine the dynamics of UBR droplets. These droplets showed good fluorescence recovery, demonstrating that they are highly fluidic (Fig. 2C). In addi tion, the fusion of small droplets into larger droplets also confirmed their dynamic properties (Fig. 2D). We next examined the molecular mechanism of UBR phase separa tion. Since UBR contains many charged residues (Fig. S3), we first investigated whether electrostatic interactions drove UBR phase sepa ration by changing the salt concentrations of the buffer. The high salt concentrations significantly suppressed droplet formation in different protein concentrations (Figs. 1E and S4), and turbidity decreased as salt concentrations increased (Fig. 2G), indicating that electrostatic in teractions played a central role in the LLPS of UBR, as observed in Fig. 1G. To test whether hydrophobic interactions drive phase separa tion, we added 1,6-hexanediol to the solution of UBR, which is known to interrupt the hydrophobic interactions (Fig. 2F and H) [37,38]. UBR droplets were diminished in 1,6-hexanediol, confirming that the LLPS of UBR was associated with hydrophobic interactions such as cation-π and π-π interaction. Therefore, we identified that multiple types of weak molecular interactions contribute to UBR LLPS.

2.5. Phase separation of p53 is related to p53 activation In response to various stress signals, p53 is post-translationally modified to a fully functional and activated state [60]. Activated p53 acts as a key mediator in DNA damage response [61,62]. With mild DNA damage, activated p53 regulates downstream genes that arrest the cell cycle for the DNA damage repair, such as p21, p53R2, and GADD45. If DNA damage is severe and irreparable, activated p53 induces Fas-R, Bax, Puma, Noxa, Apaf-1, and Pidd expression, leading to cell death [57,60,63]. A classic mechanism is that protein kinases ATM/ATR (ataxia-telangiectasia mutated/ataxia-telangiectasia and Rad3-related) sense the DNA breaks and phosphorylates p53 at Ser15 to activate and stabilize p53 in the initial steps of DNA damage signaling [63–66]. To examine whether the p53 in the condensates is phosphorylated in the active form, we stained the cells with anti- phospho-p53 (Ser15) anti bodies (Fig. 4E). The p53 condensates showed good overlap with the staining, indicating a highly phosphorylated status of the p53. As ex pected, the survival rate of cells transfected with the mutant plasmid was higher than that of cells transfected with WT-p53 (Fig. 4G and H)., and these cells may be insensitive to DNA damage due to a lack of LLPS of p53. We then examined the expression of p21, a well-known target gene of p53 that blocks the cell cycle, and the assay showed that p21 expression decreased significantly with mutations (Fig. 4I and J). Therefore, we believe that the LLPS of p53 is related to the activation of p53 after DNA damage, and mutations disrupt the LLPS thus diminishing the activation of p53 target genes.

3. Discussion

4.2. Protein expression and purification

With the increasing incidence of cancer, exploring novel pathogenic mechanisms of cancer and developing new therapies have become imperative. The protein p53 is an important target in cancer research due to its mutations linked to more than half of cancers. The study of p53 biological function and its mutant pathogenesis is significant for developing and treating p53-related cancer medicines, as the vital role of p53 in cancer. The connection between protein phase separation and cancer has been recently demonstrated, including tumor suppressor SPOP, 53BP1, co-activators BRD4, and MED1. The WT p53 and its mutants were found to be characterized as forming amyloid aggregates under specific conditions. Recently, phase separation of p53 was re ported as an intermediate process towards fibril aggregation, especially the ones mutated on DBD. Herein we provide a new mechanism of p53 pathology in a different aspect. By expressing the UBR and UBR trun cated p53 and observing their phase separation under different condi tions, we revealed that disordered UBR mediates p53 phase separation, which is driven by electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions. Knowing that the UBR is critical for the LLPS of p53, we further explored how the mutations in this area can affect the phase separation and further the functions. Our experiments have shown that tetramer formation facilitated by TD is required for LLPS of p53.
