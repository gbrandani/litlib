Abstract The human genome is folded into chromatin loops by the cohesin complex, forming functional chromatin domains that underlie transcription and DNA replication/repair. However, the physical properties of these domains in living cells, especially in active euchromatin, and how they are

regulated by cohesin remain elusive. To address these questions, we combined singlenucleosome imaging/tracking and super-resolution 3D-structured illumination microscopy (3DSIM) with euchromatin-specific labeling of histone H3.3. Using this nanoscopic approach, we revealed that euchromatin is organized into condensed domains constrained by cohesin-mediated loops, challenging the established textbook view of euchromatin as largely open. Transcription

machinery appear to be located near the condensed domain surfaces. Cohesin loss increased nucleosome fluidity of these domains without altering their compaction, leading to local mixing of domains and compromising transcriptional insulation. These findings uncover an unexpected physical role of cohesin in maintaining the integrity of condensed euchromatic domains and ensuring proper higher-order regulation of gene expression.

Introduction In human cells, two meters of genomic DNA are wrapped around core histones to form nucleosomes 1,2. Various imaging studies have long suggested that a string of nucleosomes is folded irregularly into functional chromatin domains in the interphase nucleus 3-10. Genomics

analyses, such as Hi-C 11, have also identified similar domains or topologically associating domains, TADs, with distinct epigenetic marks 12-15. The textbook view of genome chromatin describes that transcriptionally inactive heterochromatin forms condensed domains, whereas active euchromatin forms open ones, thereby supposedly making euchromatin accessible to transcription machinery 16,17. However, the expression profile of euchromatic genes varies

greatly from cell to cell 18 19, implying that transcriptional regulation may be governed by higherorder chromatin organization rather than a simple “open-or-closed” binary model. Consistent with this, recent evidence challenges this established textbook view, suggesting that euchromatin is also organized into largely condensed domains, which may contribute to higher-order gene regulation 7,8,10,20-22.

While interactions between genomic sequences within and between domains are well recognized as crucial for transcriptional regulation 13,22-24, DNA replication 25, and repair 26, the physical properties of chromatin domains have been comparatively overlooked. Yet, they are equally important. Chromatin dynamics and their physical properties directly influence genomic

accessibility 27,28 and can modulate the efficiency and probability of these biochemical reactions. Therefore, elucidating how the physical properties of chromatin domains are regulated is crucial.

A key factor influencing both chromatin organization and dynamics is the cohesin complex, composed of four subunits (SMC1, SMC3, RAD21, and STAG1/STAG2)29-33. Cohesin has two

[Page 4]

main roles in organizing genome chromatin. One is to hold replicated sister chromatids together for their proper segregation during cell division (“cohesion”) and the other is to form chromatin loops within individual chromosomes to create the domains described above 34-36. Cohesin may also regulate the physical properties of chromatin in living cells. Indeed, pioneering work using

the lacO/LacI-GFP system in budding yeast demonstrated that cohesin loss increases chromatin motion, presumably via the loss of sister chromatid cohesion 37,38. Subsequent studies revealed that in higher eukaryotic cells, cohesin depletion also increases chromatin motion 5,19,39-41. However, it is still unclear how cohesin constrains chromatin, whether primarily through sister chromatid cohesion or loop formation, and which chromatin domains, euchromatin or

heterochromatin, are most constrained. Furthermore, how cohesin-mediated constraints affect the physical properties of chromatin domains and their genome functions remains poorly understood.

To address these questions, we developed a combined approach of single-nucleosome imaging 5,27,42,43

and 3D-structured illumination microscopy (3D-SIM) 7,44 45, incorporating a specific

labeling strategy with the histone variant H3.3, which is enriched in active chromatin regions 4648

. Using these advanced nanoscopic imaging techniques, we uncovered condensed euchromatic

domains in living human cells, providing further critical evidence that challenges the established view of euchromatin as largely open. We also demonstrated that cohesin governs the fluidity of these euchromatic domains without affecting their compaction. Together, these findings

highlight a previously underappreciated role of cohesin in preventing local mixing of condensed euchromatin domains, and thus ensuring the effective insulation of transcription among them.

Results Rapid depletion of cohesin increases local nucleosome motion in living human cells To explore how cohesin organizes chromatin domains in living human cells, we combined single-nucleosome imaging/tracking 5,27,42,43,49 with rapid cohesin depletion using the auxin5

inducible degron system 50. Single-nucleosome imaging/tracking can sensitively detect changes in the chromatin state in living cells. We used HCT116 cells expressing H2B-HaloTag (H2BHalo) 43 and RAD21-mAID-mClover (mAC) 50. H2B-Halo was labeled with tetramethylrhodamine (TMR) to visualize nucleosomes genome-wide, including those in both euchromatin and heterochromatin (Fig. S1a,b). Oblique illumination microscopy 51 enabled

imaging of individual nucleosomes as TMR-labeled dots (Fig. 1a–c), which were precisely tracked in 2D at 50 ms/frame for 15–20 s, yielding ~1400 trajectories per cell (Figs. 1b,d, S1d,e; see Methods for further details on nucleosome tracking). Tracking was specific to nucleosomeincorporated H2B-Halo, as free molecules diffused too fast to be observed (Movie S1). Mean squared displacement (MSD) analysis revealed sub-diffusive nucleosome motion with an

anomalous exponent (α) of 0.43, suggesting constrained motion (black plots in Figs. 1e and S1f). Fixation nearly abolished this motion (gray plot, Fig. 1e).

Cohesin depletion via 5Ph-IAA treatment for 1 hour led to significantly increased motion, with the MSD exponent rising to 0.48 (Figs. 1e, S1f; Movie S2). Turning angle distributions of

nucleosome trajectories and asymmetry coefficient (AC) analysis 43,52 showed reduced pullingback forces of nucleosomes after cohesin loss, suggesting that cohesin constrains nucleosome movement (Fig. 1g–i). RAD21 depletion did not induce DNA damage (Fig. S1g). Similar results were obtained in HeLa cells via siRNA (Fig. S1h). These findings confirm that cohesin constrains local nucleosome motion in living cells, consistent with previous reports 5,19,40,41.

Cohesin constrains local nucleosome motion mainly via loop formation To determine whether cohesin constrains chromatin via loop domains or sister chromatid cohesion (Fig. 2a), we knocked down Sororin, which is required for sister chromatid cohesion

but not for loop formation 53-55. Knockdown of Sororin disrupted cohesion in late S–G2 synchronized HCT116 cells (Figs. 2b, S2a) but did not alter nucleosome motion (Fig. 2c), indicating that global constraints arise from loop formation, not sister chromatid cohesion. To confirm whether cohesion affects local nucleosome motion near cohesion sites, we used a genome labeling system with tetO/lacO arrays 250 kb apart in chromosome 5 of HT1080 cells

(Fig. 2d) 56. In this system, the lacO array, but not tetO array, was located near a cohesinenriched site (Fig. 2e) 57. The lacO array more frequently appeared as two separate dots than the tetO array, suggesting that the lacO array lies near a cohesion site 56. These arrays were visualized with TetR-4XmCherry and LacI-EGFP, respectively (Fig. 2d,e). Sororin knockdown (Fig. S2b) significantly increased LacI-EGFP mobility but not TetR-4XmCherry (Fig. 2f,g),

whereas RAD21 depletion (Fig. S2c) affected both (Fig. 2f,g), suggesting that cohesion constrains local regions near cohesion sites as previously reported in yeast 38, while loop-forming cohesin globally constrains chromatin.

We tested whether increasing the number of loops enhances constraint. Since WAPL releases

cohesin from chromatin, WAPL depletion increases the number of loops 58-60, particularly when depletion is induced after the completion of DNA replication. WAPL depletion in HCT116WAPL-mAC cells (Fig. 3a) 61 synchronized in late S-G2 phase (Fig. S3a) reduced the WAPLmClover signal (Fig. 3b) and increased RAD21 chromatin binding (Fig. S3b), which led to decreased MSD and AC values (Fig. 3c,d). A similar finding was also observed in asynchronous

[Page 7]

HCT116 cells and siRNA-treated asynchronous HeLa cells (Fig. S3c–e). Thus, more cohesin loops constrain chromatin more strongly. To assess whether loop boundaries contribute to chromatin constraint, we acutely depleted CTCF in HCT116 cells (Fig. 3e) 50. Despite its role in TAD boundary formation 62, CTCF loss did not affect nucleosome motion (Figs. 3f–h, S3f,g),

consistent with unchanged cohesin binding levels 63,64 and previous genomic array data 40,41. Finally, to examine whether cohesin translocation or active loop extrusion 32,65 is required, we used a head-tethered cohesin system 66 that stabilizes cohesin via forced head domain closure using AP21967 (Fig. 3i) 67,68. Head-tethering alone did not change nucleosome motion, but the combination of RAD21 depletion with closed-head cohesin increased motion (Fig. 3j,k, S3h).

These results suggest that chromatin constraint by cohesin does not require active loop extrusion.

We previously observed heterogeneous nucleosome motions (Fig. 4a,b), which suggested the presence of domain subpopulations, potentially linked to chromatin context28,24. To get a clue of whether cohesin preferentially constrains euchromatin or heterochromatin, we used motion

classification with the Bayesian-based Richardson-Lucy algorithm (RL algorithm) 69. For RL algorithm analysis, we obtained a 10-fold increase in H2B-Halo nucleosome trajectory data (Fig. S4a,b) using the PA-JF646 HaloTag ligand 70 and constant photoactivation with weak 405 nm illumination (Fig. 4c). We then classified H2B-Halo trajectories into four motion types: “Superslow,” “Slow,” “Fast,” and “Super-fast” (Fig. 4d; Movie S3). The “Fast” and “Super-fast”

subpopulations, likely euchromatic, localized within the nucleoplasm (Fig. 4e; Movie S3). Cohesin depletion shifted these populations rightward (Fig. 4d), increasing their mobility and anomalous exponent α values (Fig. 4f,g, S4c), while the proportions remained similar (Fig. 4h), implying that cohesin constrains movements of nucleosomes mainly in euchromatin.

Euchromatin-specific nucleosome labeling reveals that cohesin constrains euchromatin To investigate euchromatin more specifically, we labeled nucleosomes using a euchromatinspecific histone variant H3.3 46-48. We stably expressed H3.3-Halo in HeLa cells (Figs. 5a, S5a). Expressed H3.3-Halo was properly incorporated into the nucleosome (Fig. S5b) and localized to

DAPI-poor regions in nuclei (Fig. 5a). Pull-down of H3.3-Halo-incorporated nucleosomes and genomic sequencing (Fig. 5b) confirmed enrichment in Hi-C A compartments (93.1%) with active histone marks, and depletion from repressive marks (Figs. 5c,d, S5c–e, S6). Notably, 75.3% of H3.3-Halo nucleosome regions overlapped with Hi-C contact domains (Figs. S5e and S6a).

Single-nucleosome imaging showed that H3.3-Halo nucleosomes were more mobile than H2BHalo ones (Fig. 5e) and had a stronger response to RAD21 or WAPL depletion (Fig. S7a), indicating a greater cohesin impact in euchromatin. In contrast, H2B-labeled nucleosomes around the nuclear periphery (basal nuclear surface; for details, see Methods), which is enriched

with heterochromatin, or lamin-associated domains (LAD) 71, remained largely unaffected (Figs. 5e, S7b). A similar observation was found in acute depletion experiments of RAD21 and WAPL using HCT116 cells 50,61 expressing H3.3-Halo (Fig. S7c, S7e) and H2B-Halo around the nuclear periphery (Fig. S7d, S7f). These results demonstrate that cohesin primarily constrains euchromatic regions.

3D-SIM uncovers condensed euchromatic domains in live human cells The finding that cohesin constrains euchromatic nucleosome motion motivated us to explore how cohesin affects the structure and physical properties of euchromatic domains. For this purpose, we used 3D-structured illumination microscopy (3D-SIM)7,44 45 for HCT116 cells expressing

[Page 9]

H3.3-Halo and observed euchromatic domain structures in live or formaldehyde (FA)-fixed cells at super-resolution. 3D-SIM enables fast multicolor 3D acquisition of whole cells with higher resolution (xy ~130 nm; z ~250 nm) and strongly enhanced contrast compared to conventional fluorescence microscopy (panels 1–2 and 4–5 in Fig. 6a; Movie S4) 7,44.

Using quality-controlled 3D-SIM (Fig. S8 and Methods)72, we observed that euchromatin in live HCT116 cells appeared as connected, condensed chromatin domains with irregular shapes and diameters in the range of a few hundred nanometers (Fig. 6b; Movie S5). A similar architecture was observed in FA-fixed cells labeled with H3.3-Halo (panel 5 in Fig. 6a). This finding of

condensed euchromatic domains in living human cells provides compelling evidence that challenges the established view of euchromatin as largely open.

Preferential localization of cohesin to euchromatin domains We observed high-resolution chromatin structures labeled with DAPI and H3.3-Halo-TMR in

FA-fixed HCT116 cells using 3D-SIM (panels 4–6 in Fig. 6a; Movie S4). Notably, euchromatin domains were more clearly visualized with H3.3-Halo-TMR labeling than with DAPI staining. Based on a reported chromatin signal classification analysis 7,10,73,74, we classified DAPI and H3.3-Halo-TMR signals into seven classes (Figs. 6c–d). Regions with lower DAPI staining showed stronger H3.3-Halo-TMR signals (Fig. 6e), consistent with the preferential incorporation

of H3.3 into euchromatin. Notably, although the lowest DAPI regions (DAPI-Class 1) were previously described as interchromatin regions 7,10,73,74, we observed H3.3-Halo signals there, indicating the presence of euchromatin (Fig. 6e–f). This suggests that DAPI staining may overlook euchromatin structures due to its preference for AT-rich DNA 75.

[Page 10]

We investigated the spatial distributions of cohesin and active transcription sites within the nucleus by combining 3D-SIM with immunostaining. RAD21 immunostaining revealed that cohesin appeared as punctate foci in SIM images (panel 7 in Fig 6a), likely representing chromatin-bound cohesin. The centroids of these foci were frequently located near the edges of

H3.3-Halo domains, suggesting that cohesin localizes to the surfaces of euchromatin domains (enlarged images in Fig. 6a). Classification analysis confirmed that cohesin is enriched in highdensity H3.3 regions (classes 3–7; Fig. 6g), indicating a preferential localization of cohesin to euchromatin domains. Consistently, cohesin enrichment was observed in low-density DAPI regions (classes 1–2) and depleted in denser regions (Fig. 6h), in good agreement with previous

observations 7. This spatial distribution of cohesin also supports our finding that euchromatic nucleosome motion is more sensitive to cohesin depletion (Fig. S7c,d).

Active RNAP II localizes around the euchromatic domain surface We next examined the localization of active transcription sites. We performed immunostaining

using antibodies against the C-terminal domain (CTD) of RNA polymerase II (RNAP II) and its phosphorylated forms at serine 5 (Ser5P) and serine 2 (Ser2P) to visualize RNAP II signals composed predominantly of inactive, active paused, and elongating RNAP II, respectively (Fig. 6i). All signals appeared as punctate foci (Fig. 6i). These signals were enriched in DAPI-poor regions (classes 1–2; Fig. 6j) and in H3.3-Halo-rich regions (classes 4–7; Fig. 6k), indicating a

euchromatin-biased localization.

We observed distinct differences in nuclear localization among cohesin and RNAP II forms. Cohesin showed a relatively uniform distribution with a preference for euchromatin (Fig. 6l). In contrast, RNAP II showed increased localization to H3.3-denser areas upon transcriptional

[Page 11]

activation (Fig. 6m–o). Within the H3.3-Halo classification, CTD signals peaked at classes 4–5 (Fig. 6m), Ser5P at class 5 (Fig. 6n), and Ser2P at classes 6–7 (Fig. 6o). These patterns suggest that inactive RNAP II remains at the periphery (or outside) of euchromatic domains, paused active RNAP II localizes near the domain surface, and elongating RNAP II may penetrate the

domain.

Cohesin depletion does not alter compaction of euchromatic domains To assess the effect of cohesin depletion on chromatin organization, especially euchromatic domains, we acutely depleted RAD21 in HCT116 cells expressing H3.3-Halo and observed their

chromatin organization using 3D-SIM. Interestingly, no obvious qualitative changes were observed in the overall chromatin organization (DAPI; Fig. 7a; Movies S6–S7) or in the euchromatic domains (H3.3-Halo; Fig. 7b) upon cohesin depletion. For quantitative analysis, we classified DAPI and H3.3-Halo signals. Cohesin depletion did not change the relative volumes of each class, i.e., the compaction levels of DAPI-stained chromatin (Fig. 7c), in good agreement

with previous findings 7. Likewise, cohesin depletion did not affect the compaction levels of H3.3-Halo-labeled euchromatin domains (Fig. 7d). Together, these 3D-SIM results demonstrate that cohesin depletion does not alter the overall compaction of euchromatin domains.

Cohesin regulates the fluidity of nucleosomes inside condensed euchromatic domains

Although euchromatic nucleosome movement increases upon cohesin depletion, euchromatic domain compaction does not change. This raised the question of whether nucleosomes might gain mobility within the domains. To address this, we introduced dual-color labeling (Fig. 7e) and tracking of two spatially neighboring single H3.3-Halo-nucleosomes (Fig. 7f,g; Movie S8). Since simple single-nucleosome imaging cannot distinguish whether individual nucleosomes

[Page 12]

fluctuate within the domain or whether the domain itself moves, we measured the distances between two neighboring H3.3-Halo-nucleosomes within euchromatic domains (average <150 nm; Fig. 7e)8 and obtained the MSD of their relative positions (two-point MSD) 8,28,40.

Two-point MSD analysis showed much greater motion between neighboring H3.3-Halonucleosomes in living cells than in FA-fixed cells (Fig. 7i), suggesting that nucleosomes fluctuate within the domain. Notably, the two-point MSD further increased upon acute cohesin depletion (Fig. 7i), indicating that nucleosomes move more inside condensed euchromatic domains and suggesting a transition to a more liquid-like state of chromatin.

Discussion In this study, we employed a combined approach of single-nucleosome imaging/tracking and 3D-SIM super-resolution imaging to explore how cohesin regulates euchromatic chromatin organization in living human cells. Our approach provides a more comprehensive view of both

global and local changes in chromatin dynamics and organization than previous studies that primarily focused on specific chromatin loci 40,41. Our results show that cohesin constrains local chromatin motion mainly through loop formation, particularly in euchromatin, rather than through sister chromatid cohesion (Fig. 2). This aligns with previous studies reporting increased chromatin motion upon cohesin depletion 5,40,41. Furthermore, our results using head-tethering

cohesin suggest that cohesin constrains euchromatin loop domains without performing active loop extrusion (Fig. 3i,j). On the other hand, disruption of sister chromatid cohesion had minimal effects on local chromatin motion, and only the putative cohesion site or its neighbor was affected (Fig. 2), presumably because only a limited number of sister regions are aligned in human interphase cells 76,77. This contrasts with yeast studies showing that chromatin motion

decreases as sister chromatid cohesion develops with the cell cycle progression 37,38.

To our knowledge, this study is the first single-nucleosome imaging analysis of replicationindependent histone variants. Histone variants, including H3.3, are known to have distinct deposition pathways and unique roles in transcriptional regulation and chromatin structure 78-80.

Similarly, mutations in key residues of core histones profoundly alter chromatin organization and gene expression, contributing to oncogenesis 81,82. Single-nucleosome tracking data provide insights into how these histone variants and mutants influence local chromatin structure and dynamics 49,83,84. Such measurements reveal not only average mobility changes across the nucleus but also heterogeneity that could reflect variant-specific effects on nucleosome packing

[Page 14]

and chromatin accessibility. In future studies, this approach will decipher how histone variants and histone mutations modulate chromatin phase separation, loop formation, and higher-order genome organization in living cells.

In the present study, we developed euchromatin-specific labeling using histone H3.3-Halo (Fig. 5). The labeled genomic regions correspond to Hi-C A-compartment contact domains 15 with active histone marks (Fig. 5). Combining this euchromatin labeling with 3D-SIM 7,44 45 enabled super-resolution imaging of euchromatic domains in live and fixed cells, revealing their condensed nature. Since the typical textbook model describes euchromatin as open and

heterochromatin as closed and condensed, our findings provide critical evidence that revises the established notion of euchromatin as largely open.

Condensed chromatin seems to be the default chromatin state in higher eukaryotic cells 22. Possible formation mechanisms are as follows. First, chromatin is inherently sticky due to

electrostatic nucleosome–nucleosome interactions because one nucleosome has ten long positively charged tails 1, which can bind to neighboring negatively charged DNA. Second, free cations such as Mg2+ in the cell or positively charged proteins like linker histone H1 contribute to condensed domain formation. Physiological concentrations of cations (~1 mM Mg2+ and ~100 mM K+/Na+) or H1 can condense chromatin by neutralizing its negative charge 85-89. Finally,

depletion attraction/macromolecular crowding effects 90-92 also play a role in compaction, because the cell is filled with high concentrations (100–200 mg/mL) of macromolecules (proteins, RNAs, lipids, etc.), which can induce an entropy-driven attractive force between nucleosomes.

[Page 15]

The physically condensed euchromatic domains can provide higher-order regulation of various DNA transaction reactions, including transcription and DNA replication, whereas extended fiber loops cannot. As shown in Fig. 6, condensed euchromatic domains likely hinder the access of transcriptional protein complexes to their target sequences, such as promoters or enhancers

located in the inner core of chromatin domains 7. These findings support the notion of higherorder gene regulation within a domain, such as a “buoy” model 22,93. Partial decondensation of such condensed domains through histone modifications may also serve as a regulatory mechanism to turn on gene transcription. In addition, condensed domains are more resistant to radiation than decondensed forms, presumably because condensed chromatin generates lower

levels of reactive radicals 94,95. This feature is especially important for maintaining the integrity of actively transcribed euchromatic regions, which are directly connected to essential genome activities. Together, these two features highlight the biological relevance of condensed euchromatic chromatin.

While previous studies suggested that cohesin is involved in long-distance promoter–enhancer interactions, particularly those spanning domain boundaries 96,97, our findings highlight a previously underappreciated role of cohesin in constraining the conformational freedom of condensed euchromatic domains (Fig. 8). We revealed how these physical properties of euchromatin domains are regulated by cohesin, and how they are coupled to genome

organization and function. 3D-SIM imaging of euchromatic domains and two-point MSD measurements between neighboring euchromatic nucleosomes showed that while cohesin depletion does not alter the compaction level of euchromatin domains, the depletion increases nucleosome fluidity within these domains (Fig. 7). We consider that nucleosome–nucleosome interactions via histone tails discussed above remain intact upon cohesin depletion 8, maintaining

[Page 16]

compaction levels within euchromatin domains. The increase in nucleosome fluidity upon cohesin loss may promote mixing of chromatin domains (Fig. 8). Indeed, cohesin depletion has been shown to promote the clustering of super-enhancers 98 and induce clustering of accessible chromatin domains (ACDs) and co-activation of gene transcription 19. Loss of cohesin has also

been reported to increase “entropy” in genome folding and variation in gene expression 99. Together, these findings establish cohesin as a key factor that preserves the stiffness of condensed euchromatic domains and prevents domain intermingling. This physical constraint ensures effective insulation of transcriptional programs, particularly between neighboring genes.

Fig. 8: Cohesin prevents local mixing of condensed euchromatic domains. Cohesin holds chromatin fibers together and constrains chromatin motion within condensed euchromatic domains. While cohesin depletion does not alter the compaction level of these domains, it increases nucleosome fluidity (conformational freedom). This enhanced fluidity upon cohesin loss may promote mixing between chromatin domains. Thus, cohesin stiffens condensed euchromatic domains, preventing domain intermingling and ensuring effective insulation of transcriptional programs.
