ABSTRACT: Protein−RNA condensates are involved in a range of cellular activities. Coarse-grained molecular models of intrinsically disordered proteins have been developed to shed light on and predict single-chain properties and phase separation. An RNA model compatible with such models for disordered proteins would enable the study of complex biomolecular mixtures involving RNA. Here, we present a sequence-independent coarse-grained, twobeads-per-nucleotide model of disordered, flexible RNA based on a hydropathy scale. We parametrize the model, which we term CALVADOS-RNA, using a combination of bottom-up and topdown approaches to reproduce local RNA geometry and intramolecular interactions based on atomistic simulations and in vitro experiments. The model semiquantitatively captures several aspects of RNA−RNA and RNA−protein interactions. We examined RNA−RNA interactions by comparing calculated and experimental virial coefficients and nonspecific RNA−protein interaction by studying the reentrant phase behavior of protein−RNA mixtures. We demonstrate the utility of the model by simulating the formation of mixed condensates consisting of the disordered region of MED1 and RNA chains and the selective partitioning of disordered regions from transcription factors into these and compare the results to experiments. Despite the simplicity of our model, we show that it captures several key aspects of protein−RNA interactions and may therefore be used as a baseline model to study several aspects of the biophysics and biology of protein−RNA condensates.

Biomolecular condensates formed from protein and RNA constitute various types of membraneless organelles, which have important and diverse roles in cellular activities.1−5 Although many mechanistic and biophysical aspects of these condensates remain incompletely understood, potential functional roles include ribosomal assembly in the nucleolus, protection of untranslated mRNA in stress granules, and RNA degradation in P-bodies. While subsets of specific proteins are thought to play central roles in the formation of these condensates, many other types of proteins and nucleic acids are also contained within them. Understanding and rationalizing the properties of condensates with multiple components would enhance our knowledge of cellular activities and the neurodegenerative diseases associated with biomolecular condensates. For example, RNA may modulate the propensity for amyloid fibril formation in hnRNPA1A.6 Condensation of RNA alone can happen in RNA repeats, potentially leading to neurotoxicity.7,8 Furthermore, protein−RNA condensates can selectively recruit other proteins. All-atom or coarse-grained (CG) molecular simulations with an implicit solvent can be used to study a larger number of protein chains on longer time scales, at the cost of reduced resolution and potential accuracy. This trade-off between spatiotemporal reach and resolution is governed by a concrete scientific question. A wide range of medium-resolution CG models have been developed for proteins, DNA, and RNA to capture the effects of protein secondary structure formation or nucleic acid base-pairing and neighboring base interactions.27−38 These models commonly employ three or more beads per residue. Complementing these approaches, hydropathy scale (HPS) models have been developed to represent disordered proteins and RNA with one-bead-per-residue mapping. These models generally do not represent local secondary structure formation (unless modified39) but enable simulations that reach longer time scales and larger length scales, and are thus a popular choice to study biomolecular condensates. The models have bonded force terms describing bonded and, in some cases, angular potentials, and nonbonded interactions such as electrostatic and short-ranged interactions.40−46 While details vary across models, the strength of short-ranged nonbonded interactions generally depends on the sizes of the beads (σ) and a set of “stickiness” parameters (λ) that capture the tendency of amino acids to interact with each other relatively to the surrounding solvent. Homogeneous RNA chains such as polyA and polyU are often used in in vitro experiments to study PS,19,47,48 and these RNA sequences often lack specific, well-defined structures, i.e., they can be modeled as mostly disordered RNA.49 As such, CG models may be particularly well suited to represent polyA or polyU chains or to capture other RNA−protein systems where nonspecific structures and interactions are the most important. We and others have previously described top-down approaches to parametrize CG models for proteins43,50−52 based on experimental data. The lack of a similarly broad and

detailed set of experimental data for RNA makes such purely data-driven parameter optimization difficult. Based on experimental data from small-angle X-ray scattering (SAXS) and FRET of specific chains, a homogeneous RNA model with local chain stiffness was proposed.46 Chou et al. proposed an HPS model of RNA, which was tuned using SAXS data and experimentally obtained dissociation constants.53 Even in the single-site model, the secondary structures of RNA are produced by employing explicit formalization of canonical base pairs and the neutralized beads.54−56 However, these models assume neutralized or reduced-charge beads for the backbone chain, and it is unclear to what extent this captures the charge-driven aspects of PS with proteins. Here we set out to develop a CG model for disordered RNA that can be used to study single-chain behavior and phase separation of RNA in the presence of specific proteins. Previously, we have developed a protein HPS model, CALVADOS, using a top-down approach.50 The interaction parameters were derived to match single-chain properties from experimental SAXS and NMR in a data-driven manner.43 Because of the symmetry between intra- and intermolecular interactions in homotypic PS, CALVADOS also captures several aspects of PS of IDRs45 and multidomain proteins.57 Here, we develop the RNA model in a consistent approach to CALVADOS 245 to study protein−RNA condensates. Our model is aimed to capture situations in which the main interactions of disordered RNA involve electrostatic interactions from the sugar−phosphate backbone and nonspecific hydrophobic interactions from the nucleobases. Based on this idea, we represent each nucleotide by two beads, striking a balance between speed and resolution. As detailed below, we use a combined bottom-up and top-down approach to parametrize bonded and nonbonded interactions. We test the model on RNA−RNA and protein−RNA interactions in multichain systems and demonstrate its utility by studying the selective partitioning of transcription factors into a condensate formed by the IDR of the MED1 coactivator and long RNA molecules.

2. RESULTS 2.1. Two-Beads-Per-Nucleotide RNA Model. We have developed a two-beads-per-nucleotide model of singlestranded (ss) RNA, consisting of one backbone bead and one base bead (Figure 1a). The backbone bead represents the sugar−phosphate backbone and is centered at the site of the phosphate atom, with ribose considered part of the backbone

[Page 3]

bead. The base bead is placed on the nitrogen atom that bonds the nucleobase to the ribose. A similar two-beads-pernucleotide model has been proposed for double-stranded DNA.58 Our model is intended to mimic the behavior of disordered RNA chains that do not adopt persistent secondary and tertiary structures. The backbone and base beads are RNA sequence-independent in our model, both to strive for as simple a model as possible and because sequence-dependent parameters did not markedly improve agreement to experimental data at this level of resolution.46 However, we note that different disordered RNA sequences may in some cases have quantitatively different properties both in single chain and phase separation behavior,20,49 limiting the range of questions the model can be used to study. 2.2. Parameter Tuning Using a Combined Bottom-Up and Top-Down Approach. The potential energy function of the model includes bonded terms to describe bonds and angles, and nonbonded terms to capture short-range, electrostatic, and stacking terms (see Figure 1b and Section 4). After the overall geometry of the model has been defined, the next step is to define the mass and size of the beads. The mass was computed as the sum of the atomistic masses of the constituent atoms. We used previously calculated volumes that were based on the analysis of crystal structures59 to define the sizes of the CG beads by assuming van der Waals spheres (Table 1). The sizes of the backbone and base beads are 0.69 and 0.62 nm, respectively, which are similar in size to the largest bead in the protein CALVADOS model (Trp; 0.68 nm).

Since the microscopic properties are primarily influenced by bonded and stacking interactions, we fixed stickiness parameters λ at this step, and confirmed that the distributions did not significantly vary after tuning λ. We validated that our model can accurately reproduce the distributions of the microscopic properties. The equilibrium length and spring constant of the backbone−backbone bond are 0.59 nm and 1400 kJ/mol/nm2, respectively, while those of the backbone−base bond are 0.54 nm and 2200 kJ/mol/nm2. The equilibrium angle and spring constant are 3.141 rad and 4.2 kJ/mol/rad, respectively. The low spring constant for the backbone−backbone bond indicates the flexibility of the backbone chain. For the stacking parameters, the diameter is 0.4 nm, and the scaling parameter is 15. These parameters produced bond and angle distributions that relatively closely matched those from the atomistic simulations (Figure 2a−c). We note, however, that the base−backbone−backbone−base dihedral angle is not as well reproduced (Figure 2d), because the A-form structure in UCCAAUC60 is not fully represented by our stacking function. Our model roughly reproduces the location of the main peak of the base−base distance distribution but differs in the tail compared to the atomistic simulations (Figure 2e). In addition to UCCAAU, we examined previously generated atomistic ensembles of polyA30 and polyU30.61 The bond and angle distributions do not significantly differ among these sequences, except for the dihedral angles, where adenine exhibits intense peaks compared to uracil (Figure S1). From these results, we conclude that our CG model successfully captures many key local structural properties of disordered RNA. We then proceeded to determine the stickiness parameters for the RNA backbone, λP, and for the base, λB, using a topdown approach with the goal of reproducing the global dimensions of the RNA chain, represented via the radius of gyration (Rg) of polyU30 and polyA30 at varying ionic concentrations (20−600 mM) based on SAXS measurements.49 Assuming that the backbone bead is hydrophilic and the base bead is hydrophobic, we performed a grid search over λP < 0.3 and λB > 1.0. Based on χ2 of Rg (see Section 4), we find the optimal hydropathy parameters around λP = 0 and λB = 1.18 for both polyA30 and polyU30 (Figure 2g) and when analyzed together (Figure 2h). At λ = 0, the AH potential describes purely repulsive interactions,62 i.e., no contribution from hydrophobic attractions. Since the radius of gyration does not change significantly for different values of the stickiness (Figure 2i), we employed a simple scenario of purely hydrophilic−hydrophilic interactions for the backbone beads, i.e., λP = 0. For the base bead, a λB of 1.18 was chosen. As a point of comparison, λ(Glu) = 0 and λ(Trp) = 0.99 for the least and most sticky amino acids in CALVADOS 2, indicating that the RNA backbone is as nonsticky as glutamate and the nucleobases are on average more sticky than tryptophan. These values can also be compared to an earlier RNA HPS model based on atomistic charges that has a slightly negative stickiness,40 and our model is overall more sticky than that of this single-bead model. Although our model resulted in semiquantitative accuracy for Rg at varying ionic strengths when compared to polyU30, the Rg of polyA30 at ionic strengths below 200 mM was not well captured (Figure 2i). Following the parametrization and testing of our RNA model for intra- and intermolecular RNA interactions, we assessed its ability to describe protein−RNA condensation. We combined the RNA model with the CALVADOS 2 model for IDRs and calculated nonbonded protein−RNA interactions using combination rules (see Section 4) to stay consistent with the definitions for intermolecular interactions between protein chains in CALVADOS 2. This is somewhat rationalized because the DH potentials for the electrostatic interaction are identical for the IDR and RNA, and the short-range interactions are determined to balance the electrostatics. In line with the experimental finding of reentrant phase transitions, we find that FUSRGG3 does not undergo PS in the absence of RNA. 2.5. RNA Concentration Influences the Formation and Selective Partitioning of Transcriptional Regulators. To illustrate the utility of the RNA and CALVADOS 2 models to study biological problems, we studied the interactions between the long IDR from MED1 in the mediator coactivator complex (for simplicity, we here term this IDR as MED1), RNA, and IDRs from transcriptional regulators. We mimicked the 477-nucleotide long Pou5f1 enhancer RNA-1, as studied by Henninger et al.,18 with our model. We note here that this comparison focuses on general “polymer-like” properties of the RNA and protein and does not, for example, include specific effects of RNA structure. As MED1 is highly charged (+43), it does not phaseseparate on its own (Figure 5a). The addition of the RNA chain, which carries a charge of −477, compensates for the total positive charge, and the system is neutralized at an RNA/ MED1 molar ratio of 0.09. We observed stable condensates also at an RNA/MED1 ratio of 0.2, but PS did not occur at a high RNA/MED1 ratio of 0.5 (Figure 5a). Subsequently, we quantified the reentrant phase behavior of MED1 and RNA condensates using a phase diagram. We performed additional simulations at RNA/MED1 molar ratios of 0.05−0.3 to obtain the density profile (Figure S8a) and used the concentrations of MED1 and RNA in the dilute and dense phases to construct the phase diagram. The phase diagram (Figure 5b) shows that the saturation concentration of MED1 decreases at RNA/ MED1 ratios below 0.25 and increases at ratios above 0.25. On the other hand, the dense phase reaches the highest concentration of both MED1 and RNA between RNA/ MED1 ratios of 0.1 and 0.2. Similarly to the case for the FUSRGG3 and polyU40 systems, both the dense phase concentration and its volume affect the dilute phase concentration. We find that the calculated phase diagram captures several aspects of the experimental observations by Henninger et al.,18 except for the left branch of the dilute phase (Figure S8b). Since the phase diagram indicates that the composition of MED1-RNA condensates varies, we examined the changes in the electrostatic environments of the dilute and dense phases. We calculated a nominal macromolecular charge concentration as QMED1CMED1 + QRNACRNA, where Q and C represent the nominal charge per chain and the molar concentration in a phase, respectively. The results show that at an RNA/MED1 ratio of 0.05, the dense phase is negatively charged, while the dilute phase is positively charged (Figure 5c). At an RNA/ MED1 ratio of 0.1, the dense phase is negatively charged, and the dilute phase is nearly neutral. At an RNA/MED1 ratio of 0.3, both the dense and dilute phases are negatively charged. These results indicate that the electrostatic environments of the phases are RNA-dependent. We note that these net charges refer only to the nominal charges of the macromolecules in our simulations, and the charge distribution of the biomolecules and their counterions is more complex in reality. It has recently been shown that IDRs from different transcriptional regulators can partition into MED1−RNA condensates and that the extent to which they do so depends on their charge and charge patterning.10 We therefore set out to study this selective partitioning using simulations. We further reasoned that changes in the electrostatic environment could alter the properties of MED1−RNA condensates and therefore the extent to which different IDRs would partition into MED1−RNA condensates. To examine these questions, we performed additional simulations of MED1−RNA con-

boxes. We therefore selected three points with RNA/protein mass ratios of 0 (no PS), 0.45 (PS), and 5.54 (no PS) at a bulk protein concentration of 2.8 mg/mL. The occurrence of PS in the simulations with a cubic box is thus consistent with the phase diagram obtained from simulations in slab geometry (Figure 4e). To examine the effect of the electrostatic cutoff distance, we performed additional simulations at 6 nm DH cutoff and observed a slight increase in the saturation concentrations for both protein and RNA (Figure S5a). However, apart from these subtle differences, the phase diagram obtained using 6 nm cutoffs was very similar to that using 4 nm (Figure S5b−d). We compared the phase diagram obtained from our simulation to that from the experiments by Kaur et al.,48 which was produced by examining PS conditions using FUSRGG3 and longer RNA chains. The experimental phase diagram shows the lowest saturation condition at an RNA/ protein mass ratio of approximately 0.5, with protein concentration around 3.5 × 10−2 mg/mL. Our simulation results thus match the RNA/protein mass ratio at which the protein saturation concentration is lowest; however, our simulations overestimate the saturation concentration. Further, the phase diagram by Kaur et al.48 indicates that PS can occur at RNA/protein mass ratios greater than 3. These results suggest that our simulations underestimate the propensity for PS. We speculate that this discrepancy arises in part because the long RNA chains (>1000 nucleotides) used in the experiments exhibit a higher propensity for PS, but differences could also be due to ion-specific effects not captured by the DH model for these more charged mixtures. We also note that calculating the values for the left branch of the phase diagram of Figure 4d for 40 and 80 RNA chains is challenging because simulation convergence would require considerably longer simulation times or larger system sizes. We represent the x-axis values for RNA chains of 40 and 80 as 0.007, but we note that these exact numbers are not reliable. To further examine the accuracy of the protein−RNA interaction, we investigate whether our RNA model can reproduce the mixing and demixing behavior of the prion-like domain of FUS (PLP). Changes in the population of different RNA structures in the dilute and dense phases may also affect phase separation. Second (and related), the model does not capture the effects of the RNA sequence. Sequence-dependent force field optimization may provide the potential to represent precise RNA−amino acid interactions20 and base−base interactions that can drive PS of RNA.7,54,72 However, such refinement requires more experimental data on structures and PS from different RNA sequences than that currently available. In the current work, we have used data reporting on the radius of gyration, but note that SAXS curves contain additional information in the intermediate q-range that could also be used in a direct optimization. Third, while we have shown that the RNA model can be used together with the protein CALVADOS model, we stress that it does not capture all specific interactions between RNA and proteins; such interactions would be important to incorporate to study the role of domain structures in phase separation.73 Fourth, we observed that the cutoff distance of the DH potential affects both intra- and intermolecular interactions at a low ionic strength. We show this effect for RNA−RNA interactions using the radius of gyration (Figure S2) and the virial coefficient (Figure 2c), and for RNA−protein interactions using the phase diagram (Figure S5); however, more systematic investigations are required. We speculate that it may be beneficial to adjust the cutoff distance based on the ionic strength. Fifth, despite the speed of the CALVADOS models, sampling extremely low saturation concentrations (<1 μM) is currently very difficult within our molecular dynamics simulation framework. Estimating thermodynamic parameters for systems that phase-separate very strongly might require the development and application of enhanced sampling methods. Despite these limitations, we envisage that our RNA model will be useful in the study of the formation of protein−RNA condensates and their biochemical functions. Our simulations of MED1 condensates suggest that the RNA concentration not only influences condensation but also alters the selectivity for IDRs of transcriptional regulators. Since MED1 forms transcriptional condensates at superenhancer regions along with DNA sequences, RNA polymerase, coactivators, and transcription factors,74−76 our results further suggest that the amount of RNA production may be one of the origins of dynamically fluctuating transcription rates in transcriptional condensates.77 Overall, the incorporation of RNA chains enables us to simulate membraneless organelles in a context more closely mimicking the cellular environment, allowing us to study the molecular mechanisms underlying their cellular activities and the diseases caused by their dysfunction.

3. DISCUSSION We have developed a two-beads-per-nucleotide model for disordered single-stranded RNA that is consistent with the CALVADOS 2 HPS model for IDPs.43,45 We term this model CALVADOS-RNA and the current version and parameter set as version 1. We optimized the model parameters using a combined bottom-up and top-down approach, utilizing atomistic simulation and experimental data available from the literature. We showed that our model can capture several aspects of intermolecular RNA−RNA interactions and PS with IDRs using simple combining rules. We hypothesize that the CALVADOS-RNA model, despite its increased complexity and computational cost, offers some potential advantages over single-bead-per-nucleotide models. First, the mass and size of each bead are comparable to those in one-bead-per-residue protein models, potentially resulting in comparable energy scales for RNA−RNA and RNA−protein interactions. Second, the two beads represent the chemical nature with a polar and charged backbone and a more apolar set of nucleobases; the interactions with the charged backbone are dominated by the DH term, whereas the nucleobases interact via nonelectrostatic interactions. This resulting flexibility from separating these interactions may help us finetune the degree of RNA compaction in response to varying ionic strength. When compared to a nonsticky one-bead-pernucleotide model, CALVADOS−RNA captures more attractive short-range interactions, leading to a higher propensity for phase separation (Figure S6). A similar trend was observed in the one-bead-per-residue model COCOMO,46 suggesting that fine-tuning of stickiness parameters of a single-bead RNA model may result in a model with similar properties as our twobead RNA model. Future benchmarking and comparison of different models will likely also require additional quantitative data on the phase diagrams of protein−RNA systems. Our model has several limitations.
