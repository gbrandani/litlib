Catching active enhancers via H2B N-terminal acetylation

Check for updates

Specific chromatin features, especially histone H3 lysine 27 acetylation, are widely used to identify active enhancers, yet current methods are imprecise. New work suggests that histone H2B N terminus multisite lysine acetylation (H2BNTac) is a notable signature of active enhancers and could substantially improve enhancer prediction. In this issue of Nature Genetics, Narita et al.1 provide a promising new method to improve the accuracy of active enhancer prediction. Enhancers are cis-regulatory DNA elements that control when and where developmental genes are expressed2. Transcription factors bind to enhancers and activate target gene transcription through the formation of enhancer–promoter loops. The identification of enhancers for a particular gene is critical for understanding the temporal and spatial regulation of its expression during developmental processes. However, the task of identifying enhancers and enhancer–promoter pairs can be challenging because enhancers typically function at a distance from their target gene promoters. Additionally, one gene can be regulated by multiple enhancers, and one enhancer can engage with multiple genes. Transcriptional reporter assays are commonly used to determine if a DNA element can act as an enhancer, although when tested, the chromatin environments and topologies where the endogenous enhancers reside are different3. Earlier studies characterizing the genome-wide distribution of histone modifications through chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by sequencing (ChIP–seq) have established that unique histone modifications, such as histone H3 lysine 4 monomethylation (H3K4me1) and lysine 27 acetylation

(H3K27ac)4–7, are enriched at active enhancer regions. These studies provided the groundwork for using local chromatin signatures as a fast, simple method to predict enhancer location. Among all the enhancer-associated histone modifications, H3K27ac has attracted special attention, partially because H3K27 can also be methylated, which is linked with transcriptional silencing. However, challenges remain as no single histone modification alone is sufficient to distinguish enhancers from other elements — for example, H3K27ac is also enriched at active promoters — so additional features of enhancers and promoters, including binding of the Mediator complex component (MED1), DNA hypersensitivity sites (DHS), enhancer transcripts (eRNA), H3K4me1 and H3K4me3, are also required to define enhancers6. Identification of additional or more specific enhancer features may help to further improve enhancer mapping. In their study, Narita et al.1 establish histone H2BNTac as a hallmark of active enhancers and demonstrate that its use as a marker could improve the accuracy of enhancer prediction compared to the commonly used H3K27ac. Inspired by their prior observations that H2BNTac is catalyzed by p300 and CBP, which can also catalyze H3K27ac8, they asked whether H2BNTac is enriched at enhancers. Earlier research had reported that H2BK20ac, one of the H2BNTac marks, is associated with enhancers5,9. However, in the current study, Narita et al.1 obtained a more comprehensive picture of H2BNTac distribution across the genome by using antibodies specific to H2BK5ac, H2BK12ac, H2BK16ac and H2BK20ac. They found that H2BNTac is enriched at promoter-distal open chromatin regions where enhancer features, including H3K27ac and MED1, are also present. This indicates that H2BNTac localizes to enhancers (Fig. 1). Interestingly, unlike H3K27ac, which is also found at active promoters, H2BNTac is not enriched at constitutive promoters, and its presence at promoters does not correlate with gene expression levels but rather reflects tissue-specific genes being regulated by p300/CBP (Fig. 1). These findings suggest that H2BNTac can serve as a more accurate marker for the identification of putative active enhancers than H3K27ac. Both H3K27ac and H2BNTac are deposited by p300/CBP, so what causes the depletion of H2BNTac, but not of H3K27ac, at promoters? The authors found a negative correlation between H2BNTac and transcription elongation and observed an increase in H2BNTac at highly transcribed regions when transcription elongation was inhibited by a CDK9 inhibitor. Moreover, because H2A–H2B dimers are exchanged at a much higher rate during transcription, these results led the authors to hypothesize that the transcription-coupled H2A–H2B turnover at promoter-proximal and gene body regions results in the loss of H2BNTac. Despite the widespread use of histone post-translational modifications in defining enhancers, a major question is whether these post-translational modifications play an active role in regulating enhancer activity. Recent studies in mouse embryonic stem (ES) cells, either substituting the variant H3.3 with an H3.3K27R mutant10 or using CRISPR–Cas9-mediated base editing to generate pan-H3K27R mutant mouse ES cells with all H3K27ac eliminated11, convergently suggest that depleting H3K27ac does not affect enhancer function. Given that the catalytic activities of p300/CBP are essential for enhancer activity12,13, this raises the possibility that other or additional substrates of p300/ CBP, such as H2BNTac, support enhancer activities. Although challenging, because of the multiple acetylated lysines on the H2B tail, it would be interesting to investigate the effects of depleting H2BNTac on enhancer function. One way to start testing this could be to delete the N-terminal tail of H2B, similar to what has previously been done in yeast to show the essential role of multiple sites of histone H4 acetylation in transcriptional activation14.

It is worth noting that H2BNTac is not perfect for discriminating enhancers from promoters, because it is also enriched at p300/ CBP-regulated promoters. However, the H2BNTac/H3K27ac ratio is significantly higher at distal enhancers than at active promoters, and the H2BNTac/H3K27ac ratio could therefore potentially be a better indicator for identifying distal active enhancers. Further characterization of H2BNTac in various cell types and biological contexts should provide more insight into its roles in enhancer regulation. Importantly, however, the identification of H2BNTac as a robust mark of enhancer activity is of high interest and should be widely used by the scientific community.
