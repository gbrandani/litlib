Inhibition of p53 protein aggregation as a cancer treatment strategy Kalvin Kwan1, Omar Castro-Sandoval1, Christian Gaiddon2 and Tim Storr1 Abstract

The p53 protein plays a critical role in the prevention of genome mutations in the body, however, this protein is frequently mutated in cancer and almost all cancers exhibit malfunction along the p53 pathway. In addition to a loss of activity, mutant p53 protein is prone to unfolding and aggregation, eventually forming amyloid aggregates. There continues to be a considerable effort to develop strategies to restore normal p53 expression and activity and this review details recent advances in small-molecule stabilization of mutant p53 protein and the design of p53 aggregation inhibitors. Introduction Many proteins are prone to endogenous misfolding and conversion from a soluble folded protein into alternative oligomeric and fibrillar structures termed amyloids [1e3]. Susceptible proteins include amyloid-b (Ab), tau, TDP-43, TMEM106b, SOD1, and a-synuclein and are implicated in a wide range of diseases including Alzheimer’s disease (AD), Parkinson’s disease (PD), and amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) [4e6]. Similar to neurodegenerative diseases, protein misfolding and

aggregation play a role in cancer development, and this review focuses on misfolding of the tumor suppressor protein p53 [7-9**]. First reported in 1979 [10], the p53 protein is known as the “guardian of the genome” and plays a key role in the prevention of genome mutations in the body. The p53 protein is a tetrameric transcription factor that regulates a large number of cellular functions, including the expression of target genes responsible for apoptosis, DNA repair, and cell cycle arrest of damaged cells and cellular metabolism (Figure 1a) [11,12]. Its level of activity and selectivity on various target genes (ex. p21, PUMA) is tightly controlled by multiple post-translational mechanisms (ex. phosphorylation, acetylation) that impact on its conformation. The fact that over 50% of cancers result from point mutations directly to p53 [13,14], and the aggressive nature of cancers exhibiting p53 malfunction, notably gastric cancers [15], have identified restoration of normal p53 activity as a promising cancer treatment [16*-18]. Each p53 monomer can be divided into five key regulatory domains (Figure 1b) including the intrinsicallydisordered N-terminal transactivation domain (TAD), a proline-rich region (PRM), the DNA-binding domain (DBD), the oligomerization domain (OD), and the Cterminal domain (CTD) [19]. Hotspot mutations are shown in Figure 1b (black vertical lines), with relative frequency, indicating that amino acids at positions 175, 248, and 273 are most frequently mutated. It is estimated that the R273 amino acid is mutated in 9.45% of all cancers [20]. Critical to the stability of the DBD is a structural Zn, which is tetrahedrally coordinated by a histidine (H179), and three cysteines (C176, C238, C242) in a Zn-binding motif. Levels of p53 are tightly regulated via posttranslational modifications and key regulator mouse double minute 2 homolog (MDM2). However, under conditions of cellular stress p53 degradation is inhibited and the protein is concentrated in the nucleus [21,22]. This leads to the upregulation of target genes and initiation of response mechanisms to avoid propagation of damaged cells [23]. One example is in the presence of DNA damage, wherein p53 can either activate a reversible cell-cycle arrest process to allow for DNA repair, or lead to removal of damaged cells [11,12]. p53 mutation and aggregation Research has shown that the wild-type (WT) p53 DBD exhibits limited kinetic and thermodynamic stability [24], and many mutations cause further instability resulting in protein unfolding. The most common p53 DBD mutations are shown in Figures 1b and 1c and can be broadly classified as influencing DNA interactions or overall stability [25-27**]. The most frequently mutated DNA-contact amino acids are R248, R273, and R280, which impair the p53-DNA binding interaction while affording little change to the overall structure and stability of the protein [28]. In contrast, stability mutants induce conformational changes within the protein that can result in a loss of function due to decreased stability, protein unfolding, impaired Zn-binding, and aggregation [27**,29]. Cell studies with common mutants such as R175H, R248Q, R249S, and R282W show p53 aggregation as a common feature, with aggregates impairing the function of WTp53 [8,30,31]. p53 aggregation results in loss of function (LoF), and aggregated p53 exhibits deleterious gain of function (GoF) effects by reacting with WTp53 and also the homologous proteins p63/p73 to form amyloid oligomers and fibrils Current Opinion in Chemical Biology 2023, 72:102230

[9**,30-33]. The classification of p53 as an amyloidogenic protein is relatively recent, and amyloid aggregates are associated with more aggressive and invasive tumors [7,8,34]. While the mechanism of unfolding and aggregation is complex, destabilization causes exposure of an adhesive sequence (amino acids 251e258 in pink, Figure 1c), which binds to the same segment in other p53 units, resulting in aggregation. Therefore, the development of molecules that stabilize the folded structure and inhibit aggregation, or break up preformed aggregates, are of considerable interest (Figure 2) [16*,35].

Small-molecule stabilization of folded p53 A number of strategies have been developed to stabilize mutant p53 protein in its folded and active form. A series of Michael acceptors have been developed, including CP31398 and PRIMA-1 (Figure 3a), that were reported to restore native conformation to mutant p53 and induce p53-dependent cytotoxicity [36e38]. The primary targets of these molecules are accessible p53 cysteine residues such as C124 and C277. However, CP31398 displayed p53-independent activity and multiple offtarget effects. Interestingly, PRIMA-1 was shown to reverse R248Q mutant p53 aggregation in cancer cells, thus this compound reduces aggregation while also recovering function [38]. PRIMA-1Met has completed a number of clinical trials, and unfortunately, a recent phase III trial (NCT03745716) in combination with DNA damaging agent azacytidine failed to show an enhanced effect relative to azacytidine alone. MIRA-1 (Figure 3a) also acts as a Michael acceptor and was determined to primarily target an open pocket between loops L1 and L3, which contain C124, C135, and C141 as potential targets [39]. Similarly to CP-31398, this compound showed a mutant-p53 independent mechanism, exemplifying the difficulty in selectively targeting cysteine residues. An interesting recent development in this area was published by Lu et al.[*40] who showed that arsenic trioxide (As2O3) was an effective p53 stabilizing molecule that binds to the same cysteine residues as MIRA-1. As2O3, which has recently been approved as a treatment for acute promyelocytic leukemia, was found to restrict p53 aggregation and stabilize nineteen different structural p53 mutants, with a similar broad cytotoxicity across a panel of p53 mutant cell lines. In addition, a number of non-covalent small-molecule mutant p53 stabilizers have been reported by Fersht et al. [41,42], specifically targeting the p53-Y220C mutant. The Y220C mutant is one of the most common thermally unstable mutants, in which tyrosine to cysteine substitution creates an open cleft at the surface

of the protein, thus lowering stability and causing unfolding (Figure 1c) [42]. Molecules such as PhiKan083 (Figure 3b) were identified via a combination of in silico methods and fragment-based screening and were found to bind to the mutant cavity, refold mutant p53, and activate p53-dependent apoptosis and cell cycle arrest [41]. Further development of extended iodophenols such as PhiKan5196 (Figure 3b) enhanced pocket binding via a combination of halogen-bonding interactions and extension of the molecule further into the binding pocket [42]. This work has led to the commercial development of PC14586 by PMC Pharma (structure yet to be disclosed), a reversible small molecule binder for the p53-Y220C mutant pocket. PC14586 is showing promising results in a human trial [43]. Finally, stictic acid (Figure 3b) was shown to stabilize R175H and G245S mutants via high affinity binding in the open pocket between loops L1 and L3, thus acting as a non-covalent stabilizer in the same region as covalent binders MIRA-1 and As2O3 (vide supra) [44].

Protein aggregation inhibitors Mutant p53 aggregates have become a recognized drug target and a number of interesting strategies have been reported to either interrupt p53 aggregation or break-up insoluble aggregates to reactivate mutant p53 protein (Figure 3) [8,9**]. A major breakthrough in this area was the development of a cell-penetrating peptide ReACp53, which was designed to interact with the aggregation prone 252e258 segment (LTIITLE, see Figure 1c) and inhibit p53 aggregation [45**]. In our own work, we took inspiration from molecules designed to modulate amyloid-beta peptide aggregation and developed a set of multifunctional molecules that act as Zn metallochaperones (vide infra) and also inhibit mutant p53 aggregation (Figure 4a) [*48]. We showed that the iodinated analogue (LI) limited aggregation of the p53-Y220C mutant in solution and in NUGC3 cells, and increased p53 transcriptional function. Interestingly, when the iodine was substituted for hydrogen (LH) no aggregation inhibition was observed, and we hypothesized that the iodine substituent could interact with the exposed aggregation-prone, hydrophobic protein segment, which inhibited p53 aggregation. In addition, only the LI analogue demonstrated p53-dependent upregulation of P21 and NOXA target genes. In elegant work, Magzoub et al. recently reported the ability of ADH-6 (Figure 4a), a tripyridylamide previously shown to inhibit amyloid formation in AD and type II diabetes, to inhibit mutant p53 aggregation and dissociate mutant p53 aggregates in cancer cells [**49]. ADH-6 restores p53 function and results in regression of p53 mutant xenografts prolonging survival. The studies in this section highlight that similar approaches to inhibit amyloid formation are applicable across multiple diseases.

Zn binding and availability is critical for the maintenance of p53 activity, and therefore modulating cellular Zn concentrations has become an attractive strategy to reactivate p53 mutants [50]. In seminal work, Loh and co-workers have shown that p53 is delicately partitioned between folded and unfolded conformations in the cell, and available Zn is determined to be a major factor in folding and activity [27]. The in vitro administration of Zn-curcumin complexes (Figure 4b) resulted in an increase in intracellular Zn concentrations and was shown to increase proper folding and activation of the Znbinding mutants R175H and R273H [51,52]. In a major development, Carpizo et al. reported a series of thiosemicarbazones (Figure 4b) that restored WT structure and function to the R175H mutant via Zn metallochaperone activity [**53]. In this case, administration of the thiosemicarbazone ligand ZMC1 resulted in binding to extracellular Zn and transport across the cell membrane, leading to increased intracellular Zn concentrations. Administration of the pre-formed Zn thiosemicarbazone complex had a similar effect to ligand alone in the restoration of the R175H mutant. Further studies showed that the mechanism of action of these ZMCs is at least in part due to the generation of intracellular reactive oxygen species (ROS). ROS generation contributes to the reactivation of p53 via the induction of post-translation modifications, but can also result in

ROS generation was attributed to internalization of redoxactive Cu and/or Fe, and highlights the difficulty of selective metal ion binding and redistribution in cancer therapy. For example, recent work has shown that Cu uptake via metallochaperones can promote targeted lipoylation (and aggregation) of TCA cycle proteins leading to cell death [55]. In addition, Cu-catalyzed glutathione oxidation is accelerated by the thiosemicarbazone Dp44mT (Figure 4b), and the Cu complex of Dp44mT accelerates the production of hydroxyl radicals in comparison to Cu alone [56]. A structureeactivity relationship (SAR) study identified a benzimidazole framework (Figure 4b, C85) that exhibits a similar p53 reactivation profile to ZMC1 with lower ROS generation, which the authors ascribed to reduced Cu2þ affinity and lower Cu2þ/Zn2þ selectivity ratio [57]. We have focused on the development of multifunctional ligands designed to act as Zn metallochaperones, while also acting as mutant p53 stabilizers and/or aggregation inhibitors. Our first generation compounds (L4-L5, Figure 4b) incorporated an iodinated phenol to bind and stabilize the p53-Y220C mutant cavity, while also including a Zn-binding moiety for metallochaperone activity [58]. These compounds increased intracellular Zn, restored p53 transcriptional activity, and L5 induced mutant-selective apoptosis. Our second generation compound (LI see Figure 4a) increased intracellular Zn, inhibited mutant p53-Y220C aggregation (vide supra), promoted caspase-dependent and -independent cell death pathways, and was well-tolerated in a noncancerous intestinal organoid model [*48]. Even so, our multifunctional compounds exhibited cytotoxicity in WTp53 cancer cell lines likely due to ROS generation. We are now investigating design improvements that shift the Cu2þ/Zn2þ selectivity ratio to promote p53 mutant specific cytotoxicity.

Conclusions and outlook

(a) p53 aggregation inhibitors including cell penetrating peptide ReACp53, polyphenol resveratrol, tripyridylamide ADH-6, and thioflavin T analogues LI and LH. (b) Zn metallochaperones Zn-curcumin, thiosemicarbazones ZMC1 and Dp44mT, benzimidazole C85, and aminophenolates L4 and L5.

Due to the importance of the p53 protein for prevention of genome mutations in the body, and the fact that over 50% of cancers result from point mutations directly to p53, there has been widespread interest in the development of therapies to reactivate mutant p53. Targeting p53 mutants can be considered as the ultimate selective target to treat cancer as, i) p53 mutations occur often at an early stage of the tumorigenesis, and can therefore be considered as a driver mutation, ii) they can also occur at a later stage to favor metastasis, which is the cause of patient death iii) the mutation(s) are uniquely present in cancer cells, and iv) restoring p53 function favors efficacy of chemotherapy. Studies have shown that WTp53 exhibits limited kinetic and thermodynamic

[Page 6]

stability [24], and many point mutations cause further instability resulting in protein unfolding and aggregation. This review centers on the development of strategies to maintain the active WTp53 conformation and limit aggregation. This includes the development of covalent binders, which to this point have mostly focused on accessible cysteine residues. Future work in this area will benefit from more selective targeting of these agents to the p53 protein in order to limit offtarget effects. In addition, the development of noncovalent stabilizers has shown considerable promise with reports focused on molecules that bind preferentially to the mutant-induced cavity in the Y220C variant, or by binding to the open pocket between loops L1 and L3 (which is not associated to a specific mutant, and thus a more general strategy). As validation of this approach, PMC Pharma is pursuing clinical trials on a non-covalent stabilizer of the Y220C mutant. For the Y220C mutant only, which represents 2% of p53 mutation in thirty different tumor types, the estimated market is two billion dollars, generating significant investor interest. p53 mutations, while lowering stability, can also result in the loss of a structural Zn ion, and a novel bioinorganic strategy involves the use of Zn metallochaperones to re-populate the Zn site and promote re-folding and activation [27]. Overall, these efforts could provide an opportunity for synergism with DNA-damaging drugs via activation of p53-mediated cell death following DNA damage [59,60]. In addition to efforts to stabilize the folded active form of mutant p53, researchers have pursued the development of molecules that directly inhibit the aggregation process of mutant p53. This includes peptides and small molecules, and design features parallel recent drug development efforts for diseases in which amyloid formation is a common outcome. For example, cellular and biochemical studies show that protein aggregation and amyloid formation occurs in Alzheimer’s disease, Parkinson disease, amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, and prion disease. We look forward to new developments that target mutant p53 aggregation as a therapeutic strategy for cancer.
