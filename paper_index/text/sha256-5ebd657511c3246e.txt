Autonomous development of decorrelation filters in neural networks with recurrent inhibition H J J Jonker†§, A C C Coolen‡ and J J Denier van der Gon† † Helmholtz Institute, University of Utrecht, Princetonplein 5, NL 3584 CC Utrecht, The Netherlands ‡ Department of Mathematics, King’s College London, Strand, London WC2R 2LS, UK Received 4 December 1997 Abstract. We perform a quantitative analysis of information processing in a simple neural network model with recurrent inhibition. We postulate that both excitatory and inhibitory synapses continually adapt according to the following Hebbian-type rules: for excitatory synapses correlated pre- and post-synaptic activity induces enhanced excitation; for inhibitory synapses it induces enhanced inhibition. Following synaptic equilibration in unsupervised learning processes, the model is found to perform a novel type of principal-component analysis which involves filtering and decorrelation. In the light of these results we discuss the possible role of the granule-/Golgi-cell subnetwork in the cerebellum.

1. Introduction Recurrent inhibition is found to occur in many different places in the central nervous system, e.g. in the spinal chord through Renshaw cells, in the thalamus, in the hippocampus and in the cerebellum. In this paper we analyse the role of recurrent inhibition in a simple neural network model with binary neurons in Ising representation, i.e. neuron states are described by variables si ∈ {−1, 1}, with the identification si = 1 if neuron i fires, si = −1 if it is at rest. Dale’s principle states that all synapses emanating from one neuron release the same kind of neurotransmitter, see e.g. [1], so the simplest model of a recurrent inhibitory module will have the structure shown in figure 1. The layers of origin and destination of synapses are indicated with superscripts. Synapses are assumed to adapt according to Hebbian-type [2] rules. Hebb’s proposal, which was originally formulated for excitatory synapses only, states that correlated preand post-synaptic activity increases the strength of a synapse. It is thought to be realized through changes in the (relative) numbers of active ionic channels and/or a change in the amount of neurotransmitter released upon spike arrival. Excitatory synapses thus become more excitatory following correlated pre- and post-synaptic activity. The idea that plasticity also occurs for inhibitory synapses has not always been generally accepted [3, 4], but current insights in this matter are clearly changing [5–7]. If for inhibitory synapses it also holds that correlated pre- and post-synaptic activity, as before, increases their efficiency, then we must assume that inhibitory synapses become more inhibitory following correlated pre- and postsynaptic activity. We prefer to avoid the latter term, however, since it wrongly suggests a non-Hebbian mechanism. In this paper we shall first study in section 2 the behaviour of modules as in figure 1 with ‘fixed’ synapses, i.e. with stationary synaptic strengths that resulted from a previous supervised learning stage of the type (1). For large system sizes, their operation can be characterized in terms of dynamic equations for macroscopic observables, which can be solved explicitly in neuronal equilibrium. In section 3 we consider an unsupervised learning process in which both neuronal states and synaptic strengths are allowed to change in time. This complicates analysis considerably. The usual strategy in the literature is therefore to resort to the adiabatic limit, i.e. the neuronal dynamics is assumed to equilibrate before the neurons ‘feel’ that the synapses are not static. In order to go beyond general statements [17–19] or simulations [13, 20], however, one will actually have to calculate the state correlations of the neuron pairs that drive the synaptic adaptation. Apart from networks with linear neurons [21, 22], this can be done for the binary (Ising) neurons used in the present paper, as in [14, 23]. For large systems we shall be able to derive exact macroscopic equations describing the synaptic adaptation process and study quantitatively the information-processing properties of the synaptically equilibrated system. The final result of our analysis turns out to be quite satisfactory, in that it consists of mathematical relations for eigenvalue spectra of covariance matrices which are surprisingly simple, yet could not have been found via numerical simulations alone. At some stage we shall make a modest attempt to relate our results to the cerebellum. We choose the cerebellum because it is completely devoid of excitatory feedback (see figure 2). Much of the literature on the function of cerebellar circuits concentrates on the mossy-fibre/granule-cell/climbing-fibre/Purkinje-cell system, because this determines its ‘input’ and ‘output’. 2. Operation of recurrent inhibitory modules In this section we study the operation of recurrent inhibitory modules as in figure 1 with binary neurons and with fixed synapses, formed according to Hebbian-type rules. We study evolution equations for macroscopic order parameters and calculate their equilibrium solutions for the case where the neuron states in the first (input) layer are stationary.

3. Unsupervised Hebbian-type synaptic adaptation In this section we study the process of unsupervised Hebbian-type synaptic adaptation in recurrent inhibitory modules. Neuronal dynamics and synaptic dynamics are no longer decoupled, but we make the biologically plausible assumption that the characteristic timescales of synaptic evolution are much larger than those of the neuronal dynamics. We introduce macroscopic quantities and corresponding dynamical equations to describe the synaptic evolution. General discussion In this paper we have performed a mathematical analysis of information processing in recurrent inhibitory modules, in which both excitatory and inhibitory synapses are postulated to adapt according to Hebbian-type rules, which we interpret as referring to an increase in efficiency of synapses following correlated pre- and post-synaptic activity. The statistical properties of the states of the neurons in the input layer are assumed to be prescribed; the states of the neurons in the excitatory output layer and in the inhibitory layer are the result of a stochastic relaxation process. For stationary synapses formed according to Hebbian-type rules the present model turns out to share with the simplified two-layer model studied in [14, 23] the important property that the state correlations of the neuron pairs which are relevant for the modification of synaptic interactions can be calculated analytically. This enabled us to derive detailed analytical results, not only for the case of supervised learning, but also for the more complicated situation of unsupervised learning, where both neuron states and synaptic strengths are dynamical variables. In the latter case, the property that Hebbian learning rules preserve an initial separable structure of the synaptic matrices enabled us to describe the evolution in time of the synapses at a macroscopic level of order parameter matrices. The dynamical equations describing the evolution in time of these order parameter matrices could not be solved in general, however, in synaptic equilibrium we can express the eigenvalue spectra of the macroscopic covariance matrices in the various layers in terms of the eigenvalue spectrum of the macroscopic covariance matrix in the input layer. The synaptically equilibrated system turned out to perform a novel type of principal-component analysis, which involved filtering and decorrelation. Components in the input signal which are not sufficiently strong are suppressed, whereas those which are allowed through are in addition uniformly normalized. In the present study, Dale’s principle has not been fully taken into account, in that the adaptation rules used can, and generally will, lead to synaptic sign changes. However, this can be dealt with in a straightforward manner by initializing all synapses with a uniform value, sufficiently large to rule out future sign changes. In combination with an appropriate neuronal threshold (see e.g. [36]) one then usually finds the same macroscopic behaviour as in the model with zero or random synaptic initialization. We have not followed this procedure here for reasons of simplicity. Our study emphasizes the enormous potential of adaptation of inhibitory synapses, in addition to the generally accepted plasticity of the excitatory ones. The fact that inhibitory synapses are indeed modified appears to have been recently confirmed experimentally [5–7]. What the adaptation rules are, however, is, as far as we are aware, still largely unknown. We hope that the present study provides additional motivation for the latter question to be addressed experimentally.

4.2. The potential role of granule-/Golgi-cell subnetwork in the cerebellum The model presented in this paper was primarily inspired by a subcircuit of the cerebellum: the mossy-fibre/granule-cell/Golgi-cell circuit. As mentioned in the introduction, this subcircuit can be regarded as a separate, autonomously functioning module, which is clearly capable of mediating the input to the Purkinje cells (see figure 2). Here, we shall try to relate the results of our analysis to the cerebellum and discuss how the granule-/Golgi-cell subnetwork may contribute to the functionality of the cerebellum. First, an important result of section 2, unaltered in the next section, concerned the linear operation of the recurrent inhibitory module: the output of the system (i.e. layer b, to be associated with the granular cells) is at a macroscopic level always linearly related to the input, even though the states of the constituent elements, the neurons, are modelled as binary variables. This is a non-trivial and very important feature of negative feedback exerted by inhibitory action [14, 23]. Although inappropriate for content-addressable memories, such as Hopfield’s model [8], linearity is a very useful property for information transfer, particularly so because it allows for superposition of multiple signals [37], which in turn may facilitate parallel processing and integration of sensory information. However, we believe that the granule-/Golgi-cell subnetwork is capable of more than integrating and relaying sensory signals conveyed by mossy fibres. In view of the functional aspects of the cerebellum, an intriguing aspect of the present model is the behaviour that resulted from the unsupervised learning process. The system was found to evolve, purely on the basis of autonomous self-organization, into a non-trivial filter of input signals; the characteristics of this filter are determined solely by the statistical properties of the input signals. The resulting filter operates in such a way that components that have been significantly present in the input previously are retained, whereas the others are suppressed. As mentioned earlier, this way of filtering resembles principal-component analysis [38] (also known as empirical orthogonal functions (EOFs)), a method commonly used to extract relevant information from large data sets. However, in addition to filtering, the model also effectively decorrelates the components that are allowed to pass through (see for instance figure 3B or figures 5A and 5B). Of course, filtering alone cannot account for the operation of the cerebellum (the accurate control of motor activity); the main work is still to follow. Most probably, this task will be carried out by the Purkinje cells, since they provide the sole output of the cerebellar cortex. Therefore in some way the action of the Purkinje cells has to be adjusted† to the sensory input that converges upon their dendritic trees via the parallel fibres (output of layer b in the model). Based on our findings, we think that the contribution of the granule-/Golgi-cell subnetwork to this process could be twofold: first, by its filtering properties it can to a great extent reduce the abundance of sensory data with which the cerebellum is confronted via the mossy fibres and, second, it can deliver decorrelated data to the Purkinje cells. It is quite conceivable that this kind of preprocessed data is favourable for the Purkinje cell/climbing-fibre system. As already mentioned in the introduction, in this study we have not (yet) taken into account the interesting microscopic structure of the cerebellum. In order to focus on the effects of recurrent inhibition alone, we have restricted ourselves to the situation where the three neuron types are present in equal numbers, which for the cerebellum is a crude † With regard to a possible mechanism for adjusting Purkinje-cell action we refer to Marr’s theory [29] on the function of climbing fibres. Because the excitatory effect of climbing fibres is so dominant, they appear to be able to actually clamp the activity of the Purkinje cells, so as to control efficacies of the synapses between parallel fibres and Purkinje cells. See in this respect also Ito’s work on the vestibulo-occular reflex, e.g. [39].
