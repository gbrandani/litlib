p53 mutant aggregation can lead to loss-of-function (LoF), dominant-negative (DN) and gain-of-function (GoF) effects, involving in tumor growth. Finding inhibition methods of p53 mutant aggregation is a key step for developing new therapeutics against aggregation-associated cancers. Recent studies have shown that a cell-permeable peptide, ReACp53, can inhibit aggregation of the p53 mutant and restore p53 nuclear function as a transcriptional factor, showing extraordinary therapeutic potential. However, the molecular mechanism underlying the inhibition of p53 mutant aggregation by the ReAp53 peptide is unclear. In this work, we used all-atom molecular dynamics (MD) simulations to investigate the effect of ReACp53 peptide on the structural and dynamic properties of the p53 core domain (p53C) of the aggregation-prone R175H mutant. Our simulations revealed that the ReACp53 peptide can stabilize the ordered secondary structure and decrease the flexibility of disordered loops of the R175H mutant through increasing the intrainteractions of p53C. Moreover, we found that ReACp53 peptide specifically binds to the fragment (residues 180–233) of the R175H mutant through strong hydrophobic interactions with residues L188 and L201 and a salt bridge or hydrogen bond formation with residues D186, E198, D204, E221 and E224. have been shown to cross-seed the aggregation of wild type (WT) p53 and spread to other cells in a prion-like manner.24,30,31 They induce not only the aggregation of wild type p5332 but also the co-aggregation of its paralogs p63 and p73.33,34 Heterotetramers consisting of mutants and wild types show dominant-negative (DN) effects.35,36 Co-aggregation of p53 with p63 and p73 might activate new genes and exhibit gain-of-function (GoF) effects,24 which may increase cancer aggressiveness and progression.35,37 Hence, there is a very clear need for finding therapeutics to target p53 mutant aggregation.14,38 Many researches have shown that small molecule compounds and peptides have the potential to modulate p53 mutant aggregation.38–41 The PRIMA-1 molecule can reactivate mutant p53 by covalent binding to the DNA-binding core domain42 and also inhibits p53 mutant aggregation.43 Arsenic trioxide can rescue mutation-expedited aggregation of p53C, such as R175H and R249S mutants, by stabilizing the DNAbinding loop–sheet–helix motif alongside the overall b-sandwich fold.44 Polyarginine and its analogues can inhibit p53 mutant aggregation and cancer cell proliferation in vitro.45 Recently, a designed peptide with rich polyarginine, coined ReACp53, exhibited p53 mutant (R175H and R248Q) aggregation inhibition and tumor suppression in vitro and in vivo, showing tremendous therapeutic potential for aggregation-associated cancers.46,47 The peptide was originally designed to target the p53 aggregationprone region (residues 252–258) in p53C. However, the experiments performed by Wang et al.48 indicated that denatured p53 mutants may contain several aggregation-prone sequences and that the first three main segments contributing to the aggregation process of p53 are residues 182–213, 249–267 and 268–282. This means that the detailed molecular mechanism of ReACp53 peptide inhibiting the aggregation of mutant p53 is undefined. The hypothetical inhibition mechanism of ReACp53 peptide on mutant p53 might be accounted for by the fact that folded, partially unfolded, and aggregated p53 are in a dynamic equilibrium.49 Experiments have shown that ReACp53 can stabilize the p53–R175H mutant.46 It is reasonable to infer that when the R175H mutant transiently exhibits a wild-type conformation, the peptide might stabilize it by specific binding and gradually shifts the population equilibrium towards its wild-type conformation. This strategy indeed has been successful for many molecules in animal experiments.39,44,50,51 In addition, the ReACp53 peptide was also designed to mask aggregation core fragments, changing the dynamic equilibrium of p53 aggregation and preventing further aggregation.46 Hence, we were concerned with the stabilization of wild-type states by the peptide in R175H mutant systems and the exposure of aggregation core fragments. p53C displays similar thermodynamic stability and aggregation behavior to that of full-length p53.12,48 Here, to explain the inhibition mechanism of the ReACp53 peptide on p53 aggregation, we chose p53C as a system model and used all-atom molecular dynamics simulations to investigate the effect of the ReACp53 peptide on the structural and dynamic properties of the aggregateprone R175H mutant. Our results show that the R175H mutant exhibits a relatively higher disordered structure, a larger

These conformational properties can result in a higher propensity to aggregate.52 The aggregation inhibitor ReACp53 can stabilize the R175H mutant in the native conformation of WT p53C via increasing the internal interaction of p53C and decreasing the interaction with water molecules. Materials and methods WT p53C, R175H mutant and R175H mutant + ReACp53 peptide Three protein systems (WT p53C monomer, R175H mutant monomer, and R175H mutant monomer with an ReACp53 peptide) are studied. The initial coordinate of WT p53C was obtained from the solution structure (PDB ID: 2FEJ).18 To maintain the integrity of the experimental structure, we chose residues 94–297 as the p53C model. Considering the absence of the experimental structure, starting states of the R175H mutant (without and with ReACp53) were generated by mutating the residue R175 at the corresponding sites of WT p53C and were shown as folded WT states. The N-terminus and C-terminus of p53C were respectively capped by acetyl (ACE) and amine (NH2) to mimic the uncharged state of the two terminus residues in the full-length protein. In accordance with neutral experimental pH conditions, the side chains of lysine and arginine were protonated (Arg+ and Lys+), and the side chains of glutamic acid and aspartic acid were deprotonated (Glu and Asp). Histidine with protonation on ND1 were residues 178, 214, and 233, while those with protonation on NE2 were residues 115, 168, 179, and 193.53 Si-Ming Liao et al. suggested that the protonation states of histidine are associated with environmental residues through multiple interactions.54 Considering that the protonation of NE2 is more likely to promote the formation of hydrogen bonding between R175H and E180 (Fig. S1, ESI†), the NE2 atom of R175H mutation is protonated. We used the bonded model of zinc55 and covalently bonded it to these four residues: Cys176(SG), Cys238(SG), Cys242(SG) and His179(ND1). The ReACp53 peptide consists of an aggregation-prone fragment LTRITLE with an N-terminal polyarginine cell-penetrating tag (R = 9), followed by a three-residue linker derived from the p53 sequence (RPI).46 ReACp53 peptides are placed in six different directions of the R175H mutant for 6 MD simulations (MDs) (Fig. S2, ESI†).

Simulation details Twelve 1000 ns MD simulations (3 MDs for WT systems, 3 MDs for R175H mutant systems, and 6 MDs for hybrid systems of the R175H mutant with a ReACp53 peptide) were performed using the GROMACS-9.1.3 software package. We chose the AMBER99SBILDN force field,56 which has been widely used in p53 protein research.31,57 The p53C protein is placed in a box filled with TIP3P water, with a minimum distance of 1.2 nm between the protein and the box edges. To keep the charge neutrality, neutralizing ions Na+ and Cl were added to each system. All MD simulations were performed under an NPT ensemble. The pressure was kept at 1 bar using the Parrinello– Rahman method and the temperature was maintained at 310 K using a velocity-rescaling coupling method.58,59 The electrostatic interactions were calculated using the particle mesh Ewald (PME) method with a real space cut-off of 1.0 nm.60 The van der Waals interactions were treated using a cut-off of 1.0 nm. Constraints were applied to all-bond lengths using the Settle algorithm61 for water molecules and the LINCS method62 for the protein and peptide. The integration time step is 2 fs. Analysis methods Trajectory analysis was carried out using the facilities implemented in the GROMACS V.9.1.3 software package and our in-house codes. The DSSP program was used to calculate the secondary structure of p53C.63 Gromacs tools were used to analyze the backbone root-mean-square-derivation (RMSD), the C root-mean-square-fluctuation (RMSF), the solvent-accessible surface area (SASA) and the number of hydrogen bonds. Our in-house codes were used to calculate the residue–residue contact number, the probability of a salt bridge, and the number of water molecules. Here, contact was considered if the distance between two carbon atoms of non-sequential residues lies within 0.54 nm or if the distance between any other two atoms of non-sequential residues lies within 0.46 nm.64–66 A salt bridge was formed if the distance between the charge center of the charged side chain of four residues, including ARG+, LYS+, GLU and ASP, is r0.4 nm.67 The water number was counted if the distance between the water molecules and protein is within 0.35 nm.31 We also estimated the binding free energy between the ReACp53 peptide and the R175H mutant using the molecular mechanics/linear Poisson–Boltzmann surface area (MM/PBSA) method implemented in the free AMBER tools package.68 The binding energy is calculated as: DGbinding = DEvdW + DEelec +DGpolar + DGnon-polar. Here, EvdW and Eelec are, respectively, the van der Waals and the electrostatic interaction energies in a vacuum. Gpolar and Gnon-polar are the electrostatic and non-electrostatic contributions in the solvent.

The R175H mutant exhibits more coil structure and higher loop flexibilities than WT p53C, while the ReACp53 peptide can stabilize the structure of the R175H mutant. As seen in Fig. 1a, the structure of p53C consists of a large immunoglobulin-like b-sandwich scaffold with eleven b-strands (b1–b10) and a conserved DNA-binding surface, which includes a loop–sheet–helix motif (Loop 1, b2, b2 0 and Helix 2) and two large loops (Loop 2, and Loop 3) coordinating a zinc ion.16 The R175H mutation is located in the Loop 2 region. We first explored the effect of R175H mutation on the structure of p53C by calculating the average probabilities of the secondary structure over the MD runs for each system (Fig. 1b). Compared with WT p53C, the probability of the coil structure of the R175H mutant increases and the probabilities of the b-sheet, bend, turn and helix (including the 3-, a- and 5-helix) reduce. These phenomena indicate that R175H mutation can induce the structural transition from an ordered state to a disordered state. When the ReACp53 peptide was introduced into the R175H system, the probabilities of all order structures of the R175H mutant increased and the probability of the coil structure decreased. We noted that the length of Helix 1 (residues 177–182) obviously increases in R175H + ReACp53 systems, as seen in Fig. 1c. These results indicate that the R175H mutation indeed influences the native structure of p53C, but the ReACp53 peptide can rescue the secondary structure of the R175H mutant. We then investigated the influence of the R175H mutation and the ReACp53 peptide on the conformational flexibility of p53C by calculating the average Ca-RMSFs (Fig. 1d). The Loop 1 (L1: residues 113–124), Loop 2 (L2: residues 163–194) and Loop 3 (L3: residues 237–250) regions show a higher flexibility than the eleven b-sheet regions in the WT p53C system. The R175H mutation mainly induces an increase of the flexibility of four loop regions including Loop 2, Loop 3, the loop between b6 and b7, and the loop between b7 and b8. However, the ReACp53 peptide effectively weakens the effect of the mutation on these loops and decreases their flexibility, especially for Loop 2 and Loop 3. The enhanced flexibilities of Loop 2 and Loop 3 are shown in Fig. 1e. These results indicate that the peptide inhibitor can promote the structural stability of p53C. Certainly, the possibility that the peptide captures a partially unfolded state of the R175H mutant and directly brings it back to the WT state cannot be excluded. Our results also show that the ReACp53 peptide specifically binds to the region (residues 180–233)

[Page 9]

through strong hydrophobic interactions with some hydrophobic amino acids and forming rich salt bridges or hydrogen bonds with negatively charged amino acids of the R175H mutant, thus directly decreasing the exposure of the aggregationprone region (residues 182–213). The positively charged arginine of the ReACp53 peptide plays a key role in this specific binding pattern due to electrostatic attraction, while the negatively charged glutamic acid residue is repulsive to the binding region. Hence, we guessed that mutations of the negatively charged residue to the positively charged residue may facilitate the binding. Moreover, we noted that the complex formed by the ReACp53 peptide and the R175H mutant has a strong positive net charge, which is not good for trapping wild-type p53 protein with the same charge due to electrostatic repulsion. This would lead to the change in the aggregation equilibrium and preventing further aggregation. As a result, our results revealed the binding sites of the aggregation inhibitor ReACp53 peptide with the R175H mutant and offered mechanistic insight into the inhibition of ReACp53 on the aggregation of the p53–R175H mutant.
