The Rockefeller University, New York, New York 10021 The interaction with point ions of a line charge supplemented by a distance of closest approach is studied by means of the Mayer theory of ionic solutions. A number of points involved in a recent theory of polyelectrolyte solutions (Papers I and II of this series) are thereby clarified. It is demonstrated that the counterion condensation phenomenon occurs in the limit of zero concentration, that the Debye-Hiickel approximation is applicable to the coions and uncondensed counterions, and that the relevant dielectric constant is that of the pure bulk solvent.

In Papers I and II of this series1.2 a theory of polyelectrolyte solutions was presented which is strikingly similar to the classical Debye-Hlickel-Bjerrum theory of solutions of simple electrolytes. All deviations from the Debye--Hlickel approximation (superposition of screened Coulomb potentials) were attributed to the "condensation" of counterions on the polyions, the extent of condensation being calculated from a singularity in the configuration integral. The result is a theory whose parameters are fixed on an a priori basis and which is in excellent agreement with measurements of a wide variety of equilibrium and transport properties. The model used for the polyion, an infinitely long line charge, is not entirely realistic, however, because it lacks short-range repulsive forces. Moreover, the aforementioned singularity in the configuration integral occurs precisely at the lower limit of the integral, that is, when the counterion (taken as a point charge) is separated from the line charge by an infinitesimally small distance. There is a real question, therefore, as to whether the condensation phenomenon is not merely an artifact of the model. On the other hand, certain results of the theory were shown to be identical with previous calculations based on solutions of the PoissonBoltzmann equation for impenetrable cylinders, as long as the limit of low concentration is taken in the latter. It was concluded that the line charge model, with its associated condensation phenomenon, yields results valid as "limiting laws." For further clarification, the problem will here be investigated from the point of view of the Mayer theory of ionic solutions,3-5 which is generally agreed to be the best explanation of why the Debye--Hlickel

theory of simple electrolytes is a limiting law. It will be seen that the condensation phenomenon is not an artifact, but exists even when the line charge model is supplemented by a "distance of closest approach." In addition, the validity of the use of the bulk dielectric constant, simply assumed in Papers I and II, will be demonstrated. An earlier theory based on the Mayer approach6 ,7 is now understood to be highly approximate and is regarded by the author as being superseded by the present series of papers.
